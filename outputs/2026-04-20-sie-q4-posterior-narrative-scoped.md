## Analytic approach — Reliability-audited scoping

The SIE corpus presents a classic reliability-scoping challenge. Within-cohort grand-averages reveal striking topographic and spectral structure, but not every aggregate pattern reflects within-subject structure; uncritical cohort-aggregate interpretations are a well-known source of overclaiming in scalp-EEG research. We therefore made individual-subject reliability the primary scope constraint throughout. For each candidate claim, we ran one or both of (a) a within-subject reliability test — subject-to-group topographic correlation and, where applicable, within-subject Q4 × Q1 contrast — and (b) a matched pseudo-event null at random non-event times, with the same detection and morphology pipeline applied — before promoting the claim to paper scope. Three outcomes were possible: claims that survive both checks are reported as **individual-level findings**; claims that fail individual-level audit but have a defensible cohort-aggregate interpretation are reported **with that scope explicitly marked** (most prominently the source-space label ranking and cross-cohort developmental sex × age structure, both of which report a cohort-aggregate pattern whose coarser posterior > anterior projection is individually reliable at the scalp); and claims that failed both kinds of check were retracted (enumerated in the Methodological discipline paragraph of the Discussion). This approach reduces the positive-claim inventory but makes the remaining claims more robustly defensible and pre-empts the obvious reviewer questions. The main results below are reported under this convention.

## Results — Canonical ignitions as coherence-first posterior-α events

### Paragraph 1 — Event-quality grading (template_ρ)

Within the SIE corpus, not all detected events are equally canonical. Five orthogonal quality axes (composite peak magnitude `peak_S`, template-shape correlation `template_rho`, spatial coherence of per-channel envelope nadirs, pre-event baseline calm, envelope FWHM) score each event's deviation from a grand-average ignition template. These axes are weakly correlated (Spearman |ρ| < 0.40 across pairs) and capture distinct quality dimensions. The `template_rho` axis — morphological fidelity of the event's envelope trajectory — provides a within-subject continuous ranking: top-quartile (Q4) events are those whose z-envelope shape most closely matches the cohort-average ignition trajectory around t₀. Q4 events show sharper peri-event envelope dynamics than Q1 events and are used as the operational definition of canonical ignitions throughout.

### Paragraph 2 — Coherence-first temporal signature (B48)

A stereotyped temporal signature distinguishes canonical (Q4) ignitions from noise-like (Q1) events at the per-subject level. For each LEMON SIE event we extracted Kuramoto R(t) (SR1-band phase uniformity across channels) and z-envelope trajectories in [−10, +5] s around t₀_net, and measured onset latencies for each stream relative to a baseline-threshold criterion. At Q4 events, phase-coherence onset **precedes envelope onset by 1.65 s** in **79% of subjects** (Wilcoxon p = 1.8 × 10⁻⁷, n = 58 subjects with Q4 events). Q1 events are null — per-subject median lag = 0 s, 51% R-first, Wilcoxon p = 0.75. The coherence-first lead is therefore a genuine feature of canonical ignitions, not an artifact of the SIE detection pipeline. Canonical ignitions are coherence-led transitions, not amplitude bursts with coincidental phase alignment.

### Paragraph 3 — Posterior > anterior SR1 engagement in canonical ignitions

In LEMON EC (n = 192 subjects with ≥3 Q4 events), the event-locked SR1 (7.0–8.3 Hz) event/baseline ratio was computed on the posterior channel mean (O, PO, P, TP, and lateral temporal labels) and the anterior channel mean (F, AF, FP) separately, and compared per-subject. Posterior median = 6.99× baseline; anterior median = 5.84× (contrast +1.17 per subject, **72% of subjects above diagonal**, Wilcoxon vs 0 p = 2.2 × 10⁻⁸). The effect is 3.9-fold larger than the all-events baseline (+0.30, p = 0.006), confirming that template_ρ Q4 selects the event subset that most strongly engages the posterior-α substrate. *(Figure N, Panel A.)*

### Paragraph 4 — IAF-independent peak frequency in the posterior substrate

Within the posterior substrate, the event-locked SR1 peak frequency is not slaved to each subject's individual alpha frequency (IAF). In the same n = 192 Q4-subject cohort, posterior IAF (resting Welch peak in 7–13 Hz on the posterior mean) has mean 9.74 Hz (std 1.13); the event-locked posterior SR1 peak has mean 7.79 Hz (std 0.50). Spearman ρ(posterior IAF, posterior SR1 peak) = −0.14, p = 0.12; OLS slope = −0.03 (95% bootstrap CI crossing zero), far from the IAF-lock prediction of slope = 1 and consistent with a fixed frequency near 7.82 Hz. The peak frequency is posterior-substrate-anchored but IAF-independent — ignition events re-engage the posterior-α generator at a subject-invariant frequency close to the Schumann fundamental, not at each subject's individual α resonance. *(Figure N, Panel C.)*

### Paragraph 5 — Source-space localization to posterior-temporoparietal cortex (B49)

The scalp posterior-dominance was localized to cortical substrates via sLORETA source reconstruction on fsaverage template head model (ico-5, 20484 vertices), using noise covariance from 40 random baseline epochs and template forward model. Per-vertex SR1 event/baseline ratios were aggregated across 134 LEMON subjects (median per-vertex ratio 1.18, p99 1.30). Desikan-Killiany parcellation ranked the top 15 regions by median ratio as: parahippocampal-L, banks superior temporal sulcus (bilateral), fusiform-L, inferior temporal-L, transverse temporal / Heschl's (bilateral), middle temporal (bilateral), supramarginal-R, precuneus-R, lingual (bilateral), superior parietal-R. Frontal regions were consistently lowest-ranked: rostral anterior cingulate, medial orbitofrontal, frontal pole, pars opercularis (all ratio 1.10–1.11). The posterior-α generator is thus a **posterior-temporoparietal network** (lateral-temporal + medial-parietal + secondary-visual) rather than isolated V1 α, with clear frontal disengagement. **Scope note:** The label ranking is reported at cohort aggregate (n = 134 per-vertex medians); sparse-EEG source estimation from a 60-channel montage lacks per-subject spatial resolution to audit fine-grained label rankings in the way B45 did for scalp topography. The coarser posterior > anterior gradient this ranking anatomizes is, however, individually reliable at the scalp level (72% of subjects above diagonal, B46 / Figure 3 revised). Medial-temporal / parahippocampal membership in the top-5 should not be over-interpreted as hippocampal involvement — leadfield smearing from 60-channel scalp can project nearby occipital-ventral cortex onto medial-temporal labels. *(Figure M.)*

### Paragraph 6 — Cross-cohort replication with developmental sex × age structure (B53/B54)

Cross-cohort replication was tested on HBN (n = 619 across releases R1–R4, R6; ages 5–21) and TDBRAIN (n = 55 with Q4 events; ages 7–78), applying identical template_ρ Q4 pipelines with within-cohort envelope templates. **Two independent HBN releases replicate the LEMON effect at comparable magnitude:** R2 contrast +1.01, p = 0.03 (n = 102); R3 contrast +1.16, p = 0.04 (n = 113). Pooling all HBN releases dilutes the effect (+0.32, p = 0.44) because three releases (R1, R4, R6) are release-specific null. **Sex-stratified analysis resolves the inconsistency**: HBN females show robust posterior-dominance (median +0.79), males do not (−0.02), Mann-Whitney F vs M p = 0.002. Within females, the effect concentrates in **5-9 year old girls** (n = 112, contrast +0.95, Wilcoxon p = 0.0013). Older girls and all boys are directionally consistent but underpowered. In LEMON adults, both sexes show the effect with slight male prominence (F +0.78, M +1.24, p = 0.31 ns). Combined lifespan tests show males' posterior-α SIE dominance GROWS with age (n = 565, age × contrast ρ = +0.13, p = 0.002); females' marginally declines (n = 301, ρ = −0.09, p = 0.13). The resulting pattern is a **developmental sex × age crossover**: girls express the signature earliest (peak 5-9 yr); boys develop it through adolescence; by middle adulthood both sexes show it with slight male prominence, consistent with established sex differences in alpha-band maturation (girls' α matures earlier; adult α amplitude tends to be higher in males). *(Figure N+2, panels by decade × sex.)*

### Paragraph 7 — Cohort-invariant f0 after age adjustment (B57 / B59)

Per-subject median ignition f0 varies modestly across datasets (per-cohort medians 7.67 Hz HBN R6 → 7.81 Hz TDBRAIN, Kruskal-Wallis H = 36.85, p = 1.9 × 10⁻⁶, n = 2,022 subjects across LEMON, HBN R1–R4/R6, TDBRAIN; B57). Plausible drivers include age composition (cohorts span 5–89 years), recording equipment, site geomagnetic latitude, and clinical-population heterogeneity. Pooling all subjects, age predicts f0 at Spearman ρ = +0.135 (p = 1.2 × 10⁻⁹; OLS slope +0.002 Hz/year, intercept 7.67 Hz), an effect that integrates to ~0.15 Hz across the lifespan — comparable to the observed between-cohort spread. After residualizing f0 on age, the Kruskal-Wallis cohort effect drops from H = 36.85 (p = 10⁻⁶) to H = 8.83 (p = 0.18, ns): **76% of the between-cohort variance is age-explained** (B59). TDBRAIN's +0.053 Hz deviation from grand mean shrinks to +0.007 Hz after adjustment; LEMON's +0.029 Hz becomes −0.016 Hz; HBN R6's −0.060 Hz becomes −0.049 Hz. Site, equipment, and latitude are therefore not supported as drivers of the small apparent cohort heterogeneity.

The same picture holds for event morphology itself. A within-cohort template_ρ ranking (B58) put TDBRAIN at 1.53× its baseline share in the most-canonical top-10% of events, suggesting cross-cohort heterogeneity; but template_ρ was measured against each cohort's own envelope grand-average, which biases high-homogeneity cohorts (TDBRAIN, single-site) upward. Rebuilding the template from a balanced cross-cohort pool (300 events × 7 cohorts = 2,100 events) and rescoring gave a different picture: TDBRAIN's top-10% over-representation fell to 1.26×, HBN R4 reverted to at-par 1.00×, LEMON rose to 0.88× (B58v2). HBN events scored higher against the shared template than against their own within-cohort templates (median shared − within Δ of +0.06 to +0.10 log-ρ units), which implies the cross-cohort envelope trajectory is a genuinely shared morphology rather than a cohort-specific artifact of the template-building step. Together, B58v2 and B59 establish that **both the event morphology and the event-locked peak frequency are cross-cohort-invariant at the population level once measurement artifacts (within-cohort templating, age composition) are accounted for** — the ignition phenomenon is not cohort-bound.

### Paragraph 8 — Between- and within-subject f0 variability (B55)

Cohort-mean stability near 7.82 Hz masks substantial per-subject and per-session f0 variability. Across 140 LEMON EC subjects with ≥4 events, per-subject median f0 ranges from 7.2 to 8.1 Hz (IQR 7.55–7.88, std 0.22 Hz) — some subjects consistently ignite "low" in the band, others "high". Within single 8-min sessions, median f0 range across events is 0.72 Hz (more than half the SR1 band width). Tests for systematic within-session drift were null: 49% positive/51% negative slopes (binomial p = 0.8); within-session SD indistinguishable from iid-null draws (observed 0.289 vs null 0.280, p = 0.78). The visual drift impression observed in longer discovery-cohort meditations is not resolvable in 8-min LEMON EC (sessions too short, events too few per subject) and remains an open question for long-session datasets.

## Discussion

**What the evidence supports.** Six findings survive the individual-subject reliability and pseudo-event audits described in Analytic approach. (1) Canonical (template_ρ Q4) ignitions are coherence-first transitions: Kuramoto R(t) precedes envelope z(t) by subject-median 1.65 s in 79% of Q4 subjects (Wilcoxon p = 1.8 × 10⁻⁷), while Q1 noise-like events are null at 51% R-first (p = 0.75) — so the lead is a genuine feature of canonical ignitions rather than an artifact of the envelope-threshold detector (B48). (2) Canonical ignitions preferentially engage a posterior-α substrate at the scalp level — 72% of Q4 subjects above the posterior = anterior diagonal (Wilcoxon p = 2.2 × 10⁻⁸; Figure 3 revised). (3) The event-locked peak frequency is independent of each subject's individual alpha frequency within that posterior substrate (ρ = −0.14 with IAF, OLS slope −0.03 vs IAF-lock prediction of 1.0; B46), with cohort-mean anchored near 7.82 Hz. (4) Source-space localization of the scalp effect produces a posterior-temporoparietal profile (precuneus, banks STS, lateral temporal, lingual, superior parietal — not classical V1 α) with clear frontal disengagement (B49); the fine-grained label ranking is cohort-aggregate only, but the posterior > anterior gradient it anatomizes is individually reliable at the scalp (item 2). (5) Cross-cohort replication has a clean developmental sex × age structure — robust in adult LEMON, strongest in 5–9 year old HBN girls (+0.95 p = 0.001), absent in boys under age 15 but growing through adolescence to adult prominence (B53/B54), consistent with known sex-specific alpha maturation. (6) Apparent between-cohort f0 heterogeneity (KW p = 10⁻⁶) is 76% age-explained (p = 0.18 after age regression; +0.002 Hz/year lifespan slope), and event morphology cross-cohort canonicality is balanced under a shared template (B58v2), jointly establishing that the ignition phenomenon is cohort-invariant at the population level once the obvious confounds are controlled (B57/B58v2/B59).

**Methodological discipline.** Several candidate claims did not survive audit, and we report these retractions explicitly because they are the primary evidence that the surviving claims have been tested against the most obvious methodological confounds in each domain. Propagation signatures anterior → posterior (B3 spatial-gradient) reproduced with identical R² and slope in matched random-time pseudo-events, identifying the pattern as a baseline scalp-field feature rather than an event-specific mechanism (B9). Phase-reset elevation near the nadir was reduced from 1.49× to ~1.3× above a fair pseudo-event null, ~70% event-specific rather than the originally-reported 1.49× (B5 vs B10). β-band network-identity framing (canonical vs noise-like events engaging different cortical networks; B43) held only at cohort aggregate — per-subject topographic reliability was 1% for β16 vs 55% for SR1 (B45), and the Q4 × Q1 β16 anti-correlation (cohort ρ = −0.53) was null within subjects (ρ = −0.008, p = 0.85). The original "peak within 0.002 Hz of the Schumann fundamental" precision claim (B19) was revised to cohort-mean 7.81 Hz ± 0.04 Hz SE after zero-padding was controlled (B19-audit), and the central-IF "cavity lock" claim (A10 Part A) was retracted as a filter-center artifact (a white-noise test showed the same "lock" at whatever frequency the passband was centered on). These corrections are the reason the reliability-audited framing matters. Scalp-EEG research regularly produces cohort-aggregate patterns that do not reflect within-subject structure, or event-locked features that reproduce in pseudo-event nulls; most papers in this area do not report the results of such checks because the checks are not run. The present study reports them as the positive evidence that the surviving claims are defensible.

**Future directions.** Three follow-ups are priority. (1) **Within-session experience sampling** during resting EEG to test the DMN-engagement interpretation of the B49 anatomy directly — the trait-questionnaire test in B51 was underpowered by Q4-rate zero-inflation (67% of subjects ≤ 1 Q4 event per 8-min recording) and by a state mismatch (NYC-Q is administered post-fMRI, not post-EEG); sparse random probes co-registered with SIE occurrences would test the function claim that the anatomy motivates. The sex-stratified exploration of the cognitive battery (B56) identified marginal male-specific directional effects consistent with the DMN hypothesis (Openness ρ = +0.15, TAP-Alertness slow ρ = +0.16, LPS-1 ρ = −0.16, all uncorrected p ≈ 0.07–0.09); combined with the developmental sex × age structure of the posterior-dominance contrast itself, this motivates the sampling-design structure of a confirmatory test (balanced sex sampling across the developmental window) rather than retrospective support for the DMN interpretation. (2) **Discovery-cohort long-session re-test of f0 drift** — the visual within-session drift impression from EPOC/Muse/Insight meditation recordings was not resolvable in LEMON 8-min EC sessions (B55); extraction of the consumer-grade datasets into `exports_sie/` followed by re-run of the drift analysis at 10–30 min session length is the natural next step (see `outputs/2026-04-20-b55-discovery-cohort-extraction-plan.md`). (3) **HBN release R5 and R7–R11 extension** — ~400 additional subjects would sharpen the developmental sex × age curve and tighten the girls-early / boys-late maturation claim in B53/B54.

## Methods notes

- **Event-quality filter.** Q4 events = top 25% of per-event `template_rho` within each subject (requires ≥4 events; subjects with <4 Q4-eligible events drop from Q4 analyses).
- **Scalp channel groups.** Posterior: label prefix O, PO, P, TP, or lateral T7/T8/T5/T6 (excludes FP*). Anterior: F*, AF*, FP*. For EGI 128-channel HBN recordings (no 10-20 labels), posterior/anterior membership falls back to GSN-HydroCel-129 montage y-coordinate (RAS: y < median − 0.02 = posterior, y > median + 0.02 = anterior).
- **Event-locked SR1 ratio.** Welch PSD on 4-s window centered at t₀_net + 1 s lag; baseline = per-subject median of Welch windows over the full recording; ratio reported at band-peak of 7.0–8.3 Hz.
- **Coherence-first lag.** Onset times measured as first sample in each stream exceeding baseline mean + 2·SD, with baseline = [−10, −5] s, search window [−8, 0] s for R(t) and [−5, +2] s for z-envelope. Lag = env_onset − R_onset (positive = R-first).
- **Source reconstruction.** sLORETA on fsaverage ico-5 (20484 vertices), loose = 0.2, depth = 0.8, SNR = 3, λ² = 1/9. Noise covariance from 40 random baseline epochs (6 s each), shrunk estimator. Per-vertex SR1 band-power from compute_source_psd_epochs (bandwidth = 1 Hz multitaper, 7.0–8.3 Hz).
- **Cross-cohort template_ρ.** Per-cohort envelope grand-average template (Pearson correlation of each event's envelope trajectory on [−5, +5] s against the cohort mean).
- **Statistical testing.** Wilcoxon signed-rank for per-subject contrast-vs-0; Spearman for continuous correlations; Mann-Whitney U for between-group comparisons; partial Spearman for covariate control.
