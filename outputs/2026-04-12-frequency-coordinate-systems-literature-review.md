# Coordinate Systems for EEG Frequency Bands: Literature Review

## 1. The Natural Logarithm Linear Law (N3L) -- Penttonen & Buzsaki

**Citation:** Penttonen & Buzsaki (2003). Natural logarithmic relationship between brain oscillators. *Thalamus & Related Systems*, 2(2), 145--152.

**Coordinate system:** Center frequencies of brain oscillation bands form an arithmetic progression on the natural logarithmic scale. The ratio between neighboring band centers is approximately Euler's number e (~2.72). This is the "Natural Logarithm Linear Law" (N3L).

**Proposed bands:** Ultra-slow through ultra-fast oscillations, spaced by factor ~e on a log scale. The framework encompasses oscillations from ~0.025 Hz to ~600 Hz.

**Within-band structure:** Not explicitly addressed. The framework defines band *centers* and implies bandwidth scales proportionally, but does not provide a principled within-band coordinate.

**Empirical validation:** Observational -- compiled known oscillation frequencies from mammalian electrophysiology and showed they fall on the log-linear progression. No formal statistical test of the spacing constant.

**Theoretical basis:** Biophysical -- each frequency band is generated by a distinct biological mechanism. The irrational spacing ratio (e) ensures minimal cross-frequency interference, preventing spurious synchronization between neighboring bands.

**Key insight:** This was the first formal proposal that the spacing between EEG bands is governed by a specific mathematical constant, not arbitrary convention.

## 2. Buzsaki's Oscillatory Hierarchy and Neural Syntax

**Citations:**
- Buzsaki & Draguhn (2004). Neuronal oscillations in cortical networks. *Science*, 304, 1926--1929.
- Buzsaki (2006). *Rhythms of the Brain*. Oxford University Press.
- Buzsaki, Logothetis & Singer (2013). Scaling brain size, keeping timing: Evolutionary preservation of brain rhythms. *Neuron*, 80(3), 751--764.

**Coordinate system:** Log-frequency hierarchy where oscillation classes are evenly spaced on a logarithmic scale, preventing mutual entrainment and cross-talk. The phase of slower oscillations modulates the power of faster ones (cross-frequency coupling), creating a hierarchical nesting structure.

**Within-band structure:** Addressed through cross-frequency phase-amplitude coupling -- the *phase position* within a slow oscillation cycle determines when bursts of faster oscillations occur. This provides a temporal coordinate within each cycle that carries information (the "neural syntax" framework).

**Empirical validation:** Extensive. Theta-gamma coupling in hippocampus, ripple oscillations nested in sharp waves, cross-species comparisons showing preserved frequency ratios despite thousand-fold variation in brain size.

**Theoretical basis:** Biophysical and evolutionary. Brain size scaling preserves oscillation frequencies through deployment of large-diameter axons. The hierarchical structure supports a "neural syntax" -- letters (gamma cycles) grouped into words (theta cycles) grouped into sentences (slower modulations).

**Key insight:** Despite several-thousand-fold increase in brain volume during mammalian evolution, the hierarchy of brain oscillations is remarkably preserved.

## 3. The Golden Ratio Framework -- Pletzer, Roopun, Kramer

### 3a. Pletzer et al. (2010)

**Citation:** Pletzer, Kerschbaum & Klimesch (2010). When frequencies never synchronize: The golden mean and the resting EEG. *Brain Research*, 1335, 91--102.

**Coordinate system:** Classical EEG frequency bands form a geometric series with ratio phi (1.618...). Band centers at approximately: delta ~3, theta ~5, alpha ~8, beta1 ~13, beta2 ~21, gamma ~34 Hz -- resembling the Fibonacci sequence.

**Within-band structure:** Not explicitly addressed, but the framework implies that the *ratio* between any two frequencies is more fundamental than their absolute position.

**Empirical validation:** Analyzed resting EEG and showed that the spectral peaks in standard bands approximate a geometric phi series. Demonstrated mathematically that phi-related oscillators can *never* synchronize their excitatory phases.

**Theoretical basis:** Mathematical -- phi is the "most irrational" number, meaning oscillators at phi-related frequencies achieve maximal desynchronization. This is optimal for the resting state, where independent processing channels should not spuriously couple.

### 3b. Roopun et al. (2008)

**Citation:** Roopun et al. (2008). Period concatenation underlies interactions between gamma and beta rhythms in neocortex. *Frontiers in Cellular Neuroscience*, 2, 1.

**Coordinate system:** In vitro neocortical oscillations show that gamma (~40 Hz) and beta2 (~25 Hz) have a frequency ratio approximating phi. When excitatory drive decreases, these concatenate into beta1 (~15 Hz) via period addition (1/beta1 = 1/gamma + 1/beta2).

**Within-band structure:** Demonstrates mechanistic transitions *between* bands via period concatenation, which inherently produces phi-ratio relationships.

**Empirical validation:** Direct electrophysiological recordings from rat cortical slices showing the period concatenation mechanism in real neural tissue.

**Theoretical basis:** Biophysical -- period concatenation is a concrete neural circuit mechanism that generates phi-spaced frequencies from the interaction of two oscillatory populations.

### 3c. Kramer (2023)

**Citation:** Kramer (2023). Golden rhythms as a theoretical framework for cross-frequency organization. *Neurons, Behavior, Data Analysis, and Theory*. (Also available as arXiv:2111.09953.)

**Coordinate system:** Frequencies organized as f_k = f_0 * phi^k, where f_0 is a base frequency and k is an integer index. Starting from 40 Hz: ...15.3, 25, 40, 64.7, 104.7 Hz.

**Within-band structure:** Explicitly addressed. The key innovation is that triplets of golden-ratio-spaced frequencies satisfy f_{k+2} = f_{k+1} + f_k (the Fibonacci recurrence), which means they automatically support cross-frequency *integration* (the sum frequency equals the next band). This provides a principled relationship between bands, not just separation.

**Empirical validation:** (1) Simulations of 8-node coupled oscillator networks showing selective cross-frequency coupling at golden-ratio frequencies. (2) Literature survey of hippocampal frequency pairs showing clustering near phi (~1.6) and phi^2 (~2.6). Code available at github.com/Mark-Kramer/Golden-Framework.

**Theoretical basis:** Mathematical -- phi uniquely satisfies the constraint that frequencies must be simultaneously maximally *segregated* (for multiplexing independent channels) and hierarchically *integrable* (for cross-frequency coupling when needed). No other ratio achieves both.

## 4. Klimesch's Binary Hierarchy and Brain-Body Architecture

**Citation:** Klimesch (2018). The frequency architecture of brain and brain body oscillations: an analysis. *European Journal of Neuroscience*, 48(7), 2431--2453.

**Coordinate system:** A binary hierarchy where center frequencies follow fd(i) = 1.25 * 2^i Hz:
- fd(0) = 1.25 Hz (heart rate, ~75 bpm)
- fd(1) = 2.5 Hz (delta)
- fd(2) = 5 Hz (theta)
- fd(3) = 10 Hz (alpha)
- fd(4) = 20 Hz (beta)
- fd(5) = 40 Hz (gamma)
- fd(6) = 80 Hz (high gamma)

Band *boundaries* are then defined using phi: upper limit of fd(i) = fd(i+1)/phi, lower limit = fd(i-1)*phi. This yields non-overlapping bands:
- Delta: 2.0--3.1 Hz
- Theta: 4.0--6.2 Hz
- Alpha: 8.1--12.4 Hz
- Beta: 16.2--24.7 Hz

**Within-band structure:** Partially addressed. Klimesch distinguishes two coupling mechanisms: (1) amplitude/envelope modulation (any m:n ratio), and (2) phase-phase coupling (requires harmonic/integer ratios). The binary (2:1) ratio between neighboring center frequencies supports stable phase coupling, while phi-defined boundaries ensure separation.

**Empirical validation:** Strong. (1) IAF-heart rate correlation: predicted IAF = HR * 2^3 = 9.44 Hz vs measured 9.87 Hz. (2) Respiratory frequencies at 0.30, 0.15, 0.07 Hz matching predicted sub-bands. (3) Theta-alpha coupling at 6.6:13.2 Hz (1:2 ratio) in MEG/EEG data. (4) BOLD fMRI fluctuations matching predicted ultra-slow bands.

**Theoretical basis:** Hybrid -- binary (factor-2) center frequencies for phase coupling, golden ratio boundaries for band separation. Unifies brain and body oscillations into a single system. Explains 1/f spectral slope as emergent from frequency jitter reflecting E/I balance.

**Key insight:** Uses phi and 2 for *different purposes* -- 2 for coupling, phi for separation. Extends the frequency architecture below 1 Hz to encompass heart rate, respiration, and blood pressure oscillations.

## 5. Individualized Alpha Frequency (IAF) -- Klimesch and Colleagues

**Citations:**
- Klimesch (1999). EEG alpha and theta oscillations reflect cognitive and memory performance: a review and analysis. *Brain Research Reviews*, 29(2--3), 169--195.
- Corcoran et al. (2018). Toward a reliable, automated method of individual alpha frequency (IAF) quantification. *Psychophysiology*, 55(7), e13064.

**Coordinate system:** Bands are anchored to each individual's alpha peak frequency (IAF). From the IAF, bands are defined:
- Upper alpha: IAF to IAF + 2 Hz
- Lower alpha 1: IAF - 2 to IAF Hz
- Lower alpha 2: IAF - 4 to IAF - 2 Hz
- Theta: TF - 2 to TF (where TF = individual transition frequency between theta and alpha)

**Within-band structure:** Yes -- the distinction between upper alpha (desynchronization related to semantic processing) and lower alpha (attentional processes) is itself a within-band coordinate anchored to IAF.

**Empirical validation:** Extensive across decades of work. IAF varies 4--13 Hz across individuals and correlates with cognitive performance, age, genetics, and clinical status. The transition frequency (TF) between theta and alpha ranges from ~4--7 Hz across individuals. Fixed-band analyses miss these individual differences.

**Theoretical basis:** Data-driven/functional -- different sub-bands of alpha serve different cognitive functions, and their boundaries vary across individuals. The IAF serves as the anchor point because it represents the dominant thalamocortical resonance frequency.

**Key insight:** IAF varies by ~2 Hz across healthy adults. Using fixed 8--12 Hz bands means some individuals' theta is miscounted as alpha and vice versa. Two estimation methods: peak alpha frequency (PAF) and center of gravity (CoG), the latter being more robust when peaks are weak.

## 6. gedBounds -- Cohen (2021)

**Citation:** Cohen (2021). A data-driven method to identify frequency boundaries in multichannel electrophysiology data. *Journal of Neuroscience Methods*, 347, 108949.

**Coordinate system:** Purely data-driven, subject-specific. No fixed coordinate system is proposed -- instead, the method *discovers* boundaries from each individual's data by identifying clusters of spatiotemporal similarity across frequencies.

**Method:** (1) Compute generalized eigendecomposition (GED) of narrowband vs broadband covariance matrices at each frequency. (2) Correlate the resulting spatial filters across all frequency pairs. (3) Apply DBSCAN clustering to the correlation matrix to identify frequency clusters. Boundaries are where cluster membership changes.

**Within-band structure:** Implicitly, yes -- the spatial filter correlation matrix reveals the internal structure of each band (how topography varies within a band).

**Empirical validation:** Recovers ground truth in simulated data. Applied to Parkinson's patients vs controls -- detected group differences in frequency boundaries that fixed bands miss. MATLAB code publicly available on GitHub.

**Theoretical basis:** Data-driven -- no theoretical commitment to phi, e, or any other constant. The method is agnostic about *why* bands fall where they do.

**Key insight:** Different individuals and clinical populations may have genuinely different band boundaries. The method provides higher precision for subject-specific boundaries than any fixed system.

## 7. Other Data-Driven Approaches

### 7a. Decision Tree Band Discovery -- Talebi et al. (2022)

**Citation:** Talebi, Waczak, Fernando, Sridhar & Lary (2022). Data-driven EEG band discovery with decision trees. *Sensors*, 22(8), 3048.

**Method:** Uses sklearn DecisionTreeRegressor to find optimal frequency boundaries that best reproduce the power spectrum for a given number of bands, then uses an AIC-inspired score to determine the optimal number of bands. Open-source Python code available.

**Validation:** Applied to resting-state EEG. Recovers boundaries that sometimes match and sometimes diverge from convention, depending on the dataset.

### 7b. FOOOF/specparam -- Donoghue et al. (2020)

**Citation:** Donoghue et al. (2020). Parameterizing neural power spectra into periodic and aperiodic components. *Nature Neuroscience*, 23(12), 1655--1665.

**Relevance to band definitions:** While not a band-definition method per se, FOOOF/specparam fundamentally changes how we think about bands by separating aperiodic (1/f) activity from periodic (oscillatory) peaks. This reveals that many apparent "band power" differences actually reflect changes in the aperiodic exponent, not oscillatory activity. Over 1500 citations in 5 years.

**Key insight:** Any principled band definition system must first separate periodic from aperiodic components -- otherwise "band power" conflates two distinct physiological signals.

## 8. Spectral Fingerprints -- Keitel & Gross (2016)

**Citation:** Keitel & Gross (2016). Individual human brain areas can be identified from their characteristic spectral activation fingerprints. *PLOS Biology*, 14(6), e1002498.

**Coordinate system:** Each anatomically defined brain area has a unique spectral fingerprint -- a characteristic pattern of oscillatory peaks and their relative power that can identify that region.

**Within-band structure:** Strongly addressed. Each brain area shows multiple spectral modes (peaks at different frequencies with different durations), and the *combination* of these modes is characteristic. The fingerprint is not just "which band" but the specific shape of the spectrum including sub-band structure.

**Empirical validation:** MEG resting-state data from healthy adults. Automatic classification of brain areas from their spectral fingerprints achieved significant accuracy -- test data were reliably assigned to the correct brain area based on training data fingerprints.

**Theoretical basis:** Empirical/anatomical -- spectral organization reflects the biophysical properties and connectivity of each cortical area.

## 9. Frequency Gradients Follow Cortical Hierarchy -- Mahjoory et al. (2020)

**Citation:** Mahjoory, Schoffelen, Keitel & Gross (2020). The frequency gradient of human resting-state brain oscillations follows cortical hierarchies. *eLife*, 9, e53715.

**Coordinate system:** The dominant peak frequency in each brain area decreases along the posterior-anterior axis, from fast (alpha-band) in sensory cortex to slow (theta-band) in prefrontal cortex. This gradient is anticorrelated with cortical thickness (a proxy for hierarchical level).

**Within-band structure:** Yes -- the peak frequency *within* the alpha/theta range varies systematically with cortical position, providing a spatial coordinate for within-band frequency variation.

**Empirical validation:** N = 187, resting-state MEG, mixed-effects models. Robust posterior-anterior gradient replicated across subjects.

**Theoretical basis:** Anatomical/hierarchical -- frequency reflects the cortical processing level, linking oscillatory organization to the Felleman & Van Essen cortical hierarchy.

## 10. Ursachi (2026) -- Phi-Organization in Human EEG

**Citation:** Ursachi (2026). Golden ratio organization in human EEG is associated with theta-alpha frequency convergence: a multi-dataset validation study. *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*.

**Coordinate system:** Introduces the Phi Coupling Index (PCI), which quantifies whether an individual's theta-alpha centroid ratio is closer to phi (1.618) or to the harmonic 2:1 ratio. Positive PCI = phi-organized; negative PCI = harmonically organized.

**Key results:**
- Mean theta-alpha ratio: 1.677 (N = 320)
- 80% of subjects show phi-organization (PCI > 0)
- PCI strongly correlated with theta-alpha frequency convergence: r = 0.54 (p < 10^-25), Spearman rho = 0.82
- High-phi subjects: theta = 6.24 Hz, alpha = 9.75 Hz
- Low-phi subjects: theta = 5.85 Hz, alpha = 10.20 Hz
- Frontal theta validation: r = 0.718

**Within-band structure:** Partially -- the PCI captures how the *centroids* of theta and alpha relate, which reflects within-band peak positioning.

**Empirical validation:** Two independent datasets (PhysioNet EEGBCI N=109, LEMON N=211). Effect exceeded null model by >5 SD. Replicated across datasets.

**Theoretical basis:** Mathematical -- extends Pletzer and Kramer's golden ratio framework with a quantitative individual-differences measure.

## 11. Ursachi (2026, preprint) -- Band Boundaries Converge Near e-1

**Citation:** Ursachi (2026). Data-driven EEG band boundaries converge near Euler's number: A multi-method analysis across 244 subjects. *Research Square* preprint, rs-8935579.

**Coordinate system:** Three independent data-driven methods (FOOOF, spectral derivative, cross-frequency topographic correlation) all place empirical band boundaries such that the ratio between successive boundaries approximates e - 1 = 1.718.

**Empirical boundary values (derivative method):**
- Delta-theta: 4.27 +/- 0.73 Hz
- Theta-alpha: 7.73 +/- 1.29 Hz
- Alpha-beta: 13.50 +/- 1.97 Hz
- Beta-gamma: 25.26 +/- 4.82 Hz

**Key finding:** Conventional boundaries (e.g., 8/13 = 1.625) approximate phi, but data-driven boundaries shift toward e - 1. The boundary ratio (1.826) and centroid ratio (1.788) are statistically equivalent (d = 0.075), demonstrating self-similar spectral architecture.

**Critical methodological point:** Power analysis shows N < 30 samples systematically favor phi, while adequately powered samples converge toward e - 1, suggesting some published reports of golden-ratio organization may reflect insufficient statistical power.

**Theoretical basis:** Data-driven, with connections to scale-free biological organization. The convergence toward e - 1 (rather than phi or 2) represents a novel claim distinct from both the Penttonen/Buzsaki and Pletzer/Kramer frameworks.

## Key Tensions and Open Questions

1. **Phi vs e vs e-1:** The three candidate constants (phi = 1.618, e - 1 = 1.718, e = 2.718) make different predictions. Phi governs *center frequency ratios* in some frameworks (Pletzer, Kramer) and *boundary definitions* in others (Klimesch). Penttonen/Buzsaki propose e for center spacing. Ursachi's preprint argues data-driven boundaries converge on e - 1, and that phi-findings may reflect underpowered samples. These are not necessarily contradictory -- centers and boundaries could follow different constants.

2. **Within-band structure is under-theorized.** Most frameworks define band *boundaries* or *centers* but say little about the organization *within* a band. Exceptions: IAF-anchored sub-bands (upper/lower alpha), spectral fingerprints (multi-modal within-band structure), and the cortical frequency gradient (continuous variation of peak frequency within broad bands).

3. **Individual vs population coordinates.** IAF-anchored and gedBounds approaches define subject-specific coordinates. Phi/e-based frameworks define population-level constants. These need not conflict -- individual variation may occur around a population-level attractor defined by phi, e, or e-1.

4. **Aperiodic confound.** Any band-definition system operating on raw power spectra is confounded by the aperiodic (1/f) component. FOOOF/specparam separation should be a prerequisite for empirical band-boundary estimation.

5. **Brain-body extension.** Klimesch's framework uniquely extends below 1 Hz to heart rate, respiration, and blood pressure oscillations, suggesting the frequency architecture is not brain-specific but organism-wide.
