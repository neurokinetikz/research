# Neural Resonance and Entrainment

Neural resonance is the frequency-specific amplification of brain responses when the nervous system is stimulated at frequencies matching its intrinsic oscillatory preferences, while entrainment is the process by which external rhythmic stimulation synchronizes ongoing neural oscillations to its frequency and phase. Together, these phenomena reveal that the brain is not a passive receiver but a tuned system with preferred operating frequencies. Research demonstrates intrinsic resonance peaks at specific frequencies in both cortical and subcortical structures, frequency-specific effects of sensory entrainment on cognition and memory, and inter-brain synchronization during social interaction that concentrates at [[alpha-oscillations]] frequencies. These findings have direct implications for [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]] and support the view that [[canonical-eeg-bands]] reflect physical constraints rather than arbitrary clinical conventions.

## Intrinsic Neural Resonance Frequencies

Zhang et al. (2025) provided direct experimental evidence for intrinsic neural resonance by systematically sweeping pulsed transcranial near-infrared light across 10-200 Hz in mouse brain. Neural responses -- measured via cerebral blood flow, oxygenated hemoglobin, ECoG, and hippocampal spiking -- exhibited pronounced peaks at 60-80 Hz and 120-140 Hz, with both peaks persisting under anesthesia (amplitude reduced by ~20-30%; see [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]]). The persistence under anesthesia demonstrates that these resonance frequencies are intrinsic to neural network structure, not emergent from particular activity states. The ratio between the two peaks (~1.9) and the hippocampal CA1 response being strongest at 120-130 Hz (aligning with the [[sharp-wave-ripples|sharp-wave ripple]] range) suggest these resonant modes map onto functional frequency regimes important for memory consolidation. The multi-modal convergence -- hemodynamic and electrophysiological measures agreeing on the same peaks -- rules out vascular artifacts and confirms genuine neural resonance. A small-world computational model qualitatively reproduced the dual-peak pattern, supporting the interpretation that network topology constrains resonant modes (see [[critical-frequencies]]).

## Visual Sensory Entrainment and Memory

Ostrowski et al. (2025) provided the first causal evidence that pre-stimulus alpha-band entrainment (9 Hz) enhances crossmodal associative memory formation, while theta entrainment (5 Hz) showed no behavioral effect despite successful neural modulation. In this pre-registered study of 105 participants, visual sensory entrainment at 9 Hz boosted recognition sensitivity (d' = 1.46 vs 1.18 for controls, BF = 3.29) by increasing hit rates (61.5% vs 49.8%, BF = 22.57) without changing false alarm rates. The functional dissociation between alpha and theta entrainment suggests that [[alpha-oscillations]] serve as a preparatory gating mechanism that enhances encoding quality, while theta may require active engagement during stimulus processing rather than merely preparatory enhancement. This is consistent with the broader principle that different [[canonical-eeg-bands]] serve distinct computational roles.

## Alpha-Theta Cross-Frequency Gating of Entrainment

Wang et al. (2026) demonstrated that pre-stimulus alpha power (8-12 Hz) modulates the success of theta-frequency (4 Hz) rhythmic sensory stimulation entrainment, which in turn predicts episodic memory formation. Using MEG with 24 participants, they showed that trials whose actual phase matched the intended stimulation phase were preceded by lower alpha power than trials that deviated (cluster p = 0.035, d = 0.53). Low pre-stimulus alpha benefited memory only in the synchronous condition -- in the anti-synchronous condition, the beneficial effect of attention (low alpha) was cancelled by enhanced entrainment to a detrimental phase relationship. Source localization placed the alpha effect in posterior parietal cortex, with enhanced theta-frequency phase locking between PPC and hippocampus in successfully entrained trials. This cross-frequency gating -- alpha state determining theta entrainment success -- provides mechanistic evidence for the hierarchical organization described in [[cross-frequency-coupling]].

## Auditory-Tactile Entrainment and Speech Processing

Guilleminot and Reichenbach (2022) demonstrated that vibrotactile pulses delivered at the syllabic rate (~4.5 Hz) enhance speech-in-noise comprehension by 6.3% when temporally aligned with perceptual centers of syllables. Comprehension followed a sinusoidal function of audiotactile delay at the syllabic frequency, with peak at -17 ms delay (p = 0.035). Neural multisensory gain peaked at ~114 ms latency in bilateral auditory cortex, and the late pulse response (277 ms) showed delay-dependent modulation at the syllabic frequency. The central mechanism is cross-modal phase reset: somatosensory input resets the phase of slow neural oscillations in auditory cortex, modulating syllable parsing. This is consistent with Dynamic Attending Theory, which proposes that [[attention-and-perception]] waxes and wanes rhythmically, with events at attentional peaks receiving preferential processing.

## Inter-Brain Coupling and Social Entrainment

Dikker et al. (2021) collected EEG from thousands of participants during naturalistic face-to-face interactions, revealing that inter-brain coupling occurs at specific frequency bands: relationship duration predicted coupling at 7-8 Hz (alpha), social closeness at 21-22 Hz (beta), and eye contact at 9-11 Hz (alpha peak). Changes in low-frequency (7-8 Hz) power correlations co-varied with changes in high-frequency (20-21 Hz) imaginary coherence (r = 0.28, p = 0.017), suggesting a coupled oscillatory hierarchy. Using noise-immune metrics (imaginary coherence and projected power correlations) that exclude instantaneous artifacts, the study confirmed these represent genuine neural synchronization. Goldstein et al. (2018) similarly found that brain-to-brain coupling during handholding occurred only in the 8-12 Hz alpha-mu band, with coupling strength correlating with pain reduction (r = -0.56). The convergence on [[alpha-oscillations]] frequencies for inter-brain coupling suggests these frequencies are optimized for neural synchronization both within and between individuals.

## Theta and Spatial Memory Entrainment

Guth and Baker (2026) demonstrated that right posterior theta (5-8 Hz), sourced to the parahippocampal gyrus, facilitates spatial memory encoding during virtual navigation. Reward cues produced significantly larger theta power than non-reward cues (F = 54.25, p < 0.001, eta-squared = 0.68), and individual theta power during encoding predicted recognition sensitivity (d', p = 0.024) and response bias (p = 0.018). Importantly, encoding theta predicted later memory while recall theta did not, paralleling the "coherence-first" dynamics observed in other oscillatory phenomena where preparatory states precede functional outcomes. The 5-8 Hz range spans the theta-alpha boundary region, encompassing both the theta attractor (~6 Hz) and the fundamental frequency (~7.5 Hz) that is central to [[hippocampal-memory]] processing across species.

## The Entrainment vs Evoked Response Debate

A central unresolved question in the field is whether rhythmic neural responses to periodic stimulation reflect genuine **entrainment** -- the synchronization of endogenous oscillations to an external driver -- or merely superimposed **evoked responses** that produce similar phase-locked patterns without involving intrinsic oscillatory dynamics. Duecker et al. (2024) provided a comprehensive review of this debate in the Journal of Neuroscience, identifying multiple challenges ranging from ambiguous definitions to methodological difficulties in disentangling endogenous oscillations from other stimulus-related mechanisms. The two hypotheses make distinct temporal predictions: evoked responses are typically maximal at stimulus onset and habituate subsequently, whereas a genuine oscillatory entrainment process requires time to build up and can persist beyond the cessation of stimulation. Duecker et al. (2024) highlighted recent advances in overcoming these challenges, including computational modeling, insights from dynamical systems theory, sophisticated stimulus designs that break periodicity to test for oscillatory persistence, and the study of neuropsychological impairments that selectively disrupt one mechanism but not the other. An important convergence point is that both mechanisms likely coexist, with their relative contributions varying by frequency, modality, and brain state.

Evidence from music neuroscience illustrates the complexity of this distinction. A 2025 study demonstrated that tempo-dependent selective enhancement of neural responses at the beat frequency can be mimicked by both an oscillator model and an evoked response model, underscoring the difficulty of disentangling the two mechanisms from surface EEG alone (Tal et al., 2025). However, the entrainment account gains support from findings that neural phase alignment to rhythmic stimuli persists during gaps and silences -- a pattern difficult to explain by evoked responses alone. The field is converging on the view that definitive resolution requires converging evidence from multiple paradigms rather than reliance on any single diagnostic criterion.

## Spectral Tuning and After-Effects

Kessler et al. (2024) provided evidence for dedicated, flexible, and adaptive mechanisms underlying neural entrainment by documenting two previously underappreciated phenomena: **spectral tuning** and **after-effects**. Using EEG recordings during visual entrainment at 10 Hz, they found that entrainment not only produced phase alignment and power increases at the stimulated frequency, but also a marked decrease in power at neighbouring, non-stimulated frequencies -- a "tuning" effect that increases the signal-to-noise ratio at the entrained rhythm. This tuning mechanism suggests the brain actively sharpens its frequency selectivity during entrainment, potentially enhancing communication at that specific oscillatory channel. Crucially, Kessler et al. (2024) also observed clear after-effects: following 10 Hz entrainment, a marked decrease in tuning around the previously entrained frequency occurred when new stimulation was presented, analogous to neural adaptation in sensory systems. Strong phase effects were largely specific to the entrained frequency and its harmonics, persisting beyond stimulus duration across all electrodes. These findings demonstrate that periodic stimulation interacts with endogenous [[alpha-oscillations]] rhythms rather than simply superimposing on or resonating with them, supporting the view that entrainment involves active adjustment of oscillatory dynamics.

## Arnold Tongue Properties of Neural Circuits

The concept of the **Arnold tongue** -- the V-shaped region in frequency-intensity parameter space within which a driven oscillator synchronizes to an external stimulus -- has become a central framework for understanding neural entrainment. When stimulation frequency closely matches an intrinsic oscillatory frequency, even weak stimulation can entrain the neural oscillator; as frequency detuning increases, stronger stimulation is required for entrainment to occur. Trajkovic et al. (2024) demonstrated that resting-state EEG biomarkers, particularly the spectral characteristics of [[individual-alpha-frequency]] activity, can predict individual differences in the success of rhythmic TMS entrainment, effectively serving as a proxy for an individual's Arnold tongue. The width of the resting-state alpha peak predicted the range of frequency offsets over which TMS could shift the intrinsic frequency, while the height of the alpha peak negatively predicted amplitude enhancement during stimulation at the individual alpha frequency. These results offer both a theoretical model for explaining the variance across different rhythmic brain stimulation studies and a potential biomarker for personalizing stimulation protocols.

Park et al. (2026) extended the Arnold tongue framework computationally by constructing a cortical microcircuit model with realistic neuronal morphologies, synaptic connectivity, and intrinsic oscillatory dynamics. Their model revealed that stimulation at 8 and 12 Hz -- frequencies closer to the endogenous alpha rhythm -- produced stronger entrainment than more distant 5 Hz stimulation, consistent with Arnold tongue predictions. Importantly, the model demonstrated that the Arnold tongue broadens significantly when the network's endogenous coherence is low, suggesting that brain states with weaker intrinsic oscillations are paradoxically more susceptible to external entrainment across a wider frequency range. The intensity-specific effects were markedly non-linear: at low intensities (<0.3 mV/mm), tACS desynchronized neural firing relative to endogenous oscillations, while at higher intensities (>0.3 mV/mm) neurons became entrained to the exogenous electric field. This non-linear threshold has important implications for [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]] dosing.

## Rhythmic Brain Stimulation (tACS) Entrainment

**Transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS)** entrains neural activity by inducing subthreshold oscillatory fluctuations in resting membrane potentials, causing neural firing to become rhythmically locked to the external oscillation. A comprehensive review by Li et al. (2025) documented that tACS has produced enhanced symptoms and improvements in psychopathology across neurocognitive disorders (11 of 11 studies), psychotic disorders (11 of 14 studies), and depressive disorders (7 of 8 studies), with the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex being the most common stimulation target (30.2% of published studies). The field has expanded beyond standard sinusoidal tACS to encompass three categories of non-standard applications: phase synchronization techniques (in-phase, anti-phase, and travelling wave stimulation), non-sinusoidal waveforms (composite, broadband, or triangular oscillations), and amplitude-modulated tACS combined with temporal interference stimulation that permits concurrent EEG recording and deeper cortical targeting.

Growing evidence demonstrates sustained after-effects of tACS, suggesting the induction of long-term neuroplastic changes that extend well beyond the stimulation period. Repeated sessions appear to reinforce and consolidate these effects, analogous to long-term potentiation mechanisms, which emphasizes the therapeutic potential of tACS for conditions involving oscillatory dysfunction (see [[clinical-applications]]).

## Entrainment for Cognitive Enhancement

Theta-frequency tACS has emerged as a promising tool for [[working-memory]] enhancement. Brauer et al. (2024) investigated multi-session peak-coupled theta-gamma [[cross-frequency-coupling]] tACS administered to the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex in 77 older adults over 16 sessions across six weeks. The applied paradigm consisted of a continuous 6 Hz theta component superimposed with 80 Hz gamma bursts at theta peaks, delivered at 2 mA peak-to-peak. Signal detection analysis revealed increased 2-back sensitivity and robust modulations of response bias, indicating improved working memory and decision-making -- though no effects emerged in the easier 1-back condition, indicating cognitive load dependence. Repeated tACS sessions reinforced behavioral changes with increasing effect sizes, consistent with cumulative neuroplastic consolidation. Deficits in prefrontal theta-gamma coupling are well established in age-related [[working-memory]] decline, and this work demonstrates that exogenous restoration of this coupling pattern through entrainment can partially reverse these deficits.

A 2025 study further demonstrated that amplitude-modulated tACS -- using a 200 Hz carrier frequency modulated by an individualized theta-range signal -- can enhance working memory by promoting coordinated theta-gamma coupling between prefrontal and parietal regions (Chen et al., 2025). This approach offers advantages over conventional tACS by enabling concurrent EEG monitoring during stimulation and potentially reaching deeper cortical targets through temporal interference patterns.

## 40 Hz Gamma Entrainment and Neurodegeneration

The therapeutic potential of **40 Hz gamma entrainment** has expanded significantly since the original demonstrations in animal models. Chan et al. (2025) reported the longest follow-up data to date, evaluating daily 1-hour audiovisual stimulation at 40 Hz in five patients with mild Alzheimer's disease over 2 years. Three female patients with late-onset AD retained strong EEG entrainment throughout and showed less decline on the Mini-Mental State Examination, Clinical Dementia Rating, and Functional Assessment Scale compared to matched controls from national databases (NACC, ADNI, LEADS). Plasma pTau217 -- a key AD biomarker -- showed reductions of 47% and 19% in the two participants for whom samples were available, representing the first evidence linking long-term 40 Hz therapy to AD biomarker change. Earlier results from the randomized controlled portion of the study had demonstrated lesser ventricular dilation and hippocampal atrophy, increased functional connectivity in the [[default-mode-network]], and improved circadian rhythmicity after 3 months of daily stimulation.

Independent corroboration has emerged globally: a 2024 Chinese study confirmed that 40 Hz sensory stimulation increases glymphatic fluid flows in mice, and a double-blind randomized controlled trial demonstrated that daily 30-minute sessions of 40 Hz transcranial vibroacoustic stimulation for eight weeks significantly improved cognitive function and alleviated depressive symptoms in older adults, with corresponding increases in [[gamma-oscillations]] band power (Yao et al., 2024). A national phase III clinical trial is now underway to evaluate this therapy at scale. These findings connect to the broader principle that gamma rhythms serve a neuroprotective function, potentially through the promotion of microglial phagocytosis, increased glymphatic clearance, and enhanced neural coherence (see [[gamma-oscillations]]).

## Neural Entrainment in Speech and Music Processing

The role of neural entrainment in speech comprehension has been elaborated by recent neuro-oscillatory models. Keshavarzi and Zoefel (2026) reviewed computational models exploring how [[theta-oscillations]] (~4-7 Hz), [[gamma-oscillations]], and [[delta-oscillations]] encode phonemes, segment speech into syllables and words, and integrate linguistic elements to infer meaning. A key finding is that faster syllabic rates and lower periodicity are associated with stronger auditory-motor coupling between the posterior superior temporal gyrus and inferior precentral gyrus, suggesting that the motor system compensates for reduced temporal regularity by increasing its entrainment effort (Assaneo et al., 2025). Intracranial recordings have confirmed that neural entrainment to speech in human auditory cortex is robust and frequency-specific, with theta-range phase tracking of the syllabic envelope representing a fundamental mechanism for speech parsing.

An antagonistic relationship between neural entrainment to speech and spontaneous alpha oscillations has been identified in two distinct brain networks: as entrainment to the speech envelope increases in auditory regions, alpha power decreases, consistent with the role of [[alpha-oscillations]] in gating sensory processing (Obleser and Kayser, 2019; updated by recent 2024 findings). This antagonism provides further evidence for the hierarchical interaction between entrainment and intrinsic oscillatory states described in the alpha-theta cross-frequency gating section above.

In music processing, neural entrainment to rhythmic sequences has been shown to operate across a wide range of tempi, with all rhythmic sequences across 12 different presenting rates significantly entraining neural oscillations (Kim et al., 2025). Sequences at different rates elicited distinct impacts on subjective emotional experience, with entrainment intensity associating with changes in emotional valence and dominance under specific frequency conditions. The debate over whether beat-related neural synchronization reflects genuine oscillatory entrainment or evoked tracking remains active, but converging evidence -- including the finding that premature neonatal brains show alpha-range periodicity encoding aligned to beat tempo (Edalati et al., 2024) -- supports the view that rhythmic prediction through entrainment is a fundamental property of auditory processing present from the earliest stages of [[development-and-aging]].

## Inter-Brain Synchronization: Recent Advances

Building on the foundational hyperscanning findings, recent work has revealed the dynamic network architecture of inter-brain coupling during social interaction. A 2026 EEG hyperscanning study of proposer-responder pairs during an iterated ultimatum game identified a sequence of temporally and functionally distinct inter-brain synchrony states (Kim et al., 2026). Early states occurring around stimulus onset were dominated by a posterior parietal modular configuration associated with shared [[attention-and-perception]] and visual processing, while later states during decision-feedback involved increased inter-brain synchrony in frontal and temporoparietal regions, reflecting coordinated evaluative processing between interacting partners. This temporal unfolding of distinct synchrony architectures demonstrates that inter-brain coupling is not a monolithic phenomenon but a dynamic process with functionally specific phases.

Research in educational contexts has shown that teacher-learner inter-brain synchrony depends on mutual gaze and joint attention, with synchronization concentrating over the temporoparietal junction (important for mutual understanding) and ventral premotor cortex (important for communicative coordination) (Wass et al., 2025). A systematic review of inter-brain synchrony across psychological conditions found that synchrony is generally reduced in anxiety, depression, and autism spectrum disorder, particularly in key social brain regions including dorsolateral and medial prefrontal cortices, temporoparietal junction, and inferior frontal gyrus (Garcia et al., 2025). Inter-brain synchronization has also been validated in virtual reality contexts, where it occurs at levels comparable to real-world interaction and correlates positively with collaborative task performance (de Mooij et al., 2025). However, a methodological caution has been raised: arbitrary analytical decisions in hyperscanning EEG studies can significantly skew inter-brain synchrony estimates, highlighting the need for methodological standardization (Rosenberg et al., 2024).

## Binaural Beats and Brainwave Entrainment

**Binaural beats** -- the perceptual phenomenon arising when two slightly different frequencies are presented to each ear -- have been widely promoted as a non-invasive entrainment method, but the empirical evidence remains mixed. A systematic review found that only 5 of 14 studies reported results consistent with the brainwave entrainment hypothesis, with 8 reporting contradictory findings and 1 mixed (Ingendoh et al., 2023). The methodological heterogeneity across studies -- varying beat frequencies, carrier tones, exposure durations, and outcome measures -- limits comparability and likely contributes to the inconsistent results.

More promising findings have emerged in specific clinical applications. Binaural beat stimulation has shown efficacy for tinnitus treatment, with all groups receiving stimulation showing improvement in tinnitus symptoms, depression, and quality-of-life scores (Kumar et al., 2024). However, the lack of significant differences among frequencies suggests these benefits may reflect general sound therapy effects rather than frequency-specific brainwave entrainment. A novel approach integrating continuous EEG monitoring with dynamic binaural beat adjustment -- a closed-loop paradigm -- represents a potentially transformative advance beyond traditional static-frequency protocols and may help resolve the efficacy question by adapting stimulation to individual brain states in real time (Perez et al., 2025). A parametric investigation of binaural beats at beta and gamma frequencies for sustained attention found that effects are highly dependent on specific frequency and carrier tone parameters, suggesting that negative findings in some studies may reflect suboptimal parameter selection rather than fundamental inefficacy of the mechanism (Orozco Perez et al., 2025).

## State-Dependent Modulation of Entrainment

The success of entrainment -- whether by sensory stimulation, tACS, or TMS -- depends critically on the brain's ongoing oscillatory state. This principle, already evident in the alpha-theta gating findings of Wang et al. (2026), extends broadly. Resting-state EEG biomarkers can predict individual differences in rhythmic TMS entrainment outcomes (Trajkovic et al., 2024), and network oscillations are strongly modulated by feedback signals from rhythmic bodily processes like breathing and heartbeat, opening perspectives for understanding how somatic state shapes entrainment susceptibility. Neural entrainment operates according to principles of nonlinear dynamics: two rhythmic systems achieve phase synchronization through mutual adjustment driven by complex interactions that modulate brain responses according to both external stimuli and internal physiological states (Trost et al., 2025).

The state-dependence of entrainment has direct implications for [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]] protocols. Brain states with weaker intrinsic oscillatory coherence -- such as drowsiness, attentional lapses, or certain pathological conditions -- may be paradoxically more susceptible to entrainment across a wider frequency range (broader Arnold tongue), while states with strong intrinsic rhythms may resist off-frequency entrainment but respond powerfully when stimulation matches the dominant frequency. This framework suggests that optimal stimulation protocols should monitor and adapt to ongoing brain state, potentially using closed-loop designs that adjust stimulation parameters in real time based on EEG feedback. The emerging integration of real-time EEG monitoring with brain stimulation devices is making such adaptive entrainment protocols increasingly feasible for both research and clinical applications.
