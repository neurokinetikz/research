# Attention and Perception

Attention and perception are governed by frequency-specific oscillatory mechanisms that operate on distinct timescales. [[alpha-oscillations]] (8-13 Hz) serve as a phasic gating mechanism for distractor suppression, with alpha **phase** (not power) determining when sensory inputs are inhibited -- though the canonical alpha band conceals at least two spatiotemporally and spectrally distinct attention-related oscillator populations (Van Zoest et al., 2025). [[theta-oscillations]] (~7 Hz) implement rhythmic attentional sampling, alternating the spotlight of attention between spatial locations at a rate that depends on conscious awareness, with perception fluctuating at ~4 Hz under distributed attention as an ~8 Hz rhythm divides between competing objects (Fiebelkorn & Bhatt, 2025). Pre-stimulus alpha frequency and phase jointly determine perceptual sensitivity: faster instantaneous alpha frequency increases the likelihood of catching stimuli at optimal phases (Iemi et al., 2026), while optimal alpha phase reduces internal noise and sharpens sensory tuning (Pilipenko et al., 2025). [[gamma-oscillations]] operate through discrete events rather than sustained oscillations to flexibly encode visual information (Perrenoud et al., 2025), and gamma synchrony implements perceptual grouping through coupled oscillator dynamics that improve with perceptual learning (Vinken et al., 2025). Beyond spatial attention, oscillatory dynamics shape temporal perception, multisensory integration, rhythmic entrainment to auditory and musical stimuli, and the large-scale traveling waves that organize phase dynamics across the cortex. Disruptions to these oscillatory attention mechanisms are implicated in conditions such as ADHD, linking this topic to [[clinical-applications]].

## Alpha-Mediated Distractor Suppression

Redding et al. (2026) provided direct evidence that alpha phase (~9-10 Hz) phasically gates distractor interference during spatial attention. In a visual detection task with spatially predictable targets and distractors, pre-stimulus alpha phase at frontocentral and occipital electrodes modulated hit rate, false alarm rate, and d-prime -- but **only on trials with distractors present**. Alpha phase also modulated the amplitude of distractor-evoked visual responses (163-219 ms post-stimulus), confirming a sensory suppression mechanism. Importantly, alpha **power** lateralization contralateral to distractors (peaking at 10.95 Hz) showed no behavioral effect, establishing that inhibitory gating operates through phase-dependent excitability cycles rather than tonic power modulation. This phase-centric view of [[alpha-oscillations]] connects to findings in [[working-memory]] where phase synchronization, not amplitude, carries memory content information.

The canonical alpha band conceals multiple functionally distinct mechanisms rather than a single unitary process. Van Zoest et al. (2025) used MEG to reveal a **triple dissociation** within posterior alpha: ipsilateral alpha-increases appeared early, over occipital sensors, at high alpha-frequency (10-14 Hz) and were associated with spatial attention deployment, while contralateral alpha-decreases appeared later, over parietal sensors, at lower alpha-frequency (7-10 Hz). These two alpha-oscillator populations dissociated further with respect to temporal anticipation and eye movement activity, demonstrating that what is conventionally measured as a single "alpha lateralization" index actually reflects at least two separable attention-related processes with distinct cortical origins. Complementing this oscillatory picture, Iemi et al. (2025) showed that **aperiodic** (non-oscillatory) alpha-band activity also contributes to visuospatial attention encoding, with oscillatory and aperiodic components providing distinct and complementary lateralization effects -- a finding that challenges purely oscillatory accounts of alpha-mediated attention.

Alpha distractor suppression also appears to be indirectly controlled by goal-relevant processing rather than operating as a direct inhibitory signal. Van Moorselaar et al. (2024) demonstrated that alpha power increases over regions representing distractor locations are governed by an indirect mechanism: the attention system first processes goal-relevant target information, and distractor-related alpha suppression follows as a consequence of this target-priority signal rather than arising from an independent inhibitory command.

## Rhythmic Attentional Sampling

Theta-band oscillations (~7 Hz) implement rhythmic attentional sampling, and this process is modulated by conscious awareness. Redding et al. (2026) found that pre-stimulus theta phase at central electrodes modulated target detection regardless of whether distractors were present, consistent with a general attention-sampling mechanism. Yang et al. (2025) extended this by showing that **visual awareness doubles the sampling frequency**: visible cues drove attentional sampling at ~7.5 Hz (matching the [[theta-oscillations]] fundamental), while invisible cues drove sampling at ~3.75 Hz. Frontoparietal connectivity followed the same pattern -- alpha-band (12 Hz) phase-locking for visible cues versus theta-band (6 Hz) for invisible cues. This consciousness-dependent frequency shift suggests that awareness gates which oscillatory regime the attention system operates in, with implications for understanding the [[neural-resonance-and-entrainment]] mechanisms underlying conscious perception.

Fiebelkorn and Bhatt (2025) proposed a comprehensive framework in which **attentional sampling resolves neuronal competition** along the visual hierarchy. When multiple objects appear together, neural populations representing them compete, and perception fluctuates at ~4 Hz for distributed attention -- possibly because an underlying ~8 Hz rhythm is divided between two competing objects. This sampling mechanism manifests as early as the eye (through microsaccadic rhythms) and extends to complex features in higher visual areas, suggesting that rhythmic sampling is not merely epiphenomenal but serves as the mechanism through which attention resolves competitive interactions at each stage of visual processing.

The balance between theta and alpha power dynamically determines the dominant sampling regime. Helfrich et al. (2024) showed that when attentional resources are focused on one hemifield, oscillatory patterns fluctuate at ~10 Hz and ~6-9 Hz reflecting perceptual sampling, whereas when attention must simultaneously cover two hemifields, a ~5 Hz rhythm emerges reflecting an attentional sampling process. This task-dependent frequency shift is governed by the instantaneous theta/alpha power ratio, providing a unified framework for understanding how spatial, feature-based, and temporal attention all share a common oscillatory architecture.

Remarkably, theta-rhythmic attentional sampling exhibits **hexagonal spatial organization** reminiscent of entorhinal grid cells. Koenig et al. (2025) found that in a cue-guided attention task, behavioral performance varied with spatial location such that higher theta power occurred at locations aligned to multiples of 60 degrees -- a signature hexagonal modulation. This finding links the attentional sampling mechanism to the [[hippocampal-memory]] navigation system, suggesting that covert visual attention and overt spatial navigation rely on shared grid-like neural architecture, with [[theta-oscillations]] orchestrating sampling behaviors in both domains.

Working memory and attention interact through rhythmic alternation. Huang et al. (2025) demonstrated that two visual [[working-memory]] items alternately dominate behavioral guidance in a **theta-rhythmic** pattern (4-8 Hz), with anti-correlated activation states over time. Occipital [[alpha-oscillations]] (8-14 Hz) governed item-specific prioritization, with alpha amplitude closely tracking which memory item guided behavior, while frontal theta oscillations phase-led and coupled with occipital alpha during item transitions. This "rhythmic item template" model resolves the longstanding debate over whether working memory maintains a single template or multiple simultaneous templates -- the answer is that both are correct, with items cycling in and out of the active state at theta frequency.

## Large-Scale Traveling Waves

Alexander and Dugue (2024) used stereotactic EEG from 23 participants to demonstrate that **low spatial frequencies dominate cortical phase dynamics** across all temporal frequencies from 1 Hz through 97 Hz. Spatial power decreased monotonically with increasing spatial frequency -- a 1/f-alpha spectrum -- meaning the longest wavelengths carry the most power. This held even for gamma oscillations (30-100 Hz), traditionally assumed to be purely local phenomena. The finding that large-scale traveling waves are genuine cortical phenomena (not volume conduction artifacts) provides a physical substrate for how oscillatory attention signals propagate globally. Two functionally distinct alpha-band traveling waves implement predictive coding: forward waves (occipital-to-frontal) carry feedforward sensory signals, while backward waves (frontal-to-occipital) carry top-down predictions and attentional modulation, linking attention to [[predictive-coding]] mechanisms.

## Pre-Stimulus Oscillatory Predictors of Perception

The phase and frequency of ongoing oscillations before stimulus onset critically determine whether a stimulus is perceived. Iemi et al. (2026) demonstrated in Nature Communications that **spontaneous alpha frequency fluctuations predict perceptual sensitivity**: on trials where instantaneous alpha frequency was higher, observers showed greater sensory precision and accuracy on a perceptual decision-making task. The mechanism is elegant -- faster alpha rhythms pack more oscillatory cycles into the stimulus window, increasing the probability that the stimulus coincides with an optimal phase for neural excitability.

Complementing the frequency account, Pilipenko et al. (2025) used signal detection theory and reverse correlation to show that pre-stimulus alpha phase modulates sensitivity (d') rather than criterion, with hit and false alarm rates being **counterphased**. Classification images revealed that during optimal alpha phases, spatial frequency and orientation tuning became sharper, implying that alpha phase shapes perception by reducing internal noise and sharpening sensory tuning rather than simply amplifying the signal. These results, obtained from six observers each performing ~6,020 trials, provide the most mechanistically detailed account to date of how [[alpha-oscillations]] phase gates visual perception.

The [[individual-alpha-frequency]] also shapes temporal integration in bodily perception. D'Angelo et al. (2025) showed that parietal IAF predicted temporal binding windows in body ownership and visuotactile simultaneity tasks: faster alpha frequencies narrowed the temporal window within which visual and tactile signals were bound together, increasing perceptual sensitivity. Crucially, modulating IAF through brain stimulation **causally** altered these binding windows and sensitivities, establishing that [[alpha-oscillations]] frequency is not merely correlated with temporal integration but mechanistically determines it. This extends the IAF-perception link beyond vision to multisensory bodily self-perception.

## Temporal Perception and Neural Dynamics

Oscillatory dynamics shape how the brain parses time. Duymaz et al. (2026) showed that the mode of stimulus presentation fundamentally shapes neural representation dynamics: sudden onsets produce sharp, transient decoding peaks (41.8% accuracy at ~115 ms) followed by decay, while gradual onsets produce slower-building sustained representations without discrete peaks. Despite different temporal dynamics, the underlying representational format generalizes across onset types, suggesting that attention deploys different temporal processing strategies depending on stimulus statistics.

Marsicano et al. (2026) linked individual alpha frequency (IAF) to temporal perception and ADHD traits. Higher ADHD traits predicted faster IAF (r = 0.44), narrower temporal integration windows (r = -0.51 with temporal threshold), and reduced serial dependence (less reliance on perceptual history). IAF significantly mediated the relationship between ADHD traits and temporal perception, supporting the view that [[alpha-oscillations]] frequency defines the temporal resolution of perception. The finding that faster IAF narrows the binding window connects to [[clinical-applications]] for ADHD and suggests that individual differences in alpha frequency reflect fundamental differences in perceptual sampling rate.

**Voluntary temporal attention** engages distinct anticipatory mechanisms in visual cortex. Denison et al. (2024) showed that when observers direct attention to specific anticipated moments, visual cortical activity is modulated through both periodic oscillatory modulations and transient ramping activity. Ramping activity heightens overall response reliability during an anticipated time period, while periodic modulation enables more temporally fine-grained adjustments -- selectively enhancing reliability at one precise moment while suppressing it just before and after. This dual mechanism allows the visual system to communicate accurate stimulus information at the exact moment when a task-relevant stimulus is expected, distinguishing voluntary temporal attention from temporal expectation for the first time at the neural level.

## Gamma Oscillations in Perceptual Binding

[[gamma-oscillations|Gamma-band activity]] (30-100 Hz) supports perceptual binding and flexible encoding of sensory information. Perrenoud et al. (2025) published a landmark study in Nature demonstrating that gamma activity in mouse V1 operates through **discrete gamma events** rather than sustained oscillations. Individual gamma events synchronize neural firing across cortical layers and promote enhanced visual encoding, with event rate varying with arousal state. Critically, gamma event rate increased before visually cued behavioral responses and predicted trial-by-trial performance, while optogenetic suppression of V1 gamma events impaired visual detection and evoking them elicited behavioral responses -- establishing a causal role for gamma in visual perception. These discrete events are driven by patterned input from the dorsal lateral geniculate nucleus (dLGN), positioning gamma as a mechanism for thalamocortical integration of visual information.

Vinken et al. (2025) addressed the longstanding debate over whether gamma synchrony can support perceptual grouping despite its stimulus-dependent frequency variability. Using the theory of weakly coupled oscillators, they showed that gamma synchrony (30-80 Hz) selectively groups image elements based on feature similarity and spatial proximity, with synchronization strength determined by frequency dissimilarity and coupling strength between neural populations. In a texture segregation experiment, human performance followed theoretical predictions both qualitatively and quantitatively. Most strikingly, increases in gamma synchrony due to **perceptual learning** predicted behavioral performance improvements across sessions, demonstrating that the stimulus-dependence of gamma synchrony is not a limitation but an adaptive feature that tunes to the statistics of visual experience. This work, connecting to [[cross-frequency-coupling]] mechanisms, provides the strongest evidence to date that gamma synchrony implements a viable neural grouping code for figure-ground segregation.

## Neural Entrainment to Rhythmic Structure

The brain actively entrains to rhythmic stimuli through oscillatory mechanisms at multiple timescales. Henry and Obleser (2012) demonstrated that 3 Hz frequency modulation entrains endogenous delta oscillations, with entrained delta **phase** determining perceptual performance (circular-linear correlation rho = 0.76). Critically, delta phase predicted behavior **across subjects** while stimulus phase did not, establishing the neural oscillation as the critical intervening variable between stimulus and perception. This connects to [[neural-resonance-and-entrainment]] mechanisms.

Lenc et al. (2018) showed that bass sounds (130 Hz carrier) selectively enhance cortical activity at beat- and meter-related frequencies compared to high-pitched sounds, particularly for syncopated rhythms requiring endogenous beat generation. This bass advantage had a **cortical** origin (exceeding cochlear model predictions) and required temporal attention, suggesting the motor-auditory network preferentially entrains to low-frequency sounds. Dube et al. (2026) introduced the RnB algorithm for separating rhythmic from arrhythmic (1/f) neural activity at the time-series level, enabling improved detection of oscillatory events and phase-amplitude coupling during sleep -- advancing methodology for studying entrainment and oscillatory dynamics generally.

However, the study of neural entrainment faces significant methodological and conceptual challenges. Duecker et al. (2024) outlined in a comprehensive review that the term "entrainment" has been used liberally in cognitive neuroscience to describe any phase-locked neural response, without strong evidence for an oscillatory origin. Distinguishing genuine entrainment of endogenous oscillations from stimulus-evoked responses remains difficult, because evoked responses are typically maximal at stimulation onset and habituate subsequently, while a true neural oscillator requires time to synchronize with external stimulation. The review advocates for computational modeling, dynamical systems theory, and sophisticated stimulus designs (e.g., omission paradigms, aperiodic sequences) to disentangle these mechanisms -- a methodological caution relevant to interpreting all entrainment findings in this article and in [[neural-resonance-and-entrainment]].

## Oscillations in Multisensory Integration

Multisensory perception depends on oscillatory dynamics across multiple frequency bands that coordinate the binding of inputs from different sensory modalities. Keil and Senkowski (2024) reviewed in Nature Reviews Neuroscience how oscillations operating at **multiple timescales** mediate multisensory integration through power modulations, phase resetting, phase-amplitude coupling, and dynamic functional connectivity. Delta and theta oscillations provide a temporal framework for binding cross-modal inputs that arrive with natural asynchronies, while alpha oscillations gate the relative weighting of each modality -- lower prestimulus alpha power over a sensory region increases that modality's influence on the integrated percept.

The McGurk illusion -- in which a heard syllable is altered by incongruent lip movements -- provides a window into how oscillatory states shape cross-modal binding. Noel et al. (2025) found that illusory McGurk perception was associated with reduced prestimulus alpha (8-12 Hz) and beta (15-30 Hz) power over frontal and occipital regions, combined with increased theta (4-7 Hz) power in parietal, central, and occipital areas and elevated [[gamma-oscillations]] power (31-45 Hz) across the scalp. This pattern suggests that multisensory fusion requires a shift from an inhibitory (alpha/beta-dominant) to an integrative (theta/gamma-dominant) oscillatory state, with [[aperiodic-dynamics]] also contributing to the prestimulus neural context.

Cross-modal memory formation likewise depends on oscillatory preparation. Bauer et al. (2025) used sensory entrainment to induce specific alpha oscillatory states before audiovisual encoding, finding that induced alpha activity enhanced recognition memory performance and increased sensitivity -- suggesting that [[alpha-oscillations]] prepare the brain for optimal multisensory binding. These findings connect to the broader role of alpha frequency in determining temporal integration windows (see Pre-Stimulus Oscillatory Predictors of Perception above), as the same alpha-frequency mechanism that gates unimodal visual perception also determines how precisely cross-modal signals must be temporally aligned to be bound together.

## Spatial Representations Through Oscillations

Neural oscillations create spatial representations through a hierarchy of frequency-specific mechanisms. [[theta-oscillations]] (4-12 Hz) provide the fundamental temporal scaffold for spatial coding via phase precession in the hippocampal-entorhinal system, compressing behavioral-scale spatial trajectories into millisecond-scale spike timing suitable for synaptic plasticity. [[gamma-oscillations]] nest within theta cycles at specific phases, routing distinct information streams: slow gamma (30-60 Hz) reflects memory retrieval while fast gamma (60-100 Hz) reflects current sensory encoding. [[alpha-oscillations]] precisely track the spatial spotlight of attention through inverted encoding models, while [[beta-oscillations]] coordinate motor planning and reference frame transformations in posterior parietal cortex. A posterior-to-anterior gradient in dominant oscillation frequency (from 10-12 Hz in sensory cortex to 6-8 Hz frontally) parallels increasing receptive field sizes, establishing that **lower frequencies coordinate larger spatial scales**.
