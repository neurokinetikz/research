# Predictive Coding

Predictive coding is implemented through frequency-specific oscillatory channels organized by cortical layer: within cortico-cortical circuits, gamma oscillations (30-100 Hz) originating in supragranular layers carry feedforward prediction errors, while alpha/beta oscillations (8-30 Hz) from infragranular layers carry feedback predictions. This laminar-frequency architecture, established by Bastos et al. across 28 macaque visual area pairs with Directed Asymmetry Index correlations exceeding r = 0.9 with anatomical hierarchy, extends beyond sensory cortex to motor control, corticostriatal learning, and global brain-wide connectivity states. An important caveat: the gamma-equals-feedforward rule applies to cortico-cortical communication but not to thalamocortical pathways, where feedforward signaling from LGN to V1 operates through beta (15-30 Hz) rather than gamma -- gamma is cortically emergent and absent from the LGN of awake primates (see [[cortical-layer-oscillations]]). Recent work has complicated the classical picture: Vinck et al. (2025) argue that rapid sensory inference is primarily mediated by aperiodic transients rather than sustained oscillations, with narrow-band gamma actually emerging under high spatiotemporal predictability -- a reversal of the standard assignment that aligns better with dendritic predictive coding models than with the classical hierarchical scheme (see [[aperiodic-dynamics]]). Meanwhile, thalamocortical prediction error circuits have been causally demonstrated in mouse V1, where a cooperative mechanism between pulvinar input and VIP interneuron-mediated disinhibition selectively amplifies the most stimulus-selective neurons for unexpected stimuli (Furutachi et al., 2024). The framework positions [[theta-oscillations]] (~160ms), [[alpha-oscillations]] (~100ms), [[beta-oscillations]] (~50ms), and [[gamma-oscillations]] (~25ms) as nested prediction windows operating at progressively finer temporal scales.

## The Bastos Framework: Laminar Origins of Prediction and Error

The foundational evidence for oscillatory predictive coding comes from Bastos et al. (Neuron, 2012, 2015), who demonstrated that cortical hierarchy operates through frequency-specific communication channels with distinct laminar origins. Feedforward influences are carried by theta (~4 Hz) and gamma (~60-80 Hz) synchronization originating in supragranular layers (L2/3), while feedback influences utilize beta (~14-18 Hz) synchronization from infragranular layers (L5/6). Anatomically, feedforward projections arise from Layer 3B and target Layer 4 in higher areas, while feedback projections originate from Layer 6 and target Layers 1, 2, and 5/6 -- critically avoiding Layer 4 (see [[cortical-layer-oscillations]]).

Quantitative Granger causality analysis reveals systematic frequency-direction relationships. The Directed Asymmetry Index (DAI) shows positive values in the gamma range (feedforward dominance) and negative values in the alpha/beta range (feedback dominance). Combining these into a multifrequency DAI produces correlations exceeding r = 0.9 with anatomical hierarchy -- oscillation patterns alone can predict anatomical connectivity. Predicted stimuli show enhanced alpha/beta power with suppressed gamma, while unpredicted stimuli show the reverse pattern, with effects that are both stimulus-specific and layer-selective.

## Causal Evidence: Van Kerkoerle Microstimulation

Van Kerkoerle et al. (2014) provided causal evidence for the directional frequency framework through intracortical microstimulation. V1 stimulation induced gamma increases in V4, confirming feedforward gamma signaling. V4 stimulation induced alpha increases and gamma decreases in V1, confirming feedback alpha/beta signaling. NMDA receptor blockade suppressed alpha and enhanced gamma, identifying glutamatergic transmission as critical for feedback-associated rhythms. The propagation patterns differ fundamentally: gamma waves initiate in Layer 4 and spread to superficial then deep layers, while alpha waves originate in Layers 1, 2, and 5 (feedback targets) and propagate toward Layer 4. This causal demonstration anchors what had been correlational observations in a mechanistic framework.

## Beta as Prediction Carrier

Beta power increases parametrically with predictability of action sequences, while gamma power increases with prediction error (Van Pelt et al., 2016). Beta bursts, typically 40-120ms in duration (Feingold et al., 2015), encode confidence in forward models -- Tan et al. (2016) found that post-movement beta rebound indexes confidence that predictions are accurate. The ~50ms single cycle duration of beta provides a natural update window for motor commands.

Wang et al. (2026) demonstrated that PFC beta (~19 Hz) and hippocampal CA1 beta (~22 Hz) show largely independent dynamics during spatial learning, with PFC beta power increasing across learning while CA1 beta decreases. This inverse relationship suggests that PFC beta consolidates predictive models as learning proceeds, while hippocampal beta signals diminish as stimuli become predictable. Beta bursts precede [[sharp-wave-ripples]] at goal locations, suggesting hierarchical processing where beta-range activity performs initial outcome evaluation before memory consolidation occurs.

Beyond spatial learning, beta oscillations serve as efference copy signals in temporal prediction. Leske et al. (2025) showed that pre-target beta activity originating in sensorimotor cortex encodes the predicted envelope sharpness and temporal precision of upcoming auditory targets during rhythmic beat processing. This beta activity was time-locked to expected auditory events and directed toward auditory brain regions, while being coupled to the phase of [[delta-oscillations]] (1-3 Hz) at the beat rate. Beta may thus represent the motor system's internalization of predictable temporal intervals -- a covert form of active sensing in which motor predictions sharpen auditory processing. Lissón et al. (2025) provided complementary evidence that motor beta oscillations contribute to **temporal binding** -- the subjective compression of intervals between voluntary actions and their outcomes. Reduced beta desynchronization predicted stronger temporal binding, independent of action congruency, suggesting that beta power indexes the strength of predictive motor models that link actions to their expected sensory consequences (see [[motor-control]]).

## Gamma as Prediction Error

Gamma oscillations are enhanced by unexpected stimuli and suppressed by predicted stimuli, consistent with their role as prediction error carriers. This modulation is layer-selective (concentrated in supragranular layers) and stimulus-specific. The mechanism follows the PING model: PV+ fast-spiking interneurons in L2/3 generate the rapid inhibitory cycling required for gamma-band synchronization, while prediction errors propagate through supragranular feedforward projections to Layer 4 of higher areas (see [[cortical-layer-oscillations]]).

Lee, Pennartz, and Mejias (2025) built a biologically detailed computational model incorporating PV, SST, and VIP interneurons with precise laminar organization to show that neural oscillatory activity emerges naturally during predictive inference. Their model replicates the enhanced responses to deviant stimuli seen in mismatch negativity paradigms and demonstrates that the oscillatory patterns arise from cortico-cortical interactions between excitatory prediction error and representation microcircuits -- going beyond the simplified chain-like descriptions of earlier models. The work establishes that gamma-range oscillatory prediction errors are an emergent property of realistic cortical microcircuit dynamics rather than requiring explicit error-coding populations (see [[cortical-layer-oscillations]]).

## Thalamocortical Prediction Error Circuits

The classical predictive coding framework focused on cortico-cortical interactions, but Furutachi et al. (2024) demonstrated that the [[thalamocortical-circuits|thalamus]] plays a critical causal role in computing prediction errors in mouse primary visual cortex. Unexpected visual stimuli preferentially boost responses of L2/3 V1 neurons that are most selective for the stimulus in question -- this amplification is not a non-specific surprise signal but a feature-selective enhancement. The mechanism requires cooperative action between two sources: excitatory input from the **pulvinar** (higher-order thalamic nucleus) and cortical disinhibition mediated by **VIP interneurons** (see [[cortical-layer-oscillations]]). In response to prediction errors, VIP neurons inhibit a specific subpopulation of SOM interneurons that normally gate excitatory pulvinar input to V1. This disinhibition releases pulvinar-driven excitation onto the most stimulus-selective pyramidal cells, creating a selective amplification circuit for unpredicted features.

Huang (2025) extended this interneuron-level account with a computational model incorporating all three major inhibitory cell types -- PV, SOM, and VIP neurons -- with stimulus-selective tuning and experience-dependent plasticity. The model demonstrated distinct functional roles: SOM neurons mediate feature-specific dendritic inhibition of pyramidal cells under expected conditions, selectively suppressing responses to predicted inputs; VIP neurons facilitate disinhibition by inhibiting PV neurons, thereby enhancing pyramidal cell responsiveness under unexpected conditions; and PV neurons provide broad, non-selective inhibition serving as a stable baseline for prediction-dependent modulation. Critically, stimulus selectivity in SOM and VIP neurons was essential -- without it, inhibitory control became diffuse and the expectation suppression effect collapsed. These findings connect predictive coding to the emerging understanding of [[cortical-layer-oscillations|interneuron microcircuit motifs]] and suggest that the oscillatory signatures of prediction and error arise from the dynamics of these cell-type-specific inhibitory interactions (see [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]]).

## Signed Prediction Error in the Motor System

Emanuele et al. (2026) mapped how probabilistic predictions shape rapid feedback responses in the human motor system, revealing a dissociation between synaptic input and neural output. Motor commands (EMG) reflected additive Bayesian integration of expectation and sensory input (rho = +0.65-0.77). However, synaptic input to M1 and S1 -- measured via BOLD and LFP -- carried a signed prediction error signal: BOLD showed strong negative correlations in M1 (rho = -0.823) and S1 (rho = -0.613), while LFP showed the same pattern in beta (13-25 Hz, rho = -0.763) and gamma (25-100 Hz, rho = -0.947). Premotor and parietal areas compute unsigned surprise, then transmit signed prediction error downstream -- establishing a clear cortical hierarchy for predictive [[motor-control]].

The "S1 paradox" was particularly striking: expectations reached S1 as synaptic input (strong in BOLD and LFP) but did not influence local spiking activity, which remained tightly linked to actual sensory input. The prediction error computation occurs through the mismatch between top-down synaptic input carrying predictions and bottom-up spiking carrying sensory evidence.

## Global Prediction Error Connectivity States

Bogdan et al. (2025) extended prediction error processing to a brain-wide phenomenon. Using task-fMRI, resting-state fMRI, and simultaneous EEG-fMRI across datasets totaling over 1,000 participants, they identified two connectivity states: a ventral-dorsal network (occipital-IPL, ATL-LPFC) associated with high prediction error, and a posterior-anterior network (occipital-ATL, IPL-LPFC) associated with low prediction error. The brain spontaneously fluctuates between these states even at rest, with fluctuation amplitude correlating with 3-6 Hz delta/theta EEG power (Cohen's d = 1.24 for delta, d = 1.13 for theta). This places spontaneous prediction error dynamics at [[theta-oscillations]] timescales -- the same frequency range associated with hippocampal memory encoding and attentional sampling (see [[attention-and-perception]]).

## Corticostriatal Prediction and Error Neurons

Pathak et al. (2025) developed a biomimetic [[corticostriatal-learning|corticostriatal]] model that discovered "incongruent neurons" (ICNs) -- cortical cells that predict upcoming behavioral errors approximately 1000ms before they occur. The model generates beta-frequency (~16 Hz) corticostriatal oscillations, with phase-locking value increasing across learning and differentiating correct from incorrect trials. ICNs arise from anatomical pre-weighting in sparse cortical connectivity, creating neurons with ambiguous receptive fields that develop stronger connections to incorrect action-selection pathways. This provides a mechanistic account of how prediction errors emerge within corticostriatal loops, connecting oscillatory predictive coding to reinforcement learning and decision-making.

## The Four-Frequency Prediction Hierarchy

The four canonical frequencies define nested prediction windows at progressively finer temporal scales: theta (~160ms) organizes episodic prediction contexts, alpha (~100ms) samples perceptual predictions, beta (~50ms) maintains motor and cognitive predictions, and gamma (~25ms) carries local prediction errors. Friston and Kiebel (2009) proposed that hierarchical predictive coding naturally produces these oscillatory dynamics, with higher levels encoding slow contextual changes that provide priors for faster changes at lower levels. [[Cross-frequency-coupling]] -- particularly phase-amplitude coupling where slow rhythms modulate fast activity -- creates the multi-timescale architecture through which predictions at one timescale constrain error signals at faster timescales.

## Oscillatory vs. Transient Dynamics in Predictive Processing

The standard account -- gamma carries feedforward prediction errors, alpha/beta carries feedback predictions -- has come under increasing theoretical scrutiny. Vinck et al. (2025) offered a systematic reassessment in Trends in Cognitive Sciences, arguing that the evidence supports a more nuanced picture in which **aperiodic transients** serve as the primary conduit for rapid inter-areal communication during sensory inference, while oscillations emerge later to stabilize neural representations and support plasticity. Their key observation is that unpredicted stimuli enhance broadband, non-oscillatory fluctuations and aperiodic transients, whereas predicted stimuli -- those with high spatiotemporal regularity -- actually boost narrow-band gamma oscillations. This reverses the classical assignment: gamma becomes a signature of successful prediction (representation stabilization) rather than prediction error per se (see [[aperiodic-dynamics]]).

Vinck et al. propose three distinct theoretical perspectives. First, they critically evaluate the gamma-as-error / beta-as-prediction framework and find that empirical evidence is more mixed than commonly assumed. Second, they argue that transient, non-oscillatory dynamics better account for the rapid timescales of sensory inference. Third, they suggest that when oscillations do emerge during predictive processing, this is better explained by **dendritic predictive coding** -- where apical dendrites of pyramidal neurons in L2/3 and L5 compute mismatches between top-down contextual input and bottom-up sensory drive -- rather than the classical hierarchical model in which separate populations carry predictions vs. errors. This dendritic account naturally generates oscillatory dynamics because the interplay between somatic and dendritic compartments produces resonance at alpha/beta frequencies determined by Ih channel gradients (see [[neural-resonance-and-entrainment]]).

The implications are significant for interpreting experimental results. Studies that find increased gamma for unexpected stimuli may be detecting broadband power increases that happen to overlap the gamma range rather than genuine narrow-band oscillatory prediction errors. Conversely, the robust beta suppression seen during prediction violations may reflect disruption of a stabilized predictive state rather than active signaling of the error itself. This framework does not invalidate the directional frequency channels demonstrated by Bastos et al. -- feedforward gamma and feedback alpha/beta remain robust anatomical findings -- but it suggests that their functional interpretation within predictive coding requires refinement.

## Alpha's Dual Role: Prediction Carrier and Active Inference Gate

Alpha oscillations serve dual functions in predictive coding. As a feedback frequency (alongside beta), alpha carries top-down predictions from infragranular layers. Simultaneously, alpha implements "gating by inhibition" (Jensen and Mazaheri, 2010), suppressing processing in task-irrelevant regions while enabling gamma emergence in task-relevant regions. Han et al. (2026) demonstrated that the Berger effect -- alpha increase during eyes-closed states -- is driven by genuine periodic oscillatory activity, not aperiodic changes. In visual cortex, aperiodic power actually increases during eyes-open conditions while periodic alpha decreases, confirming that alpha represents active inhibition rather than passive idling. This positions alpha suppression as a form of active inference: reducing predictions to allow sensory evidence greater influence on perception (see [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]]).

Hetenyi, Haarsma, and Kok (2025) provided direct evidence that the **content of visual predictions oscillates at alpha frequencies**. Using MEG and multivariate decoding in a cued shape discrimination task (n = 32), they showed that pre-stimulus representations of predicted shapes fluctuated at 10-11 Hz in visual cortex -- the specific identity of the expected shape could be decoded from alpha-band activity before the stimulus appeared, and this stimulus-specific alpha power correlated with expectation effects on behavioral discrimination performance. The findings support the idea that alpha oscillations are not merely gating signals but carry content-specific feedback information, consistent with the feedback role of alpha in the Bastos framework but adding the crucial detail that alpha rhythms encode **what** is predicted, not just **that** a prediction exists.

Tarasi, Alamia, and Romei (2026) demonstrated that expectation-based knowledge triggers backward-propagating alpha-band traveling waves from frontal to occipital areas. Using a probabilistic cueing paradigm, they found that backward alpha waves were stronger in participants who relied more heavily on prior expectations, while participants who weighted sensory evidence more strongly showed contrasting forward traveling wave patterns. The strength of backward alpha traveling waves was directly linked to prior-driven perceptual bias at the behavioral level. This spatial propagation evidence complements the frequency-domain findings by showing that alpha-band predictive signals have a characteristic directionality -- flowing from higher to lower cortical areas -- consistent with their role as feedback prediction carriers (see [[alpha-oscillations]]).

## Predictive Coding in Speech and Auditory Processing

The predictive coding framework has been productively extended to speech perception, where the hierarchical temporal structure of language (phonemes, syllables, words, phrases) maps naturally onto the nested oscillatory timescales of cortical processing. Dogonasheva, Doelling, Zakharov, et al. (2025) introduced the **Brain Rhythm-Based Inference (BRyBI)** model, a biologically grounded architecture in which coupled theta and gamma rhythms at the lower level parse incoming sound into syllables and phonemes, while the delta rhythm at the higher level builds contextual predictions about words and phrases that shape perception at the lower level. BRyBI captures the dialogue between bottom-up sensory input and top-down contextual predictions, orchestrated by rhythmic neural activity, and matches patterns of human speech recognition performance (see [[auditory-and-speech-oscillations]]).

BRyBI explains contradictory experimental observations about oscillatory dynamics during speech listening. When speech is highly predictable, delta-band contextual predictions efficiently constrain lower-level parsing, reducing prediction error and producing the characteristic delta-theta coherence seen in continuous speech tracking. When speech is uncertain or surprising, prediction errors at the syllabic (theta) and phonemic (gamma) levels propagate upward, disrupting delta-band contextual representations. The model thus provides a mechanistic account of how [[cross-frequency-coupling]] implements hierarchical prediction in an ecologically critical domain. Leske et al. (2025) further showed that beta activity originating in sensorimotor cortex is time-locked to expected auditory targets and directed toward auditory brain regions during rhythmic beat processing, with pre-target beta power encoding predicted envelope sharpness and temporal precision -- suggesting that the motor system contributes efference copy signals that sharpen auditory predictions about upcoming events.

## Crossmodal and Hierarchical Prediction Error Signaling

Prediction error signaling extends across sensory modalities and hierarchical levels. Chao et al. (2024) used EEG during a crossmodal audiovisual local-global oddball paradigm to trace how predictions formed in one sensory modality generate errors in another. First-level prediction errors in auditory and visual streams manifested as increases across theta, alpha, beta, and gamma bands, while second-level integrated prediction errors -- reflecting violated sequence-level expectations -- showed distinct band-specific patterns. Critically, auditory and visual prediction errors were rapidly redirected to central-parietal electrodes during learning through alpha-band interactions, suggesting that alpha mediates the crossmodal integration of prediction errors into a unified representation of environmental regularity (see [[attention-and-perception]]).

Polverino et al. (2024) further demonstrated that prediction error representations across the cortical hierarchy are **synergistic** -- the information conveyed by event-related potentials and broadband dynamics about prediction errors is greater than the sum of what each signal carries independently. Using electrocorticography in awake marmosets performing auditory oddball tasks, they found synergistic encoding even at early stages of the hierarchy within auditory cortex, and between auditory and frontal regions. A brain-constrained neural network simulation showed that this synergy requires strong, long-distance feedback and feedforward connections -- sparse or weak inter-areal connectivity produces redundancy instead. This suggests that the distributed, multi-frequency nature of prediction error signaling is not merely a byproduct of hierarchical processing but a computational strategy that maximizes information about environmental violations.

## Resonant Hierarchies and Stimulation Approaches

Snyder (2026) proposed the "resonant hierarchy" framework, arguing that the feedforward gamma / feedback alpha-beta dissociation reflects structural constraints across three nested spatial scales: dendritic compartment resonance (Ih gradients creating frequency-selective filtering), mesoscale laminar microcircuit dynamics, and macroscale inter-areal communication constrained by conduction delays. Adjacent areas synchronize at gamma; mid-range connections coordinate at alpha/beta; long-range pathways use theta. This framework grounds predictive coding's frequency channels in biophysical mechanisms rather than treating them as arbitrary conventions (see [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]]).

Stein et al. (2026) introduced transcranial endogenous current stimulation (tECS), which uses an individual's own EEG signal with the aperiodic exponent reduced by 50% as the stimulation waveform. This approach is relevant to predictive coding because the aperiodic exponent reflects the excitation/inhibition balance that determines how strongly predictions constrain sensory processing. A flatter spectrum may reduce reliance on top-down predictions, potentially increasing sensitivity to prediction errors. This methodological innovation offers a causal tool for testing how predictive processing depends on E/I balance (see [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]]).

## Predictive Coding Disruptions in Schizophrenia and Autism

The predictive coding framework has proven especially productive for understanding the pathophysiology of schizophrenia spectrum disorders (SSD) and autism spectrum disorders (ASD), which appear to represent opposite poles of a predictive processing continuum (Alamia et al., 2022). In this model, SSD is characterized by overweighting of high-level prior beliefs, leading to hallucinations and delusions when internal models override sensory evidence, while ASD is characterized by overweighting of low-level sensory input, producing the sensory hypersensitivity and reduced contextual integration that define the condition (see [[clinical-applications]]).

Alamia et al. (2024) provided direct oscillatory evidence for this framework using alpha-band traveling waves in EEG data from 146 schizophrenia patients and 96 matched controls. Patients with schizophrenia showed stronger **top-down alpha-band traveling waves** (propagating from frontal to occipital areas) during resting state compared with controls, consistent with overly precise priors at higher levels of the predictive hierarchy. During a visual backward masking task, patients also showed stronger **bottom-up alpha-band traveling waves**, interpreted as enhanced signaling of sensory precision errors resulting from the mismatch between excessively strong predictions and actual visual input. This spatial traveling-wave analysis offers a novel characterization of oscillatory dynamics in schizophrenia that goes beyond static power measures to capture the directional flow of predictive and error signals.

Alpha oscillations also serve as biomarkers for individual differences in predictive strategy along the ASD-SSD continuum. Alamia et al. (2023) found that the degree to which individuals rely on probabilistic priors is mediated by alpha amplitude modulation: participants who exhibited large alpha suppression in high- versus low-probability conditions ("believers") showed strong prior-driven bias shifts, while those with reduced alpha modulation ("empiricists") showed dampened contextual influence on perception. This places alpha oscillatory dynamics as an objective, measurable correlate of where an individual falls on the predictive processing continuum -- a potential biomarker bridging subclinical traits to clinical conditions.

At the circuit level, the well-documented reduction in **mismatch negativity** (MMN) in schizophrenia reflects impaired prediction error generation. MMN depends on NMDA receptor-mediated glutamatergic transmission (see [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]]), and NMDA receptor hypofunction -- the leading neurochemical model of schizophrenia -- disrupts precisely the feedback-associated alpha rhythms that Van Kerkoerle et al. demonstrated are NMDA-dependent. Lee, Pennartz, and Mejias (2025) showed with a biologically detailed cortical network model incorporating PV, SST, and VIP interneurons that oscillatory patterns during predictive coding are sensitive to the balance of excitation and inhibition across cell types -- perturbations that mimic the E/I imbalance seen in both schizophrenia and autism produce characteristic disruptions in the gamma and beta oscillatory signatures of prediction and error signaling (see [[gamma-oscillations]]).

## Precision Weighting and Oscillatory Gain Control

Within the predictive coding framework, attention is reconceptualized as **precision weighting** -- the process by which the brain adjusts the gain on prediction error signals to reflect their estimated reliability (Feldman and Friston, 2010). High precision on sensory prediction errors (attention) amplifies their influence on belief updating, while low precision (inattention) attenuates them. This gain modulation is implemented through oscillatory mechanisms: gamma-band synchronization increases the postsynaptic impact of prediction error signals by temporally coordinating spiking in supragranular feedforward projections, while alpha/beta power modulations adjust the gain on specific sensory channels by regulating inhibitory tone (see [[attention-and-perception]]).

The neurobiological substrate of precision weighting involves neuromodulatory systems that regulate synaptic gain. Acetylcholine enhances the precision of sensory prediction errors (promoting bottom-up processing), while dopamine modulates the precision of prior predictions (influencing top-down processing). Serotonin, particularly through 5-HT2A receptors, may globally reduce the precision of high-level priors -- potentially explaining why [[psychedelic-states]] produce perceptual destabilization and enhanced sensory vividness through relaxation of top-down constraints. The [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]] of precision weighting thus maps onto the known neuromodulatory control of oscillatory dynamics: cholinergic enhancement promotes gamma while suppressing alpha, dopaminergic signaling modulates beta power, and serotonergic 5-HT2A activation disrupts alpha/beta feedback rhythms.

Lee, Pennartz, and Mejias (2025) demonstrated that the cell-type-specific microcircuit architecture of cortical columns naturally implements precision-weighted prediction error computation. In their model, VIP interneuron activity serves as a gain control mechanism: increased VIP firing disinhibits pyramidal cells through SOM suppression, effectively increasing the precision (gain) on prediction error representations. This provides a circuit-level mechanism for attention-as-precision-weighting that connects the abstract computational theory to concrete interneuron dynamics and their associated oscillatory signatures.
