# Cross-Species Frequency Organization

Neural oscillation frequency bands are remarkably conserved across species spanning 600 million years of evolution and 17,000-fold variation in brain size. From octopus to whale, the same fundamental frequency bands -- delta, [[theta-oscillations]], [[alpha-oscillations]], [[beta-oscillations]], and [[gamma-oscillations]] -- appear with center frequencies forming a geometric progression with ratio approximately 1.618, the golden mean. This universality reflects deep biophysical constraints from conserved ion channel kinetics, with larger brains deploying proportionally more myelinated white matter to maintain timing precision rather than shifting oscillation frequencies. Recent large-scale analyses reinforce this picture: Karvat et al. (2024) examined neurophysiological spectra from 859 participants across a dozen datasets spanning rats and humans, multiple recording techniques, ages, brain regions, and cognitive states, revealing a **universal rhythmic architecture** divisible into high-rhythmicity bands exhibiting sustained oscillations and novel low-rhythmicity bands dominated by brief oscillatory bursts -- a spectral fingerprint conserved across species. Meanwhile, the discovery of **sleep-dependent infraslow rhythms** conserved across seven lizard species, birds, rats, and humans (Bergel et al., 2025) pushes the confirmed ancestry of shared oscillatory organization back approximately 300 million years to the common amniote ancestor.

## Conservation Across 600 Million Years

The evidence for universal frequency organization is striking. All mammalian oscillation types are found in all mammals studied, from tree shrew to whale, despite massive brain size variation. Ion channels that generate these oscillations -- including HCN channels (producing theta rhythm via h-current), KCNQ channels (producing beta resonance via M-current), and GABA-A receptors (enabling gamma timing) -- are conserved from cnidarians to humans, with identical time constants across mouse, rat, and human. Two-state sleep oscillations appear in zebrafish, reptiles, birds, mammals, and even octopus, suggesting oscillatory state cycling predates the vertebrate lineage.

A landmark 2025 study by Bhatt et al. systematically characterized the voltage-gated ion channel complements (VGL-chanomes) of 623 organisms, dissecting their expression across 11 whole-body cell atlases and 3 entire nervous systems. The study revealed that it is not the sheer number of ion channels but rather their **diversity and restrictive recruitment** to specific cell types that constitutes the hallmark of neuronal complexity (Bhatt et al., 2025). Cell-type-restricted VGL-chanome expression predated the emergence of nervous systems entirely, and multiple independent gene-family expansions and contractions produced convergent solutions across distant lineages. Members of the K_V family and GABA-A receptors display some of the most widespread orthologs across organisms, reinforcing the idea that the molecular toolkit for oscillation generation was assembled before neurons themselves evolved. GABA signaling, notably, is established before glutamatergic transmission during [[development-and-aging|development]] in all animal species studied, with excitatory GABA actions in immature neurons triggering sodium spikes and activating voltage-gated calcium channels to generate **primitive oscillations** that scaffold later circuit formation (Ben-Ari, 2007). This developmental recapitulation of an ancient signaling sequence hints that ontogeny preserves the phylogenetic order in which oscillatory mechanisms were recruited.

## The Octopus Evidence

Perhaps the most compelling evidence for convergent evolution comes from cephalopods. Octopus, which diverged from vertebrates 550 million years ago, lacks a cortex and distributes 60% of its neurons in its arms, yet shows 12-18 Hz oscillations during quiet sleep (corresponding to the [[alpha-oscillations]]/spindle range) and 30 Hz oscillations during active sleep and waking (corresponding to low [[gamma-oscillations]]). This independent discovery of the same frequency solutions in a radically different nervous system architecture argues that these frequencies are constrained by physics and chemistry rather than anatomy.

Two major advances in 2023 deepened the octopus picture. Gutnick et al. (2023) achieved the first recordings of brain activity from freely moving octopuses by implanting miniaturized data loggers -- originally designed to track bird brain activity during flight, adapted for waterproof use -- into the mantle of *Octopus cyanea*. Electrodes placed in the vertical lobe and median superior frontal lobe recorded continuously for 12 hours as animals slept, ate, and explored. Some brain activity patterns were similar in duration and intensity to mammalian activity, while a novel slow oscillation at approximately 2 Hz was observed for the first time in any organism (Gutnick et al., 2023). Separately, Pophale et al. (2023) used high-density electrophysiological recordings from the central brain of *Octopus laqueus* to delineate neural correlates of two-stage sleep. During **active sleep**, local field potential activity in the superior frontal and vertical lobes -- anatomically connected regions associated with learning and memory -- resembled waking patterns. During **quiet sleep**, these same regions were relatively silent but generated LFP oscillations resembling mammalian [[sleep-oscillations|sleep spindles]] in both frequency and duration (Pophale et al., 2023). The range of similarities with vertebrate [[sleep-oscillations]] indicates that two-stage sleep architecture may be a convergent feature of complex cognition, arising independently in lineages separated by over half a billion years of evolution.

## What Scales and What Does Not

Hippocampal [[theta-oscillations]] frequency scales inversely with brain size -- from approximately 8 Hz in rats to approximately 3 Hz in humans during virtual navigation. However, cortical oscillation frequencies are largely conserved: [[alpha-oscillations]] remains at 8-13 Hz, [[beta-oscillations]] at 13-30 Hz, [[gamma-oscillations]] at 30-100+ Hz, and sleep spindles at 11-16 Hz across mammals. Larger brains compensate for increased conduction distances by deploying proportionally more fast-conducting myelinated axons: white matter constitutes 6% of brain volume in hedgehog but over 40% in humans. This architectural solution preserves timing precision at the expense of metabolic cost, indicating strong selective pressure to maintain specific oscillation frequencies.

Cross-species theta comparisons have grown more nuanced. In macaque CA1, theta and gamma bands are segregated by behavioral state: beta2/gamma (15-70 Hz) dominates during visual search whereas [[theta-oscillations|theta]] (3-10 Hz; peak ≈8 Hz) predominates during quiescence and early sleep, a pattern distinct from the continuous movement-linked theta of rodents (Montefusco-Siegmund et al., 2023). The human hippocampus adds further complexity: Kahana et al. (2024) showed that humans exhibit two functionally distinct theta oscillations during spatial navigation -- a slower rhythm in anterior regions and a faster, movement-modulated oscillation in posterior hippocampus -- contrasting with the single ≈8 Hz rhythm typical of rodents. These findings suggest that the primate hippocampus has elaborated the ancestral theta mechanism into multiple frequency channels tuned to different computational demands, while preserving the core link between theta phase and [[hippocampal-memory|memory]] encoding. Traveling [[theta-oscillations|theta]] waves, well-established in rodent hippocampus, have now been confirmed in human intracranial cortical recordings, where posterior-to-anterior propagation during encoding reverses to anterior-to-posterior during recall (Mohan et al., 2024), indicating conservation of the traveling-wave mechanism despite the frequency shift.

## The Spectrolaminar Motif

Phillips et al. (2024) demonstrated that the spectrolaminar motif -- with [[gamma-oscillations]] in superficial layers and [[alpha-oscillations]]/[[beta-oscillations]] in deep layers -- is conserved across all 14 macaque cortical areas examined, as well as in marmosets and humans. The crossover frequency between gamma-dominant and alpha/beta-dominant activity falls near 32 Hz, precisely at the Layer 4 anatomical boundary. Rodents show a similar but broader motif, suggesting evolutionary elaboration in primates of a basic principle present in all mammals. Species differences are notable in that primates show clear 8-13 Hz alpha rhythms while rodents exhibit a 3-5 Hz "alpha-like" oscillation, though the laminar organization is preserved.

The spectrolaminar motif has attracted productive scientific debate. Mackey et al. (2025) questioned whether the motif is truly ubiquitous, challenging the FLIP tool used to identify it and suggesting that classic current source density analysis might suffice. In a detailed rebuttal, Mendoza-Halliday et al. (2025) confirmed the motif in diverse cortical areas using data from multiple independent research groups -- including Mackey et al.'s own dataset, where the motif was identified in 66% of laminar probes across auditory, visual, and belt cortex, consistent with the original study's 61% detection rate. The exchange refined the field's understanding: the spectrolaminar motif is a robust organizational principle of primate [[cortical-layer-oscillations]], though its expression varies in strength across probes and areas, and the deep-layer alpha-beta / superficial-layer gamma gradients are more sharply delineated in primates than in rodents.

Within macaque V1, Herrero et al. (2024) identified three distinct gamma oscillatory hotspots at different depths within a single cortical column, consistent with earlier findings in cats and humans. These multiple gamma generators within a column suggest that [[gamma-oscillations]] are not a monolithic phenomenon but arise from separable microcircuits -- a feature apparently conserved across mammalian visual cortex despite differences in laminar elaboration.

## Neuromodulatory Synchrony

Teng et al. (2025) revealed that the three major neuromodulatory systems -- norepinephrine, serotonin, and acetylcholine -- exhibit synchronized infraslow oscillations at approximately 0.02 Hz during NREM sleep in mice. Acetylcholine leads the monoamines by 2-4 seconds, and increased synchrony between neuromodulators predicts arousal transitions. All three neuromodulators are anticorrelated with EEG sigma power (9-15 Hz), creating a slow modulatory envelope that gates the faster oscillations of the [[canonical-eeg-bands]] hierarchy. Pharmacological disruption of either serotonin or norepinephrine abolishes infraslow oscillations in all three systems, demonstrating tight interdependence across neuromodulatory networks.

## Deep Brain Stimulation Across Species

Wen et al. (2025) demonstrated that theta-band (5 Hz) temporal interference stimulation of the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex enhances prediction error processing in humans, providing evidence that [[theta-oscillations]] mechanisms in deep brain structures can be causally engaged. The dACC theta rhythm for prediction error encoding appears to be a conserved mammalian mechanism, linking reward learning to the same oscillatory frequencies observed in rodent hippocampal and prefrontal circuits.

## Insect Oscillations and Convergent Frequency Solutions

Insects provide a striking test of convergent evolution in oscillatory architecture, given their small brain size (≈1 million neurons in honeybees) and independent evolutionary trajectory from vertebrates. In *Drosophila*, visual selection is achieved by endogenous 20-30 Hz oscillations that phase-lock onto temporal features of attended visual objects -- a beta-range binding mechanism functionally analogous to feature binding in humans (Grabowska et al., 2020). Olfactory processing in locusts relies on a 20 Hz oscillation initiated in feedback circuits of the antennal lobes and propagated to the mushroom bodies, where Kenyon cells synchronize their spiking to specific phases of the oscillatory cycle (Laurent & Naraghi, 1994). These mushroom body oscillations serve a role in odor discrimination that parallels the proposed binding function of [[gamma-oscillations]] in mammalian cortex.

The honeybee brain exhibits a spontaneous oscillation at approximately 18 Hz that is suppressed by olfactory stimulation -- functionally reminiscent of the mammalian [[alpha-oscillations|alpha rhythm]], which is attenuated by sensory engagement. Popov and Szyszka (2020) demonstrated that the phase of this 18 Hz oscillation biases spike timing and modulates the amplitude of high-frequency gamma activity (40-450 Hz) in the bee brain, establishing a [[cross-frequency-coupling]] relationship strikingly similar to primate alpha-gamma coupling. Intriguingly, interhemispheric coherence at 18 Hz showed asymmetric directionality -- stronger from right to left hemisphere -- suggesting lateralized functional connectivity even in an insect brain. That insects independently evolved alpha-like suppression, beta-range binding, and cross-frequency coupling underscores the idea that these oscillatory solutions are convergent responses to shared computational demands rather than inherited from a common ancestor.

## Reptilian and Avian Sleep Oscillations

Reptiles and birds bracket mammals phylogenetically, making their oscillatory repertoires critical for dating the evolutionary origins of sleep-related rhythms. In the bearded dragon (*Pogona vitticeps*), a **claustrum** identified through transcriptomic profiling generates [[sharp-wave-ripples]] during slow-wave sleep -- sharp waves with superimposed high-frequency ripples that propagate across the pallial dorsal ventricular ridge (Norimoto et al., 2020). A homologous claustrum was also found in the turtle *Trachemys scripta*, suggesting that claustrum-driven sharp-wave-ripple generation is ancestral to reptiles at minimum. Lizard sleep homeostasis has recently been characterized in detail: corticotomy in bearded dragons abolished the rebound of "e-sleep" (an electrophysiologically defined state) after sleep deprivation, demonstrating that the cortex plays a conserved role in regulating sleep pressure across amniotes (Norimoto et al., 2025).

Birds display mammalian-like slow-wave sleep and REM sleep, with SWA (0.5-4.5 Hz) increasing and decreasing as a function of time awake and asleep, respectively, indicating conserved homeostatic regulation. Songbirds show sleep replay of learned vocalizations most strongly during non-REM periods with high slow-wave activity, paralleling mammalian [[hippocampal-memory|hippocampal replay]]. However, notable absences distinguish avian from mammalian sleep: [[thalamocortical-circuits|thalamocortical]] spindles and sharp-wave ripples have not been conclusively identified in birds (Rattenborg et al., 2011), suggesting these oscillatory elements were either lost in the avian lineage or evolved independently in mammals after the divergence from the common amniote ancestor approximately 310 million years ago.

## Infraslow Rhythms Across Amniotes

Bergel et al. (2025) recorded brain activity across seven lizard species, rats, humans, and pigeons, revealing a **sleep-dependent infraslow rhythm** conserved in all species examined. This rhythm -- with a period on the order of minutes -- is tightly coupled with eye movements, muscle tone, heart rate, and breathing rate in lizards; with skin brightness in chameleons; and with pulsatile changes in cerebrovascular volume during NREM sleep in mice. The infraslow rhythm thus appears to coordinate whole-organism physiology during sleep across lineages that diverged over 300 million years ago. In mammals, it may be linked to the glymphatic clearance of metabolic waste, but its presence in reptiles -- which lack the mammalian glymphatic system in its canonical form -- suggests a more fundamental role, perhaps reflecting an ancient adaptation to the environmental constraints and predation risks of the sleeping state. This finding dovetails with the [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations|neuromodulatory]] infraslow oscillations at ≈0.02 Hz described by Teng et al. (2025) in mice, raising the question of whether the amniote-wide infraslow rhythm and the mammalian neuromodulatory infraslow cycle share a common generative mechanism.

## Zebrafish: Oscillatory States in a Minimal Vertebrate Brain

Zebrafish (*Danio rerio*) provide the simplest vertebrate model for studying sleep-related oscillations, with a transparent larval brain amenable to whole-brain calcium imaging. Leung et al. (2019) identified two major sleep signatures: **slow bursting sleep** (SBS), characterized by synchronous ON-OFF oscillations in the dorsal pallium resembling mammalian slow-wave activity, and **propagating wave sleep** (PWS), initiated by traveling waves that bear similarities to REM-associated activity. Both states occur against a background of globally reduced brain activity compared to waking. More recent work using Hidden Markov Modeling of long-term behavioral data has further resolved zebrafish sleep into distinct deep and light sub-states, with differential sensitivity to arousal stimuli and selective modulation by melatonin, serotonin, and norepinephrine signaling (Dijk et al., 2025). That the same three [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations|neuromodulatory]] systems implicated in mammalian sleep architecture also govern zebrafish sleep sub-states underscores the deep phylogenetic roots of oscillatory state control, extending conservation beyond amniotes to the base of the vertebrate lineage approximately 450 million years ago.

## Evolutionary Origins: Pre-Nervous System Oscillations

The deepest roots of neural oscillations may extend before neurons themselves. Cnidarians -- jellyfish, sea anemones, and corals -- possess diffuse nerve nets rather than centralized brains, yet their nervous systems generate oscillatory activity. In hydrozoan jellyfish, an electrically coupled network of "swim motor neurons" in the inner nerve ring functions as a pacemaker: baseline membrane potentials oscillate intrinsically, initiating spikes at the apex of each cycle, modulated by synaptic input from sensory neurons (Bhatt et al., 2025; Pallasdies et al., 2025). The ion channels driving these oscillations -- voltage-gated potassium channels, HCN channels, and GABA-A receptors -- are orthologs of the same channel families that generate [[theta-oscillations]], [[beta-oscillations]], and [[gamma-oscillations]] in mammalian cortex.

Even organisms without nervous systems display oscillatory signaling. Sponges and placozoans possess neuron-like cells that use many of the same molecular components found in true neurons -- including ion channels and neuropeptide-like signaling molecules -- raising the possibility that rhythmic electrical activity predated the evolution of neurons altogether (Bhatt et al., 2025). The evolution of complex nervous systems thus appears not as a stepwise progression of expanding complexity, but as a convergent path to diversity, with oscillatory dynamics emerging from deeply conserved biophysical building blocks that were repurposed and elaborated independently across lineages.

## Universal Rhythmic Architecture

Karvat et al. (2024) introduced a novel dimension to the understanding of cross-species frequency organization by measuring not just the frequency of oscillations but their **rhythmicity** -- the degree to which oscillatory activity is sustained versus transient. Analyzing neurophysiological spectra from 2,450 recording sites across rats and 848 humans, including both invasive and non-invasive recordings, they found a universal spectral architecture with two categories. **High-rhythmicity bands** -- including the [[alpha-oscillations|alpha]] and [[beta-oscillations|beta]] ranges -- exhibit sustained oscillatory activity suitable for maintaining ongoing neural states. **Low-rhythmicity bands** -- including portions of the [[theta-oscillations|theta]] and [[gamma-oscillations|gamma]] ranges -- are dominated by brief oscillatory bursts that may signal the brain's response to change. This architecture was consistent across ages 18-88, sexes, species, brain regions, recording techniques, and health vs. disease states, suggesting it reflects a fundamental organizational principle of neural dynamics rather than a species-specific adaptation. The rhythmicity dimension adds explanatory power beyond frequency alone: two bands at different frequencies but with similar rhythmicity profiles may be more functionally related than two bands at adjacent frequencies with different rhythmicity signatures, a finding with implications for [[brain-computation]] and [[cross-frequency-coupling]] theories.

## Why the Golden Ratio?

The golden ratio (phi = 1.618) is optimal for frequency band organization because it is the most irrational number -- having the largest minimum distance to any rational number. This prevents harmonic locking between adjacent bands, maximizes information processing capacity by avoiding interference, and enables the unique mathematical property phi^2 = phi + 1, which supports self-similar scaling. No other ratio combines these properties, making phi a mathematically inevitable solution for neural oscillatory architecture and the foundation of [[brain-computation]] across species.
