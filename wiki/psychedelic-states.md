# Psychedelic States and Oscillations

Psychedelic compounds produce a characteristic neural oscillation signature dominated by [[alpha-oscillations]] suppression (8-13 Hz), broadband cortical desynchronization, and increased neural signal diversity -- the first measured brain states to exceed the entropy of normal waking consciousness. This oscillatory reorganization reflects a fundamental shift in hierarchical [[predictive-coding]]: top-down information flow via alpha/beta decreases while bottom-up [[gamma-oscillations]] signaling increases, the [[default-mode-network]] loses coherence, and the brain moves toward dynamics with an expanded repertoire of functional connectivity patterns. These changes correlate with subjective experiences including ego dissolution, visual hallucinations, and mystical states, and they inform therapeutic applications across depression, addiction, and anxiety (see [[clinical-applications]]). A comprehensive 2025 review across moderate to high doses of LSD, psilocybin, ayahuasca, and DMT confirmed that alpha power reduction in occipital regions and increased Lempel-Ziv complexity are the two most consistent electrophysiological markers of the psychedelic state (Pallavicini et al., 2025), while directed top-down connectivity decreases and bottom-up signaling increases, indicating a transient reversal of typical hierarchical organization.

## Alpha suppression as the primary biomarker

The most robust finding across all classic serotonergic psychedelics is pronounced suppression of [[alpha-oscillations]] in posterior cortical regions. Psilocybin produces broadband desynchronization from 1-50 Hz in posterior association cortices, with alpha reduction in the posterior cingulate cortex (PCC) correlating strongly with ego dissolution ratings (R-squared = 0.66). The mechanism traces to [[cortical-layer-oscillations|layer 5 pyramidal neurons]] enriched in 5-HT2A receptors: psychedelic activation causes increased excitability of these deep-layer cells, producing asynchronous glutamate release and spike-field decoherence. Ketanserin, a 5-HT2A antagonist, completely blocks both alpha suppression and subjective effects, confirming receptor specificity. This alpha collapse reflects the relaxation of precision-weighted priors in the REBUS (Relaxed Beliefs Under Psychedelics) model, where alpha oscillations normally index the precision of top-down predictions. See [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]] for how alpha gating determines which content reaches awareness.

Dose-dependent studies have further refined the relationship between alpha power and subjective experience. Timmermann et al. (2025) administered 20 mg and 40 mg freebase DMT in a repeated-measures design and found that alpha power suppression and permutation entropy were most strongly associated with continuous subjective experience dimensions, whereas Lempel-Ziv complexity -- previously considered a robust phenomenological correlate -- was the least strongly associated neural marker. This challenges earlier assumptions about which complexity measures best track the psychedelic experience and suggests that oscillatory alpha power may be a more direct index of phenomenological intensity than broadband complexity metrics. The 40 mg dose induced more extreme visual hallucinations and emotionally intense experiences, consistent with a dose-response relationship between alpha suppression depth and subjective intensity.

## Each substance has a unique oscillatory fingerprint

While sharing the core alpha suppression signature, each psychedelic displays distinct features. LSD uniquely increases alpha peak frequency (~2 Hz upward shift) even as power decreases, and produces more pronounced delta reduction. Subramani et al. (2026) used FOOOF spectral parameterization with source-resolved MEG to demonstrate that LSD produces genuine oscillatory power attenuation independent of peak shifts, alongside flattened [[aperiodic-dynamics|aperiodic (1/f) spectral slope]] and increased Lempel-Ziv complexity. Effects concentrated in sensory, language, and emotional networks while notably sparing motor cortex. DMT shows a distinctive temporal pattern: initial alpha/beta suppression followed by delta and theta emergence during peak "breakthrough" experiences, with mean peak frequency shifting to 7.36 Hz -- resembling REM sleep patterns described in [[sleep-oscillations]]. Ayahuasca exhibits biphasic dynamics reflecting its combined DMT plus beta-carboline pharmacology, with a unique late-phase [[gamma-oscillations]] increase (30-100 Hz). Ketamine differs fundamentally as an NMDA antagonist, robustly increasing gamma (25-55 Hz) rather than suppressing it.

### LSD frequency-specific network reconfiguration

Alonso et al. (2025) applied the FREQ-NESS framework to MEG data from 14 healthy participants under LSD, isolating frequency-resolved from broadband connectivity in source space. LSD significantly restructured canonical networks in the [[alpha-oscillations|alpha]] and [[beta-oscillations|beta]] bands: high alpha (12.1, 13.3 Hz) prominence increased across all experimental conditions, and high beta (25.3 Hz) prominence increased in three of four conditions, while low beta (18.1, 19.3 Hz) and low alpha (8.5 Hz) prominence decreased. Topographically, LSD reconfigured the alpha network landscape such that lower alpha frequencies adopted topography typically associated with the mu rhythm over sensorimotor cortex, whereas higher alpha frequencies retained the classical posterior alpha distribution. This frequency-specific reorganization reveals that LSD does not simply suppress alpha -- it selectively restructures which alpha sub-bands dominate and where they are expressed, reshaping the network architecture of oscillatory communication.

### 5-MeO-DMT and complex slow wave reorganization

**5-MeO-DMT** produces an oscillatory signature markedly distinct from N,N-DMT and other classic psychedelics. In mice, 5-MeO-DMT induces a **vigilance state dissociation** -- prominent cortical slow oscillations appear alongside marked pupil dilation in behaviorally awake, moving animals (Bhatt et al., 2026). EEG slow wave activity transiently increases while [[theta-oscillations|theta]] activity is suppressed during wakefulness, yet power in faster frequencies (>30 Hz) remains comparable to the awake state, creating a hybrid state with features of both waking and NREM sleep. REM sleep is initially markedly suppressed but shows rebound overcompensation over the subsequent 48 hours. Rat hippocampal and prefrontal recordings confirm increased delta power and decreased theta power following 5-MeO-DMT (Catarina et al., 2024).

In humans, Timmermann et al. (2025) replicated the rodent finding of amplified low-frequency oscillations and extended it by characterizing the spatiotemporal organization of neural activity under 5-MeO-DMT. The compound radically reorganizes low-frequency flows, causing them to become incoherent, heterogeneous, viscous, fleeting, and nonrecurring -- they cease their typical traveling forward and backward across the cortex. This represents a qualitative shift in cortical dynamics rather than a simple power change: the very structure of slow-wave propagation breaks down, producing what the authors describe as "complex slow waves" that lack the organized spatiotemporal patterns seen in normal sleep or wakefulness.

### Psilocybin: acute and sub-acute oscillatory effects

Beyond the well-established acute alpha suppression, recent rodent work using chronically implanted Neuropixels probes has revealed that psilocybin unmasks **100 Hz high-frequency oscillations** (HFOs) in the infralimbic cortex -- the rat homologue of the human subgenual anterior cingulate cortex -- persisting for approximately one hour post-injection (Kringelbach et al., 2025). These HFOs were accompanied by decreased net neuronal firing rates and reduced spike-train complexity, and were more prominent during resting behavior than during sustained attention tasks. Critically, local field potential recordings 1, 2, and 6 days post-psilocybin showed gradually emerging increases in [[beta-oscillations|beta]] and low-[[gamma-oscillations|gamma]] (20-60 Hz) power specific to the infralimbic cortex, suggesting lasting changes in prefrontal oscillatory dynamics that may relate to psilocybin's antidepressant mechanisms.

In a clinical context, Ruffell et al. (2025) examined 21 veterans with traumatic brain injury histories who participated in psilocybin retreats and found that EEG recordings showed decreased [[delta-oscillations|delta]] and theta power in frontal and temporal regions alongside normalization of alpha and beta band activity, accompanied by clinically significant improvements in PTSD symptoms (PCL-5 scores decreased 50%), depression (PHQ-9 scores decreased 65%), and anxiety (STAI scores decreased 28%). While uncontrolled, these findings suggest that psilocybin may shift aberrant oscillatory patterns associated with neuropathology toward more normative spectral profiles.

### Ketamine oscillatory mechanisms

Ketamine differs from classic serotonergic psychedelics by acting primarily through NMDA receptor blockade. At subanesthetic doses, ketamine preferentially blocks NMDA receptors on GABAergic inhibitory interneurons, producing disinhibition of downstream excitatory pyramidal neurons that drives robust [[gamma-oscillations|gamma-band]] activity (25-55 Hz). Intracranial recordings in humans reveal that ketamine produces distinct oscillatory signatures across brain regions: gamma oscillations in prefrontal cortex and hippocampus -- structures implicated in its antidepressant effects -- and a 3 Hz oscillation in posteromedial cortex, proposed as a mechanism for its dissociative effects (Li et al., 2023). This spatial segregation of oscillatory effects suggests that ketamine's antidepressant and dissociative properties may engage partially separable neural circuits.

In the context of treatment-resistant depression, a placebo-controlled study found that subanesthetic ketamine significantly increased whole-brain temporospatial Lempel-Ziv complexity (LZCT) during infusion compared to placebo (16.90% vs. -4.84%), but elevated LZCT at infusion end was associated with less short-term symptom improvement the following day (Li et al., 2025). Conversely, lower pretreatment occipital LZCT predicted a favorable response to ketamine, suggesting that baseline signal complexity in visual cortex could serve as an EEG biomarker for treatment stratification. Ketamine also suppresses REM sleep while markedly increasing waking gamma power, with gamma enhancement approximately twice as large in depression-susceptible Wistar Kyoto rats compared to controls, pointing to a relationship between baseline affective vulnerability and the magnitude of ketamine-induced gamma changes. See [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]] for the broader role of glutamatergic and GABAergic systems in oscillatory dynamics.

## Criticality shifts rather than simple entropy increase

Irrmischer et al. (2025) used detrended fluctuation analysis (DFA) to show that DMT shifts brain dynamics away from criticality toward subcritical (inhibition-dominated) regimes in theta, alpha, and beta bands. The largest effect appeared in alpha (DFA reduction of -0.09), and critically, DFA reductions correlated with self-dissolution intensity (r = -0.56 to -0.61 in theta and alpha). This challenges simplified versions of the entropic brain hypothesis: while entropy increases under DMT (signals become more unpredictable), complexity actually decreases (signals lose temporal structure). The resolution is that entropy and criticality can dissociate -- both subcritical and supercritical shifts away from the critical point increase entropy. The authors propose that long-range temporal correlations underlie the coherent sense of self, and disrupting these correlations fragments temporal coherence, producing ego dissolution. Similar LRTC reductions occur in deep meditation and anesthesia. See [[critical-frequencies]] for the broader framework of criticality in neural dynamics.

The distinction between entropy and criticality has been further supported by whole-brain computational models. Deco et al. (2023) reproduced the overall entropy increase observed under psychedelics using a model-based approach, providing the first mechanistic explanation for how 5-HT2A activation at the cellular level translates into macroscopic entropy changes. The model showed that disrupted serotonergic signaling destabilizes attractor states in cortical networks, allowing the system to explore a wider repertoire of transient configurations -- consistent with the REBUS framework -- while simultaneously moving away from the critical regime that supports structured, temporally correlated activity.

## Enhanced 5 Hz oscillations under 5-HT2A activation

White et al. (2026) discovered transient 5 Hz oscillatory events ("bouts") in mouse visual cortex that are significantly enhanced by 5-HT2A agonists. These oscillations co-occur in retrosplenial cortex (RSC) with an ~18 ms lag, suggesting V1-driven propagation. Under psychedelic compounds, bout occurrence rate doubled, visually evoked bout duration increased from ~700 ms to ~1070 ms, and V1-RSC power coupling emerged where none existed at baseline. The authors propose this reflects strengthened top-down control of perception, with enhanced V1-to-RSC communication supporting perceptual filling and visual hallucinations. The 5 Hz frequency likely reflects species-specific scaling of the theta range relative to human alpha.

## Self-boundary dissolution has a distributed neural signature

Rohr et al. (2026) demonstrated that self-boundary dissolution states during meditation can be decoded from MEG recordings, with Lempel-Ziv complexity (LZc) emerging as the best classifier (64% accuracy) -- outperforming all frequency band features. A 27 Hz peak frequency served as a secondary marker, with power decreases in left supramarginal and supplementary motor areas predicting dissolution states. LZc showed mixed directionality across regions: some areas increased complexity while others decreased, a complex pattern invisible to univariate analysis. These findings parallel the psychedelic literature: both pharmacological (DMT, psilocybin) and contemplative (Vipassana meditation) routes to [[self-and-embodiment]] dissolution produce increased signal complexity and multi-regional coordination changes, suggesting convergent mechanisms.

## Connectivity reveals hierarchical reorganization

Psychedelics fundamentally alter information flow through the cortical hierarchy. Directed functional connectivity (Granger causality) decreases generally, yet undirected connectivity increases -- networks become more interconnected but less hierarchically organized. Cortical traveling wave analysis under DMT shows backward (top-down) waves decreasing while forward (bottom-up) waves increase, resembling visual stimulation despite eyes being closed. Default mode network integrity collapses, claustrum connectivity with DMN decreases, and [[thalamocortical-circuits|thalamocortical]] dynamics shift toward increased sensory flooding. Network modularity decreases while between-network connectivity increases, consistent with the brain shifting from segregated modular organization toward global integration. These connectivity changes represent the neural basis for the expanded state space described by the entropic brain hypothesis and relate to [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]] dynamics where hierarchical processing determines awareness.

Luppi et al. (2024) tested predictions from global theories of consciousness by comparing psychedelic states with sleep and deep sedation using fMRI. They found a striking mirror-image pattern: psychedelic states exhibit increased global functional connectivity and decreased local neural synchrony, while sleep and deep sedation show the opposite. This dissociation held for both anterior-posterior and within-posterior brain regions, providing empirical support for theories emphasizing global brain network interactions in supporting conscious experience. The finding that global brain hyperconnection correlates with heightened subjective effects reinforces the view that psychedelics expand the accessible state space of neural dynamics.

## Psychedelic states, meditation, and dreaming

The oscillatory signatures of psychedelic states share notable features with both meditation and dreaming, suggesting partially convergent neural mechanisms across altered states of consciousness. DMT in particular produces rapid increases in [[delta-oscillations|delta]] and [[theta-oscillations|theta]] activity that resemble transitions into REM sleep, leading researchers to characterize the DMT state as a "waking dream" in which conscious awareness is maintained alongside dream-like cortical dynamics (Timmermann et al., 2019). Ayahuasca-induced experiences closely resemble the conscious experience typical of vivid or lucid dreaming, with EEG patterns resembling deep meditation and trance states (Silva-Costa et al., 2025). Under ayahuasca, baseline theta predicted interoceptive intensity while baseline alpha predicted mind-wandering content, suggesting that individual oscillatory architecture shapes the character of the psychedelic experience.

Lewis-Healey et al. (2024) demonstrated that breathwork-induced psychedelic-like experiences are also associated with increased Lempel-Ziv complexity, paralleling pharmacological psychedelics. Exploratory analyses showed that the [[aperiodic-dynamics|aperiodic exponent]] of the power spectral density -- but not oscillatory alpha power -- yielded similar neurophenomenological associations, suggesting that non-pharmacological routes to psychedelic-like states may operate more through changes in broadband neural dynamics than through the specific alpha suppression characteristic of serotonergic compounds. This points toward a common neural substrate -- elevated signal complexity and flattened spectral slope -- that underlies psychedelic-like phenomenology regardless of whether the cause is pharmacological, contemplative, or respiratory.

## Therapeutic biomarkers and oscillatory predictors

The clinical translation of psychedelic therapy has created an urgent need for objective EEG biomarkers that can predict treatment response and guide dose optimization. Skosnik et al. (2023) found that auditory evoked [[theta-oscillations|theta]] power (4-8 Hz) doubled in amplitude two weeks after a single psychedelic dose of psilocybin in patients with major depressive disorder (n = 19), with no change after placebo. Improvements in depression symptoms at two weeks correlated with theta power increases, suggesting that enhanced theta may represent an EEG biomarker of psilocybin's sustained neuroplastic effects. This theta enhancement is consistent with theta's established role in synaptic plasticity and [[hippocampal-memory|hippocampal]] learning processes.

Real-time EEG monitoring during psychedelic sessions is becoming feasible as a tool for tracking drug activity. Subjective intensity ratings and plasma drug levels robustly covary with changes in spectral power and signal complexity, highlighting the potential for objective, real-time biomarkers of psychedelic drug action (Pallavicini et al., 2025). However, a 2026 preprint by Tagliazucchi et al. (2026) found limited evidence for persistent neurophysiological changes in naturalistic psychedelic users: long-term users showed largely non-significant differences in oscillatory power, complexity, and network connectivity compared to non-users across measures commonly altered during acute administration. This suggests that while psychedelics produce profound acute oscillatory reorganization, the lasting therapeutic effects may operate through neuroplastic mechanisms -- such as dendritic spine growth and synaptic remodeling via [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations|BDNF and mTOR pathways]] -- rather than through permanently altered oscillatory dynamics.

## Microdosing and low-dose oscillatory effects

Low doses of psychedelics produce subtler but measurable oscillatory changes. Murray et al. (2024) found that LSD, but not THC or methamphetamine, dose-dependently increased neural complexity while reducing [[delta-oscillations|delta]] and [[theta-oscillations|theta]] power. Inter-individual variability is substantial at low doses: a placebo-controlled study of 13 and 26 mcg LSD tartrate found that stimulatory effects were strongest in individuals with low baseline arousal and attention, while decrements in delta EEG power and enhanced pre-attentive processing persisted during the one-week follow-up (De Wit et al., 2024). A 2025 meta-analysis of microdosing cognitive effects found a significant decrease in cognitive control with no detectable effects on other cognitive domains, aligning with models in which psychedelics reduce top-down rigidity even at sub-perceptual doses (Cavanna et al., 2025). The variability in response may partly explain the inconsistency of microdosing benefits in controlled trials and underscores the need for [[individual-alpha-frequency|individual difference]] measures to stratify microdosing protocols.
