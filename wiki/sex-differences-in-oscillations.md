# Sex Differences in Oscillations

Sex and hormonal status represent underappreciated sources of variability in neural oscillations. Females tend to show faster [[individual-alpha-frequency]] by approximately 0.5-1 Hz, higher sleep spindle density, and cyclic modulation of theta, alpha, and gamma power across the menstrual cycle driven by progesterone's neuroactive metabolite allopregnanolone -- a potent positive allosteric modulator of the same GABA-A receptors that set oscillation timing (see [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]]). Recent large-sample and machine-learning studies confirm that biological sex leaves a robust signature in the EEG that can be decoded with 65-80% balanced accuracy across heterogeneous datasets (Ahamed et al., 2025), with beta-band features contributing most to classification (Vanrullen & Bhaya-Grossman, 2018). Critically, many of these sex differences are not static but interact with age -- aging amplifies sex divergence in low alpha and low beta power (Han et al., 2025) -- and with hormonal state, as a 2025 systematic review of 23 studies confirms consistent menstrual-cycle-dependent shifts in alpha and theta power (Klusmann et al., 2025). Despite these well-documented effects, the majority of oscillation studies either exclude sex as a variable, fail to report it, or are statistically underpowered to detect sex differences, leaving a significant gap in our understanding of how biological sex shapes the frequency architecture of the brain.

## Individual alpha frequency: females run faster

Multiple large-sample studies report that females show higher [[individual-alpha-frequency]] than males by roughly 0.5-1 Hz, even after controlling for age and head size (Chiang et al., 2011; Bazanova & Vernon, 2014). This sex difference is present from adolescence onward and persists into old age, though both sexes show the characteristic inverted-U trajectory described in [[development-and-aging]]. The functional significance is considerable: given that IAF predicts temporal resolution (Samaha & Postle, 2015) and correlates with processing speed (Grandy et al., 2013), a 0.5-1 Hz difference implies measurably finer temporal discrimination in females. Notably, none of the raw sources in this knowledge base -- including Balaji et al. (2026, N=24, 11 female) and Marsicano et al. (2026, N=83, 46 female) -- report sex-stratified IAF analyses, despite having adequate female representation to do so. The Menetrey & Pascucci (2026) alpha archetypes study, with over 2000 participants, similarly does not report whether compositional alpha profiles differ by sex.

A major multi-dataset analysis (N=445 EEG, N=103 iEEG) published in NeuroImage demonstrated that sex differences in alpha power are not uniform across the band but are concentrated in the **low alpha** sub-band (8-9 Hz), where males show heightened temporal power compared to females, while females show greater **low beta** (16-20 Hz) power in parietal-occipital regions (Han et al., 2025). Crucially, these differences were robust only in older adults (55-80 years) and absent in younger adults (18-30 years), suggesting that aging amplifies rather than creates sex divergence in oscillatory dynamics. This age-by-sex interaction has important implications for studies of [[development-and-aging]]: cross-sectional designs that pool across ages may obscure genuine sex effects that emerge only in midlife and beyond.

## Sleep spindles and sex

Females consistently show higher sleep spindle density, greater spindle amplitude, and longer spindle duration compared to males across the lifespan (Purcell et al., 2017; Ujma et al., 2014). Since fast spindles (13-15 Hz) are closely linked to memory consolidation and slow spindles (9-12 Hz) to cortical slow-oscillation coupling (see [[sleep-oscillations]]), these sex differences may have downstream consequences for sleep-dependent learning. The spindle sex difference is one of the most robust findings in sleep EEG research, surviving corrections for age, total sleep time, and recording methodology.

A 2026 systematic review and meta-analysis of 43 studies (13 with sufficient data for meta-analysis, N=668 normal sleepers) confirmed that female normal sleepers show significantly higher spindle density and sigma power compared with males (Terpou et al., 2026). Women also display higher NREM spectral power in the sigma frequency band (12-16 Hz), faster spindle frequency, and greater spindle amplitude, particularly in the fast spindle range. However, the meta-analysis highlighted that only 38% of studies reporting on spindle density found statistically significant sex differences, suggesting that many individual studies are underpowered to detect what is a moderate-sized effect. Developmental data add further nuance: fast spindle density peaks at approximately age 18 in females and age 25 in males, and the age of most rapid decline in spindle amplitude precedes in females relative to males by approximately 1.4 years. A separate systematic review of sex differences in sleep EEG across the lifespan (Mohammadi et al., 2025) confirmed these patterns while noting that nearly half of all tested sleep EEG features show nominally significant sex differences, though individual effect sizes tend to be small.

## GABA-A modulation by sex hormones

The mechanistic link between sex hormones and oscillations runs through the GABA-A receptor. As detailed in [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]], GABA-A receptor subunit composition determines oscillation frequency -- alpha-1 subunits support high gamma while alpha-3 subunits generate lower gamma. Allopregnanolone, a metabolite of progesterone, is among the most potent endogenous positive allosteric modulators of GABA-A receptors, enhancing chloride conductance and prolonging inhibitory postsynaptic currents. This directly affects the timing constants that set [[gamma-oscillations]] frequency: longer IPSCs from allopregnanolone modulation would shift gamma downward and strengthen inhibitory tone. Estradiol, by contrast, modulates NMDA receptor function and can enhance excitatory transmission, shifting the excitation-inhibition balance tracked by [[aperiodic-dynamics]] measures such as the aperiodic exponent.

Lambert et al. (2023) characterized the cortical EEG signatures of allopregnanolone at non-sedative doses in rodents, comparing it with pentobarbital, diazepam, and ketamine. At comparable non-sedative doses, allopregnanolone's EEG profile during active waking was indistinguishable from pentobarbital, suggesting that its purported selectivity for delta-subunit-containing GABA-A receptors does not produce a distinctive large-scale cortical signature under these conditions. Diazepam, with its alpha-subunit selectivity, showed recognizable overlap but also distinct features, while ketamine (an NMDA antagonist and rapid antidepressant comparator) exhibited no distinguishing overlap with allopregnanolone. Separately, allopregnanolone has been shown to selectively enhance high theta oscillations (6-12 Hz) in the basolateral amygdala through delta-containing GABA-A receptors (Antonoudiou et al., 2022), a circuit-specific effect distinct from other positive allosteric modulators such as benzodiazepines -- illustrating that the same neurosteroid may produce divergent oscillatory effects depending on brain region and local receptor subunit composition.

**Testosterone** also shapes oscillatory dynamics, though through partially distinct mechanisms. Testosterone modulates GABA-A receptor density and agonist affinity, and emerging evidence links developmental increases in testosterone to changes in [[gamma-oscillations]] power. A MEG study of 87 children aged 6-13 found that pubertal testosterone shifts were associated with spectrally-specific alterations in alpha and gamma oscillatory power in prefrontal, parietal, and temporal regions during selective attention, with sex-by-testosterone interactions in the anterior cingulate and prefrontal cortices suggesting sexually divergent network development (Wilson et al., 2025). Additionally, sex hormones interact with glutamatergic neurotransmission (Bhatt et al., 2023), providing a second pathway -- beyond GABAergic modulation -- through which hormonal status can shape excitation-inhibition balance and, consequently, the [[aperiodic-dynamics]] and periodic features of the EEG.

## Menstrual cycle effects on spectral power

The menstrual cycle produces measurable shifts in oscillatory dynamics. During the luteal phase, when progesterone peaks, studies report increased theta power, altered alpha power and peak frequency, and steeper aperiodic slopes consistent with enhanced inhibition (Brötzner et al., 2014; Pletzer et al., 2014). These cyclic changes can be substantial -- up to 1 Hz shifts in alpha peak frequency within the same individual across the cycle -- rivaling the magnitude of between-subject variability typically attributed to trait differences. Oral contraceptives, which suppress endogenous hormone fluctuations, attenuate these cyclic effects, providing pharmacological evidence for the hormonal mechanism.

A 2025 systematic review of 23 studies synthesized the most convergent findings across the menstrual cycle literature (Klusmann et al., 2025). The strongest pattern is that [[alpha-oscillations]] power in frontal, parietal, and temporal areas tends to **decrease** during the late follicular phase (high estradiol, low progesterone) compared to the luteal phase, while [[theta-oscillations]] power tends to **increase** during the late follicular phase. The reviewers interpreted these follicular-phase changes as reflecting greater attentional efficiency, emotional well-being, and reduced self-referential processing in the days surrounding ovulation. Findings for delta, [[beta-oscillations]], and [[gamma-oscillations]] bands remain inconclusive, with too few studies and inconsistent results to draw firm conclusions. The review proposed that resting-state EEG may serve as a biomarker for hormone-related neurophysiological changes relevant to mood disorders, particularly premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD). Indeed, PMDD has been associated with altered delta/beta power ratios across the cycle (Ertl et al., 2022), suggesting that clinical hormone sensitivity may have a measurable oscillatory signature. Bazanova et al. (2024) further examined the relationship between salivary estradiol, progesterone, and testosterone levels and resting-state EEG parameters -- including IAF, alpha power, and 1/f slope -- providing evidence that hormonal status modulates both periodic and [[aperiodic-dynamics]] features.

## Frontal alpha asymmetry and emotion

Some evidence suggests sex differences in frontal [[alpha-oscillations]] asymmetry related to emotion processing. Females tend to show greater right-hemisphere alpha suppression during negative emotion processing, consistent with greater right-lateralized engagement, while males show more symmetric patterns (Stewart et al., 2010). These differences have implications for sex-skewed prevalence of mood disorders and for the interpretation of frontal asymmetry as a biomarker for depression risk.

Recent work has strengthened and complicated this picture. Sharpley et al. (2023) examined EEG frontal alpha asymmetry (FAA) in 100 community volunteers and found that different frontal electrode sites were associated with different depression subtypes (depressed mood, anhedonia, cognitive depression, somatic depression), and critically, that the asymmetry-depression association was modulated by both sex and depression severity. A separate investigation found that inconsistency in the FAA-depression literature may partly reflect unmeasured moderators including sex, age, and peripheral inflammation (Karakas et al., 2023). High alpha power (11-13 Hz) recorded from portable prefrontal EEG devices has also been proposed as a gender-sensitive biomarker for emotional disorders (Liu et al., 2025), though validation across diverse populations remains limited. These findings reinforce that sex must be treated as a core moderating variable in any study of frontal asymmetry and affective risk -- failure to stratify by sex may explain the persistent heterogeneity in the FAA literature.

## Clinical implications

Sex differences in oscillatory physiology may contribute to sex-skewed prevalence of oscillation-linked disorders. Photosensitive epilepsy is approximately twice as common in females, potentially reflecting their faster alpha frequencies positioning them closer to the maximally provocative 12-18 Hz photic driving range described in [[clinical-applications]]. ADHD, associated with altered IAF and aperiodic dynamics (Marsicano et al., 2026), is diagnosed more frequently in males, though whether this reflects genuine oscillatory differences or diagnostic bias remains debated. Sex differences in spindle density may also be relevant to sex-skewed patterns of sleep-dependent memory consolidation.

**Catamenial epilepsy** -- seizure exacerbation linked to the menstrual cycle -- provides the clearest clinical demonstration of hormonal modulation of neural excitability. Seizure susceptibility increases when estradiol is high relative to progesterone (periovulatory and perimenstrual windows), reflecting estradiol's proconvulsant enhancement of excitatory transmission and the withdrawal of progesterone-derived allopregnanolone's anticonvulsant GABAergic inhibition (Sadri & Brewster, 2025). Conventional anti-seizure medications often provide limited benefit, and recent work has explored metformin as a neuroendocrine modulator capable of influencing estrogen, progesterone, and neurosteroid signaling in catamenial epilepsy management (Abdollahi et al., 2025). Beyond catamenial patterns, Sadri & Brewster (2025) reviewed evidence that sex dimorphism in epilepsy extends broadly: in Scn8a-mutant mouse models, heterozygous females showed greater resilience to seizure-related mortality and morbidity than males, with pathway enrichment analysis revealing sex-specific gene expression differences in energy metabolism and cholesterol biosynthesis pathways. These findings challenge the one-size-fits-all approach to understanding epilepsy mechanisms.

In **Fragile X syndrome** (FXS) -- the most common monogenic cause of autism spectrum disorder -- [[gamma-oscillations]] have emerged as a translational biomarker, but with marked sex differences. While elevated resting-state gamma power (30-60 Hz) is a hallmark EEG phenotype in males with FXS and male Fmr1-knockout mice, female Fmr1-knockout mice show no gamma alterations in adulthood (Berzhanskaya et al., 2025). However, theta and low beta power alterations are similar across sexes, while alpha, upper beta, gamma, and epsilon bands show sex-specific patterns (Ethridge et al., 2021). A recent study further demonstrated that alpha-like oscillations in the visual cortex of Fmr1-knockout mice are slowed, with the phenotype being stronger in adults than juveniles (Bhatt et al., 2026). Machine learning classifiers using combined theta, alpha, and gamma power can discriminate FXS females and non-mosaic males from controls with AUC values exceeding 0.94 (Schmitt et al., 2024), underscoring that sex must be accounted for when developing EEG-based biomarkers for neurodevelopmental disorders.

## Pregnancy and postpartum

Pregnancy and the postpartum period represent extreme hormonal transitions that reshape oscillatory dynamics. Sandoval et al. (2023) compared resting-state EEG coherence during pregnancy to 3 months postpartum and found that pregnancy was characterized by lower intrahemispheric coherence between frontal and parietal regions in the alpha-1 and alpha-2 bands, along with greater interhemispheric coherence between frontopolar regions in beta-2. The shift from pregnancy to postpartum -- with increasing intrahemispheric and decreasing interhemispheric coherence -- was interpreted as reflecting decreased inhibition of neural circuits, potentially representing an adaptive neurophysiological reorganization for motherhood. Earlier work reported that pregnancy is associated with weaker delta-band power in left temporal and middle parietal regions and stronger beta and theta power centrally (Duffy et al., 2020). These EEG changes parallel structural findings: dense-sampling MRI studies have demonstrated spatiotemporal patterns of brain volume change across the menstrual cycle associated with progesterone and estradiol levels (Zsido et al., 2025), and longitudinal postpartum imaging reveals widespread cortical volume increases from early to one-year postpartum (Paternina-Die et al., 2024). Whether EEG changes during pregnancy predict postpartum outcomes such as postpartum depression remains an active area of investigation, with one recent protocol (Gomes et al., 2025) designed specifically to examine whether aberrant EEG patterns during labor predict PPD risk.

## Developmental sex differences

Sex differences in oscillatory parameters emerge early and follow divergent trajectories. A longitudinal study tracking [[aperiodic-dynamics]] from infancy to age 7 (N=391, 46.5% female) found significant nonlinear age-by-sex interactions, with sex differences in aperiodic slope and offset emerging after approximately the first year of life (Sacks et al., 2025). Female participants showed steeper aperiodic slopes and reduced offsets compared to males at ages 3 and 5, with trajectories characterized by relative increases in early childhood followed by decreases or stabilization by age 7, varying by brain region. These early sex differences in the aperiodic exponent are noteworthy given that the exponent indexes excitation-inhibition balance (see [[aperiodic-dynamics]]) and may reflect the earlier onset of hormonal influences on GABAergic tone in females.

In adolescence, pubertal sex hormones begin to exert more pronounced effects. Wilson et al. (2025) demonstrated that developmental testosterone increases in children aged 6-13 were associated with alpha and gamma oscillatory changes in prefrontal, parietal, and temporal regions during selective [[attention-and-perception]], with sex-by-testosterone interactions in anterior cingulate and prefrontal cortices. Sleep spindle maturation also shows sex-dependent trajectories: fast spindle density peaks approximately 7 years earlier in females (age 18) than males (age 25), and the most rapid decline in spindle amplitude begins earlier in females by about 1.4 years. Basar et al. (2024) found that females display higher theta event-related oscillation responses than males across frontal, central, and parietal regions during adulthood (ages 18-50), and that the decline of theta amplitudes with age is steeper in women, producing a crossover effect in midlife. These developmental trajectories underscore that any study of [[development-and-aging]] in oscillatory parameters must account for sex to avoid confounding maturational timing differences with group-level trait effects.

## Methodological concerns

The most important takeaway may be methodological. The raw sources in this knowledge base illustrate the problem: of dozens of EEG studies analyzed, nearly all report participant sex in their demographics table but none analyze it as a variable of interest. Several studies (e.g., Guth-Baker et al., 2026, with only 5 of 27 participants female) have substantial sex imbalances that limit generalizability. Until oscillation studies routinely report sex-stratified results or are powered to detect sex effects, the field risks treating male-typical oscillatory patterns as universal defaults. Reporting sex-stratified means for IAF, spectral power, and aperiodic parameters should be considered a minimum standard.

The machine-learning literature reinforces both the reality and the nuisance potential of sex differences. Ahamed et al. (2025) achieved 65-80% balanced accuracy in classifying biological sex from EEG across multiple heterogeneous datasets, confirming that sex-specific oscillatory patterns are real and detectable. However, this same detectability means that biological sex can act as a confound in clinical EEG classification tasks -- a model trained to detect pathology may inadvertently learn sex-correlated features rather than disease-specific ones (Ahamed et al., 2025). The Terpou et al. (2026) spindle meta-analysis highlights another methodological pitfall: only 38% of individual studies found statistically significant spindle density sex differences, despite meta-analytic confirmation that the effect is real, illustrating that many individual studies lack the power to detect moderate-sized sex effects. The 2025 systematic review of menstrual cycle effects on EEG (Klusmann et al., 2025) further emphasizes that cycle phase should be recorded and reported, particularly for studies of theta and alpha power. Failure to account for menstrual cycle phase introduces within-subject variability that can exceed between-group differences in many clinical comparisons. Taken together, these considerations argue for three minimum standards: (1) report sex-stratified descriptive statistics for all oscillatory measures, (2) record menstrual cycle phase and contraceptive use in female participants, and (3) test for sex-by-condition interactions even when sex effects are not the primary research question.
