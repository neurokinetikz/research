# Sharp-Wave Ripples

Sharp-wave ripples (SWRs) are brief (50-100 ms), high-frequency oscillations in the 80-200 Hz range that occur in hippocampal area CA1, generated when excitatory sharp waves originating from CA3 drive fast ripple oscillations in CA1 through the interaction of pyramidal cells and local interneurons. SWRs are the hippocampal oscillation most directly linked to memory consolidation, functioning as the mechanism by which neural sequences encoded during [[theta-oscillations]]-dominated exploration are reactivated in compressed form and broadcast to neocortical targets for long-term storage. They occur during two distinct behavioral states -- quiet wakefulness and NREM sleep -- and their disruption reliably impairs memory while their enhancement improves it, establishing SWRs as both necessary and sufficient for systems-level memory consolidation. Recent work has revealed that not all SWRs are equal: only a subset of large-amplitude ripples carries memory-relevant content (Robinson et al., 2026), and distinct ripple subtypes -- defined by current-source-density profile -- organize parallel reactivation channels that simultaneously integrate new experiences and update older memories (Castelli et al., 2025). Human intracranial recordings have further expanded the functional repertoire of SWRs beyond spatial memory to include semantic retrieval, emotional encoding, and spontaneous self-generated thought (Iwata et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024).

## Anatomy and Generation

SWRs arise from the intrinsic circuitry of the hippocampus. The sharp wave component reflects a massive burst of excitatory activity from CA3 pyramidal cells, propagated via Schaffer collaterals to CA1. This excitatory volley depolarizes CA1 pyramidal neurons and, critically, activates fast-spiking parvalbumin-positive (PV+) basket cells that provide rapid perisomatic inhibition. The interplay between pyramidal excitation and PV+ interneuron inhibition generates the fast ripple oscillation (typically 100-200 Hz, centering near 150 Hz) that rides atop the slower sharp wave envelope. This inhibitory rebound mechanism -- where hyperpolarization deinactivates sodium channels, enabling precisely timed rebound spikes -- produces the millisecond-precision spike timing that is essential for downstream plasticity (Sejnowski, 2025). The ripple frequency (~100 Hz) corresponds to the temporal window for spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP), placing SWR-associated spiking squarely within the +/-10 ms window required for long-term potentiation.

The developmental emergence of ripples is tied to the maturation of inhibitory circuits. Pochinok et al. (2024) demonstrated in mice that ripples are robustly detected only from postnatal day 10 onward, with their appearance mechanistically linked to the maturation of GABAergic inhibition from PV+ interneurons (see [[development-and-aging]]). Chemogenetic silencing of CA1 interneurons reduced ripple rate, confirming that inhibition is not merely permissive but necessary for ripple generation. Critically, early SPW-Rs that include ripple oscillations elicit stronger responses in the prefrontal cortex than sharp waves lacking ripples, suggesting that the developmental onset of inhibitory ripple generation establishes the functional hippocampal-cortical communication channels required for [[hippocampal-memory]] consolidation.

## Replay and Temporal Compression

The defining functional property of SWRs is replay: the compressed reactivation of neural firing sequences that were originally established during waking experience. During active exploration, place cells fire in ordered sequences across multiple [[theta-oscillations]] cycles as an animal traverses space, with phase precession compressing spatial trajectories into ~125 ms theta cycles. During subsequent SWRs, these same sequences are replayed in approximately 5-20x temporal compression, condensing seconds of real-time experience into the 50-100 ms ripple window (Sejnowski, 2025; Dupret et al., 2025). This extreme compression brings the inter-spike intervals of sequentially activated neurons into the critical STDP timing window, enabling synaptic strengthening of the replayed sequence. Dupret et al. (2025) characterize ripple-timescale replay (50-100 ms) as providing approximately 20x temporal compression for consolidation, and note that sequential activity during ripples constitutes a fundamental population code feature for memory.

Recent work by Castelli et al. (2025) has revealed that **ripple diversity** organizes reactivation dynamics in the offline brain. Using current-source-density analysis in CA1, they identified two functionally distinct ripple subtypes. **Radsink ripples** -- defined by current sinks in stratum radiatum -- recruit both superficial and deep CA1 principal cells, integrating recent waking coactivity motifs into denser, higher-dimensional reactivation patterns that remain stable over hour-long timescales. **LMsink ripples** -- with sinks in stratum lacunosum-moleculare -- primarily engage deep-layer cells, producing sparser, lower-dimensional patterns that carry core motifs of earlier experience and undergo gradual "reactivation drift," progressively updating pre-structured representations with new waking information. This ripple-by-ripple diversity supports parallel reactivation channels, enabling the hippocampus to simultaneously consolidate recent experience and incrementally revise older memories.

## The Consolidation Triad: SWR-Spindle-Slow Oscillation Coupling

During NREM sleep, SWRs do not operate in isolation but participate in a precisely coordinated triple coupling with thalamocortical [[sleep-oscillations]]. The consolidation mechanism follows a hierarchical nesting sequence: cortical slow oscillations (0.5-1 Hz) provide the broadest temporal frame, organizing up-states during which thalamic sleep spindles (12-16 Hz) are triggered. SWRs are temporally locked to the rising phase of spindle oscillations, which in turn nest within the up-state of slow oscillations. This SWR-spindle-slow oscillation coupling creates a temporal window during which hippocampal replay sequences are transmitted to neocortex at moments of maximal cortical receptivity (Creery et al., 2022; Canu et al., 2026).

Creery et al. (2022) demonstrated through targeted memory reactivation (TMR) that playing memory-associated sounds during slow-wave sleep produces elevated theta (4-8 Hz) and sigma/spindle (12-16 Hz) power in medial temporal lobe, with [[gamma-oscillations]] power (20-100 Hz) correlating with the magnitude of memory improvement. This hierarchical model -- slow oscillations framing spindles, spindles framing ripple-associated activity -- positions SWRs as the content carrier within a multi-frequency [[cross-frequency-coupling]] architecture optimized for hippocampal-neocortical information transfer. Canu et al. (2026) confirmed that spindle-frequency (12-15 Hz) phase synchronization peaks during N3 deep sleep, while delta-phase coupling drives broadband high-frequency modulation spanning 30-200 Hz, providing the temporal scaffolding within which SWR content is consolidated.

Staresina et al. (2023) provided the most detailed human intracranial characterization of the consolidation triad to date, combining intracranial EEG with multiunit recordings from the hippocampus and surrounding medial temporal lobe. Their results show that slow oscillation up-states set the stage for spindles, which in turn gate ripple occurrence, producing a stepwise increase in neuronal firing rates, short-latency cross-correlations among local neuronal assemblies, and cross-regional medial temporal lobe interactions. The resulting temporal alignment places neuronal spiking within the window for spike-timing-dependent plasticity and systems consolidation. Schreiner et al. (2024) extended these findings by demonstrating that **spindle-locked ripples** recorded intracranially from the human medial temporal lobe correlate specifically with experimentally induced memory reactivation during NREM sleep. Using TMR with spatial memory cues, they showed that cortical spindle activity modulates ripple amplitude in the medial temporal lobe through phase-amplitude coupling, and that items cued during sleep showed superior post-sleep recall compared to uncued items -- the first direct evidence in humans that spindle-locked ripples mediate memory reactivation.

The role of the thalamus in orchestrating this triad has been clarified by Dickey et al. (2024), who identified ~90 Hz ripple oscillations in the human anterior and posterior thalamus with characteristics resembling hippocampal and cortical ripples. However, direct thalamo-cortical ripple coupling was infrequent and weak (only 14% of anterior thalamo-cortical channel pairs showed significant coupling, compared to 52% for cortico-cortical pairs). Instead, the thalamus coordinates widespread cortical and hippocampal co-rippling through projecting spindles and up-states (see [[thalamocortical-circuits]]): cortico-cortical co-rippling increased by ~53% when accompanied by anterior thalamic spindles, and by ~68% during shared up-states. This positions the thalamus as a coordinator rather than driver of consolidation-related ripple activity.

## Awake Replay: Planning and Decision-Making

SWRs are not restricted to sleep. During quiet wakefulness -- particularly at reward locations, during pauses in exploration, and at decision points -- SWRs generate both forward and reverse replay of spatial trajectories. Forward replay may support planning and prospective evaluation of upcoming paths, while reverse replay may support credit assignment by linking current outcomes back to preceding actions. Dupret et al. (2025) emphasize that the hippocampus employs a scaffolding principle during spatial exploration, with higher-activity cells forming a stable backbone (in the CA1 deep sublayer) onto which new information from lower-activity cells (in the CA1 superficial sublayer) is integrated. This scaffolding operates during both awake and sleep SWRs, suggesting a common replay mechanism across behavioral states.

A landmark study by Yang et al. (2024) demonstrated that awake SWRs function as a **neurophysiological tagging mechanism** that selects which experiences are preserved for long-term storage. Recording from 4,469 hippocampal CA1 cells in mice traversing a figure-eight maze, they found that successive maze traversals were tracked by continuously drifting neuronal populations, and that SWRs occurring during reward consumption carried specific spike content that decoded the surrounding trial blocks. Critically, sleep replay patterns were strongly correlated with which trial blocks had been replayed during waking SWRs (R = 0.86, P < 10⁻³⁶), establishing that awake replay content directly predicts subsequent sleep consolidation targets.

However, the behavioral timescale over which awake SWRs influence memory remains debated. Deceuninck and Kloosterman (2024) disrupted awake SWRs in rats performing repeated-acquisition spatial memory tasks and found no impairment of short-term memorization of new locations. This null result suggests that the impact of awake replay on memory and behavior may be long-term and cumulative rather than immediately guiding upcoming choices, challenging assumptions about replay's role in working memory at the seconds-to-minutes timescale.

In humans, awake SWRs have been linked to functions beyond spatial navigation. Zhang et al. (2024) recorded from the amygdala and hippocampus in epilepsy patients during an emotional memory task and found increased awake ripple rates after encoding of emotionally valenced compared to neutral stimuli. Post-encoding ripple-locked stimulus similarity predicted later memory discrimination, with amygdala ripple-locked representations appearing earlier than hippocampal ones and mutual information analysis confirming amygdala influence on hippocampal activity -- providing direct evidence that awake ripples enhance memory for emotional events through amygdala-hippocampal coordination. Iwata et al. (2024) further showed, using continuous hippocampal recordings over 9-15 days in epilepsy patients, that SWR rates were elevated during periods of vivid, internally focused self-generated thought -- states characterized by mind wandering, imaginative content, and low task-relatedness -- suggesting that ripple-associated replay may underlie spontaneous cognition and [[hippocampal-memory]] retrieval during waking.

## Relationship to Theta: Complementary Encoding Modes

SWRs and theta oscillations represent complementary operational modes of the hippocampus. During active exploration, the hippocampus operates in theta mode: theta sequences encode ongoing experience through phase precession, with [[gamma-oscillations]] nested within theta cycles carrying content-specific information (Huang et al., 2026). When the animal pauses or enters quiet rest, the hippocampus transitions to SWR mode, replaying previously encoded sequences. This theta-SWR alternation implements Buzsaki's two-stage model of memory: theta encodes, SWRs consolidate (Dupret et al., 2025). Critically, Joshi et al. (2026, cited in Huang et al.) demonstrated that disrupting theta sequences impairs spatial learning even when SWR replay is preserved, establishing that both encoding (theta) and consolidation (SWRs) are independently necessary for [[hippocampal-memory]] formation.

## Large SWRs, Memory Selection, and Optogenetic Enhancement

Not all ripples contribute equally to memory consolidation. Robinson et al. (2026) identified a specific subset of **large-amplitude SWRs** in mice that are preferentially linked to memory reactivation in both the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex (PFC). The occurrence of these large SWRs selectively increased during sleep following new learning, and only this minority of sleep SWRs was associated with coordinated hippocampo-cortical ensemble reactivation. Using **closed-loop optogenetic boosting** of SWRs during sleep (see [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]]), Robinson et al. demonstrated that augmenting ripple activity enhanced ensemble memory reactivation in hippocampus and PFC, improved subsequent memory retrieval, and strengthened hippocampal-PFC coordination during waking -- causally linking large SWRs to both ensemble reactivation and behavioral memory performance. Remarkably, even mice engineered with cognitive impairments displayed restored memory consolidation when ripples were optogenetically boosted, suggesting that artificial enhancement of SWR-driven consolidation could compensate for endogenous deficits.

These findings complement the memory selection mechanism described by Yang et al. (2024), in which awake SWRs tag experiences for later sleep consolidation. Together, the two studies outline a two-stage selection process: awake SWRs during reward consumption tag trial blocks for preferential replay, and during subsequent sleep, large-amplitude SWRs carry those tagged representations to neocortical targets. The implication is that the SWR system is not a passive replay machine but an active filter that determines which experiences enter long-term storage -- a neurophysiological implementation of memory prioritization.

## Ripple Oscillations Outside the Hippocampus

Ripple-frequency oscillations (~80-120 Hz) are not unique to hippocampal CA1 but have been increasingly documented across neocortical regions, where they appear to serve complementary roles in memory consolidation and retrieval. Verzhbinsky et al. (2024) demonstrated using human intracranial recordings that co-occurring cortical ripples -- synchronous high-frequency bursts at separate cortical locations separated by up to 16 mm -- increase the integration of neuronal firing across those locations. Neurons at co-rippling sites showed increased synchronized firing, improved predictability of each other's activity, and coordinated participation in neural assemblies. These effects operated through phase-modulation of spiking to ripple peaks and were consistent across temporal and motor cortex during both sleep and waking, strengthening with additional co-rippling sites through re-entrant facilitation.

The coordination of hippocampal and cortical ripples appears to be orchestrated not by direct ripple transmission but by thalamic spindles and cortical up-states (Dickey et al., 2024). During memory retrieval, hippocampal ripples may trigger **cortical dimensionality expansion** -- a process by which compressed hippocampal memory traces are "decompressed" into high-dimensional cortical activity patterns that support the reconstruction of detailed mnemonic content. Intracranial EEG data from epilepsy patients performing an episodic memory task showed that cortical dimensionality increased following ripple events during correct but not incorrect retrieval, and this expansion correlated with faster reaction times and reinstatement of target associations (Kerren et al., 2025). Hippocampal [[theta-oscillations]] and cortical [[gamma-oscillations]] [[cross-frequency-coupling]] emerged after ripples but before cortical expansion, suggesting a mechanistic sequence from ripple initiation through theta-gamma coupling to cortical memory reconstruction.

In the domain of semantic memory, hippocampal SWRs and cortical ripples in the anterior temporal lobe -- a major hub for semantic processing -- co-occur and increase in rate prior to free recall of word lists in humans. Coincident hippocampal-cortical ripples are associated with greater cortical reactivation and show content-specific spatial selectivity, with the location of cortical ripples depending on what is being recalled (Vaz et al., 2024). These findings suggest that hippocampal-cortical ripple coordination extends beyond episodic spatial memory to encompass the retrieval architecture of human semantic knowledge.

## Spike Timing and Plasticity During Ripples

The high-frequency oscillations within SWRs impose precise temporal structure on neuronal spiking, with individual neurons firing at specific phases of the ripple cycle. Sejnowski (2025) proposes that this precision arises from the inhibitory rebound mechanism mediated by PV+ basket cells: strong transient inhibition followed by sodium channel deinactivation produces precisely timed rebound spikes. The resulting spike timing falls within the STDP window (~10 ms), enabling potentiation of synapses activated in the correct temporal order. The ripple frequency of ~100 Hz maps to the STDP timing limit, suggesting that SWRs operate at the optimal frequency for plasticity induction. Traveling waves propagating across cortex at 0.1-0.8 m/s may further distribute STDP-triggering spike sequences, creating the temporary synaptic modifications that Sejnowski terms the "second-tier network" -- a transient cognitive layer riding above permanent sensorimotor circuits.

## Clinical Relevance

SWR disruption has consistent effects on memory: optogenetic or electrical interruption of ripples during post-learning sleep impairs subsequent memory recall, while prolongation or enhancement of ripples improves consolidation. The closed-loop optogenetic SWR-boosting results of Robinson et al. (2026) provide the most direct causal evidence to date, showing that augmenting ripple activity during sleep rescues memory performance even in cognitively impaired mice -- a finding with clear translational implications.

In **Alzheimer's disease** (see [[clinical-applications]]), SWR disruption is an early pathological feature, potentially contributing to memory decline before overt neuronal loss. Both amyloid and tau pathology independently degrade SWR function. In 5XFAD mice (a model of early amyloid pathology), decreased SWR frequencies co-occur with rigid CA1 place fields and disrupted medial prefrontal-hippocampal connectivity, producing persistent risk-taking behaviors indicative of impaired decision-making (Cell Reports, 2025). Separately, high-molecular-weight tau derived from Alzheimer's patients selectively impairs complex-spike burst firing in CA1 hippocampal neurons -- the very cellular mechanism underlying ripple generation -- with consequent disruption of both [[theta-oscillations]] rhythms and high-frequency ripples (Cell, 2025). In apoE4 knock-in mice, reduced SWR abundance and diminished CA3 slow [[gamma-oscillations]] power during SWRs predicted spatial memory impairments measured 1-2 months later, and early reductions in SWR-associated gamma power predicted memory deficits appearing 10 months later, positioning SWR dysfunction as a **functional biomarker** for incipient cognitive decline. Coordination of SWRs between the medial entorhinal cortex and CA1 is disrupted even before behavioral memory impairments emerge in APP-KI mice, suggesting that SWR incoordination in the entorhinal-hippocampal circuit is among the earliest network-level symptoms of Alzheimer's pathology.

Keil et al. (2024) have proposed that **artificial sharp-wave ripples** induced through noninvasive high-frequency visual stimulation could potentially restore neural synchronization disrupted in neurodegeneration, building on the broader logic that if SWR disruption contributes to memory decline, then SWR restoration or augmentation could serve as a therapeutic strategy. In epilepsy, pathological high-frequency oscillations in the ripple range may reflect corrupted SWR mechanisms. The sensitivity of SWRs to [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]] -- particularly cholinergic and GABAergic modulation -- positions them as potential therapeutic targets, and their disruption during anesthesia contributes to the amnesia characteristic of [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]] states.

## Human Intracranial SWR Studies

While much of the foundational SWR literature derives from rodent electrophysiology, a growing body of human intracranial recordings -- primarily from epilepsy patients undergoing seizure localization with implanted depth electrodes -- has confirmed and extended the animal findings. Staresina et al. (2023) and Schreiner et al. (2024) established the hierarchical nesting of slow oscillations, spindles, and ripples in the human medial temporal lobe during sleep, demonstrating that the consolidation triad operates in humans as it does in rodents. During waking, human hippocampal SWRs have been linked to visual episodic recollection, emotional memory encoding (Zhang et al., 2024), spontaneous self-generated thought (Iwata et al., 2024), and semantic retrieval (Vaz et al., 2024).

A particularly informative line of research has examined how ripple timing encodes memory content. Intracranial recordings from patients performing serial recall tasks have shown that the timing of ripple events relative to ongoing slow-theta oscillations carries information about temporal order, suggesting that the precise phase at which ripples occur within broader oscillatory frameworks contributes to the encoding of sequential relationships in episodic memory. Attention also modulates SWR dynamics: periods of sustained high attention are associated with increased SWR occurrence and tighter temporal alignment of ripples to ongoing hippocampal oscillations, indicating that the cognitive state of the individual shapes the conditions under which ripple-associated processing occurs. These human studies collectively demonstrate that SWRs are not merely a rodent phenomenon tied to spatial navigation but a fundamental mechanism of human memory operating across episodic, semantic, and emotional domains.
