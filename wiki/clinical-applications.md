# Clinical Applications

EEG oscillation research has yielded clinically relevant biomarkers and therapeutic approaches across neurological and psychiatric conditions including schizophrenia, Parkinson's disease, epilepsy, Alzheimer's disease, depression, anxiety, ADHD, autism, traumatic brain injury, chronic pain, and disorders of consciousness -- spanning the [[development-and-aging|developmental]] and degenerative continuum. These findings connect fundamental oscillatory mechanisms -- [[individual-alpha-frequency]] variability, [[beta-oscillations]] sub-band dynamics, [[gamma-oscillations]] entrainment, and [[alpha-oscillations]] gating -- to diagnostic and treatment applications, with [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]], deep brain stimulation, and sensory entrainment emerging as frequency-targeted interventions. The period 2023--2026 has seen several milestones in clinical translation: the FDA approved the first adaptive deep brain stimulation system driven by real-time beta oscillation sensing (Medtronic BrainSense, February 2025), the first implantable continuous EEG monitor for epilepsy received De Novo authorization (Epiminder Minder, April 2025), and 40 Hz gamma entrainment therapy for Alzheimer's disease advanced to a 670-patient pivotal trial with FDA Breakthrough Device Designation.

## Schizophrenia and 40 Hz ASSR Deficit

The 40 Hz auditory steady-state response (ASSR) deficit in schizophrenia is among the most replicated EEG biomarkers in psychiatry. Kwon et al. (1999) first demonstrated reduced 40 Hz ASSR power and phase-locking, and meta-analyses (Thune et al., 2016, JAMA Psychiatry) confirmed consistent deficits. The impairment is frequency-specific -- 20-30 Hz responses may be normal while 40 Hz is selectively disrupted -- with a characteristic left-lateralized phase delay in auditory cortex. The mechanism centers on NMDA receptor hypofunction on PV+ fast-spiking interneurons: ketamine produces dose-dependent 40 Hz phase-locking reductions correlating with receptor occupancy. In high-risk populations, 40 Hz deficits predict psychosis transition with 69-77% accuracy. Notably, the 40 Hz phase-locking angle (PLA) may be more sensitive than evoked power or phase-locking factor for differentiating schizophrenia from controls (Hirano et al., 2022), pointing toward refined biomarker definitions.

Recent work distinguishes **treatment-resistant schizophrenia** (TRS, ~30% of patients) from treatment-responsive forms using oscillatory measures. Patients with TRS show further decreased gamma-band ASSR relative to treatment-responsive patients (Fujioka et al., 2023). A 2025 study found that patients with refractory positive symptoms exhibit elevated **theta-gamma [[cross-frequency-coupling|phase-amplitude coupling]]** specifically in the left temporoparietal junction (Suzuki et al., 2025), suggesting that aberrant [[theta-oscillations]]-to-[[gamma-oscillations]] coupling in auditory cortex may index treatment resistance. Baseline gamma power also predicts cognitive gains after auditory-based cognitive training, and change in gamma power after training exposure predicts improvement in both positive and negative symptoms (Tarasenko et al., 2020), offering a potential pharmacodynamic biomarker for interventional trials. See also [[gamma-oscillations]] and [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]].

## Parkinson's Disease and Pathological Beta

The subthalamic nucleus (STN) in Parkinson's disease exhibits exaggerated [[beta-oscillations]] synchronization (13-35 Hz, peaks ~18-25 Hz) correlating with bradykinesia and rigidity. Tinkhauser et al. (2017, Brain) showed beta fluctuates as discrete "bursts," with longer bursts (>900 ms) correlating with motor impairment. Levodopa suppresses low beta (13-20 Hz) and limits burst evolution. High-frequency DBS (>100 Hz) suppresses pathological beta, while STN stimulation at 20 Hz actually slows movement (Chen et al., 2007), providing causal evidence for the "antikinetic beta hypothesis." De Hemptinne et al. (2015) showed DBS also reduces pathological beta-gamma [[cross-frequency-coupling|phase-amplitude coupling]].

The translation of beta-oscillation biomarkers into closed-loop therapy reached a major milestone with the **ADAPT-PD** (Adaptive DBS Algorithm for Personalized Therapy in Parkinson's Disease) pivotal trial (Gilron et al., 2025, JAMA Neurology). In 68 enrolled patients (45 completing aDBS evaluation), **adaptive DBS** driven by real-time subthalamic beta power maintained "on" time without troublesome dyskinesia in 91% of dual-threshold participants, while reducing total electrical energy delivered by 13-16% compared to continuous DBS (p = 0.007). Median beta peak frequencies were 17.6 Hz (left) and 18.6 Hz (right), within the low-beta range most associated with bradykinesia. Critically, 98% of participants chose to remain on adaptive stimulation after the evaluation period. In February 2025, the FDA approved Medtronic's **BrainSense Adaptive** DBS system -- the first commercially available adaptive neurostimulator -- based substantially on these data. A case report has also demonstrated sustained clinical benefit using finely-tuned gamma oscillations as an alternative or complementary biomarker signal (Cernera et al., 2025), hinting that beta may not be the only viable feedback signal. See also [[motor-control]] and [[corticostriatal-learning]].

## Epilepsy and Photosensitive Seizure Thresholds

Photosensitive epilepsy reveals the clinical significance of the [[alpha-oscillations]] boundary. Intermittent photic stimulation identifies 12-18 Hz as maximally provocative for photoparoxysmal responses (Kasteleijn-Nolst Trenite et al., 2012), at the alpha-beta transition near [[individual-alpha-frequency]] -- explaining the 1997 Pokemon incident. At the beta-gamma boundary, gamma oscillations (30-70 Hz) near seizure onset zones precede interictal discharges by 35-190 ms. SMR (12-15 Hz) neurofeedback, from Sterman's foundational seizure-resistance studies, has been translated to human epilepsy treatment.

A major advance in epilepsy monitoring came with the FDA's De Novo authorization of Epiminder's **Minder** system in April 2025 -- the first implantable continuous EEG monitor approved in the United States. The UMPIRE clinical study demonstrated Minder's ability to capture high-quality subcortical EEG data continuously for months to years (including a groundbreaking five-year continuous recording in one participant), delivering actionable clinical insights for nearly 90% of study participants (Epiminder, 2025). This extends the monitoring window from days with conventional scalp EEG to potentially years of uninterrupted electrographic surveillance, enabling detection of seizure cycles and interictal epileptiform discharge patterns that unfold over weeks to months. Complementary research on subcutaneous EEG (sqEEG) has shown signal quality comparable to simultaneous scalp EEG with high stability over 15+ months (Viana et al., 2025), and seizure forecasting using ultra-long-term EEG recordings achieved better-than-chance performance in 83% of patients, with meaningful 30-day forecasts in 22% (Karoly et al., 2024). Oscillatory and non-oscillatory [[sleep-oscillations|sleep EEG features]] -- including spindle characteristics, slow-wave parameters, and the Hurst exponent -- have emerged as interictal biomarkers of epileptogenicity in drug-resistant epilepsy (Gonzalez et al., 2024). Meanwhile, the FDA released an EEG-based machine learning framework (EMATS) in 2024 for classifying resting EEGs as "Normal," "TBI," or "Stroke," signaling regulatory openness to AI-driven oscillatory diagnostics. Epsilon Medical's endovascular EEG device (EP-01) also received FDA Breakthrough Device Designation, representing a novel approach to intracranial recording through blood vessels without open surgery.

## Alzheimer's Disease and 40 Hz Entrainment Therapy

The Tsai laboratory showed 40 Hz stimulation reduces amyloid-beta 44-53% in mouse hippocampus with frequency specificity. Non-invasive GENUS (Gamma Entrainment Using Sensory Stimulation) reduced plaque burden by 67% via microglial transformation and VIP-interneuron-mediated glymphatic clearance (Murdock et al., 2024, Nature). Wang et al. (2026) provided cross-species validation: seven days of 40 Hz auditory stimulation in aged rhesus monkeys doubled CSF amyloid-beta (>200%, p < 0.05), with effects persisting over five weeks -- never observed in rodents.

Long-term human data are now emerging. A pilot study from MIT assessed daily 40 Hz multimodal GENUS in patients with mild Alzheimer's over approximately two years (Chan et al., 2025, Alzheimer's & Dementia). In **late-onset** AD patients, daily audiovisual stimulation was safe, feasible, and associated with slowed cognitive decline and reduced plasma pTau217 -- a biomarker strongly correlated with AD pathology. However, two male participants with **early-onset** forms did not show significant benefit, suggesting GENUS may be differentially effective across AD subtypes, potentially owing to broad pathological differences. An ongoing prevention trial (completion expected May 2026) is enrolling 50 cognitively normal individuals aged 55+ with cerebral amyloid deposits for one-year daily at-home GENUS treatment, with primary outcome of change in brain amyloid on PET.

Cognito Therapeutics' **OVERTURE** Phase 2 trial (N=76, randomized, sham-controlled, double-blind, multicenter) reported that their Spectris device slowed decline across multiple measures: 8.66 months of "time saved" in activities of daily living (ADCS-ADL), 9.93 months in cognitive function (MMSE), and 7.48 months in whole-brain volume preservation during the open-label extension (Cognito Therapeutics, 2025). The **HOPE** pivotal study -- the largest medical device trial focused solely on Alzheimer's disease -- has completed enrollment of 670 participants across 70 U.S. sites, with a 12-month randomized, double-blind, sham-controlled treatment phase followed by a 12-month open-label extension. Spectris retains FDA Breakthrough Device Designation. Peer-reviewed publication of the HOPE primary endpoint data is pending. See also [[gamma-oscillations]], [[default-mode-network]], and [[neural-resonance-and-entrainment]].

## Depression and Anxiety

Frontal [[alpha-oscillations]] asymmetry -- greater left frontal alpha indicating reduced left prefrontal activation -- has been studied as a depression biomarker. A 2025 meta-analysis including 23 studies and >1,900 MDD patients found that frontal alpha asymmetry measured with traditional resting EEG has limited but statistically significant diagnostic value; however, alpha asymmetry recorded during emotional stimulus processing differentiates MDD from healthy controls more effectively than resting measurement (Kang et al., 2024). Major depression shows dominant [[beta-oscillations]] relative to slower rhythms at rest, and meta-analytic evidence confirms significantly increased beta power in MDD alongside increased [[theta-oscillations]] and [[delta-oscillations]] power, yielding a pattern of elevated slow and fast activity with relatively reduced alpha -- analogous to the "U-shaped" spectral profile seen in other conditions (Newson and Thiagarajan, 2024). Anxiety disorders show elevated high beta (25-30 Hz) linked to hyperarousal, with GAD patients showing increased activity (13-27 Hz) throughout the central cortical axis. Clinical "beta spindles" overlap with worry and rumination. Theta cordance, once promising as an antidepressant treatment response predictor, has shown insufficient replication in recent meta-analyses (Frontiers in Psychiatry, 2024). Research hotspots have shifted from traditional single-channel biomarkers toward **brain network analysis**, EEG-derived functional connectivity, and machine-learning classification, with AI-based approaches now achieving diagnostic accuracies exceeding 80% in some datasets (Wang et al., 2025). [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]] targeting high beta down-training (20-32 Hz) demonstrates efficacy for anxiety-depression comorbidity (Lin et al., 2020). High [[alpha-oscillations]] power in portable prefrontal EEG has been identified as a gender-sensitive biomarker for emotional disorders, with effects more pronounced in women (Li et al., 2025).

## Disorders of Consciousness

Schiff's ABCD model describes four thalamocortical recovery regimes: Type A (<1 Hz only, vegetative), Type B (theta peak, minimally conscious possible), Type C (theta plus beta 20-35 Hz, emerging), and Type D (alpha plus beta, normal consciousness). The critical vegetative-to-minimally-conscious transition involves emergence of 20-35 Hz [[beta-oscillations]]. The 40 Hz ASSR differentiates wakefulness from deep sleep with 97-100% accuracy (Plourde and Picton). The **ACES trial** (2023), a 22-center RCT in China, confirmed that right median nerve electrical stimulation at 40 Hz (20 mA, 300 μs pulses, 8 hours/day, 2 weeks) applied within 7-14 days post-TBI produces greater and more rapid emergence from coma at 6 months compared to controls (Cooper et al., 2023, Intensive Care Medicine).

Advancing beyond the ABCD staging model, recent work has characterized **EEG dynamic complexity patterns** in disorders of consciousness using microstate analysis and meta-state dynamics. Altered electrophysiological meta-state transitions distinguish minimally conscious from vegetative states with greater sensitivity than static spectral features alone (Rizkallah et al., 2025). In children with prolonged disorders of consciousness, EEG microstate analysis reveals reduced microstate diversity correlating with clinical severity (Chen et al., 2025). Multidimensional EEG features -- including aEEG center amplitude for discriminating minimally conscious state from coma, and delta/gamma relative power ratios for separating vegetative from minimally conscious states -- are being developed as bedside diagnostic tools (Frontiers in Neurology, 2024). However, reaching consensus on precise neuroimaging and EEG biomarkers remains a challenge, and the field is moving toward integrating diagnostic and prognostic approaches with deep learning and large-scale datasets. See also [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]].

## ADHD and Individual Alpha Frequency

Marsicano et al. (2026) showed ADHD trait levels correlate with faster [[individual-alpha-frequency]] (r = 0.44) and flatter aperiodic spectra (r = -0.29), predicting narrower temporal integration windows (r = -0.51). Mediation analysis revealed parallel but opposing pathways: faster IAF reduces serial dependence while lower aperiodic exponent increases it. This dual-pathway model explains 42% of temporal perception variance and suggests ADHD involves shifted excitation-inhibition balance indexed by [[alpha-oscillations]] frequency.

The **theta/beta ratio** (TBR) remains the most widely discussed ADHD EEG biomarker, reflecting excess [[theta-oscillations]] (drowsiness-related) and deficient [[beta-oscillations]] (attention-related) activity. However, a multi-center, double-blind RCT (ICAN study, N=142 children with ADHD and high TBR) found that TBR [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation|neurofeedback]] did not significantly reduce global or frontal-midline theta in resting-state EEG (Bluschke et al., 2024), emphasizing the need for refined protocols that target specific theta-band networks without suppressing frontal-midline theta linked to cognitive control. Separately, a 2025 study found that reduced occurrence of [[alpha-oscillations]] bursts during resting state predicts high ADHD trait levels -- for every additional second of alpha oscillatory bursts, the odds of being in the high ADHD group decreased by approximately 6% (Karalunas et al., 2025). Comprehensive QEEG reviews confirm that ADHD signatures include excess theta, deficient beta, elevated TBR, and [[aperiodic-dynamics|flattened aperiodic slopes]], with emerging evidence for altered EEG functional connectivity as a predictor of neurofeedback treatment response (Pettersson et al., 2025). Machine-learning analyses of pre-treatment EEG connectivity patterns can now predict which children will benefit from TBR neurofeedback, potentially enabling personalized treatment selection (Monastra et al., 2025).

## Neuropathic Pain and Thalamocortical Dysrhythmia

Mussigmann et al. (2022) reviewed 14 EEG studies (241 patients) of chronic neuropathic pain, identifying increased theta power (4-7 Hz) with left-hemisphere predominance, decreased high-alpha/low-[[beta-oscillations]] power (10-20 Hz), and possibly increased high-beta (20-30 Hz). This aligns with [[thalamocortical-circuits|thalamocortical dysrhythmia]] theory (Jeanmonod et al., 2001), where reduced thalamic input produces pathological theta enhancement and [[alpha-oscillations]] disruption. The sub-band differential -- low-beta and high-beta showing opposite patterns -- suggests distinct generators differentially affected, with high-beta increase possibly reflecting comorbid anxiety.

A secondary analysis of resting-state EEG in 36 chronic neuropathic pain patients revealed a more nuanced picture: increased [[delta-oscillations]] and [[theta-oscillations]] power in central regions was associated with **decreased** pain levels, suggesting these low-frequency oscillations may serve a compensatory role rather than purely indexing pathology (Fernandes et al., 2024). Frontal theta correlated with reduced mood impairment from pain, while parietal theta predicted decreased pain intensity over four weeks. The most robust predictive model emerged for average pain intensity over weeks, not acute pain -- implying that oscillatory biomarkers may better capture chronic pain states than moment-to-moment nociception. In postherpetic neuralgia specifically, patients show significantly higher theta and alpha power across 23 channels compared to healthy controls, accompanied by widespread increased EEG power and decreased functional connectivity (Li et al., 2025). These results support the development of [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]] protocols targeting low-frequency enhancement as a means to bolster compensatory brain mechanisms in chronic pain management.

## Autism Spectrum Oscillatory Phenotypes

Resting-state EEG in autism spectrum disorder (ASD) frequently displays a characteristic **"U-shaped" spectral profile**: excess power in slow ([[theta-oscillations]]) and fast ([[gamma-oscillations]]) bands with decreased power in the mid-range [[alpha-oscillations]] band. Meta-analyses across all five canonical EEG frequency bands offer support for this profile (Newson and Thiagarajan, 2023), and the pattern aligns with theoretical accounts of disrupted GABAergic interneuron function biasing neural networks toward gamma-related excitation and away from alpha-related inhibition -- consistent with the **excitation/inhibition (E/I) imbalance** hypothesis of autism (Rubenstein and Merzenich, 2003). Children with ASD exhibit elevated 20-120 Hz activity predominantly in posterior regions, and autistic youth show elevated gamma power during speech processing that correlates inversely with language skills -- higher gamma predicting lower language ability (Pang et al., 2024, Molecular Autism). Altered [[aperiodic-dynamics|aperiodic spectral components]] during speech perception point to broadband reduction of spectral power alongside higher cortical "noise" in the ASD group (2025).

Recent large-scale studies have begun to refine these broad signatures. Barnes et al. (2025) found reduced functional connectivity specifically in theta and alpha bands in children with ASD, localized to frontal cortex rather than reflecting global coherence deficits. Distinct modes of functional neural organization in ASD have been identified through dynamical systems analysis of resting-state EEG, revealing altered attractor landscapes compared to neurotypical controls (Kang et al., 2025). EEG-recorded gamma activity in ASD research remains challenging to interpret due to electromyographic contamination, and methodological rigor in artifact rejection is essential for valid gamma-band conclusions. See also [[gamma-oscillations]] and [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]].

## Traumatic Brain Injury and Concussion

Quantitative EEG (qEEG) has emerged as a promising objective assessment tool for traumatic brain injury (TBI). Immediately following mild TBI, qEEG most commonly shows reduced mean [[alpha-oscillations]] frequency, increased [[theta-oscillations]] power, increased [[delta-oscillations]] power, and elevated theta:alpha ratio (Rapp et al., 2015). Patients with mTBI show significantly higher theta:beta amplitude ratios and more slow waves while awake compared to age-matched controls, with global EEG coherence significantly correlated with severity of post-concussive symptoms. A 2024 study of 19 diagnosed concussion patients using qEEG in acute assessment showed that power spectral and connectivity analyses significantly differentiate mTBI from matched neurotypical controls (NeuroRegulation, 2024).

Analysis from the CAPTAIN-rTMS trial (2025) demonstrated that resting-state relative power spectral densities correlate with neuropsychological recovery: relative [[alpha-oscillations]] power associates with higher cognitive performance while relative [[delta-oscillations]] power associates with lower performance during recovery from moderate-severe TBI. EEG biomarkers including functional connectivity and dominant frequency are reportedly predictive of clinical outcome, and their combined application can more accurately assess recovery potential. A 2024 protocol study is assessing quantitative EEG biomarkers alongside inflammatory indicators to predict mortality and functional recovery in severe TBI patients (Gonzalez-Martinez et al., 2024). The FDA's 2024 release of the EMATS (EEG-based Machine or Deep Learning Algorithms for TBI & Stroke) classification system -- which preprocesses resting EEGs of >3 minutes and classifies them as "Normal," "TBI," or "Stroke" -- signals growing regulatory acceptance of AI-driven oscillatory diagnostics for acute brain injury.

## Neurofeedback for Motor Performance and Dual-Controller Interference

Onagawa et al. (2023) meta-analyzed 33 neurofeedback studies targeting [[motor-control]] performance, finding an overall SMD of 0.74 (p = .031) for EEG-based neurofeedback. Within the RCT subgroup (16 RCTs, 374 subjects), the effect was larger (SMD = 0.85), with a dose-response threshold: cumulative training exceeding 125 minutes required for reliable benefit. However, Bazanova et al. (2026) revealed paradoxical interference when combining [[alpha-oscillations]]-EEG/EMG neurofeedback with binaural beat stimulation -- simultaneous application degraded all measures despite each working independently, suggesting volitional self-regulation and external entrainment operate as competing controllers.
