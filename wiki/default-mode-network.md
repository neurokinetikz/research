# Default Mode Network

The Default Mode Network (DMN) is a set of interconnected brain regions -- medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), posterior cingulate cortex (PCC)/precuneus, lateral temporal cortex, and angular gyrus -- that are maximally active during rest, self-referential thought, and mind wandering, and suppressed during externally directed tasks. From an oscillatory perspective, the DMN is characterized by dominant [[alpha-oscillations]] and beta power in its core nodes during the resting state, with [[theta-oscillations]] emerging during spontaneous internal mentation. The DMN's oscillatory dynamics provide a window into consciousness, selfhood, and the brain's intrinsic functional architecture: its integrity tracks the presence of conscious awareness, its disruption correlates with ego dissolution under psychedelics, and its progressive decoupling marks the transition from wakefulness through sleep into disorders of consciousness. Recent intracranial EEG studies have clarified that intra-DMN communication relies predominantly on slow-wave (<4 Hz) phase synchronization, while cross-network interactions with task-positive regions operate through beta and [[gamma-oscillations]] frequencies (Kosciessa et al., 2022), and large-scale iEEG replication efforts across 177 participants have established that the salience network's anterior insula directs information flow to DMN and frontoparietal nodes during episodic memory operations (Kam et al., 2024). A growing body of evidence now positions DMN functional connectivity as a **transdiagnostic biomarker** of cognitive health, with its anticorrelation strength predicting performance across attention, memory, and executive function domains in both healthy aging and neuropsychiatric disease (Tripathi et al., 2025).

## Oscillatory signature of the DMN at rest

The DMN's resting-state signature is dominated by alpha-band activity (8-13 Hz), particularly in the PCC/precuneus, and beta-band activity in medial prefrontal regions. Alpha power at rest correlates positively with relaxation across frontal, central, and occipital sites (Sugimoto et al. 2025), and this resting alpha reflects the thalamocortical idle rhythm that characterizes internally oriented processing. When external task demands arise, alpha desynchronization in DMN regions signals the suppression of self-referential processing and the reallocation of resources toward task-relevant networks. Beta oscillations in DMN regions similarly decrease during active task engagement, consistent with beta's role in maintaining the cognitive status quo (see [[beta-oscillations]]).

A mechanistic link between alpha oscillations and DMN connectivity has been strengthened by recent stimulation work. Ma et al. (2025) used simultaneous EEG-fMRI before and after alpha-frequency transcranial alternating current stimulation (α-tACS) and demonstrated that α-tACS tightened the dynamic coupling between spontaneous fluctuations in alpha power and DMN connectivity, particularly in the posterior DMN between the PCC and bilateral angular gyrus. No equivalent coupling changes were observed for other oscillatory frequencies or other major resting-state networks, providing causal evidence that alpha oscillations represent the primary neural synchrony linking the core hubs of the DMN. Separately, individual alpha frequency (IAF) tACS at each participant's peak alpha frequency reduced static functional connectivity across the DMN (Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 2025), suggesting that driving alpha rhythms at a person's endogenous frequency can modulate the network's baseline integration state -- a finding with implications for [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]] approaches targeting DMN-related disorders.

## PCC alpha: the strongest DMN oscillatory marker

The PCC stands out as the DMN's most distinctive oscillatory hub. Griffiths (2026) demonstrated that alpha-band rhythmicity (~10 Hz) in posterior regions -- overlapping with the PCC and precuneus -- serves as the mechanism by which internally reactivated memory representations gain access to conscious awareness. Stimulus-specific content can be reactivated in sensory cortex without reaching awareness; what distinguishes memories that become conscious is the degree of alpha-rhythmic fluctuation in their neural representation. Global alpha desynchronization and stimulus-specific alpha rhythmicity are statistically independent and additively predict overt recall, suggesting two separable mechanisms: desynchronization clears representational noise, while alpha rhythmicity actively projects reactivated content into awareness. This positions PCC alpha as central to the DMN's role in self-referential processing and autobiographical memory access.

## Intracranial electrophysiology of the DMN

Intracranial EEG (iEEG) and stereo-EEG recordings have provided unprecedented spatiotemporal resolution on DMN oscillatory dynamics, overcoming the spatial blurring inherent in scalp EEG. Kosciessa et al. (2022) reported that the DMN exhibits significantly greater intra-network phase synchronization in the slow-wave frequency band (<4 Hz) during resting-state, while cross-network interactions between the DMN and task-positive regions are dominated by beta and gamma frequencies. This frequency-specific architecture suggests that the DMN's internal coherence operates on fundamentally slower timescales than its communication with external networks.

Kam et al. (2024) analyzed iEEG recordings from 177 participants across four episodic memory experiments in the largest intracranial study of triple-network dynamics to date. Phase transfer entropy analysis revealed consistently higher directed information flow from the anterior insula -- a key salience network hub -- to both DMN and frontoparietal network (FPN) nodes, with this directed influence significantly stronger during memory encoding and recall than during rest. Slow-wave intra-DMN phase synchronization increased during successful memory retrieval, suggesting that the DMN's low-frequency coherence is not merely a resting-state default but is actively recruited for [[hippocampal-memory|episodic memory]] operations.

Bartoli et al. (2024) employed stereo-EEG in 13 patients to probe DMN electrophysiology during creative thinking. Theta and gamma activity displayed opposite modulation patterns -- lower theta paired with enhanced gamma -- and gamma power changes were primarily driven by lateral DMN sites, while theta showed task-specific spatial effects. During the alternate uses task (divergent thinking), theta changes were spatially uniform across the DMN, while mind wandering engaged an early lateral and late dorsomedial DMN pattern. Critically, direct cortical stimulation of DMN regions selectively decreased the originality of divergent thinking responses without affecting fluency, providing the first causal evidence that DMN oscillatory activity is necessary for creative cognition.

Fabera et al. (2024) recorded iEEG from 25 epilepsy patients with electrodes in both DMN and dorsal attention network (DAN) regions during internal-external attention switching tasks. The study revealed antagonistic dynamics: activation-related increases in high-frequency broadband power (>50 Hz) in one network were accompanied by increased low-frequency power (<30 Hz) in the opposing network. The temporal profile of information flow indicated that the currently activated network actively inhibits the other through amplification of low-frequency oscillations -- a gating mechanism that may underlie the well-documented fMRI anticorrelation between these networks (see [[attention-and-perception]]).

## DMN-TPN anticorrelation

The DMN operates in an anticorrelated relationship with task-positive networks (TPNs), including the dorsal attention network and the frontoparietal network (FPN). When TPN engagement increases, DMN activity is suppressed via alpha desynchronization in DMN regions. Peng et al. (2026) showed that alpha-band functional connectivity within the FPN retains behavioral relevance during sustained cognitive tasks, with temporal variability of alpha FPN connectivity predicting performance recovery from mental fatigue. This alpha-mediated push-pull between the DMN and FPN reflects the brain's allocation of resources between internal and external processing modes (see [[attention-and-perception]]).

The oscillatory basis of DMN-TPN opponency has been further clarified by recent EEG-fMRI work. Higher peak alpha and beta frequencies correlate positively with the strength of DMN-DAN anticorrelation (Mahjoory et al., 2023), linking [[individual-alpha-frequency]] to the efficiency of network switching. Individuals with faster alpha peaks show sharper segregation between internal and external processing modes, while those with slower peaks exhibit blurred network boundaries -- a pattern that deteriorates further with aging and cognitive decline (see [[development-and-aging]]). The fluidity of switching between DMN-dominant and TPN-dominant states has been proposed as a general index of cognitive health, with reduced anticorrelation strength emerging as a transdiagnostic marker across Alzheimer's disease, depression, ADHD, and schizophrenia (Tripathi et al., 2025).

## Mind wandering, spontaneous thought, and creativity

Mind wandering -- the DMN's signature cognitive mode -- produces a characteristic EEG profile: increased alpha and theta power with decreased beta during externally oriented tasks (Kam et al. 2022). Critically, alpha's direction during mind wandering is context-dependent: it increases during external tasks (reflecting inhibition of task-irrelevant external input) but decreases during internal tasks like breath-focused meditation (reflecting that mind wandering is more externally oriented than deep meditative focus). Frontal theta and elevated theta-beta ratio during mind wandering index reduced executive control over spontaneous thought. Freely moving thoughts -- the kind associated with creative insight -- correlate with increased frontal alpha power and greater alpha-power variability (Kam et al. 2021). Right temporal alpha oscillations at ~10 Hz actively inhibit dominant semantic associations, enabling access to more remote and creative ideas (Luft et al. 2018), linking DMN alpha dynamics to divergent thinking and creative cognition.

A systematic review of electrophysiological markers of mind wandering (Groot et al., 2022) confirmed that increases in delta, theta, and alpha power -- along with decreased beta -- represent the most consistent spectral signatures preceding self-reported mind wandering episodes. These changes are topographically distributed: alpha power increases globally, theta power increases along centroparietal areas overlapping DMN hubs, and beta decreases are most prominent over right frontal and right centroparietal regions. Beyond simple power changes, recent work has identified distinct oscillatory markers for seven dimensions of ongoing thought, with off-task thoughts, goal-oriented thoughts, and "sticky" (perseverative) thoughts each characterized by separable spectral profiles in the theta, alpha, and beta bands. This multidimensional view moves beyond the binary on-task/off-task framework to capture the rich phenomenological variety of DMN-mediated spontaneous cognition.

## DMN and meditation

Meditation produces characteristic modulation of DMN oscillatory dynamics that varies with both meditation type and expertise level. Experienced meditators across multiple contemplative traditions show relative deactivation of core DMN nodes -- mPFC and PCC -- during practice compared to novices (Brewer et al., 2011). From an oscillatory perspective, mindfulness meditation induces a state-level increase in alpha mean phase coherence alongside a trait-level decrease in EEG functional connectivity, reflecting a paradoxical sharpening of moment-to-moment neural coordination even as habitual mind wandering networks become less automatically engaged.

Intensive meditation retreat training produces replicable reductions in beta-band power over anteriocentral and posterior scalp regions, and [[individual-alpha-frequency]] decreases across retreats in proportion to the amount of meditative practice (Ferrarelli et al., 2013). Advanced concentrative absorption states are associated with widespread decreases in broadband oscillatory power coupled with increases in Lempel-Ziv complexity -- a pattern strikingly similar to the neural entropy signature of [[psychedelic-states]], though achieved through endogenous attentional control rather than pharmacological intervention. Long-term meditation training also reorganizes the DMN's modular structure: after four months of practice, the right and left posterior DMN modules show decreased EEG operational synchrony in post-meditation resting state, while the frontal DMN module exhibits increased synchrony, suggesting a redistribution of the network's oscillatory balance from posterior self-referential hubs toward frontal executive regions.

Northoff and Buccellato (2025) proposed that meditation and psychedelic states share a common neural topology -- a flattened DMN-centric configuration -- positioned between the heightened DMN dominance characteristic of depression and the heightened sensorimotor-centric reorganization of mania. Both nonordinary states of consciousness are characterized by balanced neural dynamics and a phenomenological focus on the present moment with minimal expansion into past and future, suggesting that the DMN's temporal scaffolding function is central to the experience of "inner time consciousness" (see [[self-and-embodiment]]).

## DMN collapse under psychedelics

Psychedelics produce dramatic DMN disintegration. Functional connectivity between mPFC and PCC decreases under psilocybin, while cross-network connectivity increases. Alpha suppression in the PCC correlates strongly with ego dissolution ratings (R-squared = 0.66, p = 0.00016), making PCC alpha collapse the most robust neural correlate of self-dissolution (see [[psychedelic-states]]). The claustrum -- which has the highest 5-HT2A receptor density of any brain region and may normally coordinate large-scale network states -- decouples from DMN and frontoparietal networks under psilocybin. The mechanistic chain runs from [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations|5-HT2A]] activation of layer 5 pyramidal neurons through alpha/beta suppression to DMN disintegration, increased connectivity entropy, relaxed predictive priors, and the subjective experience of ego dissolution (see [[self-and-embodiment]]).

A 2025 review of psychedelic effects on oscillatory brain activity across EEG and MEG studies confirmed that alpha power suppression (8-13 Hz) is the most consistent finding across moderate to high doses of LSD, psilocybin, ayahuasca, and DMT, particularly in occipital and posterior midline regions overlapping with the DMN (Doss et al., 2025). Below 30 Hz, broadband desynchronization is typical across all substances, though DMT uniquely preserves or even increases delta/theta activity during peak experiences -- possibly reflecting its immersive visual phenomenology and resemblance to [[sleep-oscillations|REM-like]] states. Measures of signal diversity (e.g., Lempel-Ziv complexity) reliably increase during all psychedelic states, and real-time evaluations of subjective intensity covary with spectral power changes and complexity, highlighting the potential for objective EEG biomarkers of drug activity.

DMT's effects on the DMN have received particular attention. Functional MRI reveals that DMT causes rapid collapse of functional connectivity within the DMN, with the most prominent disruptions between mPFC and PCC (Timmermann et al., 2025). Under DMT, the brain becomes less modular and more fluid, reorganizing into a hyperconnected state with increased activity in subcortical structures -- the thalamus, amygdala, and hippocampus -- that are normally under cortical inhibitory control. This subcortical disinhibition parallels the oscillatory shift from cortical alpha/beta dominance toward subcortical theta/delta rhythms, consistent with the REBUS (Relaxed Beliefs Under Psychedelics) model's prediction that top-down hierarchical control dissolves under serotonergic psychedelics (see [[predictive-coding]]).

## DMN during sleep and memory consolidation

The DMN progressively decouples across [[sleep-oscillations]] stages. During NREM sleep, alpha drops out and is replaced by sleep spindles (12-15 Hz) and slow oscillations, reflecting thalamocortical gating that prevents sensory information from reaching cortex. REM sleep supports local gamma activity but lacks the long-range coherence that characterizes waking DMN integrity, consistent with the fragmented, non-self-referential quality of dream consciousness.

Recent evidence has revealed a more active role for the DMN during sleep than the simple "deactivation" model suggests. A 2025 review (Current Opinion in Behavioral Sciences) highlighted the prominent involvement of the DMN with the hippocampus in NREM memory consolidation, linking near-critical cortical activations to [[sleep-oscillations|sleep spindles]] that organize activity conducive to memory transfer. The canonical slow oscillation-spindle-ripple coupling sequence -- in which cortical slow oscillations set global windows of excitability, nested spindles partially reactivate cortical learning networks, and hippocampal [[sharp-wave-ripples]] replay encoded content -- preferentially engages DMN regions as consolidation targets. Spindle-slow oscillation coupling in DMN frontal nodes (particularly mPFC) correlates with next-day memory performance, and this coupling strengthens selectively in cortical regions that were engaged during prior learning, suggesting that the DMN serves as a preferential cortical "receiving network" for hippocampal memory output during sleep.

Sleep deprivation selectively impairs DMN dynamics during subsequent recovery sleep, with dynamic configurations of DMN activity in slow-wave sleep significantly disrupted after sleep loss while REM-state DMN dynamics are comparatively preserved. This differential vulnerability suggests that the DMN's slow-wave-dependent consolidation function is particularly sensitive to homeostatic sleep pressure -- a finding with implications for understanding memory deficits in conditions characterized by chronic sleep disruption, including Alzheimer's disease and major depression (see [[clinical-applications]]).

## DMN during disorders of consciousness

In disorders of consciousness, DMN integrity tracks the level of awareness through Schiff's ABCD model (see [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]]). Type A spectra (only sub-1 Hz activity) correspond to vegetative states with complete thalamocortical deafferentation and no DMN function. Type B (theta peak emergence) marks possible minimally conscious states. Type C (theta plus beta at 20-35 Hz) reflects partially reconnected thalamocortical circuits. Type D (alpha plus beta) indicates intact thalamocortical communication and normal DMN function. The critical transition from vegetative to minimally conscious state involves the emergence of beta-range activity, suggesting that long-range integration capacity -- necessary for DMN coherence -- is the oscillatory prerequisite for conscious awareness.

## DMN connectivity and aging

The DMN undergoes characteristic oscillatory changes across the lifespan that parallel cognitive trajectories. Within-network DMN functional connectivity decreases with age, while between-network connectivity -- particularly DMN-DAN coupling -- paradoxically increases, reflecting a loss of network segregation that blurs the boundary between internal and external processing modes (see [[development-and-aging]]). These changes are frequency-dependent: alpha2-range connectivity (10-12 Hz) between mPFC and other DMN regions decreases in older adults, while theta and beta phase synchronization between mPFC and PCC increases, suggesting compensatory recruitment of slower and faster oscillatory channels as the dominant alpha-band communication pathway deteriorates (EEG resting-state aging study, 2022).

A multimodal MEG study across 599 adults spanning ages 18-88 (CamCAN dataset, 2025) revealed that DMN-FPN dynamics shift from anterior-ventral to posterior-dorsal configurations until midlife, concurrent with heightened alpha-band oscillations, before reversing in later decades. Midlife emerges as a turning point for spontaneous network oscillatory dynamics, with language performance and word-finding ability tracking these network reconfigurations. [[individual-alpha-frequency|Peak alpha frequency]] slows with age across the lifespan, and this slowing correlates with reduced DMN-DAN anticorrelation strength, suggesting a common thalamocortical mechanism underlying both alpha deceleration and network dedifferentiation.

In Alzheimer's disease (AD), DMN oscillatory disruption is among the earliest detectable biomarkers. A TMS-EEG study (Science Advances, 2025) demonstrated network-specific local hyperexcitability in parietal DMN regions alongside disrupted connectivity with frontal DMN nodes in early AD, with these perturbation-based measures uniquely predicting distinct cognitive impairments and mediating the link between structural brain integrity and cognition. Standard resting-state EEG revealed global neural slowing and disrupted synchrony but lacked the network specificity of perturbation-based approaches, suggesting that targeting the DMN directly through TMS-EEG may provide superior biomarker sensitivity for early detection. Alpha oscillation decreases in AD reflect both cholinergic deficits and [[thalamocortical-circuits|thalamocortical]] dysfunction, while DMN connectivity metrics derived from resting-state fMRI can predict long-term forgetting rates, with stronger PCC-hippocampal connectivity predicting slower memory decay (PLOS Computational Biology, 2025).

## Clinical relevance

DMN disruption features prominently across neuropsychiatric conditions. In Alzheimer's disease, DMN connectivity is among the earliest biomarkers to deteriorate, with reduced alpha coherence between PCC and mPFC preceding clinical symptoms. In major depression, DMN hyperconnectivity and excessive rumination correlate with increased alpha power in medial prefrontal regions, reflecting maladaptive self-referential processing; alpha-band functional connectivity within the DMN can predict depression severity and distinguishes MDD patients from healthy controls. In schizophrenia, aberrant DMN-TPN anticorrelation and disrupted alpha gating contribute to difficulties in distinguishing internally generated from externally sourced information (see [[clinical-applications]]). In ADHD, weakened DMN suppression during task engagement -- indexed by failure of alpha desynchronization in PCC -- produces intrusive mind wandering and attentional lapses. These clinical patterns reinforce that the DMN's oscillatory dynamics are not merely correlates of rest but active computational mechanisms supporting selfhood, memory, and conscious awareness.

Tripathi et al. (2025) proposed that DMN functional connectivity serves as a transdiagnostic biomarker of cognitive function, with the fluidity of DMN-TPN switching indexing cognitive health across diagnostic categories. Increased theta and reduced gamma power in frontal DMN regions correlate with rumination severity in depression, while disrupted [[cross-frequency-coupling|theta-gamma coupling]] across DMN hubs suggests impaired integration of emotional and cognitive processing. The ease of estimating functional connectivity from resting-state paradigms, combined with large-scale normative datasets, now enables standardized DMN connectivity benchmarks against which individual patients can be compared -- though the review emphasizes that reliability of network estimation remains a significant methodological challenge that must be addressed before clinical deployment.
