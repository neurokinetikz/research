# Auditory and Speech Oscillations

Neural oscillations serve as the temporal infrastructure for auditory processing, speech comprehension, music perception, and vocal production. Delta and [[theta-oscillations]] (1-8 Hz) track the syllabic and prosodic structure of speech, [[alpha-oscillations]] (8-12 Hz) gate attentional sampling of auditory scenes and index listening effort, and [[gamma-oscillations]] (30-100+ Hz) encode fine spectrotemporal features and sustain auditory steady-state responses critical for clinical biomarkers. The brain actively constructs beat and meter representations through endogenous oscillatory mechanisms -- even in premature neonates as young as 32 weeks gestational age (Edalati et al., 2024) -- and cross-modal phase synchronization at specific frequencies enhances speech comprehension. Cortical tracking of the speech envelope, once considered straightforward oscillatory entrainment, is now understood to involve both evoked responses to acoustic edges and genuine oscillatory phase alignment (Oganian et al., 2023; Haegens & Zion Golumbic, 2018), establishing oscillatory [[neural-resonance-and-entrainment]] as a multifaceted mechanism of auditory cognition that extends to sign language, tonal language processing, bilingual sentence integration, and inter-brain synchrony during conversation.

## Frequency Modulation Entrains Delta Oscillations

Henry and Obleser (2012) demonstrated that 3 Hz frequency modulation in complex tones entrains endogenous delta oscillations without any amplitude fluctuations -- proving that spectral fluctuations alone can pace neural phase reorganization. In their gap detection task (N = 12), hit rate was strongly correlated with both stimulus phase (rho = 0.79, p < .001) and neural delta phase (rho = 0.76, p < .001), but only neural phase predicted behavior consistently across subjects (Rayleigh z = 3.72, p = .02). The N1 component (50-150 ms) showed near-perfect correlation with brain phase (rho = 0.99), and individual differences in stimulus-behavior timing were explained by the intervening neural oscillation (rho = 0.46, p = .033). This establishes that slow oscillatory phase determines perceptual sensitivity during auditory processing, consistent with dynamic [[attention-and-perception]] theory.

## Cortical Tracking of Speech: Entrainment vs. Evoked Responses

The mechanism by which low-frequency neural activity aligns to the speech envelope has become one of the most actively debated questions in auditory neuroscience. The **entrainment hypothesis** posits that endogenous [[delta-oscillations]] and [[theta-oscillations]] phase-lock to quasi-periodic fluctuations in the speech amplitude envelope, with ongoing oscillatory phase shaping perceptual sensitivity to upcoming syllables. The alternative **evoked response hypothesis** holds that apparent envelope tracking reflects a series of discrete transient responses to acoustic edges and landmarks in the speech signal, rather than genuine oscillatory alignment.

Oganian et al. (2023) provided important evidence favoring the evoked account, showing that phase alignment of low-frequency neural activity to the speech amplitude envelope reflects evoked responses to acoustic edges rather than oscillatory entrainment. Using MEG recordings and computational models that predicted distinct outcomes under slowed speech conditions, they found transient theta and delta phase-locking patterns fully compatible with the evoked response model but incompatible with pure oscillatory entrainment. The theta-band (4-8 Hz) cortical tracking of the speech envelope shows a linear phase property -- the phase lag between cortical activity and speech envelope changes linearly across frequency -- a signature more consistent with evoked than entrained responses (Zoefel & VanRullen, 2021). A 2024 study further examined how stimulus periodicity influences auditory cortical entrainment to continuous sounds, finding that periodicity enhances the neural response in ways that can be partly distinguished from purely evoked mechanisms (Zuk et al., 2024).

The resolution of this debate likely involves acknowledging that both mechanisms coexist. Delta-band tracking (1-4 Hz) appears to encode higher-level speech comprehension and linguistic structure, while theta-band tracking (4-8 Hz) more closely reflects acoustic clarity and spectrotemporal features of the signal (Ding & Simon, 2014). Cortical tracking of speech has also proven clinically informative: patients with disorders of consciousness show less reliable neural tracking of speech overall, yet those who do show reliable tracking preserve the characteristic linear phase lag between 3.5 and 8 Hz regardless of consciousness state, suggesting that envelope-tracking involves an automatic process not strongly modulated by conscious awareness (Gui et al., 2023). Atypical cortical tracking has been associated with language impairments across hearing loss, stroke-related and dementia-related aphasias, dyslexia, and specific language impairments (Gillis et al., 2024), establishing it as a potential clinical biomarker for auditory processing deficits.

## Auditory Attention and Alpha Lateralization

[[Alpha-oscillations]] play a central role in auditory selective attention, functioning as an inhibitory gating mechanism that suppresses processing of unattended auditory streams -- the neural solution to the **cocktail party problem**. During dichotic listening tasks, hemispheric lateralization of ~10 Hz alpha power over auditory and parietal cortex reflects the direction of spatial attention: alpha power increases ipsilateral to the attended source (suppressing the unattended side) and decreases contralateral to it. Two dissociable lateralized alpha responses have been identified -- one reflecting target selection and another reflecting distractor suppression -- generated in distinct cortical regions, with suppression-related alpha originating from more anterior, frontal areas (Wöstmann et al., 2019).

Recent work demonstrates that auditory attention is embodied: neural filtering via alpha lateralization and behavioral filtering via head rotation operate as sequentially interlocked mechanisms, with head movements integrating with brain signals to guide spatial attention deployment (Holtze et al., 2025). Parietal alpha power has emerged as a robust marker of **listening effort** -- the cognitive resources allocated to speech comprehension in adverse conditions. Alpha event-related synchronization decreases with decreasing signal-to-noise ratio during speech-in-noise tasks, indexing greater cognitive demand (Poulsen et al., 2025). This alpha-based measure of listening effort converges with pupillometric indices, as both larger pupil dilation and decreased parietal alpha power accompany more effortful listening conditions, providing complementary physiological windows into the same underlying resource allocation process.

## Auditory Steady-State Responses and GABAergic Mechanisms

The **auditory steady-state response** (ASSR) -- an oscillatory brain response to periodically modulated auditory stimuli -- is notably maximal for sounds amplitude-modulated at 40 Hz, making it a key probe of [[gamma-oscillations]] circuitry in the auditory system. The 40 Hz ASSR has become a promising noninvasive physiological biomarker for schizophrenia and related neuropsychiatric disorders (see [[clinical-applications]]), where ASSR amplitude and phase-locking are consistently reduced.

Recent research has clarified the synaptic mechanisms underlying this response. Farahani et al. (2024) demonstrated that the 40 Hz steady-state response in human auditory cortex is shaped by GABAergic neuronal inhibition: the GABA_A receptor agonist lorazepam increased the amplitude of the 40 Hz ASSR, while no effect was detectable under the NMDA blocker memantine. This establishes that the 40 Hz ASSR involves synaptic inhibition via GABA_A receptors rather than glutamatergic excitation, connecting to broader principles of inhibitory control in [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]]. Animal studies have further shown that the neural response synchronizing to 40 Hz stimuli is most prominent in GABAergic neurons in the granular layer of auditory cortex and the ventral division of the medial geniculate body, regulated by the thalamic reticular nucleus (Lee et al., 2024) -- consistent with [[thalamocortical-circuits]] models of gamma generation.

The large ASSR in the EEG to 40 Hz AM tones results not from larger instantaneous signal amplitude or greater phase alignment, but from decreased latency variability and enhanced temporal consistency of the 40 Hz response -- a network resonance phenomenon (Farahani et al., 2024). Novelty detection modulates the 40 Hz ASSR in a complementary manner, with ASSR amplitude increasing in tandem with greater novelty-related neural activity (Choi et al., 2025). These findings have direct therapeutic implications: 40 Hz [[gamma-oscillations|gamma]] entrainment using sensory stimulation (GENUS) -- combining auditory and visual 40 Hz stimulation -- has shown promise in Alzheimer's disease treatment, with phase II clinical trials demonstrating slowed brain atrophy and improved cognitive measures compared to untreated controls (Chan et al., 2025). Long-term daily use of combined audiovisual GENUS in mild Alzheimer's dementia showed that late-onset patients exhibited annual MMSE score decline 0.8-1.2 points slower than matched controls, with statistically significant CDR improvement (p = 0.02), and the combined audiovisual modality proved more effective than either stimulus alone.

## Bass Sounds Enhance Beat Tracking

Lenc et al. (2018) showed that bass sounds (130 Hz carrier) selectively enhance cortical activity at beat- and meter-related frequencies compared to high-pitched sounds (1236.8 Hz), using EEG frequency tagging (N = 14). The beat frequency of 1.25 Hz and meter-related frequencies showed low-tone enhancement that exceeded cochlear model predictions specifically for syncopated rhythms requiring endogenous beat generation (d = 1.01 for meter frequencies). This cortical enhancement disappeared when participants performed a non-temporal attention task, demonstrating that the bass advantage requires temporal [[attention-and-perception]]. The 1.25 Hz beat frequency corresponds to 75 beats per minute -- the average resting heart rate -- connecting to Klimesch's theory that heart rate serves as the fundamental scaling factor for brain oscillation frequencies.

## Neural Entrainment and Music: Oscillations, Consciousness, and Development

The relationship between neural entrainment to musical rhythm and beat perception has been a focal point of recent research. A key debate concerns whether beat-related neural synchronization reflects phase alignment of endogenous oscillations to external regularity (true entrainment) or successive evoked responses to rhythmic stimuli. Lenc et al. (2025) addressed this directly, finding that tempo-dependent selective enhancement of neural responses at beat frequency can be mimicked by both oscillator and evoked response models -- the enhancement itself does not unequivocally distinguish the two mechanisms. Nevertheless, [[delta-oscillations]] (1-3 Hz) and [[beta-oscillations]] (15-30 Hz) consistently show beat-related modulation, with optimal behavioral synchronization at tempos of ~500-600 ms inter-onset intervals (approximately 2 Hz), and neural entrainment to the beat predicts sensorimotor synchronization skill alongside working memory capacity (Bauer et al., 2025).

The connection between musical entrainment and altered states of consciousness has received empirical support. Stupacher et al. (2025) recorded EEG from 20 participants listening to electronic music excerpts at different tempos (1.65, 2.25, and 2.85 Hz) and found tempo-driven modulation of entrainment at the group level, with stronger entrainment at 1.65 Hz than 2.85 Hz. Music at the slower 1.65 Hz tempo aroused greater feelings of unity -- a proxy for altered states of consciousness. A broader review of rhythmic auditory stimulation across cultures (Aparicio-Terres et al., 2025) suggests that repetitive rhythmic sounds induce absorption and relaxation through thalamo-cortical entrainment to low-frequency rhythms, paralleling mechanisms in [[psychedelic-states]] and meditative states.

Remarkably, beat and meter processing emerges before term birth. Edalati et al. (2024) evaluated neural oscillatory activity in premature newborns (N = 19, mean gestational age 32 +/- 2.59 weeks) during auditory rhythm exposure and found systematic modulation of alpha power synchronized to expected beat locations -- coinciding with both tones and rests -- independent of whether the beat was preceded by a tone or silence. These alpha oscillations in premature neonates may indicate neural predictions of beat timing, extending earlier findings that selective enhancement of neural responses occurs at both beat- and meter-related frequencies even in the premature brain (Moser et al., 2023). The prenatal auditory environment is characterized by omnipresent rhythmic sounds -- maternal heartbeat and respiration -- suggesting that the oscillatory infrastructure for beat processing is shaped by prenatal experience and emerges as a fundamental capacity of the developing auditory system, consistent with broader patterns described in [[development-and-aging]].

## Vibrotactile Enhancement of Speech Comprehension

Guilleminot and Reichenbach (2022) demonstrated that vibrotactile pulses delivered at the syllabic rate (approximately 4.5 Hz) enhance speech-in-noise comprehension by 6.3% when temporally aligned with syllable perceptual centers (N = 19). Comprehension followed a sinusoidal function of audiotactile delay at the syllabic frequency (p = .035), with peak enhancement at -17 ms delay. Neural multisensory gains peaked at 114 ms latency in bilateral auditory cortex, and a late neural component at 277 ms showed delay-dependent modulation. The approximately 4.5 Hz syllabic rate falls close to the [[theta-oscillations]] range, and the cross-modal phase reset mechanism -- where somatosensory input resets the phase of slow auditory cortex oscillations -- provides direct evidence for [[neural-resonance-and-entrainment]] as a functional mechanism in speech processing.

## Singing vs. Speaking: Distinct Oscillatory Control

Pracar et al. (2025) used intracranial EEG (N = 13, 1600 electrodes) to show that singing and speaking engage fundamentally different sensorimotor dynamics despite producing identical linguistic content. Speaking activates left sensorimotor cortex with strong mu-band (approximately 10 Hz) synchrony at speech onset, while singing preferentially activates right sensorimotor cortex with progressive interhemispheric mu-band synchrony that ramps up throughout production. Granger causality analysis revealed that speaking follows a left S1-led control architecture, while singing uses a right M1-led pathway that progressively recruits left hemisphere circuitry via interhemispheric coupling. The approximately 10 Hz mu rhythm -- corresponding to the [[alpha-oscillations]] band -- serves as the coordination frequency for bilateral sensorimotor integration during singing, explaining why individuals with left hemisphere damage (Broca's aphasia) can often sing fluently despite impaired speech.

## Cortical Tracking of Sign Language

The oscillatory mechanisms that support spoken language comprehension extend to the visual domain in sign language processing, demonstrating that neural entrainment to linguistic structure is modality-general. Cortical activity becomes entrained to the quasi-periodic movements of sign language articulators between 0.4 and 5 Hz, peaking at approximately 1 Hz over occipital and central channels -- reflecting the slower periodicity of manual articulation compared to speech syllable rates. Brookshire et al. (2025) used MEG to record neural activity from proficient signers and sign-naive participants watching videos in known and unknown sign languages, finding that neural tracking in the delta band (0.5-2.5 Hz) was modulated by language knowledge: proficient signers showed enhanced cortical tracking of their known sign language compared to an unknown sign language, while sign-naive participants showed no such differentiation. This parallels the spoken language finding that delta-band tracking encodes higher-level linguistic comprehension rather than mere acoustic or visual feature processing, and supports a supramodal role for [[delta-oscillations]] in language understanding.

## Tonal Language Processing and Oscillatory Dynamics

In tonal languages such as Mandarin Chinese, where lexical meaning is conveyed by pitch contours (tones), neural oscillations at different timescales parse tone and segmental information through distinct mechanisms. [[Theta-oscillations]] support global syllabic integration -- tracking the ~4-5 Hz syllabic rhythm common to all languages -- while [[gamma-oscillations]] resolve local phonetic features including fine-grained pitch modulations that distinguish lexical tones. Recent transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS) work has directly tested these roles: theta tACS applied to bilateral auditory cortices impaired consonant identification in quiet conditions in Mandarin speakers, confirming the causal role of theta-band activity in segmental speech processing (Li et al., 2025).

ERP studies of Mandarin word production reveal a two-stage model of tone encoding: the main effect of tonal relatedness emerges in an early time window before syllabic relatedness effects appear, with significant interaction between tonal and syllabic processing only in later time windows (Zhang et al., 2024). This temporal separation suggests that tone is extracted and processed before being integrated into the full syllable representation -- a processing architecture shaped by the linguistic demands of tonal languages. Intracranial recordings from Mandarin speakers have enabled decoding of tonal information from neural activity, with the VocalMind stereotactic EEG dataset (Chen et al., 2025) providing a resource for studying vocalized, mimed, and imagined speech in tonal language contexts.

## Bilingual Speech Processing and Oscillatory Signatures

Bilingual speech processing recruits oscillatory dynamics that differ both from monolingual processing and across tasks varying in cognitive demand. Zheng et al. (2025) examined EEG oscillatory patterns in 46 German-English bilinguals across four tasks -- L2 listening, L1 speaking, L2 shadowing, and backward interpreting -- and found that sensory (listening) and articulatory (speaking/interpreting) tasks diverged markedly in the delta-theta band and the beta-gamma band. The sensory task elicited stronger delta-theta synchronization, consistent with envelope tracking and prosodic parsing, while articulatory tasks showed greater beta-gamma engagement related to motor planning and lexical retrieval.

Across-language semantic generalization -- the activation of common conceptual representations from words in different languages -- becomes possible around 550-600 ms post-stimulus onset, as revealed by EEG decoding studies showing that slow oscillations below 12 Hz contribute significantly to both within-language and across-language classification (Correia et al., 2015). This temporal window aligns with the N400 and late positive complex, suggesting that bilingual conceptual integration is mediated by low-frequency oscillatory dynamics that operate across language boundaries. The emerging field of oscillatory bilingualism research increasingly incorporates waveform morphology and [[aperiodic-dynamics]] beyond traditional frequency-band analysis.

## Brain-to-Brain Coupling During Social Interaction

Goldstein et al. (2018) demonstrated that interpersonal touch during pain creates real-time brain-to-brain coupling specifically in the [[alpha-oscillations]]-mu band (8-12 Hz), using dual-EEG hyperscanning in 20 romantic couples. Touch during pain produced the strongest interbrain coupling network (22 links vs 5 links for pain without touch), and coupling strength in somatosensory regions correlated with pain reduction (r = -0.56, p = .012) while coupling in temporoparietal regions correlated with empathic accuracy (r = -0.66, p = .002). This coupling was exclusive to the alpha-mu band -- no significant coupling appeared in beta -- establishing alpha-range oscillations as the privileged frequency for inter-brain communication and social [[neural-resonance-and-entrainment]].

Inter-brain synchrony during verbal interaction extends beyond the alpha-mu band. EEG hyperscanning during speaking and listening reveals that brain oscillations synchronize between listener and speaker during oral narratives, mediated partly by low-level speech-to-brain entrainment but also by the interactive communicative process itself (Perez et al., 2017). During speech coordination, theta/alpha amplitudes (6-12 Hz) synchronize in temporal and lateral-parietal regions, with inter-brain synchronization tightly linked to speech rhythm synchronization between interlocutors. Trait empathy, social closeness, and engagement predict synchronization magnitude, most prominently in low alpha (~7-10 Hz) and beta (~20-22 Hz) bands. A 2025 study of online seminars found that cooperative problem-solving tasks exhibited lower synchrony during active discussion but a sharper rise during presentation phases, reflecting delayed alignment that emerges when consensus must be enacted rather than negotiated (Rodriguez et al., 2025). Methodological concerns have been raised, however, as arbitrary analytical decisions -- such as choice of connectivity metric, frequency band definition, and epoch length -- can substantially skew inter-brain synchrony estimates in hyperscanning-EEG studies (Ayrolles et al., 2024), warranting caution in interpreting effect sizes across the literature.

## Hearing Loss and Oscillatory Reorganization

Age-related hearing loss (ARHL) produces systematic changes in neural oscillatory activity that extend well beyond the auditory periphery. Greater pure-tone-average hearing loss is associated with reduced resting [[alpha-oscillations]] power, and poorer hearing ability in middle-aged and older adults correlates with diminished alpha peak frequency and amplitude (Xia et al., 2026). However, the interpretation of age-related alpha reductions requires caution: after correcting for [[aperiodic-dynamics]] (the 1/f-like spectral slope), peak alpha power no longer differs significantly between age groups, though peak alpha frequency remains slower in older adults (Merkin et al., 2023). This suggests that some apparent alpha-power reductions in hearing-impaired older adults may reflect broadband aperiodic changes rather than oscillatory-specific deficits.

During speech-in-noise processing, hearing loss alters compensatory neural mechanisms. A combined EEG-fNIRS study revealed changes in theta-band activity and left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex engagement across healthy older adults, those with mild hearing loss, and those with moderate-to-severe hearing loss, indicating progressive decompensation of frontal compensatory circuits as hearing deteriorates (Wang et al., 2024). Theta-band neural tracking of speech is notably stronger in older adults compared to younger adults, potentially reflecting increased reliance on top-down predictive processing, while delta-band tracking shows no age-related difference.

Cochlear implant (CI) users demonstrate striking neuroplastic changes in oscillatory activity during auditory rehabilitation. Wöstmann et al. (2025) tracked neural adaptation in unilateral CI users at one and seven months post-activation, finding that spatial attention induced robust lateralization of alpha oscillations that remained stable over time. Critically, attentional alpha modulation was stronger in the hemisphere contralateral to the nonimplanted ear, regardless of testing time or CI status. Neural phase-locking to amplitude-modulated sounds showed a differential longitudinal trajectory: phase-locking to 40 Hz stimuli increased over six months while responses to 4 Hz stimuli relatively decreased, suggesting a progressive refinement of higher-frequency auditory processing as the brain adapts to electrical hearing. These findings connect to the ASSR literature and suggest that the 40 Hz gamma response may serve as a biomarker of successful CI adaptation.

## Implications for Multi-Scale Temporal Architecture

Across these studies, a hierarchy of auditory oscillatory processing emerges: delta frequencies (1-4 Hz) track prosodic and phrasal structure and encode linguistic comprehension in both spoken and signed language, theta frequencies (4-8 Hz) parse syllabic structure and acoustic clarity, alpha/mu frequencies (8-12 Hz) coordinate sensorimotor integration, gate auditory spatial attention, index listening effort, and mediate inter-brain coupling, beta frequencies (15-30 Hz) support beat prediction and motor-auditory interactions, and gamma frequencies (30-100+ Hz) encode fine spectrotemporal features and sustain steady-state responses dependent on GABAergic inhibition. This multi-scale temporal architecture parallels the [[canonical-eeg-bands]] organization and demonstrates that [[attention-and-perception]] in the auditory domain operates through the same oscillatory mechanisms found in visual, somatosensory, and memory processing -- while also revealing auditory-specific phenomena such as the 40 Hz ASSR resonance, the bass advantage in beat tracking, and the modality-general role of delta-theta entrainment in language comprehension. The clinical translation of these findings -- from GENUS therapy for Alzheimer's disease to cortical tracking biomarkers for [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]] assessment and cochlear implant rehabilitation -- underscores the practical significance of understanding auditory oscillatory dynamics.
