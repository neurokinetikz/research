# Beta Oscillations

Beta oscillations constitute a broad and functionally heterogeneous frequency band spanning from the upper alpha boundary (~13 Hz) to the onset of [[gamma-oscillations]] (~30 Hz). Far from a unitary rhythm, beta comprises at least three distinct subtypes generated by different cortical layers and serving different computational roles: beta1 (13-20 Hz) emerges through period concatenation of gamma and beta2 cycles in superficial layers, beta2 (20-30 Hz) arises from intrinsically bursting layer V pyramidal cells through gap junctional coupling, and beta3 (approximately 30-35 Hz) occupies the transitional zone into gamma. Beta oscillations are the primary rhythm of [[motor-control]], the carrier frequency for top-down predictions in [[predictive-coding]] hierarchies, and a critical mediator of long-range cortical communication. Their pathological enhancement in Parkinson's disease and characteristic modulation in psychiatric conditions establish beta as one of the most clinically significant entries in the [[canonical-eeg-bands]]. Recent large-scale invasive recordings have revealed that beta is the most spatially distributed cortical rhythm, mapping onto cortico-subcortical networks modulated by dopamine (Hirschmann et al., 2024), while a growing consensus treats beta not as a sustained oscillation but as a series of transient **beta bursts** that implement timely functional inhibition across cognitive and motor domains (Lundqvist et al., 2024).

## Neural Generators and Layer Specificity

Beta oscillations have distinct biophysical origins depending on sub-band. Roopun et al. (2006) demonstrated that beta2 (20-30 Hz) is generated by deep layer V intrinsically bursting pyramidal neurons through axo-axonic gap junctional coupling and M-current (KCNQ channel) dynamics, independently of synaptic transmission. Beta1 (~15 Hz) emerges through a period concatenation mechanism where one beta1 cycle equals one gamma period plus one beta2 period, enabling persistent cell assembly maintenance for [[working-memory]]. This layer-specific architecture explains why beta1 and gamma arise from superficial layers through GABAergic interneuron-pyramidal cell interactions while beta2 originates in deep layers. Williams et al. (2026) further clarified that excitation strength tunes oscillation frequency: weak excitatory-to-inhibitory connectivity supports faster interneuron network gamma, while stronger connectivity shifts dynamics toward slower PING-mediated beta-gamma rhythms, establishing a continuous mechanistic bridge between beta and [[gamma-oscillations]]. A 2025 cortical microcircuit model (bioRxiv) extended this work by demonstrating that distinct inhibitory interneuron subtypes -- particularly somatostatin-positive and parvalbumin-positive cells -- exert separable control over beta and gamma regimes, with somatostatin interneurons preferentially gating the transition from gamma to beta through dendritic inhibition of pyramidal cells in superficial layers.

## The 20 Hz Convergence and Motor Beta

The 20 Hz frequency represents a remarkable convergence point in neural dynamics, sitting at the fourth level of Klimesch's binary hierarchy (1.25 x 2^4 = 20 Hz) and corresponding to the beta1/beta2 functional divide. The rolandic mu rhythm's ~20 Hz component, generated in precentral motor cortex, is the only sensorimotor oscillation showing coherence with contralateral EMG during isometric contraction -- corticomuscular coherence peaks at 17-21 Hz during sustained grip tasks. Georgiev et al. (2026) demonstrated that movement-induced beta desynchronization is non-focal, spreading beyond the somatotopic area directly involved in movement to affect neighboring cortical regions, with beta event-related synchronization (rebound) peaking 500-800 ms after movement termination. This supports the status quo hypothesis of Engel and Fries (2010): beta oscillations maintain the current sensorimotor state, and their suppression is required for behavioral change.

Tomassini et al. (2026) provided fine-grained evidence for frequency-specific functional roles within the sensorimotor system: cortico-motor alpha-band coherence (~8-13 Hz) predicts reaction time, while beta-band coherence (18-30 Hz) predicts accuracy during visuomotor control. These effects emerge from phase dynamics rather than power, with single-trial deviations from preferred cortico-motor phase relationships predicting behavioral impairments. The GABAergic basis of motor beta is well established -- benzodiazepines increase power and decrease frequency of ~20 Hz oscillations, and GABA concentrations in motor cortex correlate positively with post-movement beta rebound amplitude.

Zhang et al. (2024) provided a mechanistic link between beta rebound and subsequent motor preparation: larger post-movement beta synchronization prolonged reaction time during Go trials, and this effect was mediated specifically by GABA-A receptor-dependent short-interval intracortical inhibition (SICI), which increased at the beta rebound peak and correlated negatively with rebound magnitude. This establishes a direct chain from beta rebound through GABA-A inhibition to motor preparation time, refining the status quo hypothesis by showing that beta rebound does not merely signal state maintenance but actively modulates the excitability landscape for upcoming movements through [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations|GABAergic mechanisms]].

## Regional Independence and Frequency Specialization

Wang et al. (2026) revealed that beta oscillations in different brain regions operate largely independently. In hippocampal CA1 versus prefrontal cortex during spatial navigation, PFC beta peaked at ~19 Hz (narrow distribution) while CA1 beta peaked at ~22 Hz (broad distribution), with low cross-regional coherence and striking inverse learning trajectories: PFC beta power increased while CA1 beta power decreased across learning. This frequency mismatch and independence challenges simple models of beta as a unified long-range communication channel and suggests region-specific beta generators tuned to local circuit properties. The ~19 Hz PFC frequency aligns with the [[critical-frequencies]] boundary at 20 Hz, while the broader CA1 distribution reflects convergent inputs from multiple sources.

## Corticomuscular Beta Transmission and Burst Propagation

Recent work has transformed the understanding of how cortical beta reaches the periphery. Glories et al. (2025) provided the first direct demonstration that beta bursts propagate from motor cortex to individual motor units in forearm extensor muscles: using high-density surface EMG and EEG during isometric contractions, they showed that motor unit beta bursts are time-locked to cortical beta bursts, confirming coordinated burst transmission along the corticospinal tract rather than a tonic oscillatory drive. Complementing this, Pereira et al. (2025) used decomposition of motor unit firing across the full recruitment range and found that cortical beta oscillations project with similar strength to motor neurons of all sizes, with a stable corticospinal delay of ~14 ms reflecting transmission through the fastest corticospinal fibers. This uniform projection challenges the view that beta preferentially targets low-threshold motor units and instead supports beta as a global modulatory signal across the entire motor neuron pool. Stronger beta synchronization was found in the extensor's ulnar versus radial regions, indicating muscle-specific topography in beta projections.

A comprehensive 2024 review of beta-band [[motor-control|corticomuscular coupling]] (Luo et al., 2024) synthesized evidence that beta CMC is modulated by sport training, fatigue, pain, aging, and neurological disorders. In stroke rehabilitation, increased beta-band CMC values during recovery indicate greater functional movement ability in both upper and lower limbs. Dynamic motor practice -- involving position-control tasks -- increases beta-band CMC through enhanced descending signaling, whereas isometric force-control practice leaves coherence unchanged (Heise et al., 2024), suggesting that the type of motor engagement differentially recruits corticospinal beta pathways.

## Beta in Working Memory and Cognitive Control

Beta oscillations play a central role in [[working-memory]] beyond motor functions. Myers et al. (2026) demonstrated that central-parietal beta (15-25 Hz) power reduction during priority switches uniquely tracks both the magnitude of representational shifts and the fidelity of updated representations, positioning beta as the primary oscillatory mechanism for selecting and promoting items into the focus of attention. This relationship was frequency-specific (only 10-24 Hz), trial-type specific (only switch trials), and item-specific (only the newly cued item). Frontal theta signaled the need for control without magnitude sensitivity, establishing a dissociation between control detection (theta) and control implementation (beta).

Lundqvist et al. demonstrated that working memory maintenance involves discrete oscillatory bursts rather than sustained rhythms: gamma bursts accompany encoding and reactivation while beta bursts correlate with suppression of informative spiking. Schmidt et al. (2019) proposed that moderate beta provides a "protection mode" preserving memory contents from interference, while high beta implements a "clear-out mode" erasing information. Fronto-parietal beta synchrony specifically conveys task-relevant category information, supporting only behaviorally relevant representations (Antzoulatos and Miller 2016).

Daume et al. (2024) revealed an age-related dissociation in beta's working memory roles: in younger adults, load-dependent decreases in beta-band neural variability during the maintenance period predicted memory performance, while in older adults the critical predictor shifted to post-response beta variability -- reflecting the efficiency of memory deletion rather than maintenance. This supports the inhibition deficit theory of aging and positions beta variability as a biomarker that tracks distinct [[development-and-aging|age-dependent]] working memory processes. Jung et al. (2025) extended the working memory protection framework by demonstrating that beta-band coherence between lateral prefrontal cortex and occipitoparietal areas transiently increases at the anticipated time of a distractor, but only on successful trials, directly implicating beta synchrony in proactive distractor suppression. This distractor anticipation signal operates alongside [[theta-oscillations|theta-band]] reactivation of memory representations, establishing a dual-oscillatory mechanism for interference management during maintenance.

## Prediction Error and Predictive Coding

Beta oscillations carry top-down predictions in cortical hierarchies, consistent with the [[predictive-coding]] framework where gamma mediates feedforward signals and beta mediates feedback. Emanuele et al. (2026) provided critical evidence by dissociating synaptic input from neural output in the motor system: while motor commands (EMG) reflect additive Bayesian integration of expectation and sensory input, the synaptic input to M1 and S1 (measured via BOLD and LFP) carries a signed prediction error signal. Beta-band LFP (13-25 Hz) showed strong negative correlation with expected-input relationships (rho=-0.763), directly demonstrating prediction error encoding in the beta range. Bogdan et al. (2025) extended this to global brain dynamics, showing that prediction error processing involves connectivity reorganization at theta-band timescales, with spontaneous fluctuations between high and low prediction error states occurring even at rest.

In the language domain, beta oscillations index the maintenance of predictive models during sentence comprehension. Beta power increases when upcoming words are highly predictable and decreases when semantic expectations are violated -- a pattern consistent with the [[predictive-coding]] framework where beta carries top-down generative models and their disruption signals prediction error (Lewis and Bastiaansen, 2015). Weissbart and Martin (2024) confirmed that pre-stimulus beta power (16-20 Hz) scales with semantic predictability during naturalistic spoken language comprehension, while a 2024 study on word production demonstrated that beta activity encodes distinct task-rule contexts, with spatiotemporal beta patterns differentiating between rule categories (Sikora et al., 2024). These findings extend beta's predictive role from sensorimotor to [[auditory-and-speech-oscillations|linguistic]] processing, supporting a domain-general function for beta in maintaining and updating internal models across cortical hierarchies.

## The 25 Hz Attractor and Beta-Gamma Boundary

The high beta range centered at ~25 Hz functions as a neural attractor where multiple neurophysiological constraints converge. Nougaret et al. (2024) demonstrated a double dissociation: low beta (<20 Hz) dominates primary motor cortex and correlates with reaction time, while high beta (>20 Hz) dominates dorsal premotor cortex and relates to temporal prediction, with PMd beta peaking precisely at 25 Hz. This frequency is a natural subharmonic of 50 Hz gamma, and [[cross-frequency-coupling]] between theta phase and ~28 Hz beta amplitude predicts individual working memory performance (Axmacher et al. 2010).

The beta-gamma transition at approximately 30-35 Hz marks where GABAergic mechanisms shift from supporting motor stability to enabling perceptual binding and conscious cognition. Below ~30 Hz, oscillations support maintenance and long-range communication; above ~30 Hz, local excitatory-inhibitory interactions dominate for active sensory processing. This [[critical-frequencies]] boundary reflects GABA-A receptor decay time constants (~25-30 ms), fast-spiking interneuron resonance properties, and the minimum frequency capable of implementing theta-nested gamma coding. The transition is visible in motor control as corticomuscular coherence shifts from beta to gamma with increasing force requirements, and clinically as anesthetic-induced frequency traveling from gamma through beta to alpha during loss of consciousness.

## Beta in Decision-Making

Beta oscillations encode categorical decisions through frequency-specific channels. Rassi et al. (2023) demonstrated in monkeys performing duration- and distance-categorization tasks that two distinct beta-band frequencies in dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC) and pre-supplementary motor area (preSMA) consistently mapped to the two relative categories, regardless of the stimuli's objective physical properties. Activity in these frequency-specific channels predicted the animals' behavioral responses, and category-selective neurons synchronized at their respective beta frequencies -- establishing beta as a multiplexed decision signal where different frequencies carry different categorical contents through the same cortical network. This frequency-multiplexing mechanism extends the role of beta beyond simple maintenance toward active representational coding.

In the basal ganglia, Herz et al. (2024) showed that subthalamic nucleus beta (12-20 Hz) oscillations track effort on a single-trial basis during reward-effort trade-off decisions, while prefrontal [[theta-oscillations]] (4-7 Hz) signal previous trial reward. This dissociation positions basal ganglia beta as an energetic cost signal that informs value-based [[corticostriatal-learning|corticostriatal]] decision computations, consistent with the broader link between beta and dopaminergic networks. Bracco et al. (2025) further demonstrated that beta oscillations emerge in a cerebello-cortical network up to 2 seconds before action initiation, progressively building across trials as environmental features are learned and behavioral outcomes stabilize. Within this network, the cerebellum initially drives prefrontal activity during the pre-movement period before this directional influence reverses near movement onset, and single-trial beta bursts predict upcoming performance based on previous outcomes -- identifying cerebellar-cortical beta as a predictive signal for behavioral stability.

## Criticality and Dynamics

Daffertshofer et al. (2018) demonstrated that beta-band phase synchronization (measured via the Kuramoto order parameter) shows power-law scaling with exponent alpha=0.57, indicating the resting brain operates near criticality. Beta phase dynamics are more flexible than amplitude dynamics (alpha=0.80), suggesting phase synchronization supports rapid, entropy-rich coding while amplitude provides slow, persistent memory processes. This distinct criticality of phase versus amplitude explains why beta manifests as transient bursts rather than sustained rhythms -- the system operates at a critical boundary enabling spontaneous transitions between synchronized and desynchronized states.

Lundqvist et al. (2024) synthesized the bursting perspective in a major review, arguing that brief beta bursts -- rather than sustained oscillatory power -- are the fundamental unit of beta's cognitive influence. Beta bursts provide transient functional inhibition that gates information flow: their precise timing on individual trials correlates with behavioral outcomes in working memory, attention, and motor control tasks in ways that trial-averaged power analyses obscure. The intermittent and nonlinear nature of beta bursts also provides novel insights into interareal interactions, as burst timing can implement flexible, event-driven communication between distant cortical areas without requiring sustained phase-locking. This bursting framework reconciles the apparent contradiction between beta's association with the "status quo" and its role in active cognition -- bursts provide momentary windows of inhibitory control that can either stabilize current representations or clear them for updating, depending on their timing relative to task demands.

## Clinical Significance

Beta abnormalities characterize multiple [[clinical-applications]]. Parkinson's disease involves exaggerated 13-35 Hz synchronization in subthalamic nucleus, with longer-duration beta bursts (>900 ms) correlating with motor impairment. Deep brain stimulation at beta frequencies (20 Hz) slows movement, while high-frequency DBS suppresses pathological beta. The theta/beta ratio is the most robust EEG biomarker for ADHD. Elevated high beta (25-30 Hz) associates with anxiety and hyperarousal. In anesthesia, the characteristic "beta buzz" at ~20 Hz during early propofol induction shifts to ~12 Hz as unconsciousness approaches, marking the transition through [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]] states. Sleep spindles at the low beta boundary (12-15 Hz) bridge beta and alpha dynamics, with fast spindles (13-15 Hz) linked to memory consolidation through [[cortical-layer-oscillations]].

Werner et al. (2025) provided causal evidence linking subthalamic beta to motor slowing by applying DBS at specific beta frequencies (10, 16, 20, 26, and 30 Hz) while recording magnetoencephalography in 14 Parkinson's patients. STN stimulation in the beta range boosted cortical beta power lateralization and proportionally reduced finger-tapping rate, directly demonstrating that artificially enhancing beta synchrony through the subthalamo-cortical loop is sufficient to slow movement. Crucially, medication and DBS exert dissociable effects on beta sub-bands: levodopa primarily suppresses low beta (13-20 Hz), while therapeutic high-frequency DBS broadly decreases the entire 13-35 Hz range (Darcy et al., 2024), suggesting partially independent pathophysiological contributions of low and high beta to parkinsonian motor symptoms.

The clinical translation of beta-based biomarkers accelerated with the FDA approval in February 2025 of the first **adaptive deep brain stimulation** (aDBS) system -- Medtronic's BrainSense -- which continuously monitors subthalamic beta power and adjusts stimulation intensity in real time (ADAPT-PD trial). The pivotal ADAPT-PD trial demonstrated that the majority of patients achieved good on-time without troublesome dyskinesia relative to stable continuous DBS, and long-term follow-up confirmed tolerable and effective therapy over more than one year (Gilron et al., 2025). The subsequent ADAPT-START study (Little et al., 2026) reported that aDBS yielded ~35% greater motor improvement than continuous DBS, with gait showing the most consistent benefits. These developments establish subthalamic beta as not merely a correlate of motor impairment but a viable closed-loop control signal for [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation|personalized neurotherapy]].

## Dopaminergic Networks and Unifying Principles

Hirschmann et al. (2024) analyzed over 30 hours of invasive recordings from 1772 cortical channels in epilepsy patients and demonstrated that beta is the most spatially distributed cortical rhythm. By mapping beta-dominant cortical areas onto whole-brain functional and structural MRI connectomes, they identified a shared cortico-subcortical network encompassing motor, prefrontal, and basal ganglia structures. Critically, this beta network showed significant overlap with dopamine uptake maps derived from positron emission tomography, providing a circuit-level explanation for why beta abnormalities feature so prominently in dopaminergic disorders -- from Parkinson's disease to ADHD to addiction. This unifying framework suggests that beta oscillations emerge as an intrinsic property of dopamine-modulated cortico-basal ganglia loops, and that the diverse cognitive and motor functions attributed to beta may reflect a common underlying circuit architecture rather than independent generators. The finding also supports extending the investigation of beta as a feedback signal for closed-loop therapies beyond movement disorders to psychiatric conditions involving dopaminergic dysregulation.
