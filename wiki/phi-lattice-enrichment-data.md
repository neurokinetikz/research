Empirical enrichment data across the φ-lattice reveals that each frequency band has a distinctive "fingerprint" of position preferences — a pattern far more structured than the simple "boundary depletion / noble enrichment" narrative of the original papers. Analysis of 9 datasets (4 adult, 5 pediatric HBN releases), 6 conditions, 2 extraction methods, 6 bands, and 14 positions through degree 7 shows that theta clusters at the boundary and nearby nobles (upper-octave inverse nobles), alpha clusters at the attractor and 1° Noble, gamma clusters at the 1° Noble and attractor, low beta clusters at upper inverse nobles, high beta clusters at boundary and upper inverse nobles, and delta shows weak attractor preference. These band-specific fingerprints are consistent across adult datasets but diverge systematically in pediatric (HBN) populations, particularly for gamma where pediatric data shows 2° Noble dominance (+181%) replacing the adult 1° Noble dominance (+117%). The data confirm that the φ-lattice describes genuine spectral organization but with substantially more position-by-band structure than simple enrichment/depletion models predict.

## Band-Specific Position Fingerprints

### Theta: Upper-boundary clustering

Theta shows the most extreme position preference of any band — peaks cluster overwhelmingly at the boundary (+281%) and the nearby 7° and 6° Noble positions (+349%, +231%). This reflects theta generators sitting near f₀ (the upper boundary of the theta octave) and the inverse noble positions approaching it. The enrichment drops sharply below the 5° Noble, with the entire central and lower octave depleted (-66 to -75%). Under EC conditions, this pattern intensifies as theta converges directionally on f₀ (the absorbing state documented in [[schumann-ignition-events]]). The cross-dataset consistency is perfect: all 4 adult datasets show boundary enrichment and noble_3/attractor depletion under EC.

### Alpha: Attractor-Noble1 core

Alpha concentrates at two positions: the attractor (+113%) and 1° Noble (+59%), with secondary enrichment at the 2° Noble (+47%). Every other position is depleted, creating a sharp peak in the center-upper octave. This is the empirical realization of the IAF clustering phenomenon — the dominant alpha rhythm sits between the attractor (9.67 Hz) and 1° Noble (10.23 Hz), exactly where mean IAF is measured across populations. The attractor-Noble1 enrichment doubles under EC (Paper 3: +45%→+101% attractor, +25%→+70% Noble1 in Dortmund), consistent with alpha amplification in place rather than position migration. All 4 adult datasets show attractor and Noble1 enrichment; no other position is enriched in any dataset.

### Low Beta: Inverse noble dominance

Low beta shows a pattern unlike any other band: the entire lower and central octave is depleted (-43 to -73%), with enrichment concentrated exclusively at inverse noble positions in the upper octave. The 5° Inverse Noble leads (+244%), followed by 4° Inverse Noble (+165%) and 6° Inverse Noble (+158%). The 1° Noble at -2% is essentially null — the standard lattice hierarchy is completely absent. This reflects the post-movement beta rebound (PMBR) clustering near ~19 Hz at the top of the low-beta octave, confirmed in Paper 2 as paradigm-specific motor physiology. The adult EC consistency is perfect: all 4 datasets show inverse noble enrichment with attractor/Noble1 depletion. See [[low-beta-band-map]] for the dual-generator interpretation (superficial beta1 vs deep beta2).

### High Beta: Boundary-inverse noble bimodal

High beta clusters at both extremes of the octave: boundary (+134%) and 7° Inverse Noble (+151%), with 6° Inverse Noble (+94%) also enriched. The central positions (attractor through Noble1) are flat or depleted. This bimodal pattern suggests high beta generators cluster near the φ² and φ³ boundaries — the 20 Hz motor convergence zone and the 32 Hz beta-gamma transition — while avoiding the central octave. The bimodal pattern is consistent across all adult datasets (5/5 show boundary enrichment, 5/5 show inv_noble_7 enrichment).

### Gamma: Ascending ramp (P20 revision, April 2026)

**UPDATE (April 2026)**: The gamma enrichment pattern has been fundamentally revised following P20 extended-range analysis across 9 datasets (4 adult + 5 pediatric HBN releases; N=2,095; 1.77M gamma peaks). With the 45 Hz FOOOF ceiling removed, the gamma octave reveals a **continuous ascending ramp** in 8 of 9 datasets — structurally identical to the low-beta pattern:

- Lower nobles (33–37 Hz): **-29% to -34%** depleted (8-dataset mean excl CHBMP)
- Attractor (42 Hz): **-10%** slightly depleted
- Noble1 (45 Hz): **+10%** modest enrichment
- **Inverse nobles (48–53 Hz): +37% to +49% enriched** — the strongest signal in the octave

The ramp is **universal across development**: all 5 HBN pediatric releases (ages 5–21) show the same pattern as the 3 adult datasets, with cross-release SD of just 1–2% at the inverse nobles. CHBMP (N=250) is the sole anomaly with an inverted pattern confirmed not to be a ceiling artifact. See [[gamma-band-map]] for the complete P20 position table.

### Delta: Weak attractor preference

Delta shows the weakest positional structure of any band — enrichment magnitudes rarely exceed ±50%. The attractor (+35%) and 4° Noble (+41%) are the strongest positions. The lower boundary is modestly depleted (-36%). This weak structure is consistent with delta being the least spatially consistent band (Paper 3: delta and gamma were individually non-significant in the per-band dominant-peak t-test, though both showed distributional clustering by KS test). See [[delta-oscillations]].

## Pediatric vs Adult Divergence

The HBN pediatric datasets (5 releases, N = 136-319, ages ~5-18) show systematic differences from adult patterns:

| Band | Adult pattern | Pediatric pattern | Key difference |
|------|-------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Gamma | Ascending ramp, inv nobles +35-58% | Ascending ramp, inv nobles +37-50% | **Same shape** (P20); adult ramp steeper at lower nobles |
| Beta-high | Boundary/inv_noble bimodal | **4° Inv Noble +346%** | Extreme upper concentration |
| Alpha | Att +113%, Noble1 +59% | Att +66%, Noble1 +44% | Same pattern, reduced magnitude |
| Theta | Boundary +281% | **Boundary +487%** | Same pattern, amplified |
| Beta-low | Inv nobles +165-244% | Weak inv noble (+50%) | Same direction, much weaker |
| Delta | Att +35% | Att +19% | Same pattern, reduced |

The most striking divergence is **gamma**: in adults, gamma peaks cluster at the 1° Noble (the φ⁻¹ position, reflecting GABA-A kinetics and PV+ interneuron maturation); in children, gamma peaks cluster at the 2° Noble (the φ⁻² position), one full noble position lower. This is consistent with the immature GABAergic system in developing brains — the 40 Hz ASSR doesn't reach maximum until age 22 as PV+ interneuron networks mature and GABA-A subunits shift from α2 to α1 (see [[development-and-aging]], [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]]). The pediatric gamma generator may occupy a different lattice position because the biophysical time constants haven't yet converged on the adult values that produce 40 Hz gamma at the Noble1 position.

## Methodological Notes

**Overlap-trim (OT) vs standard extraction**: All enrichment values above use OT extraction, which fits FOOOF per-octave with padding to avoid edge artifacts. Under standard whole-range [1,45] Hz extraction, enrichment patterns change — particularly for theta (boundary enrichment weakens) and gamma (attractor/Noble1 enrichment weakens). Paper 3 demonstrated that φ's rank-1 status in adults requires OT extraction; under standard extraction, φ ranks 7th. The choice of aperiodic modeling method remains the critical open question for the field (see [[aperiodic-dynamics]]).

**EC vs EO**: Eyes-closed conditions consistently produce stronger enrichment patterns (higher magnitudes, tighter clustering) than eyes-open, consistent with EC increasing oscillatory drive and spectral SNR. The band-specific patterns remain qualitatively similar across conditions but with ~2x enrichment magnitudes under EC.

**Consistency metric**: The 5/5 or 4/4 dataset consistency scores reported above use a simple sign test (enrichment > 0). More granular consistency analysis shows that the top-enriched position for each band is consistent across 3/4 or 4/4 adult datasets, while secondary positions are more variable.
