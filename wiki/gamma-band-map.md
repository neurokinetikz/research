The gamma φ-octave (33.17–53.67 Hz, n+3) spans from f₀×φ³ to f₀×φ⁴. The adjacent high gamma φ-octave (53.67–86.84 Hz, n+4) spans from f₀×φ⁴ to f₀×φ⁵ but is largely inaccessible to scalp EEG due to EMG contamination dominating above ~55 Hz (Whitham et al. 2007). **P20 extended-range analysis (April 2026)** removed the previous 45 Hz FOOOF ceiling and measured the full gamma octave for the first time, revealing that gamma shows a **continuous ascending ramp** — lower nobles depleted (-53% to -65%), inverse nobles enriched (+23% to +66%) — structurally identical to the low-beta pattern. The old "Noble1 peak with cliff above" was entirely an artifact of the 45 Hz ceiling. The ascending ramp replicates across **8 of 9 datasets** (2,095 subjects, 1.77M gamma peaks): 3 adult datasets (EEGMMIDB N=109, LEMON N=202, Dortmund N=608) and all 5 HBN pediatric releases (R1–R6, N=926 combined, ages 5–21). CHBMP (N=250) is the sole anomaly showing an inverted pattern. In the high gamma n+4 band, density-corrected analysis shows only two positions (noble_7 and noble_6 at 54–55 Hz) retain enrichment after accounting for the steep frequency-dependent peak density drop — consistent with the literature showing scalp EEG has negligible neural SNR above ~55 Hz. The pediatric ascending ramp is virtually identical to the adult pattern, with cross-release SD of just 1–2% at the inverse nobles — the most consistent finding across any set of independent datasets in the entire lattice framework.

## Zone 1: Lower Gamma (32.19–38.69 Hz) — The Transition Zone

**Boundary at 32.19 Hz (+73% EC, +82% EO, 3/4 adult datasets positive) — The φ³ consciousness gate.** The beta-gamma boundary. Enrichment here is continuous with the upper-octave peak in the high-beta band — the 7° Inv Noble of high beta (31.66 Hz, +151%) sits just below this boundary, and gamma boundary enrichment (+73%) represents the same spectral peak seen from the gamma side. The "traveling peak" under propofol migrates from above this boundary to below it at loss of consciousness (see [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]]). The spectrolaminar crossover occurs near 32 Hz — gamma-dominant superficial layers above, alpha/beta-dominant deep layers below (see [[cortical-layer-oscillations]]). EC enrichment is slightly lower than EO (+73% vs +82%), an unusual pattern — most positions amplify under EC. This may reflect EC suppressing broadband low-gamma in favor of narrowband ~40 Hz alpha amplification. In pediatric data, the boundary is depleted (-34%), consistent with children's gamma concentrating at higher positions (~39 Hz, 2° Noble).

**7° Noble at 32.73 Hz through 5° Noble at 33.62 Hz (+55% to +15% EC).** A descending gradient from the boundary. The 5th Schumann Resonance harmonic (~33.8 Hz) falls at the 5° Noble (33.62 Hz, Δ = 0.011 from the position), the closest SR-lattice alignment in the gamma band. These positions represent the low gamma zone where slow gamma oscillations operate — Colgin et al. (2009) identified slow gamma (30-50 Hz) from CA3 as supporting memory retrieval, and the hippocampal slow gamma lower edge sits in this range (see [[cross-frequency-coupling]], [[hippocampal-memory]]).

**4° Noble at 34.54 Hz through 3° Noble at 36.07 Hz (+20% and +13% EC).** The CHBMP dataset shows anomalous enrichment here (4° Noble +93%, 3° Noble +105%), while other datasets are near-zero or negative. This zone may be where broadband gamma from high-density electrode systems (CHBMP: 120 channels) is resolved but missed by lower-density systems.

**2° Noble at 38.69 Hz (+4% adult EC, +181% pediatric).** Near-null in adults but the dominant pediatric gamma position. In frequency, 38.69 Hz falls just below the canonical 40 Hz — the ASSR frequency where developing brains show immature responses. The pediatric +181% enrichment (15/15 HBN datasets positive) places the immature gamma generator precisely one noble position below the adult peak, at a frequency (~39 Hz) consistent with longer GABA-A IPSC decay times in the developing brain (alpha-2 subunit dominance producing ~30 ms decay rather than the adult alpha-1 subunit's ~25 ms). The developmental trajectory: 2° Noble (~39 Hz, pediatric) → Attractor (~41 Hz) / 1° Noble (~43 Hz, adult) as GABA-A subunits mature by age 22 (see [[development-and-aging]], [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]]).

## Zone 2: Core Gamma (38.69–43.34 Hz) — The 40 Hz Zone

**Attractor at 40.95 Hz (+79% EC, +64% EO, 4/4 adult datasets positive) — The Goldilocks frequency.** The geometric midpoint of the gamma octave maps to ~41 Hz — the convergence of GABA-A IPSC decay (~25 ms), pyramidal membrane time constants (20-30 ms), PV+ interneuron resonance (12-25 ms), and STDP windows (10-30 ms). The canonical "40 Hz" of the neuroscience literature (ASSR, GENUS Alzheimer's therapy, gamma binding, Llinas-Ribary thalamocortical oscillation) sits at u = 0.451, within 0.05 of the attractor. The 40 Hz ASSR demonstrates cortical resonance — Galambos et al. (1981) found uniquely large brain responses at this frequency. GENUS 40 Hz entrainment reduces amyloid-beta 44-53% in mice with frequency specificity (not 8, 80 Hz, or random). Wang et al. (2026) validated across species in primates. Cognito OVERTURE trial: 78% slowing of functional decline (company-reported, pending peer review). The ASSR matures through adolescence reaching maximum around age 22, paralleling PV+ interneuron network maturation (see [[gamma-oscillations]], [[clinical-applications]]).

The attractor enrichment is moderately condition-dependent (+64% EO → +79% EC, +15% gain) — smaller than the dramatic EC amplification seen in alpha or high beta. This is consistent with gamma being driven more by sensory input and task demands than by arousal state (Paper 2 showed gamma reallocation driven by visual input, not motor task per se). Individual datasets vary: EEGMMIDB +173% (strong), Dortmund +92%, LEMON +35%, CHBMP -27% (depleted — the CHBMP anomaly extends to the attractor). Paper 3's dominant gamma peak median = 39.85 Hz, placing it between the 2° Noble and attractor — at the FOOOF resolution limit between these positions.

**1° Noble at 43.34 Hz (+102% EC, +95% EO, 4/4 adult datasets positive) — The highest enrichment in the accessible gamma range.** Paper 1 reported +144.8% at the 1° Noble for gamma — the strongest per-band adherence in the entire analysis. The 9-dataset mean (+102% EC) is lower but still the peak position. The 1/φ position in the gamma octave maps to the upper edge of the ASSR range, near the boundary of what scalp EEG can reliably detect. Dortmund drives the aggregate (+250% EC, the single strongest dataset×position enrichment in the adult data); other datasets are more modest (EEGMMIDB +124%, LEMON +11%, CHBMP -60%).

The Noble1 enrichment is nearly condition-invariant (+95% EO vs +102% EC, only +7% gain), making gamma Noble1 the most EC-insensitive enriched position in any band. This is the opposite of alpha's attractor (+35→+113%, +86% gain) and suggests the gamma Noble1 generator maintains its position and amplitude regardless of arousal state — consistent with gamma reflecting ongoing local cortical computation rather than state-dependent gating.

Gamma's Noble1 at 43.34 Hz is where Paper 1 reported the strongest φ-lattice adherence of any band (+144.8%). But the alpha-IAF confound does not apply here: there is no "canonical 43 Hz" that could create generic frequency clustering. The 43 Hz Noble1 enrichment — if robust across methods — would be the cleanest evidence of φ-specific lattice organization, as it cannot be explained by dominant frequency clustering. However, the CHBMP anomaly (-60%) and the LEMON result (+11%) reduce confidence in universality.

## Gamma's Dynamics Within the Landscape

**Gamma is the least condition-dependent band.** Unlike theta (dramatic EC convergence), alpha (3× EC amplification), low beta (EC doubles ramp), and high beta (7× EC bimodal emergence), gamma's enrichment profile is **nearly identical under EC and EO**. The Noble1 shifts by only +7% (95→102%), the attractor by +15% (64→79%), and some positions actually decrease under EC (boundary: +82→+73%). This condition-invariance is consistent with gamma reflecting local cortical computation — PV+ interneuron circuits operating independently of arousal-state modulation.

Paper 2 found that visual input (EO vs EC) drives gamma reallocation: opening the eyes shifts gamma peaks from boundary toward attractor positions (Δπ_att = +0.070, p_Bonf = 0.003, r = +0.44). However, this reallocation was not φ-specific (persists across all bases and random positions, p_emp = 0.44). The enrichment data corroborates: the small EC→EO shifts are real but modest, and the overall profile shape is preserved.

**Developmental findings revised (P20, April 2026).** The old characterization of a "pediatric 2° Noble shift" (+181%) from the 45 Hz extraction is superseded. Under P20 extended-range extraction, the pediatric gamma pattern is an **ascending ramp virtually identical to the adult pattern** — not a different shape with a shifted peak. The 5 HBN releases (N=926, ages 5–21) show:
- **Lower nobles**: -24% to -31% depleted (adults: -37% to -44% — steeper in adults)
- **Attractor**: -16% (adults: +2% — pediatric attractor is more depleted)
- **Noble1**: +6% (adults: +18% — weaker but same sign)
- **Inverse nobles**: +37% to +50% (adults: +35% to +58% — comparable magnitudes)

The ramp is present from childhood. The adult-pediatric difference is in **magnitude and steepness**, not shape: adults show stronger lower-noble depletion and a higher zero-crossing point (at the attractor), while children show a more symmetric ramp with the zero-crossing below the attractor. The GABA-A maturation mechanism (alpha-2→alpha-1 subunit switch, PV+ myelination) may steepen the ramp rather than shift a peak, though this interpretation requires age-stratified analysis within HBN to test directly (see [[development-and-aging]], [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]]).

**CHBMP remains the sole anomaly.** Under P20, 8/9 datasets (3 adult + 5 pediatric) converge on the ascending ramp. CHBMP shows an inverted pattern confirmed not to be a ceiling artifact. The old concerns about gamma being "more dataset-specific than any other band" are partially resolved: the ramp is highly consistent when measured with extended-range extraction. The CHBMP anomaly likely reflects dataset-specific factors (variable channel count, 200 Hz sampling, Cuban population) rather than a fundamental limitation of gamma enrichment analysis. IRASA/eBOSC replication (P22) remains valuable for confirming method-independence.
