# Self and Embodiment

Neural oscillations play a central role in constructing and maintaining the sense of self, body ownership, and self-other boundaries. Research converges on [[alpha-oscillations]] frequency -- particularly [[individual-alpha-frequency]] -- as a temporal gating mechanism for multisensory integration underlying bodily self-perception, while theta dynamics track the processing of self-ambiguity and vestibular self-motion perception. The sense of agency depends on pre-movement alpha phase in sensorimotor cortex, which gates connectivity with parietal and prefrontal regions (Bertoni et al., 2025). Interoceptive self-awareness -- indexed by the **heartbeat-evoked potential** (HEP) -- engages theta synchronization and modulates [[default-mode-network]] activity through alpha-band coupling (Fló et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2025). Self-boundary dissolution states, as studied in meditation and [[psychedelic-states]], are characterized by distributed changes across multiple frequency bands: [[beta-oscillations|high-beta]] attenuation in posterior medial cortex during meditation (Trautwein et al., 2024) and subcritical shifts in alpha-band dynamics under DMT (Irrmischer et al., 2025). Together, these findings reveal embodied selfhood as a fundamentally oscillatory computation linking [[attention-and-perception]], [[predictive-coding]], and [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]].

## Alpha Frequency and Body Ownership

D'Angelo et al. (2026) provided converging correlational, causal, and computational evidence that individual alpha frequency (IAF) in the posterior parietal cortex determines the temporal binding window (TBW) for body ownership perception. In their rubber hand illusion paradigm (N = 46 for EEG, N = 30 for tACS), task-related IAF correlated strongly with body ownership TBW (r = -0.651, p < .001): faster alpha frequency produces narrower binding windows and higher temporal sensitivity. This relationship was specific to frequency, not power -- alpha power showed no correlation with TBW. Causal manipulation via tACS confirmed the finding with large effect sizes: 8 Hz stimulation widened the body ownership TBW to 208 ms while 13 Hz narrowed it to 138 ms (eta-squared = 0.637). Bayesian causal inference modeling revealed that IAF modulates sensory uncertainty -- faster IAF reduces uncertainty in asynchrony measurement, enabling more precise visuotactile discrimination. These results establish [[alpha-oscillations]] as a temporal gating mechanism for self-related multisensory signals, with direct implications for [[attention-and-perception]] and [[individual-alpha-frequency]] research.

Beyond frequency, the rubber hand illusion also engages distributed power changes. Evans and Blanke (2022) demonstrated that illusory ownership is accompanied by a spatially distributed reduction of both alpha and [[beta-oscillations|beta]] power across frontal and parietal electrodes (N = 36), with beta reduction emerging as the most consistent marker across contrasts isolating the illusory state. Complementary work on mu rhythm desynchronization during embodied rubber hand observation (Fossataro et al., 2023) found that visuo-tactile synchrony modulates the strength and duration of mu event-related desynchronization (ERD) over sensorimotor cortex in an ownership-dependent manner -- weaker and briefer mu ERD appeared when body continuity was disrupted. The mu rhythm thus provides a bridge between body ownership and the mirror neuron system: mu suppression during action observation is enhanced when the observed body part is experienced as one's own. In virtual reality embodiment paradigms, Esteves et al. (2025) identified increased [[beta-oscillations|beta]] and [[gamma-oscillations|gamma]] power over occipital cortex as EEG biomarkers for sense of embodiment (N = 41), while emphasizing that no single frequency band or brain region fully accounts for the phenomenon -- embodiment operates as a complex, dynamic process spanning time, frequency, and spatial domains.

## Self-Boundary Dissolution in Meditation

Rohr et al. (2026) demonstrated that self-boundary dissolution states during meditation can be decoded from MEG recordings in experienced Vipassana practitioners (N = 36). Using Lempel-Ziv complexity (LZc), they achieved 64% within-participant classification accuracy for distinguishing self-boundary dissolution from rest -- outperforming all individual frequency band features. The 27 Hz frequency emerged as a significant marker, with reduced power in parietal and motor regions during dissolution states. High [[gamma-oscillations]] (60-90 Hz) showed the strongest individual frequency band classification accuracy (59.8%). Critically, LZc showed mixed directionality across brain regions -- some areas increased complexity during dissolution (paracentral lobule, left parietal) while others decreased it (right insula) -- suggesting a complex reorganization of neural dynamics rather than simple increases or decreases. The increased signal complexity during self-boundary dissolution aligns with the entropic brain hypothesis and parallels findings in [[psychedelic-states]], where LZc exceeds normal waking baseline.

Trautwein et al. (2024) extended these findings using a neurophenomenological approach with 45 long-term meditators (mean lifetime practice ≈ 3,832 hours). Participants were instructed to modulate and attenuate their embodied self-experience while MEG was recorded. Full-blown suspension of self-experience -- characterized by loss of first-person perspective, altered self-location, and suspension of agency -- was associated with robust reductions of **high-beta oscillatory power** (peak ≈ 27 Hz) in posterior medial cortex, including posterior cingulate cortex, precuneus, somatomotor, superior parietal, and temporoparietal cortices (sensor level p = 0.022; source level p = 0.003). These beta reductions were frequency-specific in the dissolution-versus-maintenance contrast, appearing only in the high-beta band, though broadband reductions spanning all bands except high [[gamma-oscillations|gamma]] appeared when dissolution was compared to resting baseline. Importantly, neural changes correlated with phenomenological interview ratings and lifetime meditation hours, but not with standard self-report questionnaires -- highlighting the value of fine-grained phenomenological methods for studying self-related states. The convergence of high-beta attenuation at ≈ 27 Hz across both Rohr et al. (2026) and Trautwein et al. (2024) suggests this frequency may be a reliable oscillatory signature of embodied self-disruption, potentially reflecting reduced sensorimotor prediction in the [[predictive-coding]] hierarchy.

## Theta Dynamics and Self-Ambiguity

Avramescu et al. (2026) investigated how perceptual ambiguity in self-face recognition alters neural dynamics using morphed self-other face stimuli (N = 24). The [[theta-oscillations|theta band]] (5-8 Hz) emerged as the critical frequency range for self-other processing: theta peak frequency sliding (PFS) decreased significantly from rest (6.54 Hz) to task (5.39 Hz), and was lower for ambiguous than unambiguous stimuli (5.37 vs 5.40 Hz, d = 0.83). Alpha PFS showed no ambiguity effect, establishing frequency-band specificity. Resting-state theta PFS predicted drift rate under ambiguity (r = .45, p = .028), meaning faster resting theta dynamics support better evidence accumulation when self-other boundaries are unclear. Spectral entropy increased from rest to task and was higher for ambiguous stimuli (d = 1.24), with the rest-to-task entropy change predicting behavioral efficiency (R-squared = 36.1%). These theta dynamics connect to [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]] research, as the rest-to-task shift from approximately 6.5 Hz toward 5.4 Hz may represent movement toward a theta attractor frequency during internally directed processing.

## Sense of Agency and Sensorimotor Oscillations

The subjective experience of controlling one's actions -- the **sense of agency** -- depends critically on pre-movement oscillatory dynamics. Bertoni et al. (2025) demonstrated this using a brain-machine interface in a tetraplegic participant combined with EEG experiments in healthy volunteers (N = 30 and N = 10 in separate experiments). In the tetraplegic BMI user, the phase of low-alpha oscillations (≈ 8 Hz) in primary [[motor-control|motor cortex]] (M1) predicted agency judgements approximately 250 ms before movement onset (phase opposition cluster p = 0.0004, corrected). In healthy participants, pre-movement alpha oscillations in M1 and supplementary motor area (SMA) correlated with agency ratings, with SMA showing the strongest effect (p = 0.0002). Post-movement connectivity analyses revealed that agency modulated SMA-to-temporal cortex coupling in the 9-12 Hz range (p = 0.005), consistent with a model in which alpha phase gates the comparison between motor predictions and sensory outcomes. Local field potential recordings showed ≈ 6% higher firing rates during the favorable oscillatory phase, providing a potential cellular mechanism for oscillatory agency gating. These findings connect the sense of agency to [[alpha-oscillations]] phase dynamics and the broader framework of [[predictive-coding]], where mismatches between intended and actual outcomes generate prediction errors that diminish the feeling of control.

## Interoception and Heartbeat-Evoked Potentials

The sense of self extends beyond exteroceptive body boundaries to include awareness of internal bodily states -- **interoception**. The heartbeat-evoked potential (HEP), an EEG signal time-locked to cardiac R-peaks, provides a neural index of how the brain processes visceral signals. Fló et al. (2024) used classification approaches to distinguish interoceptive from exteroceptive attentional states in 20 healthy participants and 5 brain-injured patients. During interoceptive attention, they observed increased frontal [[theta-oscillations|theta]] oscillations, decreased aperiodic complexity (lower Kolmogorov complexity), and reduced permutation entropy in centroparietal areas. Power spectral density classifiers achieved exceptional accuracy (20/20 participants, AUC = 0.75 ± 0.10), substantially outperforming standalone HEP-based classification (17/20 participants, AUC = 0.55 ± 0.03). Critically, interoceptive attention produced a distinct oscillatory signature: increased theta connectivity and decreased beta bandwidth, whereas exteroceptive attention produced overall broadband power increases across 1-30 Hz. In brain-injured patients, one unresponsive wakefulness patient demonstrated willful HEP modulation consistent with command-following -- suggesting interoceptive processing may persist even when external responsiveness is absent, with implications for [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]] assessment.

Complementary work has shown that conscious cardiac awareness engages additional oscillatory mechanisms. Increased [[beta-oscillations|beta synchronization]] between key nodes of the interoceptive network -- including dorsomedial prefrontal cortex and insular cortex -- accompanies explicit heartbeat detection, along with earlier HEP modulation and greater prevalence of EEG microstates involving temporal and opercular regions. The HEP amplitude itself increases when participants have subjective awareness of changes in their interoceptive state, particularly during emotional situations (Villena-González et al., 2025), linking interoceptive oscillatory dynamics to affective self-awareness. These findings align with [[predictive-coding]] accounts of interoception, where the brain generates predictions for each heartbeat and interoceptive attention amplifies the neural response to prediction errors.

## Default Mode Network and Alpha-Band Self-Processing

The [[default-mode-network]] (DMN) -- encompassing posterior cingulate cortex (PCC), medial prefrontal cortex, and angular gyrus -- is consistently activated during self-referential thought. Ma et al. (2025) established a mechanistic link between [[alpha-oscillations|alpha oscillations]] and DMN function using simultaneous EEG-fMRI and alpha-frequency transcranial alternating current stimulation (α-tACS) in 32 healthy participants. Alpha-frequency stimulation at 10 Hz tightened the dynamic coupling between spontaneous fluctuations in alpha power and posterior DMN connectivity, specifically between ventral PCC and bilateral angular gyrus (vPCC-lAG: t = 2.57, p = 0.011; vPCC-rAG: t = 1.82, p = 0.045). This enhancement was frequency-specific -- neither [[theta-oscillations|theta]] (4-7 Hz) nor [[beta-oscillations|beta]] (13-17 Hz) showed analogous coupling changes -- and network-specific, with no effects on executive control or salience network connectivity. These results suggest that alpha oscillations serve as the temporal carrier for DMN-mediated self-referential processing, and that disruptions to alpha-DMN coupling -- as seen in [[psychedelic-states]], [[consciousness-and-anesthesia|anesthesia]], and depersonalization -- may fundamentally alter the neural substrate of self-experience.

## Psychedelic Self-Dissolution and Oscillatory Criticality

Psychedelic compounds produce some of the most dramatic disruptions of self-experience, and recent work has linked these effects to specific oscillatory mechanisms. Irrmischer et al. (2025) investigated DMT-induced shifts in neural criticality using EEG, finding that DMT pushes brain oscillations away from criticality toward subcritical regimes in [[alpha-oscillations|alpha]] and adjacent frequency bands. Using the functional excitatory-inhibitory ratio (fE/I) metric, they demonstrated that criticality shifts in alpha and [[theta-oscillations|theta]] bands correlated with subjective intensity ratings of **self-dissolution** -- the hallmark phenomenological feature of the psychedelic experience. Under DMT, entropy increased while complexity decreased, consistent with the entropic brain hypothesis and parallel to the complexity reorganization observed during meditation-induced dissolution (Rohr et al., 2026).

At the level of self-referential processing, Dila Suay et al. (2025) examined how an ayahuasca-inspired DMT/harmine formulation affects neural differentiation between self and other faces in 30 healthy male participants (double-blind, placebo-controlled). DMT/harmine selectively reduced the P300 amplitude difference between self and other faces, while early visual processing (P1 -- increased amplitude) and structural face encoding (N170 -- decreased amplitude across all face categories) were altered non-selectively. The P300 self-other distinction reduction correlated with subjective ego dissolution ratings, providing an ERP marker of diminished self-referential processing. Notably, familiar face processing was preserved, suggesting that socially meaningful representations resist psychedelic disruption more than self-representations do. Silva-Costa et al. (2025) further confirmed that ayahuasca consistently decreases [[alpha-oscillations|alpha]] power while inducing visual, bodily, and mystical experiences -- reinforcing alpha reduction as the most reliable oscillatory signature of the psychedelic state. Together, these studies connect [[psychedelic-states]] phenomenology to specific oscillatory mechanisms: alpha-band criticality disruption for global self-dissolution, and P300 attenuation for self-referential processing changes.

## Vestibular Processing and Self-Motion Perception

The vestibular system provides foundational input for the bodily self, anchoring self-location and the experience of existing as an embodied agent in space. Hadi et al. (2024) dissociated bottom-up vestibular sensory activation from higher-order self-motion perception using an elegant experimental design: participants seated in a motorized rotating chair in darkness at near-threshold velocities were compared to stationary participants experiencing illusory self-motion. [[alpha-oscillations|Alpha band]] activity correlated with bottom-up vestibular sensory activation -- the brainstem-mediated reflex component -- while [[theta-oscillations|theta band]] activity was specifically linked to the subjective experience of self-motion perception itself, regardless of whether actual rotation occurred. [[beta-oscillations|Beta band]] changes reflected updating of the prior brain state in response to vestibular input. Source localization analyses identified cingulate cortex area CSv as a key node for vestibular-evoked activity, with potentials related to motion direction. These frequency-specific dissociations parallel the broader architecture of self-processing: alpha gates sensory input, theta tracks perceptual interpretation, and beta indexes predictive updating -- a hierarchy consistent with [[predictive-coding]] models of vestibular self-representation.

## Depersonalization-Derealization and Network Dysfunction

**Depersonalization-derealization disorder** (DPRD) represents a pathological disruption of embodied selfhood, characterized by persistent feelings of detachment from one's body and mental processes. EEG studies have identified theta and alpha dysregulation, connectivity disruptions, and attenuation of the P300 component as electrophysiological markers of dissociative states (Basu et al., 2025). Zheng et al. (2025) examined resting-state fMRI in 47 drug-naïve DPRD patients versus 49 healthy controls, revealing significantly decreased functional connectivity within and between major brain networks -- including the visual network, somatosensory-motor network, dorsal and ventral attention networks, limbic network, frontoparietal network, and [[default-mode-network]]. A critical finding was a significant negative correlation between the "Self" factor on the Cambridge Depersonalization Scale and functional connectivity within the somatosensory-motor network, directly linking subjective bodily self-consciousness impairment to sensorimotor network dysfunction. A support vector machine classifier achieved 80% accuracy in distinguishing DPRD patients from controls using these connectivity features. The somatosensory-motor network disruption in DPRD mirrors -- in pathological form -- the beta attenuation observed during intentional self-dissolution in meditation (Trautwein et al., 2024), suggesting a shared neural substrate for voluntary and involuntary disruptions of embodied selfhood, with important implications for [[clinical-applications]] and [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation|neurofeedback]] interventions targeting oscillatory normalization.

## Integration Across Studies

Together, these studies reveal that self-related processing engages multiple oscillatory mechanisms operating at distinct timescales and anatomical levels. [[alpha-oscillations]] frequency gates the temporal precision of multisensory binding for body ownership, operating as a filter that determines how much temporal discrepancy can be tolerated before visuotactile signals are perceived as separate. Alpha phase in sensorimotor cortex additionally gates the sense of agency by modulating pre-movement connectivity between motor and association areas. At the network level, alpha oscillations couple dynamically with the [[default-mode-network]] to support self-referential processing, and disruption of this coupling -- whether by psychedelics, anesthesia, or pathology -- alters the neural substrate of self-experience. Theta dynamics track the cognitive demand of resolving self-other ambiguity and index vestibular self-motion perception, while interoceptive theta synchronization supports awareness of internal bodily states. Beta oscillations in posterior medial cortex sustain the embodied self, with high-beta attenuation at ≈ 27 Hz emerging as a convergent marker of self-dissolution across meditation traditions. And self-boundary dissolution involves distributed changes across the entire spectrum, best captured by complexity measures rather than single-band power, with psychedelic-induced criticality shifts in alpha specifically correlating with ego dissolution. The common thread is that self-perception depends on precise temporal coordination across brain regions -- a fundamentally oscillatory computation linking [[attention-and-perception]], [[predictive-coding]], and [[motor-control]] to embodied cognition.
