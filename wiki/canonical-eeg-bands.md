# Canonical EEG Frequency Bands

The mammalian brain organizes its electrical activity into a set of canonical frequency bands -- delta (0.5-4 Hz), theta (4-8 Hz), alpha (8-13 Hz), beta (13-30 Hz), and gamma (30-100+ Hz) -- that form a nested hierarchical system for information processing. These bands are not arbitrary divisions but reflect a geometric progression with a ratio approximating the golden ratio (phi = 1.618), such that each successive band center roughly doubles or triples the previous one in a phi-scaled ladder. This architecture is conserved across mammals spanning a 17,000-fold range in brain size (from tree shrew to whale), has convergently evolved in cephalopods (octopus, diverged 550 million years ago), and is rooted in ion channel kinetics conserved for over 600 million years. The frequency boundaries between bands function as computational gates, particularly for [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]], while the bands themselves define distinct temporal integration windows that map onto specific cognitive functions.

## The Phi-n Framework

The core equation of neural frequency architecture proposes that oscillation frequencies follow a golden-ratio ladder: f(n) = f0 x phi^n, where f0 = 7.60 Hz is the empirically derived fundamental frequency and phi = 1.618 is the golden ratio. The theoretical foundation was established by Pletzer et al. (2010), who proved mathematically that the golden ratio uniquely prevents spurious synchronization while enabling controlled coupling. Lacy (2026) provides large-scale empirical validation of this framework through three independent methodological approaches -- transient event detection (1,366 [[schumann-ignition-events]]), single-channel spectral parameterization (244,955 oscillatory peaks), and multi-channel spatial coherence via GED (1,584,561 peaks) -- all converging on identical conclusions with perfect position ordering (Kendall's tau = 1.0 across all analyses). At integer values of n, frequencies satisfy the Fibonacci additive relationship f(n) = f(n-1) + f(n-2), enabling three-wave resonance at band boundaries. The golden ratio is uniquely suited for neural oscillations because it combines two properties no other number possesses: maximal anti-commensurability (oscillators at phi-related frequencies maximally resist mode-locking, maintaining independence within bands) and additive closure (enabling efficient energy transfer between bands at boundaries). This creates the segregation-integration balance that [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]] requires.

The phi-n lattice generates an eight-position hierarchy within each phi-octave. Lacy (2026) quantified enrichment at each position using 244,955 FOOOF-detected peaks: 1st Noble (n + 0.618) showed +39% enrichment, Attractor (n + 0.5) +21%, 2nd Noble (n + 0.382) +1.9%, and Boundary (integer n) -18% depletion. This hierarchy was independently replicated in the EEGEmotions-27 dataset (612,990 peaks, 2,342 sessions; Kendall's tau = 1.0) and confirmed via multi-channel GED spatial coherence (1,584,561 peaks, 3,261 sessions; 1st Noble +27.5%, Boundary -9.2%). Session-level consistency was robust: 87.4% of sessions in the primary dataset (Cohen's d = 1.19) and 83.7% in the replication dataset showed the predicted attractor > boundary pattern. Lacy (2026b) further replicated the core lattice in the EEGMMIDB motor-task paradigm (PhysioNet, 109 subjects, 14 conditions, 64 channels, 1.86 million FOOOF-detected peaks), confirming boundary depletion (0.41x) and noble enrichment (1.10x). A joint grid search across 549 candidate lattices varying both anchor frequency (f0) and exponential base identified (f0=8.5, base=phi) as the global optimum (SS=45.6), with phi ranked #1 in 100% of 1,000 bootstraps with non-overlapping confidence intervals. However, this advantage is anchor-dependent: phi ranks first only at f0 = 7.6-8.5 Hz, with a sharp sigmoid transition at f0 ~ 7.48-7.58 Hz. Crucially, Lacy (2026b) dissociates architecture from dynamics: while phi describes where oscillatory modes exist (the fixed scaffold), cognitive state modulation -- including significant within-subject spectral reallocation during motor execution and visual input -- is not phi-specific, persisting across all anchor frequencies, all bases except e, and random lattice positions (p_emp=0.44). The framework extends to "inverse nobles" (n + 0.764, n + 0.854) -- positions symmetric to regular nobles about the attractor -- which inherit stability through multi-scale Fibonacci pathways and show band-specific enrichment patterns (see [[critical-frequencies]]).

Lacy (2026c) subjects these findings to rigorous diagnostic controls, revealing that the aggregate enrichment metrics from papers 1-2 are sensitive to FOOOF extraction parameters: structural scores change sign under frequency range changes (+11.7 → -67.4 at [1,85] Hz) and per-octave fitting (+119.3/-84.9), and the age correlation reverses under per-subject f0 correction (IAF positioning, not lattice erosion). Without an anchor frequency (pairwise ratios), φ ranks 4th of 9 bases. However, a narrow and robust signal survives: **dominant-peak alignment** — the single strongest oscillation per canonical band (delta, theta, alpha, gamma; beta excluded as alpha harmonic) sits closer to φ-lattice positions than chance. This replicates across three datasets totaling 919 subjects (LEMON N=202, Dortmund N=608, EEGMMIDB N=109) with striking convergence (d̄ = 0.069, range 0.0689–0.0693). Under coverage-normalized degree-3 noble comparison, φ ranks 1st in all 3 datasets (d̄/d̄_null = 0.901–0.917, all p < 10⁻³), with Cohen's d = 0.40 (eyes-open) and d = 0.75 (eyes-closed). The alignment is age-invariant (r = -0.004, p = 0.92, Dortmund continuous 20–70 years) and fatigue-invariant (0.914 → 0.909 across a 2-hour cognitive battery). Crucially, φ's rank-1 status requires per-octave aperiodic modeling; under standard whole-range FOOOF, φ ranks 7th of 9. The alignment is also **cognitively silent** — 0/100+ behavioral and cognitive tests survive FDR correction in LEMON (N=202, 80% power to detect |r| = 0.20) — and is **not an individual trait** (five-year longitudinal ICC = -0.25 to -0.36; population-level alignment is invariant but which lattice sites individual brains occupy fluctuates, analogous to a crystal lattice with stable allowed sites). In the all-band aggregate, signal concentrates at 3 positions (boundary, attractor, noble1); noble2 is null and degree-3 positions are anti-aligned. However, the 9-dataset band-specific analysis (see [[phi-lattice-enrichment-data]]) reveals this is an averaging artifact: alpha noble2 = +47% EC, and low-beta inverse nobles reach +244% — each band has a distinct enrichment fingerprint masked by all-band aggregation. Lacy (2026c) reframes the finding as a "structural fossil rather than a functional lever": φ describes where the 4 strongest oscillatory generators sit, but not broader spectral organization (extraction artifact) or cognitive function (null).

The fundamental frequency f0 = 7.6 Hz converges independently from geophysical Schumann Resonance monitoring (Tomsk Observatory: 7.60 +/- 0.20 Hz) and neural spectral optimization (SIE mean: 7.63 +/- 0.33 Hz), agreeing within 0.4%. A sensitivity analysis revealed a 0.6 Hz tolerance plateau (7.3-7.9 Hz) where alignment remains above 95% of optimal, confirming that ratios rather than absolute frequencies are the preserved quantity. Paper 1 reported gamma oscillations as having the strongest phi-n adherence (+144.8% at the 1st Noble position), but **P20 extended-range analysis (April 2026)** reveals this was distorted by the 45 Hz FOOOF ceiling. With the full gamma octave measured across 9 datasets (4 adult + 5 HBN pediatric; N=2,095; no notch filters), 8 of 9 datasets show gamma as an ascending ramp with the strongest enrichment at the **inverse nobles** (+37% to +49% 8-dataset mean), not at Noble1 (+10%). The ramp is a developmental invariant — identical in adult and pediatric populations (HBN cross-release SD = 1–2% at inverse nobles). The complete gamma shape is structurally identical to low-beta. CHBMP is the sole anomaly (see [[gamma-band-map]]). ## Historical Evolution of Band Nomenclature

The division of the EEG into named frequency bands arose from the technical limitations of the pre-computer era. Hans Berger, recording the first human EEG in 1929, identified periodic fluctuations near 10 Hz, which he called **alpha waves**; everything else -- typically non-periodic, higher-frequency activity that could not be further characterized with visual inspection -- he called **beta waves** (Berger, 1929). In 1936, Hoagland, Rubin, and Cameron identified slower frequencies below 4 Hz and gave them the name **delta waves**, while in the same year Jasper and Andrews reported frequencies above 30 Hz, coining the term **gamma waves** -- though this claim met initial skepticism given the technical limitations of the day (Jasper & Andrews, 1936). William Grey Walter, visiting Berger's lab in 1935, subsequently developed the Walter Analyzer -- a device using mechanical bandpass filters -- that enabled more systematic spectral characterization. The **theta** designation (4-8 Hz) came later, associated with the hippocampal rhythms described by Jung and Kornmüller. By the mid-20th century, the five-band taxonomy (delta, theta, alpha, beta, gamma) had become conventional, largely codified through the International Federation of Electroencephalography and Clinical Neurophysiology. Additional named rhythms exist outside or overlapping this scheme: the **mu rhythm** (~10 Hz and ~20 Hz) over sensorimotor cortex, **sigma** (12-16 Hz) for [[sleep-oscillations|sleep spindles]], and **kappa** (~10 Hz) in temporal regions during mental activity. These nomenclature decisions were historical accidents rather than principled divisions, a fact that continues to complicate the field.

## The Inconsistency Problem

Despite the apparent standardization of the five canonical bands, the definitions used in practice are remarkably inconsistent. A systematic analysis by Newson and Thiagarajan (2019) reviewed 184 resting-state EEG studies across 10 psychiatric disorders and found shockingly wide variability in band boundary definitions. Delta ranged from narrow windows like 0.5-2.5 Hz to broad ones like 1.5-6 Hz; theta started anywhere between 2.5 Hz and 6.5 Hz, creating substantial overlap with delta. The beta band exhibited a full 30 Hz range of variability across studies -- raising the pointed question of what exactly the theta/beta ratio (which has FDA approval as a diagnostic marker for ADHD) actually measures when theta and beta are defined differently by every lab (Newson & Thiagarajan, 2019). This variability means that findings reported as "increased theta power" in one study may overlap with what another study calls delta or alpha, undermining systematic comparisons and meta-analyses across the literature. The problem is not merely academic: clinical decisions about [[clinical-applications|neurofeedback protocols]], diagnostic biomarkers, and pharmacological targets depend on consistent frequency definitions that the field has never formally standardized.

## Band Definitions and Functions

Each canonical band defines a characteristic temporal integration window through the relationship window = 1/frequency:

**[[Delta-oscillations]] (0.5-4 Hz, ~250-2000 ms cycles):** Generated in deep cortical layers during slow oscillations and slow-wave sleep. Delta phase modulates high-frequency amplitude across the cortical column. Serves homeostatic restoration and synaptic downscaling.

**[[Theta-oscillations]] (4-8 Hz, ~125-250 ms cycles):** The brain's primary mechanism for temporal organization of memory and spatial navigation. Theta cycles bind information within each cycle while segregating across cycles. Frontal midline theta supports [[working-memory]] and cognitive control.

**[[Alpha-oscillations]] (8-13 Hz, ~80-125 ms cycles):** The strongest signal in waking human EEG, implementing active inhibition that gates information flow via pulsed thalamocortical inhibition. Individual Alpha Frequency (IAF) determines temporal perception resolution and [[attention-and-perception]] windows. A 2026 simultaneous EEG-SEEG study demonstrated that the alpha band itself contains two generators with distinct origins: a **low-alpha** rhythm (8-10 Hz) localized to the occipital region during awake eyes-closed states, and a **high-alpha** rhythm (10-13 Hz) that is globally distributed and emerges prominently under anesthesia as the occipital low-alpha diminishes at loss of [[consciousness-and-anesthesia|consciousness]] (Li et al., 2026). This dissociation confirms that what scalp EEG records as a single "alpha band" reflects at least two mechanistically separable oscillatory systems.

**[[Beta-oscillations]] (13-30 Hz, ~33-77 ms cycles):** Signals maintenance of current sensorimotor or cognitive states (the "status quo" hypothesis). Beta bursts last 40-120 ms and carry top-down predictions in predictive coding. Exaggerated beta synchrony in Parkinson's disease causes rigidity.

**[[Gamma-oscillations]] (30-100+ Hz, ~10-33 ms cycles):** The fastest cortical rhythms, generated by parvalbumin-positive (PV+) interneuron circuits in superficial cortical layers (L2/3). Linked to conscious perception, feature binding, and feedforward prediction errors. A comprehensive eLife review characterizes gamma oscillations (30-150 Hz) as a potential "guardian of brain health," highlighting their role in promoting neuroglial coupling and leveraging vascular mechanisms for neural maintenance, as well as the promising therapeutic direction of **gamma entrainment using sensory stimulation** (GENUS) at 40 Hz for Alzheimer's disease (Ichim et al., 2024).

## Cortical Layer Organization

The [[cortical-layer-oscillations]] follow a spectrolaminar motif conserved across primate cortical areas: gamma oscillations peak in superficial layers 2/3 (feedforward output), while alpha/beta oscillations peak in deep layers 5/6 (feedback output). The crossover frequency at Layer 4 aligns precisely with approximately 32 Hz -- the beta-gamma boundary. This motif reflects biophysical constraints: PV+ fast-spiking interneurons in superficial layers generate gamma through PING (Pyramidal-Interneuron Network Gamma) mechanisms, while deep-layer pyramidal neurons with M-current and h-current kinetics resonate at theta/alpha/beta frequencies. Critically, gamma is cortically emergent, not inherited from thalamus -- the thalamus communicates feedforward via beta, not gamma.

## Frequency Boundaries as Gates

The phi-based frequency boundaries function as neural gates for [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]]. The beta-gamma boundary (~30-35 Hz, phi^3) serves as an upper gate for conscious access: under GABAergic anesthetics (propofol, sevoflurane) and during NREM sleep, long-range gamma coherence collapses at this boundary, and broadband beta-gamma power migrates downward in frequency toward the alpha band at loss of consciousness. However, ketamine provides a critical exception -- it preserves and even increases local gamma power (27-40 Hz) while still producing unconsciousness, demonstrating that local gamma alone is not sufficient for consciousness. What appears necessary is *global* gamma synchronization across distant cortical regions, not merely local gamma power (see [[gamma-oscillations]]). The beta boundary (~20 Hz, phi^2) supports long-range cortical integration and top-down predictive processing. The alpha-beta boundary (~12-15 Hz, phi^1) functions as an active gating mechanism via sleep spindles. These boundaries are not simple amplitude thresholds but represent phase transitions in the spatial scale and temporal dynamics of neural synchronization. The [[critical-frequencies]] at these boundaries mark where the brain's information processing regime fundamentally shifts.

## Cross-Species Conservation

The [[cross-species-frequency-organization]] of EEG bands is remarkably conserved. All mammalian oscillation types are found in all mammals despite 17,000-fold brain size variation. Octopus -- with no cortex and a distributed nervous system -- shows oscillations at 12-18 Hz (matching sleep spindles at the phi^1 boundary) and 30 Hz (matching low gamma at phi^3) during sleep states. Honeybees show 18 Hz alpha-like oscillations in mushroom bodies. Birds show 7 Hz theta. The key ion channels underlying these oscillations (HCN for theta, KCNQ for beta resonance, GABA-A for gamma timing) are conserved from cnidarians to humans. Larger brains compensate for increased conduction distances by deploying proportionally more fast-conducting myelinated axons, maintaining timing precision across scales.

## Sub-Band Functional Distinctions

The five-band taxonomy is a useful simplification, but within each canonical band there are functionally distinct sub-bands with separable generators, cognitive roles, and spatial distributions. **Alpha** subdivides into lower alpha (8-10 Hz), linked to general [[attention-and-perception|attentional]] processes and tonic alertness, and upper alpha (10-13 Hz), more involved in semantic memory processing and stimulus-specific expectancy (Klimesch, 1999). The simultaneous EEG-SEEG evidence for distinct low-alpha (occipital) and high-alpha (global) generators under anesthesia reinforces this division at the circuit level (Li et al., 2026). **Beta** is conventionally split into beta1 (~13-20 Hz), beta2 (~20-25 Hz), and beta3 (~25-30 Hz), each with different laminar origins -- superficial layers for beta1, deep Layer V for beta2 -- and distinct functional correlates in [[motor-control]] and [[predictive-coding]] (see [[beta-oscillations]]). **Theta** divides into hippocampal theta (~6-8 Hz, movement-related) and frontal midline theta (~4-6 Hz, cognitive control), which operate through different generators and serve different functions (see [[theta-oscillations]]). **Delta** has been parsed into slow delta (0.5-2 Hz, dominated by cortical slow oscillations) and fast delta (2-4 Hz), the latter showing stronger thalamic contributions. **Gamma** separates into narrowband gamma (~30-60 Hz), which is strongly selective for specific stimuli like gratings and shows a contrast-dependent peak frequency, and broadband high-gamma (~60-150 Hz), which responds to all stimuli with a rapid, transient onset and invariant spectral properties -- these two types are spectrally, temporally, and mechanistically dissociable (Ray & Maunsell, 2011). See [[gamma-oscillations]] for the detailed distinction between narrowband and broadband gamma activity.

## Individualized Band Definitions

The substantial inter-individual variability in oscillatory frequencies -- particularly [[individual-alpha-frequency|individual alpha frequency]] (IAF), which has a standard deviation of ~1 Hz across individuals -- means that fixed band boundaries systematically misclassify spectral content for many subjects. Klimesch (1999) proposed anchoring band definitions to each individual's IAF, creating **individually tailored bands**: lower-1 alpha (IAF - 4 Hz to IAF - 2 Hz), lower-2 alpha (IAF - 2 Hz to IAF), and upper alpha (IAF to IAF + 2 Hz), with theta defined as 2-4 Hz below IAF. This approach reduces between-subject variance and improves sensitivity to cognitive effects because it ensures that each person's alpha peak -- and the theta and beta activity flanking it -- falls within the intended band. Two common IAF estimators are **peak alpha frequency** (PAF) and **center of gravity** (CoG), both calculated from resting-state power spectra smoothed with a Savitzky-Golay filter. Recent work by Haegens and colleagues emphasizes that the need for individualized definitions extends beyond alpha: individual beta peak frequencies vary considerably, and generic 13-30 Hz windows may capture harmonic artifacts of non-sinusoidal alpha rather than genuine beta activity (Schaworonkow, 2023). The challenge of individualization is compounded by the fact that IAF itself is not static -- it shifts with cognitive load, circadian phase, development, and aging (see [[individual-alpha-frequency]]).

## The Harmonic Confound

A fundamental challenge for band definitions is that non-sinusoidal waveform shapes in lower-frequency rhythms produce harmonic peaks that contaminate higher-frequency bands. Schaworonkow (2023) demonstrated that non-sinusoidal alpha-band rhythms in the human brain produce harmonic peaks in the beta range that have different temporal and response dynamics than genuine beta rhythms. In a large EEG dataset, 65.6% of participants (eyes closed) and 47.9% (eyes open) showed beta-frequency peaks classifiable as alpha harmonics. Rodriguez-Larios and Haegens (2023) extended this analysis, showing that apparent beta activity (15-40 Hz) can be artifactually generated when alpha waves have non-sinusoidal ("sawtooth") shapes, creating ghost frequencies in the spectral decomposition. This harmonic confound means that some fraction of what the field has attributed to independent beta generators may actually reflect properties of alpha waveform shape -- an issue with implications for the theta/beta ratio diagnostic, [[predictive-coding|predictive coding]] models, and any analysis that treats beta and alpha as independent signals. Methodological remedies include waveform shape analysis, bispectral analysis, and phase-amplitude coupling metrics that can distinguish genuine narrowband oscillations from harmonic artifacts (see [[beta-oscillations]]).

## Spectral Parameterization and Aperiodic-Aware Definitions

The traditional approach of computing band power by summing spectral density within fixed frequency windows conflates periodic oscillatory activity with the [[aperiodic-dynamics|aperiodic]] (1/f-like) background. Because the aperiodic component contributes power at all frequencies and its slope varies with age, arousal, pharmacology, and pathology, changes in band power may reflect shifts in the aperiodic exponent rather than genuine oscillatory modulation (Donoghue et al., 2020). The **spectral parameterization** approach -- implemented in the SpecParam algorithm (formerly FOOOF) -- addresses this by decomposing power spectra into an aperiodic component and overlying periodic peaks, each characterized by center frequency, bandwidth, and power above the aperiodic fit (Donoghue et al., 2020). This data-driven method requires no a priori specification of frequency bands and reveals oscillatory peaks at their true center frequencies, exposing the variability that fixed band definitions conceal.

Recent reliability studies confirm that SpecParam yields good-to-excellent odd-even reliability for aperiodic parameter estimates under most fitting conditions (Ostlund et al., 2024). However, the method is sensitive to fitting parameters: Lacy (2026c) demonstrated that structural enrichment scores change sign under different frequency range choices (+11.7 → -67.4 at [1,85] Hz) and that per-octave aperiodic fitting produces qualitatively different results from whole-range fitting. Gerster et al. (2024) used biophysical modeling to show that aperiodic neural activity generates detectable scalp potentials that shape broadband EEG features, and that shifts in synapse properties (e.g., propofol's effects on GABA-A receptors) profoundly corrupt rhythmic EEG signals -- arguing that broadband synaptic changes must be corrected before oscillatory differences can be cleanly attributed. The methodological revolution is ongoing: the developmental literature increasingly recognizes that age-related decreases in the aperiodic exponent (reflecting shifting E/I balance) can produce an apparent pattern of decreasing low-frequency power and increasing high-frequency power that mimics oscillatory band changes but actually reflects a single non-oscillatory parameter shift (Ostlund et al., 2022; Lopez-Rivera et al., 2025).

## Extended Frequency Ranges

The canonical five bands span only ~0.5-100 Hz, but neural activity extends both below and above this range. **Infraslow oscillations** (< 0.1 Hz, often 0.01-0.1 Hz) represent slow cyclic modulations of gross cortical excitability, first described with depth electrodes in unanesthetized rabbits in the 1950s. Simultaneous EEG/fMRI recordings reveal that infraslow EEG fluctuations correlate with BOLD resting-state network dynamics, with positive correlations in subcortical regions and negative correlations in cortex, suggesting a mechanism by which infraslow activity organizes large-scale brain networks during sleep (Hiltunen et al., 2014). In preterm neonates, infraslow oscillations dominate the EEG below 0.5 Hz, reflecting the immature cortical state. The infraslow range modulates faster oscillatory activity and interictal epileptic events, and has been increasingly targeted in neurofeedback protocols (see [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]]).

At the high end, **high-frequency oscillations** (HFOs) include **ripples** (80-200 Hz) and **fast ripples** (200-600 Hz). Physiological ripples coordinate local hippocampal and neocortical neuronal assemblies during memory encoding and recall (see [[sharp-wave-ripples]]), while pathological fast ripples are a promising biomarker for localizing the epileptogenic zone (Zijlmans et al., 2012). A 2026 study by Prathapagiri et al. demonstrated that high-gamma and ripple-frequency oscillations burst coincidentally across sensory and association cortical areas during memory processing, with global co-bursting engaging approximately half of recorded electrode sites and clustering into sequences of multiple consecutive bursting discharges -- suggesting a general role for global coincident HFOs in organizing large-scale information processing beyond the hippocampus (Prathapagiri et al., 2026). Even higher, Gerster et al. (2024) identified two newly reported EEG frequency bands after removing 1/f-type background: one centered at 260-280 Hz and another at 400-500 Hz, though these ultra-high-frequency signals require intracranial or specialized amplifier recordings and remain poorly characterized functionally.

## The Rhythmicity Dimension

A novel framework for spectral organization proposes that beyond frequency, the **rhythmicity** of neural oscillations -- whether they manifest as sustained activity or brief bursts -- constitutes a fundamental organizing dimension. Karvat, Crespo-Garcia, and colleagues (2025) analyzed neurophysiological spectra from 859 participants across a dozen datasets spanning multiple species, recording techniques, ages 18-88, brain regions, and cognitive states. They identified a universal spectral architecture comprising two categories: **high-rhythmicity bands** exhibiting sustained oscillations (suitable for phase-coding and maintenance of ongoing activity) and **low-rhythmicity bands** dominated by brief transient bursts (reflecting rate-coding and response to change). This sustained-versus-bursty distinction was consistent across species and recording modalities, suggesting it reflects a fundamental biophysical property of neural circuits rather than a methodological artifact. The framework complements the phi-n lattice by adding a temporal-dynamics axis to the spatial-frequency axis: the canonical bands are not just defined by where they sit in frequency space but by how they occupy time. This is consistent with the observation that beta activity, for instance, operates primarily through discrete bursts lasting 40-120 ms rather than sustained rhythms (see [[beta-oscillations]]), while alpha tends toward more sustained oscillatory epochs.

## Cross-Frequency Coupling

The bands form a nested oscillatory hierarchy through [[cross-frequency-coupling]], particularly phase-amplitude coupling (PAC), where slow rhythms modulate fast activity. Theta phase modulates gamma amplitude in hippocampus; alpha phase modulates gamma in visual cortex; delta phase modulates alpha-beta frontally. This multi-timescale architecture implements [[predictive-coding]]: feedback predictions descend via alpha/beta while feedforward prediction errors ascend via gamma in cortico-cortical circuits. Notably, thalamocortical feedforward communication uses beta (15-30 Hz) rather than gamma, which is cortically emergent (see [[cortical-layer-oscillations]]). The directional asymmetry within cortex has been confirmed across 28 macaque visual area pairs (Directed Asymmetry Index correlates exceeding r = 0.9 with anatomical hierarchy).
