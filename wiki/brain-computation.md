# Brain Computation

Neural oscillations are not mere byproducts of brain activity but serve as active computational mechanisms that organize information processing across spatial and temporal scales. The brain performs computation through multiple interacting modalities: traveling waves propagate information across cortex via precisely timed spikes, ephaptic coupling allows electric fields to directly influence nearby neurons without synaptic transmission, alpha/beta oscillations create spatial "inhibitory stencils" that gate where cognitive content can be expressed, and spike-timing-dependent plasticity triggered by wave fronts creates temporary synaptic networks supporting working memory. This hybrid discrete-continuous computational architecture -- combining digital-like spike events with analog field dynamics -- is fundamentally different from von Neumann digital computation and may be essential for the flexible, energy-efficient cognition that characterizes biological intelligence. Understanding oscillations as computation connects [[cortical-layer-oscillations]], [[gamma-oscillations]], [[alpha-oscillations]], [[cross-frequency-coupling]], and the [[canonical-eeg-bands]] into a unified functional framework.

## Biological Computationalism

Milinkovic and Aru (2026) argued that [[consciousness-and-anesthesia|consciousness]] requires specific computational properties found in biological systems but absent from digital computers. They identified two essential features: scale inseparability, where lower scales endogenously generate higher scales while higher scales simultaneously constrain lower scales through continuous real-time co-determination, and hybrid discrete-continuous computation, where the brain combines continuous-valued field propagations with discrete spike events. Oscillatory electric fields are not epiphenomenal but serve as "computational syntax" -- they encode when neural events matter and what they integrate with, making spiking "computationally legible." This framework implies that the specific frequency organization of [[canonical-eeg-bands]] provides the syntactic structure for neural computation, with each band defining a distinct temporal integration window. The brain achieves its computational power through metabolic optimization under energy constraints (2% of body mass, 20% of energy), refining how structure and function co-evolve rather than simply scaling up.

## Spatial Computing and Inhibitory Stencils

Chen et al. (2025) from the Miller lab demonstrated that [[alpha-oscillations]]/beta oscillations create spatially structured patterns across lateral prefrontal cortex that act as "inhibitory stencils," controlling where cognitive content can be expressed. Recording from non-human primates across three tasks (object working memory, sequence working memory, categorization), they showed that alpha/beta power encodes task context while spiking activity encodes stimulus content, with these signals spatially anti-correlated: where alpha/beta is high, spiking is suppressed; where alpha/beta is low, spiking can express task-relevant information. These spatial patterns change dynamically with task conditions, predict trial-by-trial behavioral choices, and when misattributed (wrong pattern for the task), predict errors. This adds a spatial dimension to oscillatory control -- beyond temporal gating (when) and frequency coupling (how), oscillations determine where information is processed, unifying cognition through structured population dynamics rather than fixed circuits.

## Ephaptic Coupling and Field-Neuron Interactions

Pinotsis and Miller (2025) provided empirical evidence that mesoscale electric fields actively shape neural activity through ephaptic coupling, creating a circular causality loop: neural activity generates extracellular fields, and these fields turn around to influence the neurons that generated them. Using a bidomain model and spatial Granger causality applied to macaque PFC recordings during working memory, they found that field-to-neuron interactions are one order of magnitude stronger than neuron-to-field interactions, and that ephaptic coupling strength correlates with cross-trial power variability (r up to 0.594, p < 10^-8). This asymmetry supports the slaving principle from Synergetics: slowly-evolving electric fields act as control parameters that enslave faster neural dynamics. The implication for [[cross-frequency-coupling]] is that fields provide a fast coordination mechanism beyond synaptic transmission, directly unifying neurons into information-representing ensembles and modifying physical properties of cells through "cytoelectric coupling."

## Traveling Waves and STDP

Sejnowski (2025) proposed a unified framework linking spike timing precision, cortical traveling waves, and spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP) to explain long-term [[working-memory]]. Traveling waves -- local oscillations in all frequency bands that extend spatially across cortex at 0.1-0.8 m/s via unmyelinated horizontal fibers -- carry more information than synchronous oscillations because they distribute information across both space and time. As a wave front passes through a cortical column, it triggers precisely timed inhibitory rebound spikes in pyramidal neurons via parvalbumin-positive basket cells, creating timing relationships within the STDP window (+/- 10 ms) that modify synaptic weights. This mechanism creates a "second-tier temporary network" of modified small-spine synapses overlaying the permanent sensorimotor network, lasting hours and supporting flexible cognition. Sleep spindles (10-14 Hz) serve as global circular traveling waves coordinating memory consolidation, with hippocampal [[sharp-wave-ripples]] (~100 Hz) triggering replay at 6x normal speed.

## Cortical Temporal Modes

Liu et al. (2026) used data-driven decomposition (COBE method) on MEG from 608 adults to identify six reproducible cortex-wide common oscillation modes, each with a distinct spatial topography and frequency-loading profile. These modes do not map cleanly onto traditional band definitions -- multiple modes overlap in the alpha-beta range, distinguished by their spatial patterns and microarchitectural correlates. Modes centered on attractor frequencies showed strong associations with neurotransmitter systems (serotonin, dopamine, GABA), while modes spanning boundary regions showed stronger associations with cortical geometry and structural features. The excitation-inhibition balance acts as a tuning mechanism: inhibition-dominant regions produce sharper, more stable oscillatory peaks at attractor frequencies, while excitation-dominant regions settle near boundary frequencies. This provides a biophysical mechanism for how [[cortical-layer-oscillations]] generate the specific frequency modes observed in EEG, with Layer 4 thickness correlating with alpha-range modes (thalamocortical input) and deep layers associating with slower frequencies (long-range feedback).

## Energy Efficiency and Neuromorphic Implications

Chiel et al. (2025) argued that the brain's ~20 W power consumption -- over one million-fold less than the largest supercomputers -- reflects fundamental architectural principles that current AI does not exploit. The brain achieves energy efficiency through sparse bursty firing patterns, neuromodulatory powering-down of unused circuits, traveling wave dynamics for multiplexed signaling, and oscillatory gating that routes information without requiring dense all-to-all connectivity. The phi-ratio spacing of [[canonical-eeg-bands]] may represent the thermodynamically optimal solution for multi-band oscillatory computation under energy constraints, since golden-ratio frequency spacing uniquely maximizes the distance from rational number resonances, minimizing cross-frequency interference while enabling efficient coupling at band boundaries. This positions oscillatory frequency architecture not just as a descriptive observation but as a principled prediction from energy-efficiency constraints on biological computation.

Jamadar et al. (2025) provided a comprehensive review of the metabolic costs of cognition, revealing the striking finding that goal-directed cognitive activity increases total brain energy expenditure by only ~5% above the ongoing costs of resting neural activity and homeostasis. This paradoxically small cognitive premium underscores how deeply oscillatory gating and sparse coding are embedded in the brain's computational architecture -- rather than activating entirely new circuits, cognition largely redirects existing metabolic flows through oscillatory phase-gating. Disruptions of neural energy homeostasis are associated with cognitive decline and neurodegeneration, suggesting that oscillatory architecture serves a neuroprotective function in addition to a computational one. A complementary 2026 preprint demonstrated that **neuronal degeneracy** -- the observed high variability of neuronal parameters -- reflects structured trade-offs along a Pareto-optimal frontier between energy cost and information transmission, with metabolic constraints sculpting cortical firing rate distributions and coding strategies in a context- and cell-type-specific manner (bioRxiv, 2026). Together, these findings frame oscillatory computation not as energetically expensive coordination overhead but as the mechanism through which the brain achieves its extraordinary efficiency: oscillations route information where it is needed, suppress activity where it is not, and thereby keep cognitive costs marginal relative to housekeeping metabolism.

## The Information Bottleneck of Conscious Processing

Zheng and Meister (2024) brought renewed attention to a deep puzzle in neural information theory: while sensory systems gather data at ~10⁹ bits/s, the information throughput of conscious human behavior is only ~10 bits/s. This six-order-of-magnitude compression implies that the brain operates in two fundamentally different modes -- an **"outer brain"** that handles fast, high-dimensional sensory and motor signals through parallel processing, and an **"inner brain"** that processes the reduced few bits needed to control deliberate behavior through what appears to be a single serial channel. The oscillatory architecture described throughout this article may play a central role in this bottleneck: [[alpha-oscillations]] suppress irrelevant sensory streams (inhibitory stencils), [[cross-frequency-coupling]] selects which information passes from fast gamma representations to slower integrative timescales, and traveling waves serialize information flow across cortical space. The ~10 bits/s behavioral rate aligns remarkably well with the information capacity of a single [[theta-oscillations|theta]] cycle (~4-8 Hz) encoding 1-2 bits per cycle (Amil et al., 2024), suggesting that the theta rhythm may define the fundamental clock rate of conscious cognition.

## Phase Coding and Theta Optimization

Amil, Albesa-González, and Verschure (2024) proposed a theoretical framework demonstrating that [[hippocampal-memory|hippocampal]] theta oscillations (3-8 Hz) represent an evolutionary optimization of phase coding under noise constraints. Neurons using oscillations to rhythmically sample noisy signals face an inherent **speed-precision trade-off**: higher oscillation frequencies allow faster input sampling but reduce encoding precision because each cycle affords less time for accurate phase estimation. With physiologically realistic noise levels, this trade-off yields a maximum information rate of ~1-2 bits/s specifically within the theta band, confining the optimal oscillation frequency to the low end of the spectrum. This framework accounts for key hippocampal features, including the preservation of the theta band along the dorsoventral axis despite physiological gradients, and the modulation of theta frequency and amplitude by running speed. The analysis further suggests that theta-band phase coding could support efficient stimulus encoding in visual cortex and olfactory bulb, extending the computational principle beyond the hippocampus. In a complementary finding, Vieira et al. (2024) demonstrated that exogenous alternating-current stimulation induces **phase precession** -- the gradual shifting of preferred spiking phase relative to the local field potential -- in ~30% of entrained neocortical neurons, with multiscale modeling implicating NMDA-mediated synaptic plasticity as the driving mechanism. This provides causal evidence that phase precession is a global neocortical phenomenon, not limited to hippocampal place cells, reinforcing the view that phase-relative timing is a universal computational code.

## Communication Through Coherence: Scope and Limitations

The **communication through coherence** (CTC) hypothesis proposes that interareal information transfer depends on the phase alignment of oscillatory activity between sender and receiver populations, such that input volleys arrive at the postsynaptic population's peak excitability (Fries, 2015). Greenwood and Ward (2024) provided a rigorous computational evaluation of CTC's scope and limitations, showing that alpha-band (~10 Hz) oscillations originating in the [[thalamocortical-circuits|pulvinar nucleus of the thalamus]] are sent to communicating cortical areas, organizing [[gamma-oscillations]] there and facilitating phase coherence contingent on control signals from higher-level cortical areas to the thalamic reticular nucleus. Their modeling demonstrated that while CTC can account for several effects of top-down and bottom-up [[attention-and-perception|attentional selection]], its explanatory reach has limits -- in some conditions, oscillatory coherence appears to be a consequence of communication rather than its cause. This suggests that CTC and its inverse ("communication causes coherence") may operate in parallel, with the dominant direction depending on task context and the specific cortical hierarchy involved. The finding that both 10 Hz and 40 Hz interactions can enhance coherence and information transmission between modeled cortical areas supports the multi-band nature of oscillatory routing, consistent with the hierarchical organization of [[canonical-eeg-bands]] where different frequencies mediate feedback versus feedforward signaling.

## Traveling Waves: New Computational Principles

Recent work has substantially expanded the understanding of cortical traveling waves beyond the STDP framework described above. Dugué and Chavane (2025) proposed a unifying taxonomy distinguishing **first-order traveling waves**, which result directly from the propagation of activity between neurons via axonal connections, from **second-order traveling waves**, which emerge from higher-order interactions among neuronal populations. Despite arising from different generative mechanisms, both types subserve a canonical computation: ordering neuronal processing in space and time to impose a **computational syntax**. First-order waves propagate at speeds determined by axonal conduction velocity (unaffected by stimulus conditions), while second-order waves involve complex interactions of first-order waves emitted at each oscillation cycle, producing patterns that vary with stimulus parameters. This framework bridges traditionally separate views linking oscillatory dynamics, transient events, and broader network-level phenomena.

Davis et al. (2024) demonstrated that horizontal cortical connections -- the patchy, long-range fibers present in V1, V2, MT, IT, PFC, and motor cortex -- shape intrinsic traveling waves into **feature-selective motifs** that regulate perceptual sensitivity. In marmoset area MT, many traveling wave motifs exhibited direction-selective modulations of ongoing spiking activity, and when a visual target matched the preference of the active motif, both neural gain and perceptual detection performance increased. A spiking network model incorporating feature-selective patchy connections reproduced these motifs as emergent consequences of excitatory/inhibitory balance shifts as action potentials traverse the horizontal fiber network. This adds a selectivity dimension to traveling wave computation: waves do not merely propagate information indiscriminately but are sculpted by anatomical connectivity into content-specific patterns.

Koller, Schirner, and Ritter (2024) demonstrated that the direction and frequency characteristics of cortical traveling waves are shaped by **connectome topology**. Structural connectivity instrength gradients -- the gradually varying sum of incoming connection strengths across cortex -- direct traveling waves and shape frequency gradients, with alpha and beta band waves propagating in opposing directions along these gradients. Their cortical network model fit resting-state MEG functional connectivity best in a regime where instrength-directed traveling waves and frequency gradients naturally emerged, providing a structural basis for the diverse wave patterns observed in human neuroimaging.

Halgren et al. (2025) showed that traveling waves directly link human visual and frontal cortex during working memory-guided behavior. Using EEG, they identified bidirectional traveling waves -- a feedforward theta wave emerging after a response probe whose latency predicted response time, and a feedback wave linking frontal areas back to sensory cortex. These waves were strong predictors of both intra- and interindividual differences in behavioral timing, establishing that traveling waves serve as a functional communication mechanism for [[hippocampal-memory|working memory]], not merely an epiphenomenon of spatially distributed oscillations.

## Ephaptic Coupling: Expanding Evidence

Beyond the foundational work of Pinotsis and Miller (2025) described above, several recent studies have extended the evidence for ephaptic coupling as a computational mechanism. Vieira et al. (2024) showed that externally applied electric fields can induce phase precession through NMDA-mediated plasticity, demonstrating that field-neuron interactions are not only modulatory but can drive lasting changes in spike-timing relationships -- a prerequisite for ephaptic contributions to learning and memory. A 2025 study using printed human neural circuits with single-cell precision provided direct empirical validation of ephaptic coupling predictions: bringing axons into close physical contact produced measurable reductions in action potential velocity, increased activity synchronization, and lowered stimulation thresholds (Bhatt et al., 2025). These in vitro findings with reproducible circuit geometries confirm theoretical predictions about how endogenous electric fields of even a few neurons exhibit signatures of ephaptic coupling. Additionally, a 2026 computational study applied fully coupled finite-element methods to detailed Purkinje and layer 5 pyramidal neuron geometries, systematically characterizing how ephaptic coupling strength varies with stimulation strength, distance, frequency, and extracellular conductivity (Frontiers in Computational Neuroscience, 2026). Together, these findings strengthen the case that ephaptic coupling provides a fast, non-synaptic coordination mechanism that contributes to the brain's computational repertoire alongside synaptic transmission and gap junctions.

## Oscillatory Computation in Artificial Systems

The principles of oscillatory brain computation are increasingly being translated into artificial intelligence architectures, creating a bidirectional exchange between neuroscience and machine learning. Rusch and Rus (2025) introduced **Linear Oscillatory State-Space models (LinOSS)**, inspired by cortical dynamics and based on forced harmonic oscillators. LinOSS uses stable discretization integrated via fast associative parallel scans, is provably universal (capable of approximating any continuous causal operator between time-varying functions), and outperforms leading sequence models including Mamba and LRU by nearly 2× on sequences of length 50,000. The architecture was selected for an oral presentation at ICLR 2025 -- an honor awarded to the top 1% of submissions -- underscoring the power of oscillatory principles for long-range temporal processing.

The **Deep Oscillatory Neural Network (DONN)** architecture (Soman et al., 2025) takes a more directly biophysical approach, incorporating neural Hopf oscillators operating in the complex domain alongside traditional sigmoid and ReLU neurons, all using complex-valued weights and activations. Input signals can drive oscillators via resonance, amplitude modulation, or frequency modulation, and training proceeds via complex backpropagation. The complex-valued representation encodes both amplitude and phase information simultaneously, providing a richer representational space than binary spike events. Extensions to convolutional architectures (Oscillatory Convolutional Neural Networks) demonstrate competitive or improved performance on signal and image processing benchmarks, and recent applications model whole-brain sleep EEG dynamics.

Zhu et al. (2025) developed **HoloBrain/HoloGraph**, a framework that models oscillatory synchronization via the Kuramoto model to overcome limitations of conventional graph neural networks. Rather than the heat-diffusion paradigm underlying standard message-passing, HoloGraph treats graph nodes as coupled oscillators whose synchronization patterns encode relational structure. The approach incorporates system-level attention driven by historical activity -- analogous to thalamocortical attentional gating -- and achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple graph benchmarks while also showing clinical promise for early diagnosis of neurodegenerative diseases.

In neuromorphic hardware, the **Rhythm-SNN** framework (Wang et al., 2025) draws directly on brain oscillation mechanisms by employing heterogeneous oscillatory signals to modulate spiking neurons, enforcing periodic activation at distinct frequencies. This approach significantly reduces neuronal firing rates while enhancing temporal processing capability and robustness, achieving over two orders of magnitude energy reduction compared to deep learning solutions in the Intel Neuromorphic Deep Noise Suppression Challenge while delivering award-winning denoising performance. The convergence of these diverse approaches -- from state-space models to graph networks to neuromorphic chips -- suggests that oscillatory dynamics represent a fundamental computational principle whose value extends well beyond biological neural systems.
