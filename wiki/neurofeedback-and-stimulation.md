# Neurofeedback and Brain Stimulation

Neurofeedback and non-invasive brain stimulation techniques -- including tACS, tDCS, oscillatory tDCS (otDCS), transcranial temporal interference stimulation (tTIS), transcranial focused ultrasound (tFUS), binaural beat stimulation, and sensory entrainment -- represent a growing family of approaches for modulating brain oscillations at specific frequencies. The evidence reveals that individual frequency matching consistently outperforms fixed-frequency protocols, that different stimulation modalities interact with different cognitive abilities, and that combining multiple neuromodulation approaches can produce interference rather than synergy, underscoring the need for carefully designed frequency-targeting strategies. A 2024 systematic review of tACS for neuropsychiatric disorders spanning 53 published studies and 70 clinical trials found positive outcomes for neurocognitive disorders (all 11 studies), psychotic disorders (11 of 14 studies), and depressive disorders (7 of 8 studies), with gamma-frequency stimulation over the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex emerging as the most consistently effective protocol (Khatoun et al., 2024). Meanwhile, the field is shifting decisively toward personalized, closed-loop approaches informed by individual oscillatory biomarkers, real-time brain state estimation, and even patient-specific anatomical modeling for deep-brain targets (Bergmann et al., 2024).

## Transcranial Electrical Stimulation and Memory

Bjekic et al. (2026) compared tDCS, tACS, and otDCS effects on associative memory (N = 42) using individualized theta frequency (ITF; mean 5.18 Hz). Only otDCS -- which combines tonic DC offset with oscillatory theta -- produced significant group-level improvement in recognition sensitivity (d' increase, effect size 0.12). The study's most important finding was that individual cognitive profiles strongly moderate stimulation effects: processing speed drives a "magnification" pattern (high performers benefit more from tDCS), while mnemonic binding drives a "compensation" pattern (low performers benefit more across all modalities). This explains much of the inconsistency in the tES literature and suggests that [[clinical-applications]] of stimulation should be informed by cognitive profiling. The superiority of otDCS aligns with the idea that simultaneous modulation of baseline excitability and oscillatory dynamics better engages the [[alpha-oscillations]] and [[beta-oscillations]] architecture.

## Alpha Neurofeedback and Binaural Beats

Bazanova et al. (2026) tested combined alpha-EEG/EMG neurofeedback (NFT) with individualized binaural beat stimulation (BBS) in a rigorous crossover design (N = 22). BBS alone increased alpha power (+4.06 uV^2) and [[individual-alpha-frequency]] (+0.07 Hz), while NFT alone increased alpha power (+5.62 uV^2). However, combining NFT with BBS paradoxically decreased both alpha power and iAPF while increasing muscle tension, revealing a systematic "dual-controller interference" effect (interaction effect sizes 0.55-0.76). This demonstrates that the brain's oscillatory self-regulation system and externally driven entrainment compete rather than cooperate, with important implications for protocol design in [[clinical-applications]].

## Sensory Entrainment and Memory

Ostrowski et al. (2025) used visual sensory entrainment (N = 105) to demonstrate that pre-stimulus [[alpha-oscillations]] entrainment at 9 Hz causally enhances crossmodal associative memory (d' = 1.46 vs 1.18 control; BF = 3.29), while theta entrainment at 5 Hz showed no behavioral effect despite successful neural modulation. Hit rates increased from 49.8% to 61.5% with alpha entrainment (BF = 22.57). This functional dissociation suggests alpha serves as a gating mechanism for sensory preparation during encoding, while [[theta-oscillations]] may require active engagement during stimulus processing rather than mere preparatory enhancement. The findings support [[neural-resonance-and-entrainment]] models where frequency-specific oscillatory states determine cognitive outcomes.

## Frequency-Specific Entrainment Evidence

A comprehensive review of neuromodulation literature reveals that the Arnold tongue -- the triangular region in amplitude-frequency space where entrainment occurs -- has been empirically confirmed in awake ferrets, with synchronization maps centered on each animal's endogenous alpha frequency. Resonance phenomena at 10, 20, 40, and 80 Hz have been documented, but individual frequency matching consistently outperforms fixed frequencies. A study of 79 participants found IAF-matched tACS produced significantly greater alpha entrainment (p = 0.001) than standardized 10 Hz stimulation. At 20 Hz, [[beta-oscillations]] tACS reliably increases [[motor-control]] cortex excitability, while 40 Hz [[gamma-oscillations]] stimulation shows special neuroprotective properties not replicated at adjacent frequencies. Cross-frequency effects are bidirectional: 40 Hz gamma stimulation decreases alpha power, while theta-gamma coupled tACS suppresses alpha while enhancing theta.

## Motor Neurofeedback

Onagawa et al. (2023) meta-analyzed 33 neurofeedback studies targeting [[motor-control]] performance. EEG-based NFT showed significant effects (SMD = 0.74, p = .031), with the RCT subgroup (16 RCTs, 374 subjects) showing a larger effect (SMD = 0.85). A critical dose-response relationship emerged: cumulative training time explains approximately 50% of variance in motor improvement, and a threshold of 125 minutes of total training is needed for reliable effects. Interventions exceeding one week show larger effect sizes (SMD = 1.23, p = .043). The most commonly targeted bands were SMR (12-15 Hz), [[alpha-oscillations]] (8-13 Hz), and [[beta-oscillations]] (15-30 Hz).

## Deep Brain Stimulation via Temporal Interference

Wen et al. (2025) demonstrated that theta-band (5 Hz) transcranial temporal interference stimulation (tTIS) targeting the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex can enhance prediction error processing. Using carrier frequencies of 2000 and 2005 Hz to create a 5 Hz envelope, the stimulation increased feedback-related negativity amplitude (d = 0.82) specifically for food-related prediction errors in individuals with food addiction. This represents a novel approach for reaching deep brain structures with frequency-specific neuromodulation, expanding the toolkit beyond surface-level tACS and tDCS.

A comprehensive 2025 review of tTIS mapped the trajectory of the field from its foundational proof-of-concept in mice to expanding human applications (Zhu et al., 2025). By December 2024, human applications of tTIS had been documented in systematic review, with studies demonstrating precise targeting of the hippocampus -- where tTIS suppressed epileptic biomarkers in patients with epilepsy (Violante et al., 2023) -- and the basal ganglia for regulation of eye movements (Li et al., 2025). The technique's ability to reach structures 4-6 cm below the scalp without stimulating overlying cortex gives it a unique advantage over conventional tACS and tDCS, which are limited to superficial cortical targets. Computational modeling and electrode-array optimization have advanced rapidly, with a 2026 bioRxiv preprint demonstrating the feasibility of multi-electrode tTIS arrays for shaping interference envelopes with millimeter precision in realistic head models.

## Transcranial Focused Ultrasound Neuromodulation

**Transcranial focused ultrasound** (tFUS) has emerged as a potent non-invasive neuromodulation technology offering spatial precision of 1-5 mm -- an order of magnitude finer than TMS (3-5 cm) or tDCS (5-7 cm) -- while retaining the ability to reach deep subcortical structures. In a landmark 2025 study, Fonzo et al. (2025) conducted a double-blind, sham-controlled trial (N = 47) targeting the left amygdala with low-intensity focused ultrasound at 10 Hz pulse repetition frequency. Active treatment significantly reduced amygdala reactivity to emotional stimuli compared to sham, and a subsequent unblinded clinical trial (N = 24, 15 daily sessions over three weeks) produced significant symptom reduction on the Mood and Anxiety Symptom Questionnaire General Distress subscale (p = 0.001, Cohen's d = 0.77) across patients with major depression, generalized anxiety, PTSD, and panic disorder.

Perhaps the most striking recent advance is **metamaterial-based transcranial ultrasound stimulation** (mTUS). Attali et al. (2025) developed patient-specific acoustic metamaterials ("metalenses") that correct for skull-induced ultrasound aberrations, enabling precise focusing on deep targets personalized via diffusion tensor MRI tractography. In five patients with severe treatment-resistant depression, an intensive 5-day course (25 sessions of 5 minutes each) targeting the subcallosal cingulate reduced depression severity by an average of 60.9% (range 30-84%), with four of five patients achieving treatment response and two reaching remission. No serious adverse events occurred. This first-in-human evidence demonstrates that personalized deep-brain ultrasound can achieve clinically meaningful effects in treatment-resistant populations without surgery. Additionally, holographic tFUS techniques now allow direct control of stimulated volume and cooperative recruitment of distributed circuits, lowering activation thresholds by approximately an order of magnitude (Nature Biomedical Engineering, 2025).

A 2026 study further extended tFUS to stroke rehabilitation, examining safety and neuromodulation effects for motor recovery in patients with subacute stroke, and a parallel line of research has applied individualized tFUS to the basal ganglia for [[motor-control]] modulation using MRI-guided targeting (Deffieux et al., 2025). The convergence of AI-powered targeting, patient-specific skull modeling, and real-time monitoring positions tFUS as a potentially transformative tool for both psychiatric and neurological [[clinical-applications]].

## tACS for Neuropsychiatric Disorders

A 2024 systematic review screening 783 studies and 373 clinical trials identified gamma-frequency tACS as the dominant protocol, used in 58.5% of published studies and 52% of registered trials, typically delivered at 2 mA peak-to-peak over the DLPFC for 20 or fewer sessions (Khatoun et al., 2024). A separate meta-analysis of 102 studies encompassing 2,893 individuals found that tACS improved working memory, long-term memory, attention, executive control, and fluid intelligence across healthy, aging, and psychiatric populations (Reinhart et al., 2023). For schizophrenia, tACS has been shown to decrease auditory hallucinations and negative symptoms, with the most consistent cognitive improvements following gamma-frequency stimulation. For depressive disorders, 7 of 8 published studies reported symptom improvement. Seventeen studies documented measurable alterations in EEG power spectra around the entrained frequency band, providing neurophysiological evidence that tACS engages its intended oscillatory targets.

Theta-frequency tACS has shown particular promise for [[hippocampal-memory]] and motor learning. A 2024 study demonstrated that 5 Hz tACS selectively enhanced early-phase motor sequence learning through cerebellar modulation, with improved reaction times compared to both 50 Hz and sham conditions (Bhatt et al., 2024). This frequency specificity -- theta for learning, gamma for cognition, [[beta-oscillations|beta]] for [[motor-control|motor excitability]] -- reinforces the idea that tACS efficacy depends on matching stimulation frequency to the endogenous oscillatory architecture of the targeted function.

## tDCS Effects on Oscillatory Dynamics

Beyond the otDCS findings of Bjekic et al. (2026) described above, recent research has clarified how tDCS reshapes the oscillatory landscape. In patients with ischemic stroke undergoing neurorehabilitation, anodal tDCS over the affected hemisphere reduced [[delta-oscillations|delta]] relative power, enhanced [[alpha-oscillations|alpha]] band oscillations, and improved EEG complexity indices -- changes that correlated with functional motor recovery (Zhang et al., 2025). A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of home-based tDCS for depression found significant efficacy across randomized controlled trials, supporting the scalability of tDCS for remote treatment delivery. However, a meta-analysis of older adults revealed substantially increased response variability compared to younger populations, highlighting the need for age-stratified dosing protocols and potentially explaining inconsistencies in the broader tDCS literature (Summers et al., 2025). The interaction between tDCS and individual cognitive profiles -- the "magnification" and "compensation" patterns identified by Bjekic et al. (2026) -- appears to generalize beyond memory to executive function domains as well, reinforcing the case for cognitive profiling before stimulation.

## Closed-Loop and Brain-State-Dependent Stimulation

The most significant paradigm shift in brain stimulation is the move from open-loop protocols to **closed-loop systems** that adapt stimulation in real time based on ongoing neural activity. In EEG-triggered TMS, pulses delivered at the trough of the sensorimotor mu rhythm (~10 Hz) increase corticospinal excitability, while pulses at the peak decrease it -- a phase-dependent effect that open-loop stimulation cannot exploit (Zrenner et al., 2024). The open-source NeuroSimo platform, released in 2025, enables arbitrary EEG-TMS protocols in Python with a median timing error of just 0.2 ms, making phase-specific stimulation accessible to the broader research community.

The FDA approved the first commercially available **adaptive deep brain stimulation** (aDBS) system in February 2025 -- Medtronic's Percept RC neurostimulator with BrainSense technology -- which adjusts stimulation parameters in real time based on neural biomarkers detected from implanted electrodes. Clinicians identify each patient's optimal frequency-band biomarkers by testing different bands and brain states, then program the device to modulate stimulation intensity accordingly. This represents a fundamental shift from fixed-parameter DBS to responsive neuromodulation that tracks moment-to-moment changes in [[thalamocortical-circuits|thalamocortical]] dynamics.

A broader framework for personalized stimulation has been articulated around four dimensions of individualization: context (cognitive state during stimulation), target (brain region selection via neuroimaging), dose (intensity and frequency matching), and timing (phase-locked or state-dependent delivery) (Bhatt et al., 2025). The convergence of real-time EEG, fMRI-guided targeting, and AI-driven optimization algorithms is rapidly making this four-dimensional personalization feasible in clinical settings.

## Real-Time fMRI Neurofeedback

**Real-time fMRI neurofeedback** (rt-fMRI-NF) complements EEG-based approaches by offering superior spatial resolution for training volitional control over specific brain regions and circuits. A Phase 3 NIH-funded clinical trial targeting large-scale networks in depression with rt-fMRI-NF began enrollment in 2024, with estimated completion in 2026. For schizophrenia with medication-resistant auditory hallucinations, a randomized sham-controlled trial (N = 23) demonstrated that real neurofeedback produced greater reductions in secondary auditory cortex activation and altered connectivity between auditory cortex and cognitive control regions compared to sham (Diaz-Ponce et al., 2025). A separate double-blind trial (N = 61) showed that rt-fMRI-NF targeting the ventromedial prefrontal cortex, combined with positive episodic future thinking, enabled rapid acquisition of regulatory control over this motivation-related region (Wager et al., 2025).

Hybrid EEG-fMRI neurofeedback represents an emerging frontier. A 2024 meta-analysis of neurofeedback for PTSD found that fMRI-guided protocols drove the largest effect sizes, with PCL-5 scores improving by 14.95 points at follow-up (p < 0.0001) -- substantially larger than effects seen with EEG-only neurofeedback (Chiba et al., 2024). The combination of fMRI's spatial precision with EEG's temporal resolution may yield neurofeedback systems that can simultaneously target the right region and the right oscillatory state.

## Neurofeedback for Clinical Populations

### ADHD

The largest and most rigorous meta-analysis to date -- a 2025 JAMA Psychiatry systematic review of 38 randomized controlled trials encompassing 2,472 participants -- found that neurofeedback did not produce significant improvements in ADHD symptoms on probably-blinded outcome measures (total symptoms SMD = 0.04, p = 0.56; inattention SMD = 0.04, p = 0.69; hyperactivity/impulsivity SMD = 0.03, p = 0.59) (Westwood et al., 2025). Standard EEG neurofeedback protocols showed a small but statistically significant effect (SMD = 0.21), though the authors noted this "likely falls short of clinical value." Processing speed was the sole neuropsychological domain showing significant improvement (SMD = 0.35). A 2024 network meta-analysis of 13 studies with 1,370 children found that most neurofeedback therapies surpassed placebo, though effect sizes were modest (Wu et al., 2024). These findings suggest that while neurofeedback may engage relevant neural circuits, its clinical utility for ADHD remains uncertain at the group level, and future efforts should focus on identifying responder subgroups through precision-medicine approaches.

### PTSD

A 2024 meta-analysis of 17 studies (628 patients, 10 in quantitative synthesis) found clinically meaningful effects of neurofeedback on PTSD symptoms, with CAPS-5 scores improving by 7.01 points (p = 0.02) and PCL-5 scores by 7.14 points (p = 0.0006) (Chiba et al., 2024). Notably, effect sizes were larger at follow-up than immediately post-treatment (PCL-5 MD = 14.95 at follow-up), suggesting that neurofeedback may initiate neuroplastic processes that continue after training ends. Modulation of [[alpha-oscillations]] and connectivity within the [[default-mode-network]] and salience network correlated with symptom reduction. However, studies using passive controls showed larger effects than those using active controls, indicating that non-specific factors contribute to observed improvements.

### Depression

A comprehensive meta-analysis of 22 neurofeedback studies for depression found a significant effect on depressive symptoms (Hedges' g = -0.600), with concurrent neurophysiological changes (Trambaiolli et al., 2024). The field has moved beyond simple frontal alpha asymmetry training -- a 2025 meta-analysis found that resting frontal alpha asymmetry has limited diagnostic value for major depressive disorder, with only a small effect size distinguishing patients from controls (Smith et al., 2025). This suggests that more sophisticated neurofeedback targets, such as [[cross-frequency-coupling]] patterns or network-level connectivity, may be needed to improve clinical outcomes. rt-fMRI neurofeedback targeting the amygdala and prefrontal circuits has shown particular promise, with the fMRI-guided approach enabling access to subcortical structures implicated in emotion regulation.

## Brain-Computer Interfaces and Oscillatory Control

EEG-based **brain-computer interfaces** (BCIs) rely fundamentally on oscillatory features -- particularly [[alpha-oscillations|mu/alpha]] and [[beta-oscillations|beta]] event-related desynchronization (ERD) during motor imagery -- to decode user intentions. Recent advances in high-density electrode arrays and deep learning algorithms have narrowed the performance gap between laboratory and real-world BCI applications, achieving sufficient accuracy for wheelchair control and virtual keyboard operation. In 2025, a Nature Communications study demonstrated real-time robotic hand control at the individual finger level using non-invasive EEG motor imagery, representing a significant advance in decoding resolution. Sensorimotor rhythms (SMRs) remain the dominant control signal, with persistent modulation of alpha (~10 Hz) and beta (~20 Hz) rhythms throughout imagined movement aligning well with continuous decoding tasks. The integration of reinforcement learning algorithms into BCI-driven neurorehabilitation has emerged as a promising approach for adaptive training that optimizes feedback in real time based on individual learning trajectories.

## Sensory Entrainment for Neurodegeneration

The 40 Hz **gamma sensory entrainment** (GENUS) paradigm, pioneered by Tsai and colleagues at MIT, has advanced substantially from its initial mouse-model demonstrations toward human clinical translation. After establishing that 40 Hz audiovisual stimulation reduces amyloid-beta deposits, tau phosphorylation, neuron death, and synapse loss in Alzheimer's mouse models via glymphatic clearance mechanisms, several human studies have now been completed. A Phase I/II trial demonstrated that after 3 months of daily 40 Hz audiovisual stimulation, treated participants showed less ventricular dilation and hippocampal atrophy, increased functional connectivity in the [[default-mode-network]], improved memory test performance, and better daily activity rhythmicity compared to sham controls (Chan et al., 2025).

An open-label extension study tracking five volunteers over approximately two years found that long-term 40 Hz stimulation was safe and feasible, but outcomes diverged sharply by disease subtype (Chan et al., 2025). Three female participants with late-onset Alzheimer's disease showed improvement or slower decline on most cognitive measures, retained strong EEG entrainment at 30 months, and exhibited remarkable reductions in phosphorylated tau (pTau217) -- 47% in one patient and 19.4% in another -- a significant decline in an FDA-approved Alzheimer's biomarker. However, two male participants with early-onset Alzheimer's showed no significant benefit and progressively lost EEG responsiveness to the stimulation. This divergence suggests that the brain's capacity for [[neural-resonance-and-entrainment|entrainment]] may itself be a biomarker of treatment suitability, and that [[gamma-oscillations]] stimulation may be more effective in sporadic late-onset disease where residual oscillatory infrastructure remains intact. A Phase III national clinical trial is currently underway to evaluate 40 Hz GENUS in a larger, more diverse sample. A 2025 study in Communications Biology further demonstrated that visual 40 Hz stimulation induces gamma oscillations in the human hippocampus and alters phase synchrony, confirming that non-invasive sensory stimulation can modulate deep [[hippocampal-memory|hippocampal]] circuits.

## Binaural Beats: Refined Understanding

Beyond the dual-controller interference findings of Bazanova et al. (2026), recent research has both expanded the clinical applications of binaural beats and clarified the boundary conditions for their efficacy. A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of 14 trials with 1,047 patients found that binaural beats significantly reduced perioperative anxiety, postoperative pain (5 trials, 433 patients), systolic blood pressure, and heart rate compared to blank audio controls -- and notably outperformed non-binaural-beats audio on anxiety and pain measures (Garcia et al., 2025). A 2025 study on working memory found that binaural beats enhanced recall via increased [[theta-oscillations|theta]]-band information segregation in frontal regions, consistent with the role of theta in [[working-memory]] maintenance. A pilot study of personalized alpha-frequency binaural beats for insomnia (N = 20) found that 70% of participants with moderate-to-severe insomnia met treatment-response criteria after four weeks (Martinez et al., 2025).

However, the evidence remains inconsistent across domains. A 2025 study comparing alpha binaural beats with white noise found distinct neural mechanisms: binaural beats reliably entrained brain rhythms at the beat frequency, but performance benefits were highly dependent on carrier tone, masking noise, and timing parameters (Kim et al., 2025). The emerging consensus is that binaural beats are a genuine neuromodulatory tool -- the brain does entrain to the beat frequency -- but their cognitive and clinical effects are highly parameter-dependent and likely mediated by [[individual-alpha-frequency|individual frequency]] matching, underscoring the same theme of personalization that runs through the broader stimulation literature.
