# Motor Control

The motor system relies on oscillatory dynamics -- particularly in the [[beta-oscillations]] band (13-30 Hz) -- to coordinate movement planning, execution, and sensory feedback integration across the sensorimotor cortex. Beta oscillations in motor cortex exhibit a characteristic pattern of desynchronization before and during movement (beta ERD) followed by post-movement rebound (beta ERS), and this cycle is now understood to be non-focal, propagating across somatotopic regions. Corticomuscular coherence (CMC) in both alpha and beta bands links cortical oscillations directly to peripheral motor output, with frequency-specific roles: alpha-band coherence predicts reaction time while beta-band coherence predicts movement accuracy. [[gamma-oscillations|Gamma-band]] activity contributes through movement-related gamma synchronization (MRGS) and cross-frequency coupling between low-gamma phase and high-gamma amplitude in precentral cortices (Nie et al., 2024). The cerebellum shapes motor kinematics by encoding dynamic motor frequencies with cross-individual precision, while cerebellar-cortical [[beta-oscillations|beta]] coupling builds up as a predictive signal stabilizing behavioral performance across trials. Disruptions to these oscillatory mechanisms underlie motor symptoms in Parkinson's disease (see [[corticostriatal-learning]] for basal ganglia circuit details) and essential tremor, and are targets for [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]] interventions including frequency-specific transcranial stimulation.

## Beta Desynchronization and Rebound

Movement-related beta dynamics follow a stereotyped temporal pattern. Before and during voluntary movement, beta power suppresses (ERD) across the sensorimotor cortex. Georgiev et al. (2026) demonstrated that this beta ERD is **non-focal** -- suppression in one somatotopic area (e.g., brachium) spreads to adjacent regions (e.g., hand), reducing corticomuscular coherence with muscles not directly involved in the movement. This was shown using a dual-task paradigm combining arm rotation with sustained finger contraction, where CMC with the finger muscle was suppressed during arm movement (Cohen's d = 0.55-0.80). Following movement, beta power rebounds (ERS), typically peaking 1-2 seconds post-movement. The non-focal nature of beta ERD has implications for understanding how the motor cortex manages simultaneous actions and for interpreting [[beta-oscillations]] changes in [[clinical-applications]] such as Parkinson's disease.

The post-movement beta rebound (PMBR) is now recognized as functionally significant beyond a passive return to baseline. Zhang et al. (2024) demonstrated that larger PMBR prolongs the reaction time for subsequent movements, mediated through **GABA_A inhibition** -- short-interval intracortical inhibition (SICI) increased at the beta rebound peak compared to baseline and was negatively correlated with PMBR magnitude, while GABA_B-related long-interval intracortical inhibition showed no change. This establishes the PMBR as an active inhibitory state that constrains motor readiness, connecting beta dynamics to the [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations|GABAergic basis]] of sensorimotor rhythms.

PMBR is also modulated by movement speed and interhemispheric interactions. Zhang et al. (2025) combined TMS and EEG to show that **ballistic movements** produce stronger PMBR than self-paced movements in both contralateral and ipsilateral motor cortices. During PMBR, interhemispheric coherence was substantially elevated for ballistic (0.43 ± 0.08) versus self-paced (0.24 ± 0.07) conditions, and interhemispheric inhibition (IHI) from ipsilateral to contralateral motor cortex was significantly stronger at PMBR peak. Critically, **directed coherence reversed** between movement phases: during beta ERD, information flow was predominantly from contralateral to ipsilateral motor cortex, but during PMBR the causal direction shifted from ipsilateral to contralateral, suggesting the PMBR period involves active recalibration of interhemispheric motor balance.

Sisti et al. (2024) provided the first demonstration of PMBR during **imagined hand movement** of dial rotation (n = 21). PMBR was significantly stronger in actual versus imagined movement (F = 27.47, p < .001 for unimanual conditions), yet was clearly present even during motor imagery -- a finding with implications for motor imagery-based brain-computer interfaces. The PMBR was more pronounced in unimanual than bimanual coordination patterns, though PMBR magnitude did not correlate with performance improvement in either coordination pattern.

As a clinical biomarker, PMBR shows promise for tracking motor recovery. Biasiucci et al. (2025) evaluated PMBR in 34 chronic stroke patients and 16 healthy controls during voluntary and exoskeleton-assisted movement, finding that severely impaired patients lacked a PMBR dipole in the ipsilesional hemisphere while moderately impaired patients showed diminished responses. The **PMBR/ERD ratio** proved more reliable than ERD alone as a longitudinal biomarker, as ERD showed minimal task-specific variation across 30 rehabilitation sessions.

## Corticomuscular Coherence

Tomassini et al. (2026) provided compelling evidence that alpha and beta CMC serve **functionally distinct roles** in visuomotor control. During an isometric tracking task with unpredictable perturbations, alpha-band coherence (8-13 Hz) predicted reaction time with a cortical lead of approximately 175 ms, supporting a neurogenic component of physiological tremor. Beta-band coherence (18-30 Hz) predicted movement accuracy and reward attainment with near-zero lag, reflecting tight corticospinal coupling. Critically, these effects emerged from **phase dynamics** rather than power -- single-trial deviations from the preferred cortico-motor phase relationship predicted behavioral impairments. This frequency-specific dissociation supports the view that distinct oscillatory channels carry different types of motor information through the same corticospinal pathway, connecting to broader theories of [[cross-frequency-coupling]] and [[critical-frequencies]].

The temporal dynamics of CMC during naturalistic movement have been clarified using **partial directed coherence** (PDC). A 2025 study recording EEG from 16 cortical channels and EMG from 6 upper limb muscles in 20 participants during reach-and-grasp actions found that corticomuscular connectivity from cortex to muscle consistently decreased around movement onset, with connectivity assessed continuously across theta, alpha, low-beta (12-20 Hz), high-beta (20-30 Hz), and gamma bands (Pereira et al., 2025). This paradoxical decrease in directed coherence at movement onset may reflect a transition from tonic corticospinal drive to more distributed, less phase-locked motor commands during dynamic action.

Motor practice type differentially shapes CMC. Spedden et al. (2024) found that dynamic position-control practice (precise wrist flexions) increased beta-band CMC (p = 0.038) driven by greater descending (cortex-to-muscle) signaling (p = 0.025) in 46 young adults, whereas isometric force-control practice produced no such change. At baseline, position control elicited higher CMC than force control, suggesting that the corticospinal system preferentially engages oscillatory coupling for kinematic rather than kinetic demands -- a distinction relevant to rehabilitation protocols targeting [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation|neurofeedback]] and motor recovery.

## Prediction Error in Motor Control

The motor system uses [[predictive-coding]] to integrate prior expectations with sensory feedback. Emanuele et al. (2026) revealed a fundamental dissociation between synaptic input and neural output during sensorimotor integration. Motor commands (EMG) show additive Bayesian integration of expectation and sensory input, but the synaptic input to M1 and S1 (measured via BOLD and LFP) carries a **signed prediction error signal** computed in premotor-parietal areas. In the beta band (13-25 Hz), LFP showed a strong negative correlation with expected stimulation (rho = -0.763), indicating that beta-band synaptic input encodes how much the actual sensory feedback deviates from prediction. This hierarchical organization -- with premotor areas computing surprise and transmitting signed prediction error to primary motor and sensory cortices -- connects motor control to the broader framework of [[predictive-coding]] through [[beta-oscillations]] and [[gamma-oscillations]].

## Neurofeedback for Motor Performance

Onagawa et al. (2023) conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of neurofeedback training (NFT) effects on motor performance in healthy adults (16 RCTs, N=374). NFT targeting motor-relevant frequency bands (SMR at 12-15 Hz, alpha, theta, beta) showed a moderate-to-large overall effect (SMD = 0.85), though publication bias was significant. The most important finding was a dose-response relationship: cumulative training time exceeding 125 minutes predicted significant benefit, explaining approximately 50% of variance in motor improvement. EEG-based NFT specifically showed significant effects (SMD = 0.74, p = 0.031), validating the principle that volitional modulation of oscillatory activity at specific frequency bands can translate into behavioral motor gains. These findings support the broader potential of [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]] approaches for motor rehabilitation.

## Gamma Oscillations in Motor Execution

Movement-related gamma synchronization (MRGS) in the 60-90 Hz range typically begins approximately 50 ms before movement onset and extends to roughly 100 ms after onset, functioning as a **movement onset response** rather than a sustained execution signal. The role of gamma extends beyond this transient burst through cross-frequency interactions. Nie et al. (2024) used intracortical microelectrode arrays in two rhesus monkeys and electrocorticography in 12 human participants to demonstrate widespread **phase-amplitude coupling** between low-gamma (40-50 Hz) phase and high-gamma (70-200 Hz) amplitude across sensorimotor and premotor cortices. This low-gamma--high-gamma PAC was elevated during rest and decreased at movement onset specifically in primary motor cortex, while postcentral (sensory) cortex showed much less modulation. The consistency of coupling patterns across reaching, finger-flexion, and word-reading tasks suggests a fundamental neural mechanism for motor output gating, connecting to broader principles of [[cross-frequency-coupling]] in cortical computation.

Methodological caveats are important for interpreting gamma findings in motor EEG: scalp-recorded gamma is susceptible to contamination from muscle artifacts (EMG), and high-density EEG systems detect higher frequencies more reliably than standard montages. Invasive recordings (ECoG, microelectrode arrays) remain the gold standard for motor [[gamma-oscillations]], though improvements in artifact rejection continue to expand the utility of non-invasive approaches.

## Oscillatory Dynamics of Motor Learning

Motor learning engages frequency-specific oscillatory changes across multiple bands, with beta dynamics serving as particularly informative biomarkers. A comprehensive systematic review of 80 studies (2008-2025) by Jahanian-Najafabadi and Davoodi (2025) identified several consistent patterns. **Beta-band** (13-30 Hz) desynchronization during movement predicted individual performance within 1 hour post-training, and practice-related beta power increases from early to late learning phases related to mechanisms of skill retention -- consistent with the "status quo" hypothesis in which elevated beta reflects the maintenance of a newly acquired motor state. **Alpha-band** (8-12 Hz) oscillations influenced acquisition, retention, and motor preparation efficiency, while **theta-band** (4-8 Hz) activity was associated with successful skill acquisition and retention. Enhanced [[cross-frequency-coupling|theta-gamma phase-amplitude coupling]] correlated with improved skill acquisition, and increased [[gamma-oscillations|gamma]] activity in frontal and central regions followed complex motor training.

Age-related differences in motor learning oscillations are pronounced. Older adults show diminished beta-band modulation during motor tasks, reduced [[alpha-oscillations|alpha]] desynchronization during execution, and impaired cortical inhibitory control mechanisms (Jahanian-Najafabadi & Davoodi, 2025). Where younger adults display focal, lateralized activation patterns, older adults recruit more widespread bilateral networks, likely reflecting compensatory strategies that alter the oscillatory efficiency of motor learning circuits (see [[development-and-aging]]).

Beta dynamics during motor sequence learning depend on whether learning is implicit or explicit. In a serial reaction time task, beta power increases from early to late learning stages track the consolidation of motor sequences, with beta activity serving as a biomarker for the transition from effortful to automatic motor control. Children with **developmental coordination disorder** (DCD) exhibit atypical beta modulation: Lum et al. (2025) found that DCD children (n = 19) showed reduced beta modulation (20.1-32.7 Hz) compared to 38 typically developing peers during procedural learning, with faster reaction times associated with lower beta power. Notably, theta and alpha activity remained comparable between groups, indicating a selective disruption in automatic motor sequence encoding mediated by [[beta-oscillations]].

## Tremor and Oscillatory Dysfunction

Pathological oscillations are central to tremor disorders, with distinct oscillatory signatures distinguishing Parkinson's disease (PD) from essential tremor (ET). In PD, excessive beta-band activity (13-35 Hz) in the subthalamic nucleus (STN) correlates directly with the severity of rigidity and bradykinesia, while abnormal [[theta-oscillations|theta]] oscillations (4-6 Hz) are associated with tremor. Lensky (2025) used resting-state EEG in 15 PD patients and 16 healthy controls to identify multi-band alterations: theta-band changes were strongest in bilateral centro-parietal regions (67% classification accuracy), alpha-band alterations were widespread reflecting [[thalamocortical-circuits|thalamocortical]] circuit dysfunction, low-beta changes localized to left centro-parietal regions, and gamma-band alterations showed remarkable spatial consistency across central-parietal areas. These EEG-based oscillatory patterns may serve as non-invasive biomarkers for early PD detection, potentially in prodromal populations with idiopathic REM sleep behavior disorder.

A critical distinction between PD and ET has been established at the level of the STN: PD patients exhibit significantly increased beta frequency power in the motor subregion of the STN, whereas ET patients do not show this pathological beta elevation, confirming distinct pathophysiological mechanisms despite overlapping motor symptoms.

Essential tremor arises from synchronized oscillatory activity within the **cerebello-thalamo-cortical circuit**. Steina et al. (2025) provided the first simultaneous recordings of thalamic local field potentials, 306-channel magnetoencephalography, and EMG in 19 ET patients during surgery, demonstrating increased oscillatory coupling at individual tremor frequency (postural: 5.1 ± 0.9 Hz; kinetic: 4.4 ± 1.1 Hz) within the cerebello-thalamo-cortical network. Thalamus-cortex coherence at tremor frequency was enhanced during tremor episodes with bilateral cerebellar involvement. Motor cortex coupling strength correlated with postural tremor amplitude (r = 0.59, p = 0.017), while an inverse relationship between beta-band coherence and tremor severity was observed -- connecting tremor pathophysiology to the broader role of beta in motor state maintenance.

The impact of emotion on PD tremor has emerged as a novel research direction. Negative emotion modulates postural tremor variability, with prefrontal [[beta-oscillations]] identified as potential therapeutic targets, reframing PD tremor partly as a **limbic-motor interface** disorder.

## Cerebellar Contributions to Motor Oscillations

The cerebellum contributes to motor control through precise frequency encoding and predictive oscillatory signaling. Recent work has demonstrated that cerebellar oscillations encode dynamic motor frequencies with remarkable numerical precision and cross-individual uniformity, with cerebellar activity during finger tapping closely matching EMG signal frequencies on a second-by-second basis. This positions the cerebellum not merely as an error-correction device but as an active oscillatory encoder of motor kinematics.

Cerebellar-cortical beta oscillations serve a **predictive function**: beta coupling between cerebellum and cortex emerges approximately 2 seconds before action initiation and progressively builds across trials as environmental features are learned and behavioral outcomes stabilize. This anticipatory beta signal likely reflects the cerebellum's role in generating forward models (see [[predictive-coding]]) that the motor cortex uses to calibrate upcoming movements.

Theta-frequency cerebellar oscillations are specifically important for motor learning. Paparella et al. (2025) used a crossover design with cerebellar transcranial alternating current stimulation (tACS) at 5 Hz (theta), 50 Hz (gamma), and sham during a serial reaction time task, finding that **5 Hz theta tACS selectively enhanced early-phase motor learning** with significantly improved reaction times compared to both gamma and sham conditions. This aligns with the cerebellum's role in motor timing and error correction, and suggests that theta-band cerebellar oscillations support the initial acquisition phase of procedural learning.

In Parkinson's disease, subthalamic nucleus deep brain stimulation modulates cerebellar oscillatory patterns. Mid-cerebellar beta power during the DBS-OFF condition was significantly associated with motor severity, an association that was abolished during stimulation -- suggesting that therapeutic DBS partly acts by normalizing pathological cerebellar-basal ganglia oscillatory interactions.

Non-invasive recording of cerebellar activity via **electrocerebellograms** (ECeGs) -- surface electrodes placed below the inion -- has been proposed as a method to assess cerebellar oscillatory contributions to motor control, though spatial resolution limitations remain a significant methodological caveat.

## Motor Cortex Stimulation and Oscillatory Effects

Non-invasive brain stimulation techniques interact with motor oscillations in frequency-specific ways that are both mechanistically informative and clinically relevant. Werner et al. (2025) demonstrated that subthalamic nucleus DBS in the **beta frequency range** (10, 16, 20, 26, 30 Hz) in 14 PD patients boosts cortical beta oscillations and slows movement. Beta-band DBS accentuated hemispheric beta power lateralization and reduced finger-tapping rate proportionally. The cortical beta increase was specific to the 8-26 Hz range, outlasted stimulation, and did not depend on stimulation frequency -- suggesting a remotely induced cortical response rather than entrainment. This provides causal evidence that elevated cortical beta oscillations impede movement, supporting the longstanding "beta = status quo" hypothesis of [[beta-oscillations]].

State-dependent TMS-EEG studies have revealed that the motor cortex's response to stimulation depends on the instantaneous phase and power of ongoing oscillations. Pre-TMS mu oscillatory power and phase significantly predict both early (<50 ms) and late cortical EEG responses, with TMS delivered at different phases of mu (8-13 Hz) and beta (14-30 Hz) oscillations producing distinct corticospinal and cortical effects. This demonstrates the independent causal roles of oscillatory phase and power in motor excitability, advancing the development of **brain-state-controlled** [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation|stimulation]] protocols.

In Parkinson's disease, Liu et al. (2025) conducted a randomized controlled trial (N = 60, three groups of 20) comparing tDCS, tACS at 20 Hz, and sham stimulation targeting the left primary motor cortex. Both active stimulation modalities increased beta power and improved rigidity-bradykinesia, but **tDCS uniquely reduced tremor** by decreasing theta power. The theta/beta ratio mediated 58.82% of the total tDCS-induced tremor improvement, establishing this ratio as a potential oscillatory target for personalized stimulation protocols. These findings demonstrate that tDCS broadly modulates multiple oscillatory bands for varied symptom relief, while tACS provides more targeted frequency-specific modulation.

## Criticality in Motor Oscillations

Daffertshofer et al. (2018) demonstrated that resting-state brain dynamics operate near criticality, with distinct scaling properties for amplitude and phase. In both alpha (8-12 Hz) and beta (20-30 Hz) bands, amplitude showed higher persistence (scaling exponent alpha approximately 0.80-0.88) while phase showed more flexible dynamics (alpha approximately 0.57-0.62). This distinction has direct implications for motor control: the persistent amplitude dynamics may reflect the stable maintenance of motor states (tonic beta in the "status quo" hypothesis), while the flexible phase dynamics support the rapid, transient coordination needed for movement initiation and correction. The power-law scaling confirmed across both frequency bands supports the idea that the motor system operates at a [[critical-frequencies]] point optimized for balancing stability with responsiveness.
