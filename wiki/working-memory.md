# Working Memory

Working memory relies on a dynamic interplay of oscillatory mechanisms across multiple frequency bands rather than persistent neural firing. [[theta-oscillations]] and [[gamma-oscillations]] interact through [[cross-frequency-coupling]] to encode and refresh memory contents, with gamma bursts in superficial cortical layers carrying item information while deep-layer [[beta-oscillations]] provide top-down gating and control. [[alpha-oscillations]] organize phase-synchronized networks that both represent specific memory contents and coordinate executive control through fronto-parietal circuits. Beta oscillations play a newly appreciated role in priority management, with distinct sub-bands (~15 Hz vs. ~20 Hz) supporting different working memory operations, and beta desynchronization indexing the magnitude and fidelity of representational shifts during attentional reprioritization. Recent single-neuron recordings have revealed that [[hippocampal-memory|hippocampal]] theta-gamma phase-amplitude coupling directly controls working memory fidelity by coordinating frontal executive signals with medial temporal lobe storage (Daume et al., 2024), while the spatial computing framework proposes that alpha/beta traveling waves organize ad hoc neuronal ensembles across the prefrontal cortex to flexibly implement task rules (Lundqvist et al., 2023). A growing body of evidence also supports **activity-silent** working memory, in which deprioritized items transition from oscillatory maintenance into latent synaptic traces that can be reactivated on demand (van Ede, 2026), challenging purely oscillation-centric accounts and suggesting that active and silent storage coexist as complementary mechanisms.

## The Beta-Gamma Push-Pull Model

Miller et al. (2018) proposed a fundamental revision to the classic persistent-firing model of working memory. In their "Working Memory 2.0" framework, memory contents are maintained through brief gamma bursts (~30+ Hz) that periodically refresh short-term synaptic weight changes, rather than through continuous spiking. Executive control operates through a push-pull mechanism between deep-layer alpha/beta oscillations and superficial-layer gamma: deep-layer beta inhibits superficial-layer gamma to gate information flow. To encode a new item, beta decreases and gamma is disinhibited; to maintain items, moderate beta allows periodic gamma refresh bursts; to clear memory, beta increases and suppresses gamma. This laminar organization -- consistent with the spectrolaminar motif where superficial layers generate gamma and deep layers generate alpha/beta (see [[cortical-layer-oscillations]]) -- was validated through multi-electrode recordings across seven frontal cortical areas in non-human primates. Critically, deviations from correct beta-gamma dynamics predicted both upcoming errors and their specific type, establishing that the oscillatory control mechanism is functionally causal. Working memory capacity (~4 items) emerges naturally from competition for limited gamma burst refresh time. This model connects to broader theories of [[cross-frequency-coupling]] and the hierarchical organization of [[beta-oscillations]] and [[gamma-oscillations]] in cortical computation.

Lundqvist et al. (2024) extended the push-pull framework to human electrophysiology in a landmark study using whole-brain MEG/EEG during a sequential working memory task with distractors. The authors applied the same single-trial burst analysis previously used in non-human primates and discovered that both alpha and beta bursts correlate with the filtering and control of memory items in humans, but with distinct functional profiles depending on oscillatory frequency and cortical origin. High-power **alpha bursts** in posterior sensory regions were associated with suppression of sensory processing -- consistent with sensory gating -- whereas **beta bursts** in frontal and central sites more selectively gated information from sensory processing into working memory and proactively removed items no longer relevant to the task. This dissociation clarifies a long-standing discrepancy between human studies (emphasizing alpha) and primate studies (emphasizing beta), showing that both rhythms contribute to the push-pull control architecture but at different levels of the cortical hierarchy. The result connects to the broader laminar organization described in [[cortical-layer-oscillations]], where superficial-layer gamma carries content and deep-layer alpha/beta exerts control.

## Spatial Computing and Traveling Waves

The **spatial computing** framework (Lundqvist, Herman, and Miller, 2023) proposes that working memory control operates through the spatial organization of oscillatory activity across the prefrontal cortex. Rather than recruiting fixed neuronal populations, the brain uses alpha/beta (~10-30 Hz) traveling waves to dynamically carve out cortical patches -- ad hoc "task forces" of neurons -- that can be flexibly reconfigured for different cognitive demands. Beta waves carry information about task rules and selectively yield to gamma bursts when operations like encoding or readout are required. This mechanism explains how the prefrontal cortex sustains a consistent understanding of a process even when specific contents change continuously, as demonstrated in multi-electrode recordings from non-human primates performing rule-switching tasks (Lundqvist et al., 2023).

Riddle et al. (2025) extended the spatial computing framework to humans by identifying traveling waves in scalp EEG that link visual and frontal cortex during working memory-guided behavior. They found a feedforward (occipital-to-frontal) [[theta-oscillations|theta wave]] that emerged shortly after a response probe, with its latency predicting individual differences in reaction time, and a feedback (frontal-to-occipital) [[beta-oscillations|beta wave]] that appeared after response termination. These directional waves were specific to memory-guided conditions and predicted both within-subject and between-subject behavioral timing, providing the first human evidence that traveling wave dynamics implement the bidirectional communication required for working memory readout.

## Alpha Phase Synchronization Networks

Haque et al. (2025) demonstrated that high-alpha band (11-13 Hz) phase synchronization serves a dual function in visual working memory. Using concurrent MEG/EEG during a delayed match-to-sample task, they found that [[alpha-oscillations]] synchronization networks are **feature-selective**: shape memory engages synchronization in object-processing areas (LOC, V4/V8), color memory in color-processing regions, and location memory in dorsal stream areas. Critically, a shared fronto-parietal subnetwork (centered on FEF and intraparietal sulcus) transcended memory contents, representing a content-agnostic executive control hub. Both alpha synchronization and theta desynchronization correlated strongly with individual behavioral performance (r = 0.88 and r = -0.89, respectively), and machine learning classifiers could decode memory contents from phase synchronization patterns -- but not from local oscillation amplitudes. This establishes that **phase relationships**, not power, carry the critical information in working memory maintenance, connecting to the role of [[theta-oscillations]] in complementary inhibitory gating.

## Beta and Working Memory Priority

Two recent studies redefine the role of [[beta-oscillations]] in working memory prioritization. Fulvio and Postle (2026) discovered that prioritizing one item among two in working memory is associated with behavioral oscillations at **15 Hz** (low-beta), while neutral maintenance oscillates at ~20 Hz. The 15 Hz signature is linked to larger representational transformations of the **unprioritized** item rather than strengthening of the prioritized one -- prioritization appears to rotate the uncued item into an "action-null subspace" while the cued item occupies an "action-potent subspace." This challenges the standard "strengthening" account of retrocuing and implicates thalamocortical circuits at the natural frequency of posterior parietal cortex.

Myers et al. (2026) extended this by showing that central-parietal beta (15-25 Hz) power reduction during priority switches uniquely tracks both the **magnitude** of representational shift (angular distance between items) and the **fidelity** of the updated representation (multivariate decoding strength). Frontal [[theta-oscillations]] increased during switches but showed neither magnitude sensitivity nor decoding prediction, consistent with theta signaling the *need* for control while beta *implements* specific updates. The finding that switch costs scale continuously with angular distance implies that priority operations reflect the underlying representational geometry of stored features -- switching between dissimilar representations requires a larger "rotation" through representational space, with beta indexing this process. These results connect working memory operations to [[attention-and-perception]] through shared oscillatory control mechanisms.

## Theta-Gamma Phase-Amplitude Coupling and Capacity

The Lisman-Idiart model predicts that working memory capacity scales with the number of [[gamma-oscillations|gamma]] cycles that fit within the preferred phase of a [[theta-oscillations|theta]] cycle, typically yielding ~4-7 discrete "slots." Daume et al. (2024) provided decisive single-neuron evidence for this mechanism in humans. Recording from medial temporal lobe and frontal lobe neurons in epilepsy patients performing a multi-item working memory task, they demonstrated that hippocampal theta-gamma **phase-amplitude coupling** (TG-PAC) was strongest between theta phase (3-7 Hz) and two gamma sub-bands -- a lower range (30-55 Hz) and a higher range (70-140 Hz). Critically, TG-PAC scaled with working memory load and predicted behavioral accuracy. The authors identified a novel class of **PAC neurons** that fired selectively during nonlinear interactions of theta phase and gamma amplitude. These PAC neurons' spike timing was coordinated with frontal theta activity specifically when cognitive control demand was high, providing a direct neural mechanism by which [[cross-frequency-coupling]] integrates prefrontal executive control with hippocampal memory storage. By introducing noise correlations with persistently active hippocampal neurons, PAC neurons shaped the geometry of the population code, leading to higher-fidelity representations of working memory content.

Chota et al. (2024) demonstrated that theta-gamma PAC measured over frontal electrodes predicts n-back performance in both young and elderly healthy adults, though the specific coupling parameters that best predict accuracy differ with age -- consistent with the general decline in oscillatory precision documented in [[development-and-aging]]. These findings confirm that [[cross-frequency-coupling|theta-gamma coupling]] is a robust and generalizable biomarker of working memory capacity across the lifespan.

## Frontal Theta in Executive Control

**Frontal midline theta** (fm-θ, ~4-8 Hz) is the most reliable oscillatory signature of working memory executive control. Theta power at frontal and frontocentral electrodes increases with working memory load, conflict detection, and the need for interference resolution. Hamedi et al. (2025) showed that anterior prefrontal EEG theta power provides an objective electrophysiological measure of memory and executive function, with consistently lower theta power in epilepsy patients who show impaired performance across executive, visual, spatial, and working memory domains. The frontal theta signal reflects the *need* for cognitive control -- a demand signal -- rather than the implementation of specific memory operations, which is instead indexed by posterior alpha/beta dynamics (Myers et al., 2026).

Fronto-parietal theta phase synchronization has emerged as a key marker of working memory network coordination. During high-load conditions, theta phase-locking between prefrontal and parietal sites increases, dynamically coupling the executive control hub with posterior storage regions. This long-range theta coherence is complemented by local adjustments in alpha and beta power: as working memory load rises, posterior alpha desynchronizes to release sensory processing resources, while frontal theta coordinates the timing of gamma bursts that carry item-specific content (see [[cross-frequency-coupling]]). The interplay between these frequency bands creates a hierarchical control architecture in which theta sets the temporal framework, alpha/beta gates access, and gamma encodes content.

## Activity-Silent Working Memory

A significant revision to oscillation-centric models of working memory has emerged from research on **activity-silent** (or activity-quiescent) storage mechanisms. In this framework, not all maintained items require ongoing oscillatory signatures; instead, deprioritized or unattended items can persist through latent synaptic traces -- short-term changes in synaptic weights that allow reactivation without sustained neural firing. van Ede (2026) provided direct evidence for this dual-mechanism account: induced [[alpha-oscillations|alpha]] power reliably tracked the prioritized (tested-first) item during a working memory delay but showed no sustained signature for the deprioritized (tested-second) item. This dissociation suggests that alpha activity reflects **attentional prioritization** rather than general memory maintenance, with unattended items transitioning into a synaptic storage state that is metabolically efficient but still retrievable.

However, Kamiński et al. (2025) complicated this picture using single-neuron recordings in human medial temporal lobe. They found that both attended and unattended memory items were encoded through persistent activity at the single-cell level -- image-selective neurons maintained spiking for the unattended item even when it was not decodable from the broader population. This suggests that activity-silent storage may coexist with sparse persistent firing that is invisible to scalp-level recordings but detectable at the single-neuron level. The emerging consensus holds that working memory employs a **hybrid architecture**: prioritized items are maintained through robust oscillatory signatures (alpha phase synchronization, gamma bursts), deprioritized items rely on a combination of latent synaptic traces and sparse persistent activity, and the transition between these states is governed by the beta-mediated gating mechanism described in the push-pull model. This hybrid view reconciles the oscillatory framework with short-term synaptic plasticity models and explains why "pinged" reactivation paradigms (using TMS or sensory probes) can decode items that show no spontaneous oscillatory signature during the delay period.

## Load-Dependent Oscillatory Dynamics

Working memory load modulates oscillatory activity across multiple frequency bands in a graded, parametric fashion. As the number of maintained items increases, several canonical changes emerge: posterior [[alpha-oscillations|alpha]] power decreases (alpha desynchronization), reflecting the progressive release of cortical inhibition to accommodate more items; frontal [[theta-oscillations|theta]] power increases, signaling greater executive control demand; and the precision of [[cross-frequency-coupling|theta-gamma coupling]] tightens as the system approaches capacity limits. Yuan and Li (2025) demonstrated that scalp patterns of alpha-band activity can decode auditory working memory load, with load-related topographic patterns changing dynamically throughout the maintenance interval -- indicating that alpha desynchronization is not a static index but a temporally evolving process that tracks the moment-to-moment status of maintained representations.

A 2025 study in the *Journal of Neuroscience* (Samaha et al., 2025) further demonstrated that aperiodic-adjusted alpha power significantly decreases during visual memory encoding while aperiodic neural activity simultaneously "flattens out," highlighting the importance of separating periodic oscillatory changes from [[aperiodic-dynamics|aperiodic shifts]] when interpreting load effects. Additionally, working memory load increases movement-related alpha and beta desynchronization (Chung et al., 2024), suggesting that motor preparation circuits are sensitive to concurrent cognitive demands through shared oscillatory resource allocation.

## Distraction Filtering and Oscillatory Protection

The brain deploys multiple oscillatory mechanisms to protect working memory contents from interference. Proactive distractor filtering relies on anticipatory increases in [[alpha-oscillations|alpha]] power over regions processing expected distractors, effectively raising the inhibitory threshold before disruption arrives. However, the precise role of alpha in distraction filtering remains debated. Foster and Awh (2019) demonstrated that alpha lateralization tracks the spatial focus of attention during working memory, while more recent work (Noonan et al., 2024) argues that alpha power is controlled indirectly by the load of goal-relevant information rather than by direct inhibition of distractor representations -- a view compatible with perceptual load theory.

Jung, Sahoo, and Snyder (2025) provided converging evidence from simultaneous local field potential and EEG recordings in non-human primates, showing that distractor anticipation during working memory engages **theta-band** activity across spatial scales rather than alpha. Their cross-temporal generalization analyses revealed that memory representations were reactivated around the anticipated time of distractor onset, and during this critical window, [[beta-oscillations|beta]]-band activity exhibited transiently enhanced interhemispheric communication between lateral prefrontal cortex and occipitoparietal areas. Notably, these anticipatory oscillatory patterns were observed only on correct trials, implicating their functional role in successful distraction mitigation. The finding that theta and beta -- rather than alpha alone -- mediate distractor anticipation suggests that multiple frequency channels contribute to working memory protection, with the specific oscillatory mechanism depending on brain region and task demands.

## Aging, Clinical Populations, and Oscillatory Biomarkers

Working memory decline in healthy aging is closely linked to deterioration of the oscillatory mechanisms described above. Older adults show reduced frontal [[theta-oscillations|theta]] power, weaker theta-gamma [[cross-frequency-coupling]], and diminished alpha lateralization during working memory maintenance, contributing to both reduced capacity and increased susceptibility to distraction. Age-related increases in theta activity detected during encoding in primary visual and left prefrontal cortices, combined with stronger alpha and beta oscillations during both encoding and maintenance in prefrontal regions (Kamp et al., 2024), suggest a compensatory upregulation of top-down control to offset declining neural efficiency.

Reinhart and Nguyen (2019) demonstrated that these age-related deficits can be reversed through targeted neuromodulation: 25 minutes of frequency-tuned [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation|transcranial alternating current stimulation]] (tACS) targeting fronto-temporal theta synchronization restored working memory performance in 60-76-year-olds to levels comparable with young adults. The effects persisted beyond a 50-minute post-stimulation window and were accompanied by increased theta phase synchronization and normalized sender-receiver information flow within fronto-temporal networks. Grover et al. (2022) extended this approach to multi-day protocols, showing that 4 days of repetitive tACS produced working memory improvements lasting at least 1 month in 65-88-year-old adults, with modulation of parietal low-frequency synchronization preferentially improving working memory from day 3 onward. Lehr et al. (2024) further confirmed that peak-coupled theta-gamma tACS applied to the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex over 16 sessions improves 2-back sensitivity in a randomized, sham-controlled, triple-blinded design with 77 elderly participants. Most recently, Violante et al. (2025) assessed individualized-frequency tACS guided by EEG and MRI in patients with mild cognitive impairment, showing preliminary cognitive gains from engaging theta-gamma coupling in a clinical population.

In clinical conditions, oscillatory working memory signatures serve as emerging biomarkers. In **schizophrenia**, dorsolateral prefrontal [[gamma-oscillations|gamma]] power (30-50 Hz) is reduced during working memory tasks, and targeted gamma neurofeedback has been shown to improve both frontal gamma power and working memory performance. Theta-gamma PAC predicts individual working memory capacity in psychotic patients just as it does in healthy controls, with coupling strength during passive viewing even predicting performance on active tasks -- suggesting PAC is a stable trait marker of cognitive potential (Lopes da Silva et al., 2025). In **ADHD**, Acuff et al. (2025) identified dysfunctional alpha/beta and high-frequency oscillatory burst dynamics within the frontoparietal network as a mechanism underlying working memory deficits in adolescents. Across 224 person-sessions, they found that alpha/beta bursting at the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and posterior parietal cortex normally decreases during stimulus encoding and increases during the delay, while gamma bursting shows the opposite pattern -- and deviations from this complementary burst architecture predicted both working memory errors and clinical symptom severity. These findings identify oscillatory burst dynamics as a potential treatment target and biomarker for working memory dysfunction in [[clinical-applications|clinical populations]].
