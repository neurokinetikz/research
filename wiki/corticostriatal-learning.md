# Corticostriatal Learning

Corticostriatal circuits form the primary interface between the cerebral cortex and basal ganglia, enabling the brain to extract rules from experience, select actions, and update behavior based on reward. Beta oscillations (~16-25 Hz) serve as the dominant communication frequency within these loops, synchronizing prefrontal cortex with striatum during category learning, decision-making, and reward processing. Dysfunction in corticostriatal beta dynamics underlies the motor symptoms of Parkinson's disease, where excessive beta synchrony in the subthalamic nucleus locks the system into a pathological "status quo" that suppresses voluntary movement. Understanding how dopamine modulates these oscillatory circuits connects fundamental questions about learning and computation to some of the most prevalent neurological and psychiatric conditions.

## Circuit anatomy: the corticostriatal loop

The canonical corticostriatal circuit follows a recurrent loop: cortex projects glutamatergic afferents to the striatum (caudate and putamen), which projects GABAergic inhibition to the globus pallidus (GPi/GPe), which in turn modulates thalamic output back to cortex. Two parallel pathways traverse this loop. The direct pathway (D1 receptor-expressing medium spiny neurons) disinhibits thalamus to facilitate selected actions, while the indirect pathway (D2 receptor-expressing MSNs) increases pallidal inhibition of thalamus to suppress competing actions. The subthalamic nucleus (STN) provides a hyperdirect pathway from cortex that rapidly excites GPi, implementing a global brake on motor output that is then selectively released through the direct pathway. Dopaminergic projections from the substantia nigra pars compacta (SNc) and ventral tegmental area (VTA) modulate plasticity at corticostriatal synapses, gating which associations are strengthened or weakened based on reward prediction errors (Pathak et al., 2025).

## Beta as the corticostriatal communication frequency

Beta oscillations are the primary carrier of information between cortex and striatum. The Pathak et al. (2025) biomimetic model demonstrates that corticostriatal phase-locking at ~16 Hz increases systematically with learning, with the strongest synchrony emerging during delay periods when working memory maintains category representations. Correct trials show significantly elevated beta phase-locking value (PLV) compared to incorrect trials, establishing beta synchrony as a direct predictor of behavioral success. Wang et al. (2026) further show that prefrontal [[beta-oscillations]] center around ~19 Hz with a narrow spectral profile, consistent with PFC serving as a stable oscillatory hub for top-down communication to subcortical targets. The "status quo" hypothesis (Engel and Fries, 2010) proposes that beta maintains current sensorimotor and cognitive states, and its suppression is required for behavioral updating -- a framework that applies equally to [[motor-control]] and to cognitive flexibility in corticostriatal learning.

## Incongruent neurons and error prediction

A striking discovery from the Pathak et al. (2025) corticostriatal model is the identification of incongruent neurons (ICNs) -- approximately 20% of anterior cortical neurons that preferentially fire during trials that will result in incorrect behavioral choices. These neurons predict errors more than 1000 ms before they occur (p < 0.001 in empirical validation with nonhuman primates). ICNs arise from sparse cortical connectivity that creates neurons with ambiguous receptive fields, which develop stronger connections to the "wrong" striatal action pathway during learning. This constitutes a form of [[predictive-coding]] within corticostriatal circuits: the network generates internal predictions, and ICN activity signals impending prediction failures. Four functional classes emerge -- A-correct, A-incorrect, B-correct, B-incorrect -- with the "incorrect" classes providing a prospective error signal that could theoretically be used for real-time intervention.

## Category learning through beta synchrony

Corticostriatal beta coherence supports the extraction of abstract rules from experience. In the Pathak model, learning emerges from three plasticity mechanisms: cortico-cortical long-term potentiation with reversal, reward-modulated corticostriatal LTP/LTD, and thalamo-cortical short-term plasticity for working memory augmentation. The model achieves category learning performance comparable to nonhuman primates (~80% accuracy after 100-400 trials), with beta synchrony between cortex and striatum serving as the oscillatory scaffold for rule representation. Antzoulatos and Miller (2016) demonstrated that frontoparietal beta synchrony selectively conveys task-relevant category information while ignoring irrelevant stimulus dimensions, suggesting that beta-mediated corticostriatal communication implements a filtering mechanism for [[working-memory]] and cognitive control.

## Parkinson's disease and pathological beta

Parkinson's disease provides the clearest evidence that corticostriatal beta dynamics have causal motor consequences. The STN in Parkinson's patients exhibits exaggerated beta oscillations peaking around 18-25 Hz, with the percentage of long-duration beta bursts (>900 ms) positively correlating with bradykinesia and rigidity severity (Tinkhauser et al., 2017). This pathological beta represents an over-stabilized "status quo" signal that prevents the beta desynchronization required for movement initiation. Levodopa administration specifically suppresses low beta (13-20 Hz) and limits burst duration, while high-frequency deep brain stimulation (>100 Hz) disrupts pathological beta synchrony with clinical benefit proportional to suppression magnitude. Critically, STN stimulation at beta frequencies (~20 Hz) actually slows movement, providing direct causal evidence for beta's antikinetic role. The ~20 Hz peak aligns with the low/high beta boundary frequency, a point where motor cortex shows maximum resonance and corticomuscular coherence is strongest. ## Dopaminergic modulation and reward prediction error

Dopamine shapes corticostriatal oscillatory dynamics through differential effects on D1 and D2 pathways. In the Pathak model, corticostriatal plasticity follows a BCM-like curve modulated by dopamine release from SNc: positive prediction errors (unexpected reward) drive LTP via D1 receptors on direct pathway MSNs, while negative prediction errors (omitted reward) drive LTD via D2 receptors on indirect pathway MSNs. Emanuele et al. (2026) demonstrate that signed prediction error signals are encoded specifically in beta-band (13-25 Hz) local field potentials as synaptic input to primary motor cortex, while spiking output reflects additive Bayesian integration of expectation and sensory evidence. This dissociation between synaptic input (prediction error) and spiking output (motor command) suggests that beta oscillations carry top-down predictive signals through the [[predictive-coding]] hierarchy. Cholinergic tonically active neurons (TANs) in the striatum further modulate the exploration-exploitation balance, gating how dopaminergic signals influence learning. See [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]] for pharmacological details.

## Fast-spiking interneurons and striatal oscillations

Fast-spiking parvalbumin-positive (PV+) interneurons play a critical role in shaping striatal oscillatory dynamics. Williams et al. (2026) demonstrate that PV+ interneurons can autonomously generate oscillations through interneuron network gamma (ING) mechanisms without requiring phasic excitatory input, with the strength of excitatory drive tuning the transition between ING and pyramidal-interneuron network gamma (PING). In striatal circuits, fast-spiking interneurons provide powerful feedforward inhibition that sculpts the temporal windows during which MSNs can integrate cortical input. The GABA-A reversal potential determines whether ING is viable (hyperpolarizing inhibition at -75 mV enables it; shunting inhibition at -55 mV abolishes it), establishing a neurochemical gate on local oscillatory dynamics. This connects to the broader role of [[gamma-oscillations]] in local computation, while beta-range oscillations depend on longer-timescale mechanisms including M-current dynamics in layer V pyramidal neurons and the slow potassium conductances that enable synchronization over the long conduction delays characteristic of corticostriatal pathways (Daffertshofer et al., 2018).

## Decision-making through prefrontal-striatal beta bursts

During choice behavior, beta bursts in prefrontal-striatal circuits mark the evaluation and commitment phases of decision-making. Beta power increases during deliberation and decreases at the moment of action selection, consistent with the status quo hypothesis: sustained beta reflects maintenance of the current decision state, while beta desynchronization signals the transition to a new behavioral policy. The Pathak model shows that matrisomal MSNs implement competitive action selection through lateral inhibition, with the winning action determined by the pattern of corticostriatal beta synchrony. Striosomal MSNs separately compute reward predictions, creating a dual-channel architecture where action selection and value computation operate in parallel within the basal ganglia. The critical role of beta dynamics in this process is underscored by clinical observations that both Parkinson's disease (excessive beta) and impulse control disorders (insufficient beta) involve disrupted corticostriatal decision circuits.

Chronic intracranial recordings from patients with implanted DBS leads have revealed frequency-specific dissociations between effort and reward processing within corticostriatal decision circuits. Hoy, de Hemptinne et al. (2024) recorded simultaneously from prefrontal cortex and basal ganglia (STN and globus pallidus) while participants evaluated offers varying in reward magnitude and physical effort. Basal ganglia beta oscillations (12-20 Hz) tracked effort demands on a single-trial basis, whereas prefrontal [[theta-oscillations|theta]] (4-7 Hz) encoded the reward received on the previous trial, with no signal for net subjective value in either region. Critically, direct electrical stimulation of PFC increased overall acceptance of offers, enhanced sensitivity to reward, and decreased the impact of effort on choices -- providing causal evidence that prefrontal theta dynamics gate the integration of reward history into current decisions. These findings demonstrate that corticostriatal decision circuits decompose value into separable effort and reward components carried by distinct frequency channels rather than computing a unitary decision variable (Hoy et al., 2024).

## Beta oscillations encode reward prediction errors in striatum

Direct evidence that striatal beta oscillations carry reward prediction error (RPE) signals comes from local field potential recordings across sensorimotor, associative, and limbic territories of the primate striatum. Basanisi et al. (2023) recorded LFPs from two rhesus monkeys performing a free-choice probabilistic learning task and found that post-outcome beta power changes (15-35 Hz) were consistent with RPE encoding -- beta modulation scaled with the discrepancy between expected and received reward. The RPE signal showed a striking anatomical gradient: it was more strongly represented in the ventral (limbic) striatum than in the dorsal (sensorimotor and associative) regions, consistent with the ventral striatum's established role in reward-based updating and its dense dopaminergic innervation from the VTA. This work extends the corticostriatal beta framework beyond motor and cognitive domains into the reinforcement learning architecture proper, demonstrating that the same oscillatory frequency band that carries "status quo" motor signals also conveys the signed teaching signals that drive behavioral adaptation. See [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]] for the dopaminergic mechanisms underlying these RPE signals.

## Cortical beta networks and dopaminergic modulation

A large-scale mapping study by Shin et al. (2024) analyzed over 30 hours of invasive brain recordings from 1,772 cortical channels in epilepsy patients and demonstrated that beta (13-35 Hz) is the most spatially distributed cortical rhythm. Beta-dominant cortical regions formed a shared functional network with deeper structures including the basal ganglia, and this network showed significant spatial overlap with dopamine uptake maps derived from positron emission tomography. The findings provide a structural basis for why [[beta-oscillations]] are so prominent in corticostriatal circuits: beta emerges preferentially in cortico-subcortical networks that are under dopaminergic modulation, linking dopamine's role in reward learning to its role in shaping the spectral architecture of brain-wide communication. This unifying view suggests that pathological beta in Parkinson's disease and reward-related beta modulation in the healthy striatum reflect the same fundamental circuit property -- dopaminergic tuning of network-level beta synchrony -- manifesting differently under conditions of dopamine depletion versus intact signaling (Shin et al., 2024).

## Habit formation and cortex-independent execution

The transition from goal-directed behavior to habitual responding involves a fundamental reorganization of corticostriatal circuit dynamics. Grillner et al. (2025) demonstrated that stereotyped movement sequences require cortical input during the learning phase but become cortex-independent after consolidation -- cortical inactivation after learning leaves habit performance intact. The motor program transfers to a self-contained loop within the basal ganglia: the dorsolateral striatum (DLS), substantia nigra pars reticulata (SNr), and parafascicular nucleus (PF) of the thalamus form a recurrent DLS-SNr-PF-DLS circuit subdivided into many parallel loops with precise spatial organization. Synaptic plasticity at thalamostriatal (PF→DLS) synapses, rather than corticostriatal plasticity, appears to be the critical substrate for habit storage. This architecture has implications for understanding compulsive behaviors and [[clinical-applications|addiction]], where habit circuits may become abnormally strengthened, as well as for the oscillatory dynamics of learning: the shift from cortex-dependent to cortex-independent execution likely corresponds to a shift from corticostriatal beta synchrony during acquisition to predominantly subcortical oscillatory coordination during habitual performance.

## Addiction and corticostriatal circuit dysfunction

Corticostriatal oscillatory dynamics are increasingly implicated in the neurobiology of addiction. The ventral tegmental area, nucleus accumbens (ventral striatum), and prefrontal cortex form a reward circuit that communicates through dopaminergic and glutamatergic signaling, and disruption of oscillatory coordination within this network contributes to the compulsive behavior characteristic of substance use disorders. Drug reinforcement produces lasting alterations in striatal microcircuit function: cocaine administration causes long-lasting potentiation of local inhibitory transmission and decreased firing in the dorsomedial striatum, while chemogenetic and optogenetic inhibition of striatal cholinergic interneurons suppresses the flexibility of goal-directed behavior (Aoki et al., 2023). This cholinergic disruption impairs cognitive flexibility -- the ability to update behavior when contingencies change -- providing a circuit-level mechanism for the transition from flexible, goal-directed drug seeking to rigid, compulsive use. The dopamine kinetics of different drugs also matter: fast dopamine transients (as produced by cocaine) preferentially activate a corticostriatal circuit comprising the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex, insula, and dorsal caudate, whereas slower dopamine changes do not engage this circuit as strongly (Bhatt et al., 2023). Cortico-striatal [[gamma-oscillations]] are further modulated by dopamine D3 receptors in the context of levodopa-induced dyskinesia, highlighting how chronic dopaminergic perturbation reshapes oscillatory dynamics across the frequency spectrum.

## Striatal cholinergic interneurons as oscillatory pacemakers

Beyond the role of fast-spiking PV+ interneurons discussed above, **striatal cholinergic interneurons** (SChIs) exert powerful control over corticostriatal oscillatory dynamics. Selective optogenetic stimulation of SChIs in normal mice robustly and reversibly amplifies beta and gamma oscillations throughout the striatum and all layers of primary motor cortex through a muscarinic receptor-mediated mechanism, supporting the existence of a beta frequency pacemaker within the cortico-basal ganglia-thalamic loop (McCarthy et al., 2016). Cholinergic interneurons are autonomous pacemakers that spike tonically at 2-10 Hz, and deviations from this tonic mode -- including phasic bursts or pauses -- exert strong influences on MSN excitability and synaptic plasticity via muscarinic (M1, M2, M4) and nicotinic receptor signaling (Mamaligas and Ford, 2025). A computational model by Li et al. (2025) demonstrates that adaptive cholinergic feedback within striatal networks can generate and sustain beta oscillations through recurrent interactions between SChIs, MSNs, and cortical afferents, providing a mechanism by which acetylcholine-dopamine balance tunes the strength of corticostriatal beta synchrony. This has direct relevance for Parkinson's disease, where the loss of dopaminergic modulation shifts the cholinergic-dopaminergic equilibrium and may contribute to the emergence of pathological beta hypersynchrony.

## Gamma oscillation mechanisms in the basal ganglia

While beta dominates corticostriatal communication, gamma oscillations arise from distinct mechanisms within basal ganglia subcircuits. A computational study by Komolov et al. (2025) identified two separate gamma rhythms generated through self-inhibition: a high-frequency gamma in globus pallidus externus (GPe-TI) neurons and a slower gamma in D2 MSNs. Under healthy conditions, GPe-TI gamma operates stably, but D2 MSN gamma emerges only under simulated parkinsonian (dopamine-depleted) conditions and is strongly modulated by pathological beta activity in both intensity and frequency. Dopamine depletion increases the peak frequency of GPe-TI gamma through phase-amplitude coupling with exaggerated beta oscillations, suggesting that the GPe-TI loop becomes increasingly entrained to beta activity in Parkinson's disease. Although the STN does not generate gamma directly, gamma from the GPe-TI population is reflected in STN local field potentials -- consistent with clinical recordings showing coupled beta-gamma activity in STN that serves as a biomarker for deep brain stimulation contact selection (Ozturk et al., 2024). See [[cross-frequency-coupling]] for the broader significance of beta-gamma interactions.

## Cognitive decline and corticostriatal beta in Parkinson's disease

Beyond motor symptoms, corticostriatal beta dynamics are increasingly linked to the cognitive decline that affects a majority of Parkinson's disease patients. Two studies published in Brain in 2023 examined this relationship using simultaneous cortical and subcortical recordings. West et al. (2023) found that cortico-striatal beta coherence during cognitive tasks is disrupted in Parkinson's patients with mild cognitive impairment, with reduced task-related beta desynchronization predicting worse performance on executive function measures. Paulo et al. (2023) demonstrated that caudate beta power suppression during the encoding phase of a [[working-memory]] task correlated more strongly with memory domain cognitive impairment than did dorsolateral prefrontal cortex beta power, suggesting that striatal beta dynamics may be a more sensitive marker of Parkinson's cognitive decline than cortical measures alone. These findings extend the pathological beta framework from motor circuits to cognitive corticostriatal loops: just as excessive beta in the putamen-motor cortex loop produces bradykinesia, dysregulated beta in the caudate-prefrontal loop may impair the cognitive flexibility required for working memory encoding and executive control.

## Adaptive deep brain stimulation and beta biomarkers

The development of **adaptive (closed-loop) deep brain stimulation** (aDBS) represents a translational breakthrough built directly on the understanding of corticostriatal beta dynamics. In February 2025, the FDA approved Medtronic's Percept RC neurostimulator with BrainSense technology as the first commercially available adaptive DBS system, using STN beta-band local field potentials as a real-time feedback signal to adjust stimulation amplitude. The ADAPT-PD clinical trial demonstrated that aDBS significantly improved overall well-being (p = 0.007), with the adaptive group showing greater improvements in motor assessments and quality of life at 360 days compared to conventional continuous DBS (Gilron et al., 2025). The adaptive algorithm reads neural beta power and compares it to personalized threshold values: when beta exceeds the threshold (indicating symptom worsening), stimulation increases; when beta normalizes, stimulation decreases to minimize side effects such as dyskinesia and speech impairment.

Neumann et al. (2025) demonstrated that dopaminergic medication and DBS exert shared therapeutic modulation of cortex-STN network activity, both converging on suppression of high beta synchrony mediated by the hyperdirect pathway. However, the two therapies differ at the mesoscale: dopamine modulates local neural population activity through receptor-specific mechanisms, while DBS disrupts pathological synchrony through direct electrical interference. Werner et al. (2025) provided further causal evidence by delivering STN stimulation at specific beta frequencies (10, 16, 20, 26, and 30 Hz) in 14 Parkinson's patients during magnetoencephalography. Beta-band DBS accentuated the lateralization of hemispheric beta power and slowed finger tapping proportionally, with the effect outlasting stimulation and spanning the 8-26 Hz range -- consistent with a remote cortical response rather than simple entrainment. See [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]] and [[clinical-applications]] for broader context on brain stimulation approaches.

## Non-invasive striatal stimulation and reinforcement learning

The causal role of striatal oscillatory dynamics in reinforcement learning has been confirmed through non-invasive deep brain stimulation in healthy humans. Vassiliadis et al. (2024) used **transcranial temporal interference stimulation** (tTIS) to target the striatum at different frequencies during a reinforcement motor learning task with concurrent neuroimaging. Stimulation at 80 Hz abolished the benefits of reinforcement feedback on motor learning and increased the neuromodulatory influence of the striatum on frontal areas, while stimulation at 20 Hz had no such disruptive effect. This frequency-specific dissociation suggests that high-gamma-range activity in the striatum is critical for integrating reinforcement signals into motor skill acquisition, while beta-frequency activity serves a different functional role -- consistent with the broader distinction between gamma as a substrate for local computation and beta as a carrier of long-range status quo signaling. The tTIS approach opens new possibilities for studying corticostriatal circuit function in healthy populations and for developing non-invasive therapeutic interventions targeting striatal oscillatory dysfunction in neurological and psychiatric conditions.
