# Development and Aging of Brain Oscillations

The brain's oscillatory architecture undergoes profound reorganization across the lifespan, from the delta/theta-dominated rhythms of neonatal cortex to the mature alpha-beta-gamma hierarchy of adulthood, and then through a characteristic pattern of age-related changes that includes slowing of [[individual-alpha-frequency]], flattening of the [[aperiodic-dynamics|aperiodic spectral slope]], and declining gamma circuit integrity. These changes are not merely quantitative shifts in power or frequency but reflect fundamental transformations in cortical circuit maturation, excitation-inhibition balance, and thalamocortical timing -- making the developmental trajectory of neural oscillations a window into the assembly and eventual degradation of the brain's computational architecture.

## Neonatal and Infant Oscillations

The neonatal brain is dominated by slow oscillations. Delta (0.5-4 Hz) and theta (4-8 Hz) activity constitute the primary rhythmic output, reflecting the immature state of cortical inhibitory circuits and incomplete myelination of long-range connections. The characteristic "trace alternant" pattern of neonatal sleep -- bursts of higher-frequency activity separated by periods of relative quiescence -- represents a developmental precursor to the continuous oscillatory patterns of the mature brain. Quiet sleep in neonates appears to represent a state of transient network disconnection interspersed with synchronized bursts, a pattern widely implicated in shaping synaptic connectivity and supporting developmental plasticity. Clear posterior alpha rhythm is absent at birth; instead, the earliest rhythmic activity in the alpha range appears as a rudimentary mu rhythm detectable by 4-6 months, peaking at approximately 5.4 Hz -- far below the adult alpha frequency. This slow precursor reflects the immature state of [[thalamocortical-circuits]], whose resonant properties depend on GABAergic inhibition timescales and HCN channel expression that have not yet reached adult levels.

A comprehensive longitudinal study of 592 healthy infants aged 2-44 months (He et al., 2024) using spectral parameterization revealed age-dependent nonlinear changes suggestive of distinct milestones in early brain maturation. Among the most striking findings was the presence and then absence of a transient 9-10 Hz **alpha peak** between 2-6 months of age, suggesting a brief window of thalamocortical resonance that vanishes before the later, sustained alpha rhythm emerges. Additional developmental landmarks include nonlinear changes in high beta peaks (20-30 Hz) between 4-18 months and the emergence of a **low beta peak** (12-20 Hz) in some infants after six months -- hypothesized to reflect maturation of thalamocortical network development (He et al., 2024). Preliminary evidence from infants under GABA-modulating anesthesia showed that those with a detectable low beta peak exhibited higher anesthesia-induced alpha coherence, consistent with more mature thalamocortical connectivity. Rapid increases in **aperiodic offset** were observed in the first year, consistent with known increases in brain volume and synaptogenesis, with minimal change between ages 1 and 3 years. Notable regional differences emerged along the posterior-anterior axis, with posterior regions showing larger offset values in the first year as well as lower theta power after 9-12 months compared to anterior regions.

## Childhood: Emergence and Acceleration of Alpha

Throughout childhood, the posterior alpha rhythm gradually emerges and accelerates. Initial alpha-range activity begins around 4-6 Hz and progressively increases in frequency as cortical circuits mature. Theta activity remains dominant in frontal regions well into childhood, reflecting the protracted development of prefrontal cortical circuits. The acceleration of alpha frequency during childhood parallels several maturational processes: synaptic pruning that refines cortical connectivity, progressive myelination of thalamocortical and cortico-cortical pathways that shortens conduction delays, and maturation of GABAergic interneuron networks that sharpen the timing of inhibitory feedback loops. By late childhood, the posterior alpha rhythm approaches but has not yet reached adult frequencies. The [[canonical-eeg-bands]] architecture is still under construction during this period, with frequency band boundaries less sharply defined than in the mature brain.

McSweeney et al. (2023) provided a detailed characterization of these trajectories in a large sample of 502 children aged 4-11 years by separating [[aperiodic-dynamics|aperiodic]] and periodic components of the EEG spectrum. They found **quadratic age-related effects** for both aperiodic offset and exponent: both parameters increased from age 4 to approximately 7 years, then decreased through age 11. Periodic [[alpha-oscillations|alpha peak frequency]] increased monotonically as a function of age, and aperiodic measures as well as periodic alpha power were larger in magnitude during eyes-closed compared to eyes-open baselines. The non-monotonic trajectory of the aperiodic exponent during childhood -- rising and then falling -- complicates the simple narrative of progressive E/I shift and suggests that the maturation of inhibitory circuits passes through a phase of over-expression before settling into the adult equilibrium. Age-related decreases in relative slow-wave activity ([[delta-oscillations|delta]] and [[theta-oscillations|theta]]) emerge from approximately 4 years of age, followed by developmental increases in higher-frequency activity (alpha, [[beta-oscillations|beta]], and [[gamma-oscillations|gamma]]), a progression attributable to increasing neural communication speed driven by myelination and axon diameter growth. A longitudinal study by Sacks et al. (2025) confirmed these nonlinear aperiodic trajectories from infancy to age 7 in a US community sample (N = 391), with variation by brain region and [[sex-differences-in-oscillations|sex]], and found that maternal anxiety was negatively associated with slope at 3 years but positively associated with slope at 7 years -- suggesting that early environmental factors may shape the developmental trajectory of cortical E/I balance.

## Adolescence: Gamma Circuit Maturation

During adolescence, alpha frequency reaches adult values near 10 Hz and the full oscillatory hierarchy comes online. A particularly important developmental milestone is the maturation of [[gamma-oscillations]] circuits. The 40 Hz auditory steady-state response (ASSR) -- a direct measure of cortical gamma resonance -- is weak or absent in young children and increases monotonically through adolescence, with inter-trial coherence rising at approximately 0.01 per year and reaching maximum around age 22. This trajectory directly parallels the maturation of parvalbumin-positive (PV+) fast-spiking interneuron networks, the cellular substrate of gamma generation. During this period, PV+ interneurons undergo progressive myelination, GABA-A receptor subunit composition shifts from alpha-2 to alpha-1 subtypes (which have faster kinetics suited to gamma-frequency inhibition), and perisomatic inhibitory synapses reach adult density. The parallel maturation of alpha frequency and gamma circuit integrity during adolescence reflects the coordinated development of [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations|GABAergic neurotransmission]] across multiple timescales.

Recent animal work has sharpened the mechanistic picture considerably. Bitzenhofer (2023) reviewed evidence that fast rhythmic activity in the prefrontal cortex becomes prominent during the second postnatal week in mice, initially at approximately 15 Hz, and progressively accelerates to stabilize within the gamma frequency range (30-80 Hz) during the fourth postnatal week. In a study of adolescent and adult rhesus monkeys performing a delayed response task, Wang et al. (2022) recorded from dorsolateral prefrontal cortex areas 8a and 46 and found **strong gamma frequency oscillations in the adolescent prefrontal cortex** that exceeded adult levels -- a counterintuitive finding suggesting that the developmental strengthening of inhibition in the adult cortex increasingly suppresses spontaneous and task-irrelevant cells from being integrated into stimulus-evoked gamma rhythms. A 2025 study combining in vivo electrophysiology with selective optogenetic manipulations in mouse medial prefrontal cortex demonstrated that PV+ interneurons follow an age-dependent functional maturation enabling them to operate within the classical gamma frequency range from adolescence onwards, while somatostatin-positive (SOM+) interneurons show no such age-dependent integration and instead modulate beta oscillations across development (Chini et al., 2025). This **SOM+ to PV+ interneuron switch** was identified as a core mechanism of gamma ontogeny and the emergence of crosshemispheric prefrontal synchrony. Furthermore, Bhatt et al. (2024) demonstrated that gamma oscillation plasticity is mediated specifically via PV+ interneurons: gamma oscillations (30-80 Hz) require and potentiate glutamatergic transmission onto PV+ cells, establishing a direct link between synaptic plasticity and the developmental stabilization of gamma circuits. EEG time-frequency analysis in children aged 8-15 years has revealed a developmental shift in gamma oscillation around age 11, with younger groups exhibiting broadly distributed gamma activity with higher amplitudes that progressively narrows and refines with maturation (Schneider et al., 2023). During adolescence, theta-band connectivity also develops along an anterior-posterior axis, with decoupling between association areas linked to the emergence of [[working-memory|cognitive control]].

## Adulthood: Peak Performance and Oscillatory Stability

The adult brain represents the peak of oscillatory organization. Individual alpha frequency reaches its maximum at approximately 28 years, with a population mean around 10.28 Hz. At this point, the full frequency hierarchy -- from delta through gamma -- operates with mature cross-frequency coupling relationships and well-defined band boundaries organized according to golden ratio principles. Menetrey and Pascucci (2026) demonstrated extraordinary stability of individual oscillatory profiles in adulthood: alpha component fingerprints achieved 99.88% identification accuracy across 5-year intervals, with test-retest reliability of R-squared = 0.63-0.77 for individual archetype scores. This stability establishes that the oscillatory architecture, once mature, constitutes a remarkably stable neural trait -- consistent with the 81% heritability of [[individual-alpha-frequency]] reported by Smit et al. (2006).

## Aging: Component Rebalancing and E/I Shifts

The traditional view that aging produces uniform "alpha slowing" has been overturned by recent evidence. Menetrey and Pascucci (2026) showed that the age-related decrease in [[individual-alpha-frequency]] -- from approximately 10.28 Hz at peak to around 7 Hz by age 90 -- reflects component rebalancing rather than uniform deceleration. The [[alpha-oscillations|alpha rhythm]] comprises at least three discrete archetypes (slow, middle, and fast alpha) with distinct cortical generators. With aging, the slow alpha component (~8-9 Hz, ventral stream origin) increases in relative dominance while the fast alpha component (~11-12 Hz, dorsal stream origin) decreases, shifting the composite peak frequency downward without any single oscillator actually changing its frequency.

In parallel, the [[aperiodic-dynamics|aperiodic spectral slope]] flattens with age, reflecting a shift in excitation-inhibition balance toward greater excitation. This E/I shift has been linked to declining GABAergic inhibition and reduced PV+ interneuron function. Theta power increases frontally in older adults, and gamma deficits emerge -- particularly in the 40 Hz ASSR, which declines from its peak at age 22. Regional specificity has become clearer with recent MEG-based source reconstruction: in healthy aging, caudal theta and high-gamma power decrease while rostral alpha and [[beta-oscillations|beta]] power increase, and delta power decreases globally with peaks in bilateral middle temporal gyri (Gómez et al., 2025). Alzheimer's disease accelerates these changes, with posterior alpha peaks dropping from approximately 9.4 Hz to 8.0 Hz and theta power increasing, providing biomarkers with approximately 90% predictive accuracy for conversion from mild cognitive impairment to Alzheimer's.

Recent work on the functional consequences of age-related aperiodic flattening has demonstrated that flatter spectral slopes in older adults mediate declines in [[working-memory]] performance. Younger adults consistently show steeper aperiodic slopes than older adults, and flatter exponents during both fixation and late retention periods are associated with greater **neural inefficiency** during stimulus processing (Tabi et al., 2025). However, the relationship between aperiodic exponent and cognition in aging is modulated by educational attainment: in older adults with high education, a higher exponent paradoxically predicted slower processing speed and less working memory capacity, while the opposite trend held for those with low education (Pinto et al., 2024), suggesting that cognitive reserve interacts with cortical E/I balance in complex ways.

## The Aperiodic Exponent as a Lifespan Marker

The aperiodic exponent provides a unifying measure across the developmental trajectory. In childhood, the spectral slope is steep, reflecting strong inhibitory dominance as GABAergic circuits mature. Through adolescence and adulthood, the exponent stabilizes as E/I balance reaches its mature equilibrium. With aging, progressive flattening reflects the gradual erosion of inhibitory tone. This trajectory of the aperiodic exponent -- from steep in youth through moderate in adulthood to flat in old age -- parallels and may partly explain the developmental and degenerative changes observed in oscillatory frequencies. Marsicano et al. (2026) showed that even within young adults, the aperiodic exponent correlates with ADHD traits (r = -0.29), linking flatter slopes to increased cortical excitability and altered temporal processing.

A landmark 2025 study by Schaworonkow et al. used a large international cohort of 1,563 participants aged 5-95 during resting state with eyes closed to delineate the full lifespan trajectories of aperiodic and periodic EEG components. They identified two fundamental developmental patterns: a **monotonic decrease** in aperiodic activity and a **growth-then-decline** pattern for periodic activity, with both components showing inflection points around age 20 and transitioning to a stable senescent phase around age 40. Spatially, anterior regions mainly exhibit aperiodic activity while periodic activity concentrates on posterior regions, and these topographic patterns remain stable throughout life. Critically, the periodic component's growth-and-decline trajectory aligns with known timecourses of GABAergic function and myelination, while the monotonically decreasing trajectory of aperiodic activity mirrors fundamental biomarkers of biological aging such as DNA methylation and telomere length. Transforming age to a logarithmic scale simplifies these nonlinear trajectories into a linear decreasing model (aperiodic) and a piecewise concave linear model (periodic), providing a parsimonious framework for quantifying maturation and identifying neurological deviations. These findings refine the earlier U-shaped narrative: the aperiodic exponent decreases monotonically across the lifespan, but periodic power follows a distinct inverted-U trajectory peaking in early adulthood.

Complementing these spectral findings, a 2025 PNAS study demonstrated that **temporal autocorrelation** (AC) of neural signals is the most robust single predictor of brain age across the lifespan, with AC at a 36 ms delay achieving a correlation of r = 0.75 between predicted and true age (mean absolute error = 10.34 years). Distinct patterns of AC within visual and temporal cortex were most informative, and these simple linear time-series measures outperformed conventional spectral descriptors for age prediction -- suggesting that the temporal dynamics of neural signals carry fundamental developmental and aging information beyond what power spectra alone reveal.

## Clinical Implications: Neurodevelopment and Neurodegeneration

The developmental trajectory of oscillations explains why both neurodevelopmental and neurodegenerative disorders manifest as frequency disturbances.

### Neurodevelopmental Disorders

In ADHD, Marsicano et al. (2026) found that higher ADHD trait levels are associated with faster IAF (r = 0.44) and flatter aperiodic slopes, suggesting that ADHD involves an E/I balance shifted toward excitation with corresponding alterations in temporal processing windows. Children with ADHD often show elevated **theta/beta ratios** (TBR) reflecting immature oscillatory patterns, though the replicability of this finding has been debated -- several studies fail to reproduce the elevated TBR pattern, and a 2025 review noted that ADHD oscillatory biomarkers remain inconsistent across studies (Sá et al., 2025). More promisingly, a small but growing body of research suggests that children with ADHD show **delayed maturation of the aperiodic exponent** from ages 6-12, with the dynamic aperiodic exponent following a negative quadratic trajectory in both ADHD and typically developing children but with group differences suggesting that aperiodic activity may be a candidate marker of cortical efficiency in childhood (Tran et al., 2024). The association between aperiodic exponent and cognition was driven by performance on executive functioning tests, reinforcing the link between aperiodic dynamics and higher-order cortical processes relevant to ADHD.

In autism spectrum disorder (ASD), Finn et al. (2023) demonstrated that **peak alpha frequency** follows a distinct maturational trajectory in autistic children. In a sample of 45 autistic and 34 neurotypical children aged 8-18, age was positively associated with [[individual-alpha-frequency|PAF]] among neurotypical children but not among autistic children; conversely, nonverbal IQ -- but not age -- was positively associated with PAF among autistic children. This dissociation suggests that PAF indexes distinct trajectories of neural maturation in autism, with cognitive ability rather than chronological age driving the development of thalamocortical resonance. A proposed U-shaped spectral profile in ASD -- with elevated power at both low and high frequencies -- has been both supported and contradicted across studies, highlighting the heterogeneity of the condition.

### Neurodegeneration and Cognitive Decline

At the other end of the lifespan, Alzheimer's disease produces alpha slowing and gamma deficits that may reflect accelerated aging of the same circuits -- particularly the PV+ interneuron networks whose developmental maturation defines adolescence and whose degradation characterizes neurodegeneration. Recent comprehensive reviews have consolidated the EEG biomarker evidence for AD: the most prominent changes in amnestic mild cognitive impairment (aMCI) are decreased beta power and increased delta and theta power, with changes strongest in temporal regions. Reduced alpha-band coherence -- especially in the right central-parietal region -- may serve as an early biomarker for aMCI (Chen et al., 2025). Specific power ratios such as the alpha/theta ratio and the delta-theta/alpha-beta ratio (DTABR) have proven useful for differentiating AD stages. However, 2024 clinical guidelines issued by both the US National Institute on Aging-Alzheimer's Association and European Neuroscience Societies stopped short of endorsing routine use of resting-state EEG biomarkers in clinical workup, while acknowledging consistent evidence that delta, theta, and alpha abnormalities correlate with established AD biomarkers. Simmatis et al. (2025) developed an automated end-to-end EEG pipeline for detecting and monitoring AD progression, successfully identifying alterations in delta and theta band power, network connectivity disruptions, and increased slowing, with strong correlations between EEG-derived metrics and MMSE scores.

The therapeutic potential of frequency-targeted interventions has advanced significantly. Long-term clinical follow-up of **40 Hz gamma sensory stimulation** (GENUS) in mild AD patients has shown that three months of daily audiovisual stimulation produced lesser ventricular dilation and hippocampal atrophy, increased functional connectivity in the [[default-mode-network]], and improved performance on the face-name association delayed recall test. In a 2-year follow-up of five patients, three female patients with late-onset AD retained strong EEG entrainment and showed less cognitive decline compared to matched controls, with plasma pTau217 reductions of 47% and 19% in two participants -- suggesting potential disease-modifying effects (Chan et al., 2025). The shared involvement of [[thalamocortical-circuits]] and GABAergic inhibition in both developmental maturation and age-related decline positions the oscillatory trajectory as a bridge between developmental neuroscience and geriatric neurology, with [[clinical-applications]] spanning biomarker development, early detection, and frequency-targeted interventions like [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation|neurofeedback]] and 40 Hz entrainment therapy.

## Critical Periods and Oscillatory Plasticity

The concept of **critical periods** -- windows of heightened neural plasticity during which experience can permanently shape circuit architecture -- is intimately linked to oscillatory development. Hensch and colleagues (2023) identified a transient gamma-power signature of critical period plasticity in the mouse visual cortex: monocular deprivation acutely induced a brief (<3 hour) peak in EEG gamma power (~40 Hz) specifically within visual cortex, but only when the critical period was open -- in juvenile mice or in adults after dark-rearing, Lynx1 deletion, or diazepam-rescued GAD65 deficiency. This transient gamma burst reflected a rapid cortical interneuronal gamma (ING) rhythm in networks of PV+ cells bearing gap junctions, which effectively dissociated thalamic input from cortical spiking and drove rapid loss of previously strong thalamocortical-to-PV connections through spike-timing-dependent plasticity rules. The finding that a specific oscillatory signature -- a transient 40 Hz peak -- marks the open state of critical periods has important implications for understanding why [[gamma-oscillations]] circuit development during adolescence may represent a prolonged critical period for higher cognitive functions, and why disruption of PV+ interneuron maturation during this window is associated with [[clinical-applications|neurodevelopmental and psychiatric disorders]].

The broader principle that emerges is one of oscillatory plasticity cascading across brain regions according to a sensorimotor-association axis: sensory cortices undergo their critical periods earliest, while association cortices -- particularly prefrontal regions -- maintain extended windows of plasticity through adolescence (Sydnor et al., 2023). The age-related reductions in aperiodic activity, together with the observed shift of maximal aperiodic activity from posterior to anterior regions during childhood and adolescence, may reflect this progressive cortical specialization and functional reorganization, with each region's critical period closing as its oscillatory architecture stabilizes.

## Premature Birth and Oscillatory Development

Premature birth provides a natural perturbation that illuminates the dependence of oscillatory maturation on gestational timing and early environmental exposure. Jansen et al. (2023) found that preterm birth **accelerates the maturation of the aperiodic EEG component** in the visual cortex: preterm infants showed a significantly flatter 1/f slope compared to term infants at equivalent ages, a result of decreased spectral power in the theta and alpha bands that correlated with the degree of prematurity. This accelerated aperiodic flattening may reflect premature exposure to the extrauterine environment driving early shifts in E/I balance before cortical inhibitory circuits are fully prepared. EEG microstate analysis of preterm infants has revealed acceleration of brain dynamics between the preterm and term period, with microstate duration shortening from 250-300 ms at 30 weeks postmenstrual age to 150-200 ms after 37 weeks, indicative of intensive white matter myelination and cortical microstructure maturation during this interval (Fabrizi et al., 2025).

A 2025 study by Amorim et al. used spectral parameterization of high-density EEG in 41 very preterm infants and 13 full-term controls to characterize aperiodic and theta activity during active/REM sleep at term-equivalent age and at 2 months corrected age. Lower gestational age at birth was associated with lower aperiodic exponent, and [[sex-differences-in-oscillations|male sex]] was characterized by lower theta power. Notably, higher theta power correlated with lower cortical fractional anisotropy (measured by diffusion MRI), consistent with the interpretation that theta oscillations in infancy index cortical maturation -- as cortical microstructure becomes more complex and anisotropic, theta power paradoxically decreases. Longer gestation was associated with steeper spectral slopes in both neonates and toddlers, suggesting that gestational duration has significant and potentially lasting effects on brain activity with implications for later behavioral and cognitive outcomes. These findings establish EEG spectral parameterization combined with spatial analysis as a sensitive framework for characterizing postnatal brain maturation and identifying early vulnerabilities associated with prematurity and perinatal adversity.
