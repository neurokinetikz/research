# Hippocampal Memory

Hippocampal oscillations constitute the primary neural mechanism for encoding, consolidating, and retrieving episodic memories. [[theta-oscillations]] (2-9 Hz) provide a temporal scaffold for sequential coding via phase precession and theta sequences, compressing behavioral timescales by roughly 10:1 into windows suitable for spike-timing-dependent plasticity. [[gamma-oscillations]] nest within theta cycles to encode individual memory items, with slow and fast gamma sub-bands serving dissociable retrieval and encoding functions routed through distinct hippocampal inputs. [[sharp-wave-ripples]] compress and replay experience during rest and [[sleep-oscillations]], coupling with thalamocortical spindles and cortical slow oscillations to drive systems consolidation. Recent work has substantially refined this picture: phase precession has been demonstrated in humans during non-spatial, naturalistic memory tasks (Zheng et al., 2024), awake sharp-wave ripples have been shown to selectively "tag" experiences for subsequent sleep consolidation (Yang & Buzsaki, 2024), replay has been dissociated from ripples themselves (Widloski & Foster, 2025), and optogenetic boosting of large ripples during sleep is sufficient to rescue otherwise-forgotten memories (Robinson et al., 2025). The functional heterogeneity of hippocampal populations -- divergent electrophysiological responses, deep versus superficial CA1 sublayers, rate versus temporal coding trade-offs -- reveals a far more complex picture than classical "canonical theta" models suggest.

## Theta Phase Precession and Temporal Compression

As a rodent traverses a place field, hippocampal place cells fire at progressively earlier phases of the ongoing theta oscillation -- a phenomenon called **phase precession**. This compresses a spatial trajectory spanning seconds into a single theta cycle of roughly 125 ms, achieving approximately 10:1 temporal compression (Sejnowski, 2025). Within each theta cycle, sequences of place cells fire in order representing past, present, and future locations at the 100-150 ms timescale (Dupret et al., 2025). This compression is functionally critical: it brings the relative spike timing of sequentially active neurons into the +/-10 ms window required for spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP), allowing spatial experience to be directly converted into synaptic weight changes (Sejnowski, 2025). See [[brain-computation]] for more on spike timing as a computational primitive.

Zheng et al. (2024) provided the first direct evidence that phase precession supports non-spatial, naturalistic memory in humans. Recording single-neuron activity and local field potentials in the medial temporal lobe of 22 neurosurgical patients watching and recalling movie clips, they found that bouts of theta phase precession occurred following **cognitive event boundaries** during encoding and following stimulus onsets during retrieval. Critically, phase precession strength predicted memory accuracy: stronger precession during encoding predicted better subsequent recall, and stronger precession during retrieval predicted correct recognition and temporal order judgments. Different neurons exhibited precession in different task periods, indicating dynamic recruitment rather than a fixed precessing population. This extends phase precession beyond the rodent spatial navigation context, demonstrating that the same temporal compression mechanism that encodes spatial trajectories in rodents also organizes episodic event sequences in the human hippocampus.

Computational modeling has also advanced. A 2025 study proposed that phase precession may emerge from **chimera dynamics** -- a state in which synchronized and unsynchronized neuronal oscillators coexist within the same network (Frontiers in Neural Circuits, 2025). By adjusting intrinsic oscillator frequencies, the frequency ratio between the synchronized population (~8 Hz, matching the local field potential theta) and unsynchronized phase-precessing neurons (~9 Hz) reproduces the characteristic ~1 Hz difference that drives the progressive phase advance across successive theta cycles. This provides a network-level mechanism complementary to the single-neuron dual-oscillator models originally proposed by O'Keefe and Recce.

## Theta-Gamma Coupling: The Encoding-Retrieval Double Dissociation

Huang et al. (2026, UCL) provided the first noninvasive human evidence that [[cross-frequency-coupling]] between theta and gamma in the hippocampal formation supports sequential planning during navigation. Using MEG and an abstract grid task (N=23), they found that hippocampal theta power (2-5 Hz) increased with distance to a hidden goal during planning, while theta-gamma phase-amplitude coupling (PAC) increased as the goal was approached -- exactly as the Lisman-Idiart model predicts, since fewer remaining locations concentrate gamma activity within each theta cycle. A striking double dissociation emerged between gamma sub-bands: fast [[gamma-oscillations]] (70-140 Hz) from entorhinal cortex dominated during novel path planning (encoding), while slow gamma (30-70 Hz) from hippocampus dominated during retrieval of previously traversed paths. This replicates in healthy humans what Colgin et al. (2009) demonstrated in rodent CA1, confirming cross-species conservation of the theta-gamma architecture. Friedrich et al. (2026) extended this principle beyond spatial memory, showing that frontal-midline theta (4-7 Hz) from dorsomedial prefrontal cortex dynamically coordinates posterior gamma (60-70 Hz) amplitude during both visual [[working-memory]] and social cognition tasks, with gamma locked to the theta trough under high cognitive load -- consistent with PAC as a domain-general computational mechanism rather than a task-specific phenomenon.

## The Broadband vs. Oscillatory Theta Debate

Huang et al. (2026, Stanford) used intracranial EEG in 26 epilepsy patients (187 hippocampal sites) and explicitly challenged pure oscillatory interpretations of hippocampal theta. Raw power spectra did not reveal a narrowband "bump" above the 1/f curve in the 1-8 Hz range. Both functionally distinct site types showed low-frequency activity increases spanning the entire 1-8 Hz band, and aperiodic exponent changes accompanied these power changes. The authors stated they "did not see clear oscillatory activity in a narrow theta band," suggesting that heightened "theta" power in prior literature may partially reflect broadband spectral tilt rather than pure oscillatory processes. Blue sites (HFA-decreasing) showed significant steepening of the 1/f slope during retrieval, consistent with increased inhibition. This has important methodological implications: future work must separate genuine oscillatory theta from [[aperiodic-dynamics]] using spectral parameterization tools such as FOOOF. Notably, the UCL MEG study verified their theta effects survived FOOOF controls, confirming genuine oscillatory dynamics in that paradigm.

## Causal Evidence: Optogenetic Theta Disruption

Joshi et al. (2026, eLife) provided causal evidence that theta-timescale temporal coordination is necessary for hippocampal-dependent learning. Using closed-loop, theta-phase-specific optogenetic stimulation of medial septal parvalbumin neurons in rats, they disrupted theta sequences during locomotion without abolishing theta entirely. Place fields were preserved, but temporal coding -- the fine-timescale sequential ordering of spikes within theta cycles -- was profoundly disrupted. Memory-demanding spatial alternation trials dropped to chance performance (approximately 0.5 vs 0.9 in controls), while simple place-action associations remained intact. Critically, sharp-wave ripple replay during immobility was completely preserved in rate, duration, and replay speed, yet learning still failed. This demonstrates a fundamental dissociation: theta sequences during active behavior are required for encoding, and replay alone is not sufficient when the initial theta-organized encoding is disrupted.

## Divergent Hippocampal Populations

The Stanford intracranial study (Huang et al. 2026) also revealed a fundamental organizational principle: functionally distinct neuronal populations with opposing electrophysiological responses are intermingled in a "salt-and-pepper" mosaic throughout the hippocampus. Approximately 31% of sites showed increased high-frequency activity during retrieval ("red sites"), 35% showed decreased HFA ("blue sites"), and 31% showed no change. Memory success depended not on activity magnitude at individual sites but on temporal coordination between populations -- the correlation between HFA at red sites and low-frequency activity at blue sites predicted hit versus miss trials (p < 0.0001). Red sites responded before blue sites (0.64 vs 0.89 seconds post-stimulus), and causal connectivity mapping revealed that posterior cingulate preferentially connects to red sites while dorsal anterior insula connects to blue sites.

## Population Codes and CA1 Sublayer Scaffolding

Dupret et al. (2025) proposed that brain memory circuits implement trade-offs between competing population code demands -- veridicality, storability, accessibility, stability, accuracy, plasticity, flexibility, and generalizability -- operating within an optimal "safe zone" of neural activity space. The hippocampus employs a scaffolding principle during spatial exploration: a stable module of higher-firing cells in the CA1 deep pyramidal sublayer provides a rigid spatio-contextual backbone, while lower-firing cells in the CA1 superficial sublayer integrate new motifs on demand. This deep-superficial architecture supports across-timescale multiplexing: a single neural population performs multiple computations simultaneously, with theta-timescale sequential activity (100-150 ms) encoding past/present/future and ripple-timescale replay (50-100 ms) supporting consolidation. The framework highlights how rate coding and temporal coding coexist in complementary sublayers.

## Sharp-Wave Ripples and Replay

During quiet wakefulness and slow-wave sleep, hippocampal CA1 generates [[sharp-wave-ripples]] (80-200 Hz) that compress and reactivate neural sequences experienced during prior theta-organized behavior. Replay during SWRs achieves approximately 20x temporal compression relative to real-time experience, with replay speed around 10 m/s (Joshi et al., 2026; Dupret et al., 2025). Sharp-wave ripple replay operates at the 50-100 ms timescale, faster than theta sequences, and brings spike timing squarely into the STDP window (Sejnowski, 2025). SWR events carry content -- specific spatial trajectories, specific memory items -- and their disruption impairs consolidation, yet the Joshi et al. finding that preserved replay cannot rescue disrupted theta encoding underscores that replay is a consolidation mechanism, not a substitute for initial encoding.

Yang and Buzsaki (2024, Science) demonstrated that awake sharp-wave ripples function as a **memory selection mechanism**. Using large-scale neural recordings in mice navigating a maze, they tracked how successive traversals were represented by continuously drifting neural populations. When brain state changed during reward consumption, SWRs occurred on some but not all trials, and their spike content decoded the specific trial blocks surrounding them. During postexperience sleep, SWRs selectively replayed those trial blocks that had been most frequently reactivated by awake SWRs -- establishing that waking ripples effectively "tag" experiences for subsequent consolidation. This solves a long-standing puzzle: given that not all experiences are consolidated, the awake SWR provides the neurophysiological mechanism by which the hippocampus selects which episodes to preserve.

Widloski and Foster (2025, Nature Communications) challenged the assumption that ripples and replay are inseparable by demonstrating that **replay can occur without ripples**. In rats performing an open-field spatial memory task, sequence replay events were detected both with and without accompanying ripple oscillations. Ripples were confined to discrete **ripple fields** -- spatially restricted regions defined over the virtual locations depicted during replay, independent of the rat's physical location. These ripple fields were direction-independent, stable within sessions, adapted to environmental changes such as barriers, and were conserved across animals exposed to the same environment. The results indicate that replay is a broader phenomenon than previously appreciated, with ripples selectively tagging a subset of replays linked to learning or novelty rather than being a prerequisite for all sequential reactivation.

## Systems Consolidation and Sleep

Memory consolidation depends on a hierarchical oscillatory triad during NREM sleep: cortical slow oscillations (less than 1 Hz), thalamocortical spindles (12-16 Hz), and hippocampal sharp-wave ripples, temporally nested so that SWRs occur in spindle troughs during slow oscillation up-states (see [[sleep-oscillations]]). Creery et al. (2022, PNAS) provided direct intracranial evidence for this architecture using targeted memory reactivation (TMR) in epilepsy patients. Playing memory-associated sounds during slow-wave sleep produced a 36% memory advantage for cued versus uncued items. Theta (4-8 Hz) and sigma/spindle (12-16 Hz) power increased differentially for memory-associated cues versus novel sounds, serving as reactivation markers. Gamma power (20-100 Hz) correlated specifically with the magnitude of memory improvement, suggesting gamma reflects the actual consolidation strengthening process while theta and spindles reflect reactivation. All effects were localized to medial temporal lobe contacts.

Robinson et al. (2025, Neuron) provided causal evidence that **large sharp-wave ripples** are the specific SWR subtype that drives hippocampo-cortical memory reactivation during sleep. In mice, only a minority of sleep SWRs were associated with memory reactivation in both hippocampus and prefrontal cortex, but large-amplitude SWRs selectively increased following new learning. Using closed-loop optogenetic boosting to amplify SWRs during post-learning sleep, the researchers enhanced ensemble memory reactivation in both hippocampus and PFC and -- remarkably -- enabled mice to successfully recall experiences from a learning paradigm that was otherwise insufficient for consolidation. Even mice engineered with cognitive impairments showed restored memory consolidation after SWR boosting, establishing a direct causal link between large ripple amplitude, hippocampo-cortical reactivation, and behavioral memory. This complements the Yang and Buzsaki (2024) finding on awake SWR selection: waking ripples tag which experiences to consolidate, and large sleep ripples execute the consolidation by driving coordinated hippocampal-cortical reactivation.

Personalized **targeted memory reactivation** (TMR) protocols have also advanced. A 2025 study demonstrated that adjusting cue delivery based on individual retrieval performance and task difficulty significantly reduced memory decay and improved error correction compared to uniform TMR, with effects mediated by enhanced slow oscillation and spindle activity (npj Science of Learning, 2025). These findings support TMR as a practical translational tool, though whether awake TMR is effective remains debated -- one 2024 study found that contextual targeted memory reactivation during awake rest enhanced discovery of new, non-directly trained associations, while another found no benefit of awake TMR for directly cued memories (Schechtman, 2024).

## Prestimulus Theta and Retrieval Readiness

Addante et al. (2011, PNAS) demonstrated that prestimulus [[theta-oscillations]] (4-8 Hz) predict successful episodic memory retrieval before the retrieval cue is even presented. Theta power was enhanced 300-0 ms before test items that subsequently elicited accurate source recollection, but not before items recognized without contextual detail. This prestimulus effect was specific to source memory (recollection of episodic context), not item recognition. Frontal prestimulus theta predicted parietal poststimulus theta (r-squared = 0.36, p < 0.01), suggesting a preparation-enables-retrieval cascade. This implies that memory retrieval is not purely stimulus-driven but depends on ongoing oscillatory states -- a theta "gate" that must be open for full recollective access. See [[attention-and-perception]] for related state-dependent processing effects.

## Theta-Gamma PAC as a Cognitive Biomarker

Friedrich et al. (2026) showed that theta-gamma PAC serves as a sensitive biomarker of cognitive function. In a study of 98 participants, frontal-midline theta (4-7 Hz) phase from dorsomedial prefrontal cortex coordinated posterior gamma (60-70 Hz) amplitude in a load-dependent manner: high cognitive demand produced gamma locked to the theta trough (optimal coupling), while low demand shifted gamma away from the trough. Individuals with elevated autistic traits showed disrupted or rigid coupling patterns, linking PAC dynamics to behavioral outcomes across social cognition and visual working memory. FOOOF validation confirmed a genuine oscillatory theta peak at approximately 5.5 Hz, and the strongest PAC effects at 60-70 Hz corresponded to the high-gamma range. This work supports PAC as a translational biomarker for cognitive and clinical assessment. See also [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]] for the molecular substrates underlying theta-gamma coupling.

## Spike Timing and Plasticity

Sejnowski (2025) proposed a unified framework linking spike timing precision, cortical traveling waves, and STDP. Traveling waves propagating at 0.1-0.8 m/s along unmyelinated horizontal fibers trigger precisely timed spikes via an inhibitory rebound mechanism: parvalbumin-positive basket cells hyperpolarize pyramidal cell bodies, deinactivating sodium channels and producing precisely timed rebound spikes within the +/-10 ms STDP window. During theta, this mechanism enables phase precession to directly drive Hebbian learning; during sharp-wave ripples operating at approximately 100 Hz, the rapid replay of compressed sequences triggers STDP across many synapses within seconds. Sejnowski proposed that STDP triggered by traveling waves creates a "second-tier temporary network" of transiently potentiated small dendritic spines lasting hours, overlaying the permanent sensorimotor network -- a substrate for long-term working memory that bridges the gap between transient oscillatory states and lasting synaptic change.

## The Lisman-Idiart Model and Working Memory

The theta-gamma nesting architecture provides a mechanistic basis for [[working-memory]] capacity limits. The Lisman-Idiart model predicts that capacity equals the ratio of theta to gamma cycle lengths -- approximately 6-8 gamma cycles fit within one theta cycle, explaining the 7 +/- 2 item limit. Huang et al. (2026, UCL) confirmed both predictions: theta power increases with sequence length (more items require more theta to maintain organization), and PAC increases with shorter sequences (fewer items mean more concentrated gamma and stronger coupling). The domain-generality of this mechanism -- demonstrated for both passive working memory maintenance and active spatial navigation planning -- suggests theta-gamma coupling is a fundamental neural computational primitive rather than a task-specific process.

## Hippocampal-Cortical High-Frequency Oscillatory Dialogue

Beyond the classical consolidation triad, high-frequency oscillations (HFOs) in the high-gamma and ripple-frequency range (80-200+ Hz) coordinate hippocampal and neocortical activity during both encoding and retrieval. A 2025 iScience study found that hippocampal HFOs increased during word list encoding and retrieval, with these increases correlating with recall performance (iScience, 2025). During retrieval, hippocampal HFOs reactivated the same semantic processing regions in anterior temporal cortex that had been engaged during encoding, and hippocampal-cortical HFO coincidence was associated with greater cortical high-frequency activity and cortical [[theta-oscillations]] bursts -- suggesting that HFOs support synchronization across distributed nodes of the memory network.

A 2026 study in Nature Communications extended these findings to global cortical coordination, demonstrating that coincident bursts of high-frequency oscillations occur across sensory and association cortical areas during encoding and recall (Nature Communications, 2026). Intracranial recordings from epilepsy patients performing free recall revealed that individual HFO bursts were spatiotemporally organized, with distinct phases of memory processing engaging different cortical networks. Cortical HFO networks existed partly independently of hippocampal HFOs, but hippocampal-cortical HFO coincidence increased during periods requiring optimal binding of distributed memory content. This work suggests that while the hippocampus coordinates consolidation through SWR-mediated replay, real-time memory encoding and retrieval also depend on fast oscillatory synchronization across hippocampal-neocortical circuits that operates in parallel with slower theta- and gamma-band mechanisms.

Hippocampal synchrony also dynamically gates cortical connectivity across brain states. A 2025 study found that hippocampal theta and ripple states differentially modulate cortical functional connectivity, with dorsal hippocampal ripples preferentially enhancing retrosplenial spindle activity and ventral ripples engaging mainly frontal regions (Scientific Reports, 2025). This dorsal-ventral dissociation in hippocampal-cortical coupling suggests that the hippocampus does not broadcast uniformly during consolidation but routes information through region-specific channels matched to the content being consolidated.

## Hippocampal Gamma Sub-Bands and Emotional Memory

The slow/fast gamma dissociation extends beyond spatial memory to emotional processing. A 2025 study using human intracranial recordings demonstrated that patterns of amygdala high-amplitude [[gamma-oscillations]] activity present during aversive encoding are **reactivated in the hippocampus** -- but not in the amygdala itself -- during both encoding and retrieval of aversive memories (Nature Communications, 2025). This suggests that the hippocampus serves as a hub for reinstating emotion-tagged memory representations, with gamma-band activity providing the carrier frequency for amygdala-hippocampal information transfer. Additionally, a 2024 study found that increased high-gamma flow from ventral hippocampus to medial prefrontal cortex manifested exclusively during correctly performed spatial [[working-memory]] trials, suggesting that directional high-gamma communication reflects behavioral performance and decision accuracy in hippocampal-prefrontal circuits.

## Hippocampal Oscillations in Aging and Alzheimer's Disease

Hippocampal oscillatory dysfunction is emerging as both an early biomarker and a potential therapeutic target in Alzheimer's disease (AD). Contrary to the conventional view that AD uniformly slows neural oscillations, recent work reveals a more nuanced trajectory. A 2025 study in 5XFAD amyloid-model mice found early hippocampal **hyperactivity** characterized by reduced inhibitory synaptic input, hypoactivity of fast-spiking interneurons, enhanced pyramidal cell bursting, and paradoxically increased theta and beta power (bioRxiv, 2025). This hyperexcitable state -- driven by impaired PV+ interneuron function -- may represent a prodromal phase preceding the later hypoactivity and oscillatory slowing seen in advanced disease. See [[development-and-aging]] for broader age-related oscillatory changes.

Theta-gamma [[cross-frequency-coupling]] appears particularly vulnerable. In APP/PS1 mice, a significant reduction in theta (4-12 Hz) and low-gamma (30-40 Hz) directed information flow from ventral hippocampus to medial prefrontal cortex during encoding correlated with impaired working memory performance, identifying frequency-specific communication deficits as a mechanism underlying early cognitive decline (Behavioral and Brain Functions, 2025). In P301L tau-seeding models, weakened theta coherence between prefrontal cortex and hippocampal CA1 and drastic impairment of theta-gamma phase-amplitude coupling emerged within two weeks of tau injection, suggesting that oscillatory uncoupling may serve as an early electrophysiological signature of dysfunctional networks even before significant neurodegeneration (Scientific Reports, 2017; reviewed 2025). MEG source-localized hippocampal recordings in prodromal AD patients have shown that absolute theta-band power in the right hippocampus outperforms cortex-based metrics for classifying mild cognitive impairment versus subjective cognitive decline, supporting hippocampal oscillatory markers as promising diagnostic tools.

On the therapeutic side, Tsai and colleagues' decade-long program of 40 Hz gamma sensory stimulation (GENUS) has expanded substantially. A 2024 Nature study demonstrated that multisensory 40 Hz stimulation promotes glymphatic clearance of amyloid in 5XFAD mice via vasoactive intestinal peptide interneuron-mediated regulation of arterial pulsatility (Martorell et al., 2024). Crucially, 40 Hz visual stimulation has been shown to entrain neural activity not only in cortical sensory regions but also in the hippocampus, amygdala, and insula, with increased phase synchronization between hippocampus and temporal/frontal cortex (Communications Biology, 2025). A growing body of human pilot studies supports feasibility and preliminary efficacy, though large-scale clinical trials are still underway. See [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]] for broader stimulation approaches.

## Methodological Advances: Aperiodic Components in Hippocampal Recordings

The broadband versus oscillatory theta debate (see section above) has spurred methodological refinements. A 2026 eNeuro study modified the FOOOF spectral parameterization toolbox -- originally optimized for scalp EEG -- to reliably decompose hippocampal local field potential recordings into periodic and [[aperiodic-dynamics]] components (eNeuro, 2026). Using simulated data, the modifications reduced error in assessment of low-frequency periodic components from ~3% to ~0.1%, a critical improvement given that hippocampal power spectra have steeper 1/f slopes and more prominent low-frequency periodic activity than cortical recordings. Applied to tetrode recordings from mouse CA1 and dentate gyrus, the study revealed significant aperiodic differences between hippocampal subregions. These tools are essential for resolving whether task-related "theta" increases reflect genuine narrowband oscillatory synchronization, broadband aperiodic tilt reflecting excitation-inhibition balance shifts, or a mixture of both -- a question that remains open and that has implications for interpreting virtually all hippocampal LFP studies.
