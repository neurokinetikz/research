# Neurochemistry of Oscillations

The specific frequencies observed in EEG recordings are not arbitrary but emerge from precise molecular time constants embedded in receptor kinetics, ion channel biophysics, and interneuron membrane properties. GABA-A receptor inhibitory postsynaptic current (IPSC) decay of approximately 25 ms generates the canonical 40 Hz gamma rhythm, while slower conductances such as the M-current (~50 ms), h-current (hundreds of milliseconds), and GABA-B receptors (~100-1000 ms) impose resonance at progressively lower frequencies including beta, alpha, and theta. Neuromodulatory systems -- acetylcholine, dopamine, serotonin, and norepinephrine -- further tune these molecular oscillators, shifting power across frequency bands in state-dependent fashion. Recent work has sharpened this picture: cryo-electron microscopy now resolves GABA-A receptor architecture at near-atomic resolution (Bhatt et al., 2025), optogenetic and chemogenetic tools reveal that PV+ and SST+ interneurons play asymmetric causal roles in controlling oscillation frequency versus amplitude (Veit et al., 2025), and biophysical modeling demonstrates how NMDA receptor kinetics -- not just AMPA/GABA kinetics -- can independently generate gamma oscillations under ketamine (Adam et al., 2024). This article traces the causal chain from individual molecules to the macroscopic oscillations recorded on the scalp. See also [[gamma-oscillations]], [[beta-oscillations]], [[alpha-oscillations]], [[theta-oscillations]], [[thalamocortical-circuits]], [[cortical-layer-oscillations]], [[critical-frequencies]], [[psychedelic-states]], and [[clinical-applications]].

## GABA-A receptor kinetics set the gamma clock

The fundamental principle linking receptor timing to oscillation frequency is straightforward: oscillation period approximates four times the IPSC decay time constant. GABA-A receptors at PV+ basket cell synapses have ultrafast decay constants of approximately 2.5 ms at physiological temperature, enabling oscillations exceeding 60 Hz. Standard cortical GABA-A synapses with decay constants of 20-30 ms yield the canonical 40 Hz gamma band (1000 ms / 25 ms = 40 Hz). This relationship operates through two network mechanisms: Pyramidal-Interneuron Network Gamma (PING), where pyramidal cells excite interneurons via fast AMPA receptors and interneurons feed back GABA-A inhibition creating rhythmic cycles; and Interneuron Network Gamma (ING), where mutual inhibition among fast-spiking interneurons alone sustains gamma-frequency oscillations without phasic excitatory input, as demonstrated by Williams et al. (2026) in the medial entorhinal cortex.

GABA-A receptor subunit composition critically determines frequency. Alpha-1-containing receptors with fast decay (~4-6 ms) support high gamma above 50 Hz. Alpha-3-containing receptors with slower decay (~28 ms) generate lower gamma around 30-35 Hz. Developmental maturation involves a shift from alpha-2 to alpha-1 subunit expression, corresponding to the emergence of mature gamma oscillations. Resting GABA concentration predicts individual peak gamma frequency (r = 0.68, p < 0.02 in visual cortex), and genetic variants in GABA-A receptor genes show linkage to beta frequency peaks -- evidence that oscillation frequencies are molecularly specified. A 2025 review of GABA-A structural diversity emphasizes that receptors operate as dynamic ensembles whose subunit composition shifts with cell state, activity level, and network demands (Bhatt et al., 2025). This combinatorial plasticity equips circuits to recalibrate inhibitory tone across [[development-and-aging|development]], environmental stressors, and injury. Notably, the α3 subunit undergoes an isoleucine-to-methionine substitution during early postnatal development that significantly alters deactivation kinetics, and the maturation timeline of this switch parallels the emergence of theta oscillation activity in entorhinal grid cells.

## GABA-B receptors and slower inhibitory timescales

GABA-B receptors operate on a fundamentally slower timescale (100-1000 ms) through G-protein-coupled signaling rather than direct ion channel gating. This slow kinetic profile contributes to oscillations below 10 Hz, including theta, delta, and slow-wave activity. GABA-B activation can functionally uncouple SST+ interneurons from the network, modulating the balance between fast gamma and slower oscillations. The coexistence of fast GABA-A and slow GABA-B signaling at the same synapses -- particularly those of SST+ Martinotti cells -- enables a single interneuron class to participate in multiple frequency bands simultaneously.

## PV+ fast-spiking interneuron biophysics

Parvalbumin-positive basket cells are the primary generators of [[gamma-oscillations]], with biophysical properties optimized for high-frequency operation. Their intrinsic resonance frequency centers near 40 Hz, matching the GABA-A decay time constant of their perisomatic synapses. PV+ interneurons possess myelinated axons enabling rapid conduction, narrow action potentials permitting high firing rates (up to 150 Hz in ING mode), and Kv3-family potassium channels that support fast repolarization. Optogenetic driving of PV+ interneurons -- even with non-rhythmic stimulation -- produces emergent 40 Hz gamma, while inhibiting them suppresses gamma power. The GABA-A reversal potential determines whether PV+ networks can sustain autonomous ING: hyperpolarizing inhibition (E_GABA = -75 mV) enables robust ING, while shunting inhibition (E_GABA = -55 mV) abolishes it, restricting the circuit to PING-only operation. Excitatory drive strength further tunes frequency: weak E-to-I coupling supports fast ING at 100-140 Hz, while stronger coupling shifts the circuit toward slower PING at 60-100 Hz.

Recent work has further clarified PV+ contributions to gamma dynamics. Hadler et al. (2024) demonstrated that gamma oscillations not only require but also potentiate glutamatergic transmission onto PV+ interneurons -- a form of **gamma oscillation plasticity**. In ex vivo hippocampal recordings, sustained gamma activity induced long-term potentiation of gamma power itself, and this potentiation required **calcium-permeable AMPA receptors** (CP-AMPARs) and **metabotropic glutamate receptor 5** (mGluR5), operating through a Gᵢ-sensitive, PKA-dependent signaling pathway. A computational model of CA3 gamma predicted that CP-AMPAR plasticity onto PV+ interneurons -- rather than pyramidal cell plasticity -- critically accounts for gamma power increases. Recordings from optogenetically identified PV+ and SST+ cells in mouse visual cortex confirmed that PV+ interneurons are strongly phase-locked to gamma oscillations (firing ≈6 ms earlier in the gamma cycle than SST+ cells), while SST+ cells show only weak phase-locking (Kim et al., 2025). Furthermore, Veit et al. (2025) demonstrated through combined optogenetic manipulation and computational modeling that PV+ and SST+ interneurons play **asymmetric causal roles**: PV+ cells regulate oscillation frequency and network stability, while SST+ cells control oscillation amplitude. Critically, asymmetric PV→SST connectivity is essential for generating stochastic gamma -- introducing symmetric (reciprocal) PV-SST interactions abolishes gamma oscillations entirely. Developmental embedding of PV+ interneurons appears to control the emergence of prefrontal gamma, with age-dependent functional integration of PV+ (but not SST+) interneurons driving both local gamma power and cross-hemispheric synchrony (Bhardwaj et al., 2025).

## SST+ interneurons and beta/theta generation

Somatostatin-positive Martinotti cells target the apical dendrites of pyramidal neurons and exhibit moderate intrinsic firing rates of 6-8 Hz, correlating with theta/alpha band power. Their dendritic targeting is functionally significant: Headley et al. (2024) demonstrated that [[beta-oscillations]] (~16 Hz) delivered to distal dendrites optimally modulate dendritic spike initiation and termination, because NMDA and calcium spikes in distal dendrites last approximately 50 ms -- matching the beta period. SST+ interneurons utilize both GABA-A and GABA-B receptors, with the slower GABA-B component particularly suited for lower-frequency modulation. In entorhinal cortex, SST+ cells are the primary contributors to slow gamma (~30 Hz), bridging the beta-gamma boundary described in [[critical-frequencies]].

A striking finding from Tye and colleagues (2024) revealed that beta-frequency synchronization between SST+ interneurons in the ventral hippocampus and basolateral amygdala serves as a cross-species biomarker of emotional state. SST+ interneuron beta-synchrony dynamically predicted approach-avoidance decisions, and optogenetically shifting the phase of synchronization by just 25 ms was sufficient to bidirectionally modulate anxiety-related behavior -- a demonstration that interneuron-specific oscillatory timing has direct behavioral consequences. Optogenetic activation of SST+ interneurons also enhances spontaneous inhibitory postsynaptic currents selectively at theta frequencies, consistent with their intrinsic firing properties and role in [[theta-oscillations]] generation. In visual cortex, SST+ cells exert their influence primarily on oscillation amplitude rather than frequency, complementing the frequency-control role of PV+ interneurons (Veit et al., 2025).

## VIP+ disinhibitory circuits

VIP+ interneurons operate through disinhibition, primarily inhibiting SST+ and PV+ interneurons rather than pyramidal cells directly. This motif allows VIP neurons to gate gamma oscillations during attentional states -- disrupting VIP activity dysregulates gamma in the 40-60 Hz range. VIP+ cells linearly scale local gamma power while suppressing long-range gamma synchronization, functioning as gain modulators sensitive to acetylcholine and behavioral state. They are recruited at the peak of theta oscillations, coordinating the temporal relationship between theta phase and gamma amplitude that underlies theta-gamma coupling (see [[cross-frequency-coupling]]).

## M-current, h-current, and Layer 5 resonance

Two voltage-gated conductances in Layer 5 thick-tufted pyramidal neurons generate intrinsic resonance at beta and theta frequencies, as detailed in [[cortical-layer-oscillations]]. The M-current (KCNQ/Kv7 channels) activates slowly at depolarized potentials with a time constant of approximately 50 ms, creating resonance in the theta/beta range. This current determines oscillatory period during sensorimotor beta generation and operates independently of synaptic transmission. Roopun et al. (2006) identified a specific beta-2 rhythm (20-30 Hz) generated by Layer 5 intrinsically bursting pyramidal neurons through axo-axonic gap junctional coupling, where oscillation period is determined by M-type K+ current.

The h-current (HCN channels) produces theta-band resonance at hyperpolarized potentials, with L5 pyramidal tract neurons optimally responsive to 3-6 Hz sinusoidal current injection. HCN channel density increases exponentially along the apical dendrite, creating spatially segregated filtering: perisomatic M-resonance at depolarized potentials and distal dendritic H-resonance during hyperpolarization. This dual mechanism enables L5 neurons to serve as coincidence detectors coupling inputs arriving from different [[cortical-layer-oscillations]].

Beyond cortical pyramidal neurons, HCN channels in the medial septum serve as pacemaker elements for hippocampal [[theta-oscillations]]. Blocking Iₕ in the medial septum significantly reduces the frequency of hippocampal theta without altering the behavioral contexts in which theta occurs, demonstrating that HCN channels specifically tune theta frequency rather than gating theta on or off. In GABAergic neurons of the medial septum, HCN channel expression identifies the theta-rhythmic population that projects to hippocampus. HCN1 knockout mice paradoxically show enhanced theta oscillations and improved spatial memory (see [[hippocampal-memory]]), alongside strengthened long-term potentiation at the direct perforant path input to distal CA1 dendrites -- suggesting that HCN1 normally acts as a shunt that constrains dendritic excitability and theta power. This finding has clinical implications: Goniotaki et al. (2024) demonstrated that HCN channel expression is elevated in post-mortem Alzheimer's disease hippocampus, and that tau pathology drives progressive HCN channelopathy -- including enhanced hyperpolarization-induced voltage sag, decreased dendritic branching, and disrupted synaptic connectivity -- linking [[development-and-aging|neurodegenerative]] HCN dysfunction to the theta rhythm impairments observed early in Alzheimer's disease.

## Persistent sodium current and intrinsic membrane oscillations

Persistent sodium current (INaP) amplifies subthreshold oscillations and, interacting with slowly inactivating potassium current, produces intrinsic membrane potential oscillations spanning the 10-50 Hz frequency range. This current operates below action potential threshold, enabling neurons to generate voltage fluctuations that bias their responsiveness toward specific input frequencies. INaP is particularly prominent in Layer 5 pyramidal neurons and entorhinal stellate cells, where it contributes to theta-frequency subthreshold oscillations that support grid cell firing and spatial navigation.

## T-type calcium channels and thalamic rhythmogenesis

T-type calcium channels (Cav3.x) are essential for [[alpha-oscillations]], sleep spindles, and thalamic pacemaking, as discussed in [[thalamocortical-circuits]]. In thalamocortical relay neurons, HCN and T-type channels interact regeneratively: h-current activation depolarizes the membrane, triggering a low-threshold T-type calcium spike and burst, after which calcium spike deactivation allows hyperpolarization and HCN reactivation. This pacemaker mechanism produces oscillations at 5-6 Hz (theta) or 9-10 Hz (alpha) depending on membrane potential. In the thalamic reticular nucleus, Cav3.3 serves as the major [[sleep-oscillations|sleep spindle]] pacemaker -- Cav3.3 knockout reduces sigma frequency power (10-12 Hz) by approximately 70% at NREM-REM transitions. The interplay between T-type channels and SK2 potassium channels in reticular neurons initiates and terminates spindle bursts.

## Neuromodulatory tuning of oscillatory regimes

Four major neuromodulatory systems shift oscillatory dynamics across frequency bands. **Acetylcholine** enhances theta-gamma coupling through muscarinic M1 receptors (which induce gamma by activating mixed cation currents) and nicotinic alpha-7 receptors (which facilitate theta generation on OLM interneurons). Cholinergic suppression of M-current via PIP2 depletion increases excitability and shifts theta resonance. Cholinesterase inhibitors like donepezil enhance theta and gamma power and increase theta-gamma coupling. **Dopamine** modulates beta and gamma through distinct circuits: in basal ganglia, dopamine depletion produces pathological [[beta-oscillations]] by allowing two oscillatory loops (STN-GPe and striatal-GPe-STN) to synchronize; D1 receptor activation enhances narrow-band gamma, while D2 activation affects theta. **Serotonin** (5-HT2A) suppresses [[alpha-oscillations]], as consistently demonstrated by psychedelics; this suppression correlates with subjective ego dissolution and is blocked by ketanserin. **Norepinephrine** modulates arousal-linked alpha suppression and interacts with the other systems -- Teng et al. (2025) demonstrated that all three monoaminergic/cholinergic systems oscillate in synchrony during NREM sleep at approximately 0.02 Hz, with pharmacological disruption of any single system abolishing infraslow oscillations in all others.

The dopamine-beta relationship has been further elucidated by Neumann, Parkes, and colleagues (2024), who analyzed over 30 hours of invasive cortical recordings from 1,772 channels in epilepsy patients and demonstrated that beta is the most broadly distributed cortical rhythm. Beta-dominant cortical areas map onto a shared network with subcortical structures -- particularly the basal ganglia -- and these networks show significant overlap with dopamine uptake maps derived from positron emission tomography. In Parkinson's disease, subthalamic beta band activity (13-35 Hz) serves as a real-time correlate of [[motor-control|motor symptom]] severity, and a 2024 study confirmed that beta oscillation frequency is specifically coupled to dopamine tone rather than to power or coherence measures (Neumann et al., 2024). Both dopaminergic medication and deep brain stimulation achieve therapeutic benefit through suppression of high-beta synchrony mediated by the hyperdirect cortex-STN pathway (Horn et al., 2025). These findings provide the foundation for a circuit-based understanding of beta activity extending well beyond the motor domain.

The norepinephrine system's oscillatory effects have been refined by a 2024 study in Nature Neuroscience showing that tonic and burst-like locus coeruleus (LC) firing patterns produce qualitatively distinct cortical responses (Grimm et al., 2024). Using combined optogenetics, fiber photometry, electrophysiology, and fMRI in mice, the authors found that moderate tonic LC activation engages associative cortical regions and drives alpha/beta band modulation, while burst-like stimulation biases processing toward sensory cortices. These distinct activation patterns locally couple with increased astrocytic and inhibitory interneuron activity and reconfigure brain-wide functional topology along the cortical hierarchy. Infraslow LC activity fluctuations (~0.02 Hz) also serve as gatekeepers of the NREM-REM [[sleep-oscillations|sleep]] cycle, partitioning non-REM sleep into distinct brain-autonomic states over ~50-second periods.

These neuromodulatory interactions explain characteristic pharmacological EEG signatures: benzodiazepines increase beta by prolonging GABA-A channel opening, propofol produces frontal alpha anteriorization through differential thalamic inhibition, and ketamine paradoxically increases gamma by preferentially blocking NMDA receptors on fast-spiking interneurons. Each drug's EEG fingerprint traces directly to its molecular target within the oscillation-generating machinery described above.

## NMDA receptor kinetics and oscillation generation

The role of NMDA receptors in oscillation generation extends beyond their traditional contribution to synaptic plasticity. Adam et al. (2024) developed a biophysical cortical circuit model demonstrating that NMDA receptor antagonism by ketamine can produce gamma oscillations through a mechanism entirely distinct from the classical AMPA/GABA-dependent PING and ING frameworks. In this **NMDA-dependent mechanism**, disinhibition arises when tonic inhibition is provided by interneurons with subthreshold background excitation or high resting membrane potential, and gamma oscillations emerge from the interaction between NMDA-receptor-mediated excitation and GABA-receptor-mediated inhibition. At subanesthetic doses, ketamine generates broadband gamma (>25 Hz) in human EEG; at anesthetic doses, gamma is periodically interrupted by slow-delta oscillations (0.1-4 Hz), producing a distinctive alternating pattern. The model reproduces both regimes by engaging the slow kinetics of NMDA receptors (~50-100 ms decay) rather than the fast AMPA kinetics (~2-5 ms) that drive conventional gamma.

Complementary computational work by Metzner et al. (2025) modeled ketamine-induced gamma changes as arising from reduced NMDA receptor activity on both PV+ and SST+ interneurons, with the resulting increase in pyramidal neuron firing rate producing elevated gamma power. Regional variation in gamma enhancement correlated with parvalbumin and GluN2D gene expression, suggesting that NMDA receptor subunit composition -- particularly the GluN2B and GluN2D subunits preferentially expressed on interneurons -- determines the spatial pattern of ketamine's oscillatory effects. Input-specific NMDA-dependent plasticity of GABAergic synapses onto CA1 pyramidal cells further demonstrates that NMDA receptors calibrate the inhibitory synaptic weights that set oscillation dynamics (Bhatt et al., 2024). These findings have implications for understanding both the [[consciousness-and-anesthesia|anesthetic]] and antidepressant mechanisms of ketamine, and for models of excitation/inhibition balance in [[clinical-applications|schizophrenia]], and for understanding [[aperiodic-dynamics]] shifts driven by E/I ratio changes.

## Kv3 channels and fast-spiking interneuron function

The Kv3 family of voltage-gated potassium channels (Kv3.1-Kv3.4, encoded by KCNC1-KCNC4 genes) enables the ultrafast repolarization that permits PV+ interneurons to fire at rates exceeding 100 Hz. Kv3.1 and Kv3.2 are particularly enriched in fast-spiking interneurons, where they activate at depolarized potentials and deactivate rapidly, minimizing the refractory period. Loss of Kv3 function impairs high-frequency firing and degrades [[gamma-oscillations]], contributing to the gamma deficits observed in schizophrenia. AUT00206, a selective Kv3.1/Kv3.2 positive allosteric modulator, enhances PV+ interneuron activity and rescues gamma frequency network synchrony in both in vitro hippocampal and cortical preparations. In a Phase Ib clinical trial, AUT00206 normalized gamma oscillations in schizophrenia patients and produced the first evidence of a central Kv3 modulatory effect in human subjects -- including attenuation of ketamine-induced BOLD signaling changes, consistent with enhanced GABAergic interneuron activity opposing ketamine's disinhibitory effects. These results establish Kv3 channel modulation as a pharmacological strategy for restoring oscillatory function in disorders characterized by interneuron hypofunction.

## Neurosteroids and oscillatory modulation

**Neurosteroids** -- endogenous steroid metabolites that act on GABA-A receptors -- modulate oscillatory dynamics through mechanisms distinct from classical benzodiazepines. **Allopregnanolone** (brexanolone) and its synthetic analog **zuranolone** (SAGE-217) are positive allosteric modulators of both synaptic and extrasynaptic GABA-A receptors, with particular affinity for δ-subunit-containing receptors that mediate tonic inhibition. In the basolateral amygdala, allopregnanolone selectively enhances high-theta oscillations (6-12 Hz) through δ-containing GABA-A receptors -- a frequency-specific effect not replicated by benzodiazepines, which preferentially augment gamma-subunit-containing synaptic receptors (Bhatti et al., 2023). Zuranolone administration in healthy human subjects increases power in delta, theta, and low-beta bands, again diverging from the beta-dominant signature of benzodiazepines. This mechanistic distinction -- tonic versus phasic inhibition, extrasynaptic δ-subunit versus synaptic γ-subunit selectivity -- explains the differential oscillatory and behavioral profiles of neurosteroids and benzodiazepines, and likely underlies the rapid antidepressant efficacy of brexanolone (approved for postpartum depression) and zuranolone. Cryo-EM structural studies have revealed that neurosteroids bind at transmembrane intersubunit interfaces of the GABA-A receptor, at sites distinct from the benzodiazepine binding pocket, further confirming their unique pharmacological mechanism (Bhatt et al., 2023).

## Psychedelic neurochemistry and oscillatory disruption

Classical psychedelics -- psilocybin, LSD, DMT, and mescaline -- exert their oscillatory effects primarily through agonism at **5-HT2A serotonin receptors**, which are densely expressed on Layer 5 pyramidal neurons in association cortices and in the claustrum. The canonical EEG signature of psychedelic states is broadband desynchronization: psilocybin reduces spontaneous cortical oscillatory power from 1-50 Hz in posterior association cortices and from 8-100 Hz in frontal association cortices, with [[alpha-oscillations]] suppression being the most robust and consistent finding across compounds and doses (see [[psychedelic-states]]). This alpha suppression correlates with subjective intensity and DMT plasma levels, and is fully blocked by the selective 5-HT2A antagonist ketanserin.

Recent work has substantially deepened understanding of these effects. A 2024 Nature study demonstrated that psilocybin massively disrupts functional connectivity in both cortex and subcortex, producing more than threefold greater connectivity change than methylphenidate (Siegel et al., 2024). Computational simulations incorporating brain-wide 5-HT2A receptor distribution maps show that receptor activation leads to asynchronous, irregular neural dynamics with increased firing rates and robust decreases across delta, theta, and alpha bands. The claustrum -- which has among the highest 5-HT2A and 5-HT2C receptor expression in the brain -- appears to play a specific modulatory role: psilocybin enhances claustral inhibition of cortical networks while disinhibiting subcortical areas, and sustained serotonin elevation in the claustrum may amplify cortical desynchronization via local glutamatergic signaling. Psychedelics also reverse the polarity of long-term synaptic plasticity in cortical-projecting claustrum neurons, potentially contributing to the lasting network reorganization observed after single doses (Bhatt et al., 2025). DMT produces profound alpha suppression combined with normalized or increased delta and theta power, with baseline theta power negatively predicting the intensity of mystical experiences -- a finding relevant to individual differences in [[psychedelic-states|psychedelic]] response.
