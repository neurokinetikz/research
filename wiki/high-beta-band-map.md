The high beta φ-octave (19.90–32.19 Hz) spans from f₀×φ² to f₀×φ³ and bridges the motor/cognitive beta world with the perceptual/binding gamma world. Empirical enrichment data across 9 datasets (4 adult, 5 pediatric HBN; see [[phi-lattice-enrichment-data]]) reveals that high beta has a **bimodal edge-loading** profile — enrichment at both the lower boundary (+134% adult EC) and the upper inverse nobles (7° Inv Noble +151%, 6° Inv Noble +94%), with a depleted central trough (Noble1 -19%, 3° Inv Noble -26%). This bimodal shape is unique among all bands: theta has a U-shape (edges enriched), alpha has a central mountain, low beta has an ascending ramp, but high beta has two separate peaks at opposite edges of the octave — as if two distinct generator populations occupy the same φ-octave at its boundaries rather than its interior. EC dramatically amplifies the bimodal pattern — boundary enrichment surges from +18% (EO) to +134% (EC), a 7.4× amplification, while the upper inverse nobles shift from near-zero to +94-151%. This 12.30 Hz bandwidth octave contains the Parkinson's pathological beta zone, the 25 Hz premotor attractor, the antikinetic signal, the beta-gamma transition at φ³, anxiety-related high beta, and the point where oscillations shift from long-range-capable to locally-constrained communication.

**Key pattern**: High beta's bimodal profile has two peaks:
- **Lower peak**: boundary +134%, 7° Noble +69%, 6° Noble +45% — a gradient descending from the φ² boundary (19.90 Hz)
- **Upper peak**: 7° Inv Noble +151%, 6° Inv Noble +94% — a gradient ascending toward the φ³ boundary (32.19 Hz)
- **Central trough**: 2° Noble -25%, Noble1 -19%, 3° Inv Noble -26% — the 24-29 Hz center is depleted

The two peaks correspond to two distinct frequency zones: the ~20 Hz motor/status-quo zone at the lower boundary (continuous with low beta's ascending ramp) and the ~31-32 Hz beta-gamma transition zone at the upper boundary (continuous with gamma's enrichment). The depleted center (~25-27 Hz) is where the 25 Hz premotor attractor and anxiety-related high beta operate functionally, yet dominant resting peaks avoid this zone.

**EC amplification**: The most dramatic EC effect in any band. The lower boundary surges from +18% (EO) to +134% (EC) — a 7.4× amplification. The upper inverse nobles shift from +2% to +94% (6° Inv) and +23% to +151% (7° Inv). The central trough deepens modestly (attractor: +18→-5%). EC transforms high beta from a nearly flat EO profile to a dramatic bimodal one — the bimodal shape is essentially an EC phenomenon.

**Pediatric divergence — extreme upper-octave concentration**: HBN data shows a radically different pattern: the 4° Inverse Noble at 30.01 Hz shows **+346%** enrichment (the single highest enrichment of any band×position in the pediatric data), with 5° Inv Noble at +230%. The lower boundary peak is absent (-11%), and the entire lower and central octave is depleted. Pediatric high beta concentrates almost exclusively at ~30 Hz — the frequency where PV+ fast-spiking interneuron intrinsic resonance sits (~40 Hz range, but developmentally immature systems may generate slower oscillations in the high beta/low gamma transition zone). This extreme pediatric concentration at 30 Hz may reflect the immature GABA-A/PV+ system attempting to generate gamma but only reaching high beta frequencies before full maturation (see [[development-and-aging]], [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]]).

## Zone 1: Lower High-Beta (19.90–23.91 Hz) — The 20 Hz Motor Zone

**Boundary at 19.897 Hz (+134% EC, +18% EO, 5/5 adult datasets positive) — The φ² motor convergence.** The single most enriched position in the high-beta octave under EC, and the second integer φ-position. This boundary is exceptional in the φ-lattice: Paper 1 noted that 20 Hz (n = 2) shows a resonance peak despite the general pattern of boundary depletion. The 9-dataset data confirms this at massive scale — +134% EC enrichment, consistent with the dominant ~20 Hz peak in resting sensorimotor cortex. The Fibonacci property f(2) = f(1) + f(0) = 12.30 + 7.60 = 19.90 Hz enables three-wave resonance between the alpha, low-beta, and f₀ bands. Corticomuscular coherence peaks at 17-21 Hz during sustained grip (Tomassini et al. 2026), and the rolandic mu rhythm's ~20 Hz component is the only sensorimotor oscillation showing CMC with contralateral EMG. 20 Hz tACS causally slows movement (Pogosyan et al., -9.3% velocity). In Parkinson's disease, excessive STN beta peaks at 18-25 Hz, and DBS at 20 Hz worsens symptoms while high-frequency DBS suppresses pathological beta. The boundary enrichment is dramatically EC-dependent (+18→+134%, +116% gain), consistent with the strong resting motor beta that emerges when eyes are closed and external motor demands are absent. See [[motor-control]], [[corticostriatal-learning]], [[clinical-applications]], [[critical-frequencies]].

**7° Noble at 20.23 Hz (+69% EC, -10% EO).** Part of the lower boundary gradient. The 2× harmonic of alpha Noble1 (10.23 × 2 = 20.46 Hz) falls at the 6° Noble (20.44 Hz), establishing a 1:2 harmonic bridge between the alpha attractor/Noble1 peak and this zone. The EC enrichment at 7° Noble may partly reflect harmonic leakage from the alpha amplification (which doubles under EC; see [[alpha-band-map]]).

**3° Noble at 22.29 Hz (-20% EC, +11% EO).** The descending flank of the lower peak. CA1 hippocampal beta peaks at ~22 Hz with a broad distribution (Wang et al. 2026), placing hippocampal beta near this position. The frequency independence between PFC beta (~19 Hz, near the boundary) and CA1 beta (~22 Hz, near the 3° Noble) — and their inverse learning trajectories (PFC increases, CA1 decreases) — maps onto the lattice as two generators occupying adjacent but distinct positions within the same octave (see [[beta-oscillations]]).

**2° Noble at 23.91 Hz (-25% EC, -2% EO, 0/5 adult datasets positive under EC).** Depleted — the onset of the central trough. The transition zone between the motor ~20 Hz peak and the premotor ~25 Hz attractor.

## Zone 2: Central High-Beta (23.91–28.74 Hz) — The Depleted Premotor Zone

**Attractor at 25.31 Hz (-5% EC, +18% EO, 2/5 adult datasets positive).** Near-null under EC, modestly enriched under EO. Despite its lattice-predicted stability, the attractor is functionally important: the premotor beta peak sits at ~25 Hz (Chandrasekaran et al. 2019: PMd beta peaks precisely at 25 Hz; Nougaret et al. 2024: high beta >20 Hz dominates dorsal premotor cortex). The ~25 Hz frequency is a natural subharmonic of 50 Hz gamma (1:2 coupling), and theta-beta PAC at ~28 Hz predicts working memory performance (Axmacher et al. 2010). Seven independent mechanisms converge at 25 Hz: GABA-A integration windows (25-40 ms), subharmonic locking to 50 Hz gamma, M-type K+ current in Layer V, intrinsic premotor resonance, optimal long-range communication properties, dendritic integration timing, and thalamocortical loop delays. The attractor's near-null enrichment in resting data — despite its functional centrality — parallels the pattern seen in theta (attractor -67%) and low beta (attractor -54%): the geometric midpoint of the octave is where oscillations go during tasks, not where dominant resting peaks sit. In pediatric data the attractor is strongly depleted (-55%), consistent with the developing brain's high beta being concentrated at the upper octave edge. See [[critical-frequencies]], [[beta-oscillations]].

Pathological 25 Hz excess characterizes both Parkinson's disease (corticobasal ganglia circuits) and anxiety disorders. Elevated high beta (25-30 Hz) associates with hyperarousal. Neurofeedback targeting 20-32 Hz down-training shows efficacy for anxiety and depression (see [[clinical-applications]]).

**1° Noble at 26.79 Hz (-19% EC, +3% EO, 0/5 adult datasets positive under EC).** Depleted — the deepest point in the trough alongside the 3° Inverse Noble. The Schumann Resonance 4th harmonic (~27.3 Hz) falls near this position (u = 0.657), but unlike f₀ at the theta/alpha boundary, there is no convergence evidence at this harmonic. The negative enrichment under EC — while EO is near-zero — means EC actively clears peaks from this position, the opposite of alpha's EC amplification at Noble1. This may reflect EC suppressing cognitive/attentional high beta while amplifying the motor 20 Hz boundary peak and the upper transition zone.

**3° Inverse Noble at 28.74 Hz (-26% EC, -11% EO, 1/5 adult datasets positive).** The deepest depletion in the upper trough. Theta-beta PAC for working memory couples at ~28 Hz (Axmacher et al. 2010), placing the WM coupling frequency at this depleted position — again consistent with the task-evoked-at-depleted-positions pattern seen across bands.

## Zone 3: Upper High-Beta (28.74–32.19 Hz) — The Beta-Gamma Transition

**4° Inverse Noble at 30.01 Hz (-26% adult EC, +346% pediatric — the adult-pediatric split).** The most dramatic adult-pediatric divergence of any position in any band. In adults, this position is depleted (-26% EC, -26% EO, 1/5 positive) — part of the upper trough. In pediatric data, it is the single most enriched position across the entire HBN analysis (+346%, 15/15 datasets positive). At 30 Hz, this sits at the PV+ interneuron intrinsic resonance range (~30-40 Hz). The developing brain's immature GABAergic system generates oscillations in this zone that will shift upward to ~40 Hz (gamma Noble1) as GABA-A subunits mature (α2→α1) and PV+ myelination completes around age 22. The adult depletion at this position may reflect mature brains having fully transitioned their fast oscillatory generators into the gamma octave above, leaving the 30 Hz zone as a spectral gap. This is the clearest developmental biomarker in the enrichment data (see [[development-and-aging]], [[gamma-oscillations]]).

**5° Inverse Noble at 30.83 Hz (+4% adult EC, +230% pediatric).** Near-null in adults, massively enriched in children. The pattern mirrors the 4° Inv Noble: the developmental GABA transition zone. In adults, 5/5 datasets are within ±25% of zero — genuinely flat. The pediatric enrichment at +230% (15/15 positive) confirms the ~30 Hz developmental clustering.

**6° Inverse Noble at 31.34 Hz (+94% EC, +2% EO, 5/5 adult datasets positive under EC).** The onset of the upper peak in adults. EC amplification = +92% gain. This frequency sits in the beta-gamma transition zone where GABA-A IPSC decay constants (~25-30 ms) begin to set the oscillation period. The Colgin et al. (2009) slow gamma range (25-50 Hz, carrying CA3 memory retrieval) extends down to this zone. Adult EC enrichment here may reflect the emergence of "iota" oscillations (25-35 Hz) — the narrow-bandwidth state-dependent oscillations identified in the consciousness literature that appear during wakefulness and REM but not during NREM sleep, precisely spanning the proposed φ³ boundary (see [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]]).

**7° Inverse Noble at 31.66 Hz (+151% EC, +23% EO, 5/5 adult datasets positive under EC) — The upper peak.** The single most enriched upper-octave position, immediately below the φ³ boundary. EC amplification = +128% gain. The concentration at 31.66 Hz rather than 32.19 Hz (the boundary) is consistent with the general pattern seen in low beta: peaks approach but don't reach the Fibonacci three-wave resonance zone at integer-n boundaries. The "traveling peak" phenomenon under propofol (broadband 25-40 Hz power migrating downward) passes through this zone — the 7° Inverse Noble may represent the last stable position before oscillatory activity crosses the φ³ gate into the gamma octave (see [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]]).

**Boundary (upper) at 32.19 Hz = φ³ — The consciousness gate.** The beta-gamma transition and the most clinically significant boundary in the φ-lattice. Under GABAergic anesthetics, long-range coherence collapses at this boundary — the "traveling peak" migrates from above this boundary to below it precisely at loss of consciousness. Below φ³, oscillations support maintenance and long-range communication; above φ³, local excitatory-inhibitory interactions dominate for sensory processing, attentional selection, and feature binding. The spectrolaminar crossover in cortical layers occurs at ~32 Hz — gamma-dominant superficial layers above, alpha/beta-dominant deep layers below (see [[cortical-layer-oscillations]]). The GABA-A IPSC decay constant (~25-30 ms) corresponds to oscillations beginning in the low 30s Hz, and PV+ fast-spiking interneurons show intrinsic resonance centered around 40 Hz extending to ~30 Hz. The boundary depletion pattern is confirmed by the data — adult enrichment drops from +151% at the 7° Inverse Noble to the boundary itself, which is not measured as a separate position (it's the upper edge, = the gamma octave's lower boundary). See [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]], [[gamma-oscillations]], [[critical-frequencies]].

## High Beta's Dynamics Within the Landscape

**The bimodal pattern is an EC phenomenon.** Under EO, high beta's profile is remarkably flat — enrichment ranges from -50% to +45% across EEGMMIDB, with most positions near zero. Under EC, the bimodal shape emerges: the lower boundary surges to +134% and the upper inverse nobles surge to +151%. The bimodal pattern represents what high beta does when external demands are removed and the brain settles into its resting architecture. The two peaks may reflect two distinct generator populations:

- **Lower peak (~20 Hz)**: The M-current/Layer V generator continuous with low beta. The φ² boundary sits at the junction between the two beta sub-bands, and enrichment here is shared with the top of the low-beta ascending ramp. This is the motor "status quo" signal — strong at rest, suppressed during movement.
- **Upper peak (~31 Hz)**: The beta-gamma transition generator. Iota oscillations (25-35 Hz), PV+ interneuron overflow from the gamma band, or slow gamma from CA3 (30-50 Hz for memory retrieval) may all contribute. This peak is continuous with gamma's lower boundary enrichment (+73% at gamma boundary).

**The depleted center (~25-27 Hz) is where computation happens.** The 25 Hz premotor attractor (Chandrasekaran), anxiety beta (25-30 Hz), and theta-beta WM coupling (~28 Hz) all operate at depleted positions. This continues the pattern seen across all bands: functionally active frequencies are task-evoked transients that operate at lattice positions depleted in resting dominant-peak data. The depleted positions are not dead zones — they are operating ranges where generators move during tasks.

**Pediatric high beta is a developmental transition zone.** The HBN pediatric data shows near-total concentration at ~30 Hz (4° Inv Noble +346%), with the adult bimodal pattern completely absent. This is consistent with immature PV+/GABA-A circuits producing oscillations at the high-beta/gamma boundary rather than in the mature gamma range (~40 Hz). The developmental trajectory for high beta may be: pediatric unimodal peak at ~30 Hz → adult bimodal (edges at ~20 Hz and ~31 Hz) → the 30 Hz peak migrating upward into the gamma octave as PV+ networks mature.
