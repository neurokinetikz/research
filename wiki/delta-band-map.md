The delta region (0.42–4.70 Hz) spans **five φ-octaves** from n=-6 to n=-1 — more than any other conventionally defined band. This multi-octave span creates a fundamental asymmetry in the φ-lattice framework: while theta, alpha, low beta, high beta, and gamma each occupy a single φ-octave, delta contains five nested octaves whose bandwidths shrink geometrically from 1.79 Hz (resolvable) down to 0.26 Hz (sub-resolution). Only the uppermost octave (n=-2 to -1, 2.90–4.70 Hz) has sufficient spectral resolution for meaningful position-level analysis; the three lowest octaves (below 1.79 Hz) contain fewer frequency bins than lattice positions. The enrichment data in [[phi-lattice-enrichment-data]] pools all delta φ-octaves into a single 1-4 Hz band, superimposing boundaries and attractors from multiple octaves into each lattice coordinate — producing weak, noisy enrichment (+35% attractor, +42% 4° Noble) at magnitudes 3-10× smaller than other bands. Delta's enrichment profile shows a **broad low plateau** in the central octave with modest flanking depletion, but this should be interpreted as the least reliable band in the lattice analysis. The functional content of delta — cortical slow oscillations, up/down states, sleep homeostasis, torpor, pathological delta — is rich and well-documented across the wiki, even though the φ-lattice resolution cannot cleanly dissect it.

| Octave | n range | Frequency range | Bandwidth | Bins (Δf=0.25) | Resolvable? | Name |

| **3** | -4 to -3 | 1.109 – 1.794 Hz | 0.685 Hz | 2.7 | ✗ Barely 3 bins | Low delta |

| **1** | -2 to -1 | 2.903 – 4.697 Hz | 1.794 Hz | 7.2 | ✓ Resolvable | Fast delta / delta-theta transition |

The geometric shrinking is relentless: each octave is φ× narrower than the one above (1.794 / 1.618 = 1.109, etc.). By the 5th octave (0.42–0.69 Hz), the entire φ-octave fits in a single frequency bin. This is not a limitation of the analysis — it is a mathematical consequence of logarithmic scaling at low frequencies. The φ-lattice framework assumes that each octave has internal structure at 14+ positions; below ~1.8 Hz, that assumption breaks.

**The 4° Noble anomaly**: Delta is the only band where the 4° Noble is the most enriched position. At offset 0.146, this maps to frequencies of 3.35 Hz (octave -2 to -1), 2.07 Hz (octave -3 to -2), and 1.28 Hz (octave -4 to -3). **Adult vs pediatric**: Pediatric enrichment is similar in shape but weaker across all positions. Delta shows the least adult-pediatric divergence of any band — consistent with cortical slow oscillation generators being relatively mature even in childhood.

## Octave 5: The Infraslow Zone (0.424–0.685 Hz, n=-6 to -5)

**Sub-resolution**: This octave spans 0.262 Hz — a single frequency bin at typical EEG resolution. No within-octave lattice analysis is possible.

**Functional content**: Infraslow oscillations (<0.5 Hz) are biologically real but operate at the interface of neural oscillations and hemodynamic/metabolic fluctuations. The n=-6 boundary at 0.424 Hz (2,361 ms cycle) represents the lowest frequency at which the φ-lattice framework predicts structure. Below this, the lattice concept may not apply — these are not oscillatory "modes" in the same sense as theta or alpha generators but rather slow fluctuations in cortical excitability.

Teng et al. (2025) documented ~0.02 Hz synchrony in norepinephrine, serotonin, and acetylcholine systems — neuromodulatory rhythms operating far below even the n=-6 boundary. These infraslow neuromodulatory oscillations may modulate the faster oscillatory architecture without being part of the lattice itself (see [[cross-species-frequency-organization]], [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]]).

## Octave 4: The Slow Oscillation Zone (0.685–1.109 Hz, n=-5 to -4)

**Sub-resolution**: 1.7 frequency bins. Not resolvable for lattice analysis.

**Functional content**: This octave contains the **cortical slow oscillation** (SO, ~0.75-1.0 Hz) — the most powerful oscillation in the sleeping brain and the master organizer of NREM sleep architecture. The SO frequency of ~0.75 Hz maps to n=-4.81 (u=0.188 within this octave, near the 4° Noble position). The canonical 1 Hz SO sits at n=-4.22 (u=0.785, near the 3° Inverse Noble).

**Up/down state alternation** at ~0.8 Hz (n=-4.68, u=0.322) drives the fundamental bistability of cortical circuits during NREM. Layer 5 intrinsically bursting neurons initiate Up states, with INaP amplification and calcium-activated K+ currents for termination (see [[delta-oscillations]], [[cortical-layer-oscillations]]). The Up state is not silence — it contains vigorous spiking and nested faster oscillations (spindles, ripples). The SO's ~0.8 Hz frequency provides the phase scaffold within which [[sharp-wave-ripples]] and [[sleep-oscillations|sleep spindles]] are temporally organized for memory consolidation.

The attractor of this octave sits at 0.872 Hz (1,147 ms cycle). Whether the SO preferentially operates at this position versus the boundary or noble positions cannot be determined at current resolution.

## Octave 3: Low Delta (1.109–1.794 Hz, n=-4 to -3)

**Barely resolvable**: 2.7 frequency bins. Position analysis is not meaningful.

**Functional content**: This octave contains the **SWA (slow-wave activity)** range that serves as the primary EEG marker of sleep homeostasis. Leemburg et al. (2010) showed SWA power tracks sleep pressure with exponential decay, frontal predominance, and cumulative SWE (slow-wave energy) reaching 100% recovery within 2-3 nights of recovery sleep (see [[delta-oscillations]]). The attractor at 1.41 Hz (709 ms) falls in the heart of the SWA frequency range.

During torpor in Djungarian hamsters, SWA at 1-4 Hz shows transient increases at EMG event onset, with temperature-frequency coupling (r=-0.770) constraining oscillations as metabolic rate drops (Hauglund et al. 2026). The n=-4 boundary at 1.109 Hz (902 ms) marks the approximate lower edge of conventional SWA measurement.

Paper 3 noted delta-specific enrichment at the 3° Noble (u=0.236) in 2/3 datasets, placing the dominant delta peak near f₀ × φ⁻³ ≈ 1.84 Hz — the n=-3 boundary at the top of this octave. This boundary enrichment in delta is unique (boundaries are depleted in all other bands) and may reflect that the SWA peak clusters at the octave transition rather than at attractor or noble positions.

## Octave 2: Core Delta (1.794–2.903 Hz, n=-3 to -2)

**Marginally resolvable**: 4.4 frequency bins. Coarse position analysis may be possible.

**Functional content**: Core delta frequencies (2-3 Hz) dominate pathological EEG — large focal delta indicates structural lesions (tumors, strokes), diffuse delta indicates metabolic encephalopathy (see [[delta-oscillations]], [[clinical-applications]]). In Schiff's ABCD model for disorders of consciousness, Type A vegetative states show only very low frequency activity (<1 Hz); theta emergence (Type B) marks the transition to minimally conscious states, making the core delta range the frequency of deepest unconsciousness (see [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]]).

The attractor at 2.282 Hz (438 ms) represents the geometric midpoint of the core delta octave. During N3 sleep, Canu et al. (2026) showed that delta-phase modulates broadband high-frequency activity across the entire cortical column — delta serves as the **master phase organizer** with the longest integration window of any oscillatory scaffold.

**Human hippocampal "theta" at ~3 Hz**: [[cross-species-frequency-organization]] notes that hippocampal theta scales inversely with brain size — ~8 Hz in rats, ~3 Hz in humans during virtual navigation. The 3 Hz human hippocampal frequency maps to n=-1.93, placing it just above the n=-2 boundary (2.903 Hz) — technically in the fast delta/delta-theta transition octave rather than the core delta octave. This cross-species scaling means that human hippocampal "theta" actually operates in the φ-lattice's uppermost delta octave, not in the theta octave.

This is the **only delta octave that maps cleanly onto the lattice**, and it reveals that the conventional "delta" upper boundary (4 Hz) sits at the 1° Noble position (n=-1.33, u=0.666) — not at a φ-lattice boundary. The actual φ-lattice boundary between delta and theta is at 4.697 Hz (n=-1), which falls within the conventional theta range (4-8 Hz). This 0.7 Hz discrepancy between conventional and φ-lattice boundaries is itself significant: the conventional 4 Hz cutoff splits the n=-2 to -1 octave at the Noble1 position rather than at the boundary. The Schiff ABCD model's "theta emergence" threshold for minimally conscious states would occur somewhere in this octave — the transition from delta-dominated Type A/B to theta-containing Type B/C.

**Eisen et al. (2026)** demonstrated that three molecularly distinct anesthetics (propofol, ketamine, dexmedetomidine) all convergently increase delta, theta, and alpha power during unconsciousness. In φ-lattice terms, anesthesia increases power across the entire delta region while collapsing activity above the φ³ gamma boundary — the frequency architecture shifts toward lower φ-octaves as consciousness fades (see [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]]).

**Wong et al. (2026)** showed delta-theta dynamics in REM substates: tonic REM shows bimodal peaks in the delta-theta range at phi-predicted positions. Higher tonic theta density predicted better next-day mood, while higher tonic alpha density predicted worse mood — suggesting an affective gate at the theta-alpha boundary (f₀) that extends down through this octave (see [[sleep-oscillations]]).

## Why Delta Resists Lattice Analysis

Three independent factors converge to make delta the weakest band for φ-lattice analysis:

**1. Multi-octave pooling.** The enrichment data combines 2.5 φ-octaves into one band. A peak at 1.5 Hz and a peak at 3.5 Hz both map to lattice coordinates, but they're in different octaves with different biophysical generators (cortical slow oscillations vs delta-theta transition dynamics). The enrichment at "attractor" = +35% is actually the superposition of the n=-4 attractor at 1.41 Hz, the n=-3 attractor at 2.28 Hz, and the n=-2 attractor at 3.69 Hz. Any genuine octave-specific structure is averaged away.

**2. Resolution floor.** Below 1.8 Hz, the φ-octave bandwidth is smaller than the spectral resolution. You can't measure within-octave position preferences when the octave is 3 frequency bins wide. The lower 3 of 5 octaves are fundamentally sub-resolution.

**3. Aperiodic dominance.** The 1/f spectral slope is steepest at low frequencies. FOOOF must separate genuine oscillatory delta peaks from the dominant aperiodic background — the signal-to-noise ratio for periodic peak detection is lowest in delta. The per-octave aperiodic modeling (overlap-trim) helps but cannot fully resolve octaves with <4 frequency bins.

**The honest assessment**: Delta enrichment magnitudes (+35% attractor) are 3× weaker than alpha (+113%) and 7× weaker than low-beta 5° Inv Noble (+244%). The consistency (3-4/5 datasets positive at the best positions) is lower than alpha or gamma (4-5/5). The anomalous 4° Noble enrichment (+42%) appears nowhere else in the lattice. Delta's enrichment data may reflect genuine weak lattice organization, or it may be a noisy artifact of multi-octave pooling and aperiodic confounds. The band maps for theta through gamma describe robust, replicable, band-specific fingerprints; delta's "fingerprint" is faint and uncertain.

## What Would Resolve This

**Octave-specific delta analysis**: Rather than pooling 1-4 Hz into one band, analyzing each φ-octave separately — at least octaves 1 (2.9-4.7 Hz) and 2 (1.8-2.9 Hz) where resolution permits — would reveal whether delta has genuine within-octave structure or whether the current enrichment is an averaging artifact. This would require longer recording epochs (≥4 second segments for 0.25 Hz resolution in the n=-3 octave) and per-octave FOOOF fitting with the overlap-trim method.

**Intracranial data**: The aperiodic confound is most severe for scalp EEG. Intracranial recordings from NREM sleep could resolve slow oscillation peaks with precision unattainable at the scalp, potentially revealing whether the ~0.75 Hz SO frequency maps to a specific lattice position within the n=-5 to -4 octave.

**The developmental question**: Delta shows the least adult-pediatric divergence of any band. If the φ-lattice reflects maturation of GABAergic/PV+ circuits (as gamma and high beta strongly suggest), delta's relative stability across development is consistent with slow oscillation generators being mature early — cortical Layer 5 IB neurons and the bistable mechanisms producing up/down states are among the earliest-maturing cortical circuits.
