The low beta φ-octave (12.30–19.90 Hz) spans from f₀×φ to f₀×φ² and is the most functionally heterogeneous band in the frequency architecture. Empirical enrichment data across 9 datasets (4 adult, 5 pediatric HBN; see [[phi-lattice-enrichment-data]]) reveals that low beta has a unique **ascending ramp** profile — monotonically rising from deep depletion at the boundary (-71%) through the entire lower and central octave, crossing zero near the 1° Noble (-2%), and surging to massive enrichment at the 5° Inverse Noble (+244% adult EC). This is neither the U-shape of theta nor the central mountain of alpha but a **one-sided pileup** at the upper octave edge. The enrichment peak at 5° Inv Noble (19.05 Hz) corresponds precisely to the PMBR frequency zone and PFC beta (~19 Hz), and is not motor-paradigm-specific as originally reported — it appears across all adult datasets including non-motor paradigms (LEMON EC +301%, CHBMP EC +202%, Dortmund EC +236%). EC approximately doubles the upper-octave enrichment while leaving the lower depletion largely unchanged. Low beta contains sleep spindles, the sensorimotor rhythm, thalamocortical feedforward signaling, corticostriatal learning oscillations, and predictive coding feedback. The two generator systems — superficial beta1 (~15 Hz) and deep beta2 (~20 Hz) — produce different spectral peaks that jointly create the ascending ramp: beta1 contributes weakly to the central octave while beta2 dominates the upper edge. The φ² boundary at 19.90 Hz marks the transition from long-range-capable to locally-constrained oscillatory communication.

**Key pattern**: Low beta's enrichment is an **ascending ramp** — a monotonic rise from -73% at the 7° Noble to +244% at the 5° Inverse Noble, with the zero-crossing near the 1° Noble (-2%). This is the only band where enrichment is essentially one-sided: the entire lower 60% of the octave is depleted (0/5 datasets positive at any position through the attractor), while the upper 40% is massively enriched (5/5 datasets positive at 4° through 7° Inverse Noble). The ramp peaks at the 5° Inverse Noble (19.05 Hz), not the boundary — the peak is one position below the φ² edge, not at the edge itself.

**EC amplification**: EC approximately doubles the upper-octave enrichment (5° Inv Noble: +148→+244%, 4° Inv Noble: +109→+165%) while leaving the lower depletion largely unchanged or slightly deepening it. The EC-EO gain is concentrated at positions above the 1° Noble — the same inverse noble positions identified in Paper 2's motor paradigm analysis. The critical revision: **this is NOT motor-paradigm-specific.** LEMON (resting state, N=202) shows the strongest 5° Inv Noble enrichment of any dataset (+301% EC), and CHBMP (resting state, N=249) shows +202% EC. The upper-octave pileup is a general property of low beta, not a motor artifact.

**Pediatric divergence**: HBN data shows a dramatically different profile — the ramp is weakened and shifted. The 2° Noble becomes the dominant enrichment position (+41%, versus -66% in adults), the attractor is weakly positive (+8%, versus -54% in adults), and the inverse noble enrichment is 5-10× weaker than adults (+50% at 5° Inv Noble versus +244%). The 1° Noble is strongly depleted (-46%, versus -2% in adults). This suggests the developing brain's low-beta generators sit at fundamentally different lattice positions — closer to the central octave, perhaps reflecting immature beta2 (deep Layer V) generators that haven't yet established the strong ~19 Hz M-current resonance seen in adults.

## Zone 1: Lower Low-Beta (12.30–14.78 Hz) — Sleep Spindles and the Alpha-Beta Transition

The lower zone spans the φ¹ boundary through the 2° Noble, covering the alpha-beta transition, sleep spindles, and the onset of the sensorimotor rhythm.

**Boundary at 12.297 Hz = φ¹.** The alpha/beta border and the first integer φ-position above f₀. This boundary is a major functional discontinuity:
- **Sleep spindles** straddle this boundary: slow spindles (~9-12 Hz) sit below in alpha, fast spindles (~13-15 Hz) above in low beta. The TRN generates spindles through burst-mode T-type Ca²⁺ dynamics, with the boundary marking the transition between the two spindle populations. Papers across the wiki use five different spindle ranges (10-12, 10-14, 10-15, 12-15, 12-16 Hz), reflecting the genuine slow/fast distinction centered near this φ¹ position (see [[sleep-oscillations]], [[thalamocortical-circuits]]).
- **Photosensitive epilepsy** peaks at 12-18 Hz photic stimulation — spanning from this boundary into the attractor zone. The 12 Hz boundary represents the onset of the most dangerous frequency range for seizure provocation (see [[clinical-applications]]).
- **Consciousness transition**: propofol-induced alpha anteriorization occurs at 10-12 Hz, and the "beta buzz" begins just above this boundary during early propofol induction before shifting down as unconsciousness approaches (see [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]]).

**4° Noble at 13.191 Hz.** The 1:2 harmonic of theta 6.6 Hz (Siebenhühner et al. 2016) falls at 13.2 Hz — within 0.01 Hz of this position. This harmonic coupling between theta Noble1 (6.6 Hz) and low-beta 4° Noble (13.2 Hz) represents a precise integer-ratio bridge between the two bands, consistent with phase-phase coupling requiring harmonic ratios while phase-amplitude coupling works for any ratio (see [[cross-frequency-coupling]]). The tACS stimulation at 13 Hz used by D'Angelo et al. (2026) to narrow the body ownership temporal binding window to 138 ms also falls near this position (see [[self-and-embodiment]]).

**3° Noble at 13.776 Hz — Repeller.** Peaks avoid this position. It falls in the transition zone between the spindle/alpha-boundary cluster and the core low-beta generators.

**2° Noble at 14.778 Hz (-66% EC, -38% EO, 1/5 adult datasets positive).** Deeply depleted in adults — a major revision from Paper 1's GED analysis which showed +27.5% enrichment at this position using all-band aggregate data. The band-specific data reveals that low beta's 2° Noble is part of the depletion zone, not an enrichment peak. The GED enrichment was likely driven by alpha's contribution at this lattice coordinate. However, the pediatric data shows a striking reversal: HBN 2° Noble = +41% (15/15 datasets positive), the strongest pediatric low-beta enrichment of any position. This is where the developing brain's low-beta generators sit — near ~15 Hz, the beta1/SMR range — before adult maturation shifts the dominant peak to the upper octave. The sensorimotor rhythm (12-15 Hz) spans from the boundary through this position (Onagawa et al. 2023; see [[neurofeedback-and-stimulation]]). The Bastos feedback beta (~14-18 Hz) begins here (see [[predictive-coding]], [[cortical-layer-oscillations]]).

## Zone 2: Central Low-Beta (14.78–17.76 Hz) — Predictive Coding and Corticostriatal Communication

The central zone contains the attractor and 1° Noble — the predicted enrichment positions — where predictive coding feedback, corticostriatal learning, and beta1 generators operate.

**Attractor at 15.642 Hz (-54% EC, -32% EO, 0/5 adult datasets positive).** Depleted — confirming Paper 1's original observation of band-specific avoidance. The attractor is depleted in all adult datasets under both conditions. The ~64 ms cycle corresponds to the ~50 ms beta integration window described in [[predictive-coding]], and beta1 (~15 Hz) arises through period concatenation (one beta1 cycle = gamma + beta2 period; Roopun et al. 2006). Despite the depletion, this is functionally where beta1 operates — analogous to theta's attractor being depleted in resting data but functionally active during tasks. In pediatric data, the attractor is weakly positive (+8%), consistent with the developing brain's low-beta generators sitting closer to the central octave. The EC-EO shift (-21%) deepens the depletion under EC, the opposite direction from alpha's attractor (which strengthens under EC). This may reflect the inverse relationship between alpha and beta: EC amplifies alpha while suppressing beta.

**1° Noble at 16.556 Hz (-2% EC, -2% EO, 2/5 adult datasets positive) — The zero-crossing.** The 1° Noble marks the transition from the depleted lower octave to the enriched upper octave — the inflection point of the ascending ramp. Near-zero in both conditions, identical under EC and EO. This is the most condition-invariant position in low beta: the ramp's zero-crossing doesn't move.

Despite null enrichment, ~16 Hz is functionally the **corticostriatal communication frequency**: Pathak et al. (2025) demonstrated PLV peaks at ~16 Hz during category learning, differentiating correct from incorrect trials (see [[corticostriatal-learning]]). Myers et al. (2026) showed beta (15-25 Hz) power reduction during WM priority switches (see [[working-memory]]). Kopell's framework establishes beta at ~15-30 Hz as optimal for long-range communication — the 1° Noble sits in this zone. The near-null enrichment may reflect that ~16 Hz is a functional frequency used during tasks but not a resting dominant-peak position — similar to the theta attractor at ~6 Hz (functionally critical, empirically depleted in resting data).

**Thalamocortical feedforward.** Thalamocortical feedforward communication operates through beta (15-30 Hz), not gamma, placing the LGN→V1 channel in the zone above Noble1 (see [[cortical-layer-oscillations]], [[predictive-coding]]).

## Zone 3: Upper Low-Beta (17.76–19.90 Hz) — Motor Beta and the PMBR Zone

The upper zone from the 3° Inverse Noble to the φ² boundary contains the massive enrichment peak that dominates adult low beta across all paradigms — originally interpreted as motor-specific (Paper 2) but now confirmed as universal across resting-state and motor datasets alike (9-dataset analysis: LEMON resting +301%, CHBMP resting +202%).

**3° Inverse Noble at 17.760 Hz (+72% EC, +67% EO, 4/5 adult datasets positive).** The onset of the enrichment zone — the first position with substantial positive enrichment. Enrichment is nearly identical under EC and EO (+4% difference), making this the most condition-invariant enriched position. The Bastos feedback beta range (~14-18 Hz) ends here, placing this position at the transition from predictive-coding feedback frequencies to the PMBR/motor zone above. The 4/5 adult consistency (EEGMMIDB is the exception at +128% EC vs +83% EO) confirms this is a robust population-level feature across paradigms. Pediatric data shows depletion (-28%), consistent with the developing brain's low-beta generators not yet reaching this zone.

**4° Inverse Noble at 18.548 Hz (+165% EC, +109% EO, 5/5 adult datasets positive — the ramp's steep section).** Massive enrichment with perfect cross-dataset consistency. EC amplifies by +67% over EO. Paper 2 found this position significant in EEGMMIDB motor tasks (p_Bonf = 0.002, r = +0.45), but the 9-dataset data reveals this is **not motor-specific**: LEMON resting EC = +179%, CHBMP resting EC = +158%, Dortmund resting EC = +150%. The frequency corresponds to the corticomuscular coherence range (17-21 Hz; Tomassini et al. 2026) and the beta-band LFP prediction error signal (13-25 Hz, rho = -0.763; Emanuele et al. 2026; see [[motor-control]]). Pediatric enrichment is near-zero (+2%), a dramatic adult-pediatric gap.

**5° Inverse Noble at 19.052 Hz (+244% EC, +148% EO, 5/5 adult datasets positive — THE PEAK).** The single most enriched position in the entire low-beta octave and one of the strongest enrichments of any band×position in the dataset. EC amplification is massive (+108% gain). Individual dataset EC values: LEMON +301%, EEGMMIDB +263%, Dortmund +236%, CHBMP +202% — all exceed +200%. This is where the dominant low-beta peak sits in adult brains under all conditions. Wang et al. (2026) showed PFC beta peaks at ~19 Hz during spatial navigation learning — the 5° Inverse Noble maps precisely to this PFC frequency (see [[beta-oscillations]]). The concentration at 19.05 Hz rather than 19.90 Hz (the φ² boundary) is significant: the ramp peaks one position below the boundary, not at the boundary. Peaks approach but avoid the Fibonacci three-wave resonance zone at φ². Pediatric enrichment (+50%) is present but 5× weaker than adult, suggesting partial maturation of the ~19 Hz generator.

**6° Inverse Noble at 19.371 Hz (+158% EC, +84% EO, 5/5 adult datasets positive — the descending ramp).** Still massively enriched but declining from the 5° Inv Noble peak. Paper 2 identified this as the PMBR frequency (~19.4 Hz; Jurkiewicz et al. 2006; Pfurtscheller & Lopes da Silva 1999) — the post-movement beta rebound. EC amplification = +82%. The 9-dataset analysis confirms Paper 2's positive control: the φ-lattice detects known motor cortex physiology at the correct frequency across all paradigms, not just motor tasks. Pediatric enrichment (+40%) is present but reduced (see [[motor-control]]).

**7° Inverse Noble at 19.570 Hz (+45% EC, +3% EO, 5/5 adult datasets positive under EC).** The ramp's tail — still enriched under EC but falling rapidly toward the boundary. The EC-EO gain (+46%) is large relative to the EO baseline, making this the most EC-sensitive position in low beta. Two of five adult datasets are near-zero or negative under EO, but all five are positive under EC.

**Boundary (upper) at 19.897 Hz = φ².** The low/high beta border. This boundary has exceptional significance:
- **The 20 Hz motor convergence**: corticomuscular coherence peaks at 17-21 Hz during sustained grip, and the ~20 Hz component of the rolandic mu rhythm is the only sensorimotor oscillation showing CMC with contralateral EMG. 20 Hz tACS causally slows movement in healthy humans (Pogosyan et al.: -9.3% velocity), providing direct causal evidence for beta's antikinetic role (see [[critical-frequencies]]).
- **Parkinson's STN peak**: excessive beta synchronization in the subthalamic nucleus peaks at 18-25 Hz, spanning this boundary. DBS at beta frequencies (~20 Hz) actually worsens motor symptoms, while high-frequency DBS (>100 Hz) disrupts pathological beta. Longer-duration beta bursts (>900 ms) at this frequency correlate with bradykinesia and rigidity severity (see [[corticostriatal-learning]], [[clinical-applications]]).
- **Fibonacci resonance**: as an integer φ-position, this boundary satisfies f(2) = f(1) + f(0), i.e., 19.90 = 12.30 + 7.60 Hz. This Fibonacci three-wave resonance enables energy transfer between the alpha, low-beta, and f₀ bands — but also makes it a destabilization point where oscillations are least stable. The exception noted in Paper 1: 20 Hz (n = 2) shows a resonance peak despite the general pattern of boundary depletion, suggesting the Fibonacci property creates a functional attractor at this specific boundary (see [[canonical-eeg-bands]]).
- **Consciousness gate**: the ~20 Hz boundary supports long-range cortical integration for conscious processing. The Schiff ABCD model places beta (20-35 Hz) emergence as the marker for transitioning from minimally conscious to moderately reconnected states. TMS-evoked 25-30 Hz waves propagate across cortical regions during wakefulness but fail to propagate during NREM sleep (see [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]]).
- **Klimesch's binary hierarchy**: 1.25 × 2⁴ = 20 Hz places this at the fourth doubling above heart rate, consistent with both the φ-lattice and the binary scaling framework.
- **Long-range communication ceiling**: Kopell's work established that oscillations below ~20 Hz can synchronize over conduction delays exceeding 8-10 ms (enabling long-range cortical integration), while faster frequencies are limited to more local circuits. The φ² boundary thus marks the transition between globally-capable and locally-constrained oscillatory communication.

## Low Beta's Dynamics Within the Landscape

The 9-dataset data fundamentally revises the understanding of low-beta dynamics. The ascending ramp is **not motor-paradigm-specific** — it appears in all adult datasets regardless of task:

**The ramp is universal in adults.** The original interpretation (Papers 1-2) was that non-motor paradigms show Noble1 dominance while motor paradigms show upper-octave clustering. The 9-dataset band-specific data disproves this: LEMON (resting state) shows 5° Inv Noble = +301%, stronger than EEGMMIDB (motor tasks) at +263%. CHBMP (resting, 120-channel) shows +202%. The ascending ramp with peak enrichment at ~19 Hz is a general property of adult low beta, not a motor artifact. The GED enrichment at Noble1 reported in Paper 1 (+27.5%) was an all-band aggregate effect driven by alpha and gamma; the band-specific low-beta Noble1 is near-null (-2%).

**EC amplification targets the upper octave.** EC doubles the enrichment at inverse noble positions (5° Inv Noble: +148→+244%) while leaving the depleted lower octave largely unchanged. The EC-EO difference is concentrated at positions 4°-7° Inverse Noble (+67 to +108% gains), while the boundary through Noble1 changes by <25%. This asymmetric amplification means EC sharpens the ramp — making the upper-octave dominance more extreme without rescuing the depleted positions.

**Biophysical basis of the ramp.** The ascending profile reflects the two generator systems:
- **Superficial beta1 (~15 Hz)**: generated by GABAergic interneuron-pyramidal cell interactions in L2/3 through period concatenation (one beta1 cycle = gamma + beta2 period; Roopun et al. 2006). This generator contributes weakly, producing the depleted attractor region (-54%) — beta1 is a task-evoked transient, not a dominant resting peak.
- **Deep beta2 (~19-20 Hz)**: generated by Layer V intrinsically bursting pyramidal neurons through M-current (KCNQ, ~50 ms time constant) and gap junctional coupling, independent of synaptic transmission (see [[cortical-layer-oscillations]], [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]]). This is the dominant resting generator, producing the massive upper-octave enrichment.

The ramp peaking at 19.05 Hz (5° Inv Noble) rather than 19.90 Hz (φ² boundary) is significant: generators approach but avoid the Fibonacci three-wave resonance zone at the boundary. This is consistent with the general pattern of boundary avoidance — but uniquely, low beta's avoidance is asymmetric (only the lower boundary is depleted; the upper boundary approach zone is massively enriched).

**Pediatric divergence — the ramp hasn't formed.** In developing brains (HBN, ages ~5-18), the ascending ramp is dramatically weakened. The enrichment peak shifts from the 5° Inv Noble (+244% adult, +50% pediatric) down to the 2° Noble (+41% pediatric, -66% adult). The 1° Noble is strongly depleted in children (-46%) while near-null in adults (-2%). This suggests a developmental trajectory: immature low-beta generators sit near ~15 Hz (the attractor/2° Noble range), and maturation shifts the dominant peak upward to ~19 Hz as Layer V M-current resonance strengthens and myelination establishes the deep-layer beta2 generator. The pediatric-to-adult transition in low beta may parallel the GABA-A subunit maturation (α2→α1) documented in [[development-and-aging]] and [[neurochemistry-of-oscillations]].

**Corticostriatal dynamics.** Beta at ~16 Hz (Noble1, -2% enrichment) serves as the corticostriatal communication channel despite null resting enrichment — similar to theta's attractor and alpha's upper positions, task-evoked activity can operate at depleted lattice positions. PLV at this frequency differentiates correct from incorrect trials (Pathak et al. 2025; see [[corticostriatal-learning]]).

**Anesthesia.** The "beta buzz" at ~20 Hz during early propofol induction is a transient marker — as anesthetic depth increases, the spectral peak migrates from the φ² boundary zone downward through the ramp, passing the zero-crossing at Noble1 into the depleted lower octave, and continuing into the alpha band at ~12 Hz. This downward migration through the lattice mirrors the Schiff ABCD recovery trajectory in reverse (see [[consciousness-and-anesthesia]]).
