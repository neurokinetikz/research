\begin{abstract}
Human neural oscillations are organized according to golden ratio ($\phisym = 1.618$) mathematics: frequencies follow $f(n) = f_0 \times \phisym^n$ where $f_0 \approx 7.6$ Hz. This architecture manifests as spectral peak depletion at integer $n$ positions (band boundaries) and enrichment at half-integer positions (band centers), providing empirical validation of the $\phisym^n$ architecture theorized by Pletzer et al.\ \parencite{pletzer2010golden} and identifying the absolute fundamental frequency $f_0 = 7.6$ Hz. We discovered and validated this organization through four complementary studies.

\textbf{Study 1---Transient Events:} Analysis of 1,366 Schumann Ignition Events (SIEs)---transient episodes of multi-band network synchronization at Earth-resonant frequencies---across 91 participants, 661 sessions, and three EEG devices characterized harmonic frequencies that suggested $\phisym^n$ relationships ($<$1\% mean ratio error). Individual frequencies varied independently across events (all $|r| < 0.03$), yet ratio precision was preserved---an ``independence-convergence paradox'' indicating population-level rather than event-level constraints. Null controls confirmed genuine organization (Cohen's $d = 1.44$, $p < 0.0001$).

\textbf{Study 2---Single-Channel Spectral Architecture:} Spectral parameterization of 244,955 oscillatory peaks across 968 sessions confirmed predictions derived from the $\phisym^n$ framework: boundaries showed $-18\%$ depletion, attractors $+21\%$ enrichment, and noble positions ($n + 0.618$) $+39\%$ enrichment. The framework extends to an eight-position hierarchy including ``inverse nobles'' ($n + 0.764$, $n + 0.854$)---symmetric to regular nobles about the attractor---which inherit stability through multi-scale Fibonacci pathways. Gamma exhibited strongest adherence ($+144.8\%$), consistent with functional requirements for precise phase relationships. Independent replication in the EEGEmotions-27 dataset \parencite{huy2025eeg} (612,990 peaks, 2,342 sessions) confirmed the same qualitative pattern with Kendall's $\tau = 1.0$.

\textbf{Study 3---Multi-Channel Spatial Coherence:} Generalized Eigendecomposition (GED) analysis of 1,584,561 peaks across 3,261 sessions confirmed $\phisym^n$ organization using spatial coherence---a fundamentally different criterion than single-channel spectral peaks. The combined dataset showed 77.8\% session-level consistency (attractor $>$ boundary) with perfect position ordering (Kendall's $\tau = 1.0$). An extended 12-position hierarchy revealed enrichment monotonically decreasing from 1\degree\ noble ($+27.5\%$) through attractor ($+14.8\%$) to boundary ($-9.2\%$).

\textbf{Study 4---Independent Replication and Structural Specificity:} Analysis of the EEGMMIDB dataset (109 subjects, 64 channels, 1.42--1.86 million peaks at two spectral resolutions) replicated the core $\phisym^n$ lattice structure: boundary depletion ($0.41\times$) and noble enrichment ($1.10\times$) at the aggregate level. A structural specificity analysis tested nine exponential bases---four irrational ($\phisym$, $\sqrt{2}$, $e$, $\pi$) and five rational (1.4, 3/2, 1.7, 1.8, 2)---each evaluated with its own natural lattice positions. $\phisym$ achieved the highest structural score (SS $= 45.6$); irrational bases collectively outperformed rational bases across a broad ``irrational dominance window'' ($f_0 = 7.1$--$9.4$ Hz, bootstrap $p < 0.001$), which entirely contains the Schumann Resonance variation range (7.0--8.5 Hz). Non-exponential (linear grid) controls scored near zero, validating exponential scaling as the correct functional form.

\textbf{Synthesis:} Four independent methodological approaches---transient event detection, single-channel spectral parameterization, multi-channel spatial coherence, and large-scale independent replication with structural specificity testing---converge on identical conclusions: neural oscillations follow $\phisym^n$ organization with perfect position ordering (Kendall's $\tau = 1.0$) across all analyses. The fundamental frequency $f_0 = 7.6$ Hz emerges independently from geophysical monitoring of Earth's Schumann Resonance and from neural spectral optimization, agreeing within 0.4\%. The empirical demonstration that irrational bases collectively outperform rational bases---and that this advantage spans the SR frequency window---transforms the theoretical ``most irrational number'' argument into an empirically validated claim. These findings support a ``substrate-ignition'' model: the $\phisym^n$ lattice exists continuously as an architectural scaffold organizing neural oscillations, while transient high-coherence events (SIEs) represent moments when this substrate is amplified and frequencies ``snap'' into tighter compliance. The golden ratio's unique mathematical properties---maximal resistance to mode-locking between frequency bands combined with precise Fibonacci-mediated cross-frequency coupling pathways---may represent evolution's solution to a fundamental computational challenge: maintaining independent parallel processing streams (segregation) while enabling flexible, controlled communication between them (integration).

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Transient Synchronization Phenomena in EEG}

Neural oscillations are fundamental to brain function, coordinating activity across distributed networks to support perception, cognition, and behavior \parencite{gy_rgy_buzs_ki_c98dac5c, pascal_fries_7b9d1ced}. These rhythmic fluctuations in neuronal population activity occur across characteristic frequency bands---from slow delta oscillations ($<4$ Hz) through high gamma ($>80$ Hz)---each associated with distinct functional roles \parencite{wolfgang_klimesch_be1f7786, engel2010beta, cohen2017where}. Although substantial research has characterized sustained oscillatory states and their behavioral correlates, transient synchronization phenomena---brief episodes of enhanced coordination across brain regions---have received comparatively less systematic investigation despite their potential significance for understanding rapid neural state transitions and information integration \parencite{siegel2012spectral, canolty2010functional}.

Well-characterized examples of transient oscillatory events include sleep spindles \parencite{fernandez2020sleep}, hippocampal sharp-wave ripples \parencite{buzsaki2015hippocampal}, and task-related gamma bursts \parencite{fries2001modulation}. These phenomena share common features: discrete temporal boundaries, elevated power relative to baseline, and enhanced inter-regional synchronization. Such transient events may carry functional significance distinct from sustained oscillations, potentially serving as temporal windows for memory consolidation, inter-regional communication, or state transitions \parencite{john_lisman_100e4cf6}.

The overlap between Earth's Schumann Resonance frequencies (fundamental $\sim$7.83 Hz with harmonics at $\sim$14.3, 20.8, 27.3, and 33.8 Hz) and canonical EEG bands has prompted investigation into potential brain-environment relationships \parencite{konig1974eeg, cherry2002schumann, persinger2008possible, saroka2016similar}. However, prior studies have mainly employed correlational designs examining continuous SR-EEG relationships rather than systematically detecting and characterizing discrete events. The temporal structure, duration, and network dynamics of individual SR-frequency episodes have remained poorly characterized.

During exploratory analysis of EEG recordings from meditation sessions collected between 2019 and 2024, we repeatedly observed transient periods of elevated power spectral density at approximately 7.6--7.8 Hz across multiple brain regions. These events involved coordinated power increases at multiple frequencies, accompanied by marked elevation in inter-channel coherence and phase synchronization---suggesting network-wide coordination rather than isolated oscillatory bursts. This discovery prompted systematic investigation, revealing an unexpected mathematical structure in the frequency relationships that forms the basis of the present paper.

\subsection{The Problem of EEG Frequency Band Definitions}

Since Hans Berger's discovery of the human alpha rhythm in 1929, electroencephalography has been organized around a taxonomy of frequency bands: delta ($<4$ Hz), theta (4--8 Hz), alpha (8--13 Hz), beta (13--30 Hz), and gamma ($>30$ Hz). These bands emerged from empirical observation and clinical utility rather than theoretical derivation.

A systematic review of band definitions across published EEG studies reveals substantial inconsistency:

This variability suggests that current band definitions may not reflect natural categories in neural organization. If frequency bands represented genuine biological discontinuities, we would expect convergence on boundary positions. The observed divergence implies either (a) bands are arbitrary conveniences, or (b) the true boundaries exist but have not been identified.

Evidence for the latter comes from Pletzer et al. \parencite{pletzer2010golden}, who observed that adjacent EEG frequency bands form a geometric series with ratio approximating the golden mean ($\phisym \approx 1.618$): delta2/delta1 $\approx$ 1.67, beta1/alpha = 1.60, gamma1/beta2 = 1.60. They proved mathematically that this architecture provides maximal desynchronization between bands during rest while enabling controlled coupling during active processing. Recent independent work by Kramer \parencite{kramer2022golden} provides complementary theoretical support, proving that $\phisym$ is the \textit{unique} multiplicative factor enabling cross-frequency coupling where all participating frequencies belong to the same geometric series. However, prior frameworks used individual alpha frequency ($\sim$10 Hz) as a relative anchor rather than identifying an absolute fundamental frequency. The present work extends this theoretical foundation by discovering the fundamental frequency $f_0 = 7.6$ Hz and validating the $\phisym^n$ architecture empirically across 244,955 spectral peaks.

As will be demonstrated in this paper, the discovery of golden ratio frequency ratios in transient high-coherence events provides a principled mathematical framework that may resolve this long-standing ambiguity.

\subsection{Schumann Resonance as Potential Reference}

The Earth's ionospheric cavity supports a set of global electromagnetic resonances, termed Schumann Resonances (SR), arising from electromagnetic waves circling the Earth-ionosphere waveguide driven primarily by global lightning activity \parencite{schumann1952strahlungslosen, nickolaenko2014schumann}. For a simplified cavity model, the fundamental frequency is:
\begin{equation}
f_1^{\text{approx}} = \frac{c}{2\pi r} = \frac{299,792,458}{2\pi \times 6,371,000} = 7.49 \text{ Hz}
\end{equation}
where $c$ is the speed of light and $r$ is Earth's radius.

Long-term geophysical monitoring provides direct measurement of SR parameters. The Tomsk Space Observing System (Russia), operating continuously since 1994, reports the fundamental centered at $7.6 \pm 0.2$ Hz with diurnal and seasonal variations of $\pm 0.3$ Hz. The ``canonical'' value of 7.83 Hz frequently cited in literature represents a theoretical approximation; empirical monitoring consistently yields lower values near 7.6 Hz.

\begin{criticalpoint}
Before proceeding, we emphasize that correspondence between neural and Schumann frequencies need not imply direct electromagnetic coupling. Three explanations are possible:

\textbf{(1) Evolutionary tuning:} Schumann Resonances have existed for $\sim$4 billion years (since Earth developed an ionosphere), providing ample evolutionary time. If SR frequencies offer an optimal ``temporal scaffold'' for neural coordination, organisms whose oscillations aligned with this environmental constant may have gained selective advantages: improved internal synchronization, enhanced inter-organism coordination in social species, and access to a stable external reference clock.

\textbf{(2) Biophysical convergence:} Independent optimization to similar frequencies due to shared physical constraints (characteristic length scales, resonance conditions in conductive media).

\textbf{(3) Direct coupling:} Real-time electromagnetic interaction between brain and ionosphere (least supported given $\sim$picoTesla field strengths).

These explanations are not mutually exclusive---evolutionary tuning \textit{to} biophysically optimal frequencies is plausible. The present study documents the $\phisym^n$ architecture without requiring resolution of this question, though Section 8.3 discusses the evidence for each hypothesis.
\end{criticalpoint}

The proximity of the SR fundamental ($\sim$7.6 Hz) to the theta-alpha boundary in human EEG has been noted in previous work. However, investigations have been limited by lack of a theoretical framework specifying \textit{what pattern} to expect if neural frequencies were organized relative to SR. The present study arose from the unexpected discovery of precise mathematical relationships in the harmonic structure of transient neural events---relationships that suggested a framework based on the golden ratio rather than simple integer harmonics.

\subsection{Study Overview: From Discovery to Validation}

This paper follows the actual trajectory of discovery:

\textbf{Phase 1 (Discovery):} During analysis of meditation EEG recordings, we detected transient high-coherence events showing multi-band synchronization at specific frequencies falling within the Schumann Resonance range. We termed these ``Schumann Ignition Events'' (SIEs). Initial characterization revealed 1,366 events across 91 subjects and five cognitive contexts, with robust network synchronization signatures.

\textbf{Phase 2 (Pattern Recognition):} Upon examining the harmonic frequencies detected in SIE events---particularly the relationships between SR1 ($\sim$7.6 Hz), SR3 ($\sim$20 Hz), and SR5 ($\sim$32 Hz)---we observed that their ratios approximated powers of the golden ratio ($\phisym = 1.618$). This observation, while potentially confounded by the SR-derived search windows (which by design fall near $\phisym^n$ lattice positions), motivated systematic investigation through unconstrained methods.

\textbf{Phase 3 (Hypothesis Generation):} The discovery of $\phisym^n$ ratios in transient events raised a fundamental question: Is this organization specific to high-coherence states, or does it reflect a deeper architectural principle governing all EEG frequency organization? We formalized the hypothesis as $f(n) = f_0 \times \phisym^n$ and derived specific predictions for how spectral peaks should be distributed.

\textbf{Phase 4 (Validation):} Systematic analysis of 244,955 oscillatory peaks across 968 EEG sessions confirmed the predicted boundary-attractor structure: depletion at integer $\phisym^n$ positions, enrichment at half-integer positions, and maximal enrichment at ``noble'' positions ($n + 0.618$).

\textbf{Phase 5 (Spatial Validation):} A critical question remained: does $\phisym^n$ organization reflect genuine multi-channel network structure, or could it arise from single-channel spectral artifacts? Generalized Eigendecomposition (GED) analysis of 1,584,561 spatial coherence peaks across 3,261 sessions confirmed the identical position hierarchy (Kendall's $\tau = 1.0$) using a fundamentally different criterion---multi-channel spatial coherence rather than single-channel spectral prominence.

\textbf{Phase 6 (Synthesis):} Convergent evidence from three independent methodological approaches---transient event detection, single-channel spectral parameterization, and multi-channel spatial coherence---supports a ``substrate-ignition'' model in which the $\phisym^n$ lattice exists continuously, with SIEs representing transient amplification of this underlying structure.

\textbf{Phase 7 (Independent Replication and Structural Specificity):} To test the robustness and specificity of $\phisym^n$ organization, we replicated the core lattice structure in the fully independent EEGMMIDB dataset (109 subjects, 64 channels, 1.42--1.86M peaks). A structural specificity analysis then tested nine exponential bases---four irrational ($\phisym$, $\sqrt{2}$, $e$, $\pi$) and five rational---each with its own natural lattice positions, revealing that irrational bases collectively outperform rational alternatives across the Schumann Resonance frequency window.

The following sections present these phases in sequence, beginning with the full methodological and empirical characterization of SIE discovery (Section~2), followed by the theoretical framework that emerged from that discovery (Section~3), single-channel spectral validation testing predictions in continuous EEG (Section~4), multi-channel spatial coherence validation via GED (Section~5), independent replication and structural specificity analysis (Section~6), and the integration of all four lines of evidence (Section~7).

\subsection{Methods: SIE Detection and Analysis}

\subsubsection{Overview}
This study employed a two-phase design: (1) discovery and initial characterization of Schumann Ignition Events (SIEs) in longitudinal meditation EEG recordings, and (2) validation and extension in independent datasets collected during cognitive tasks. All analyses were performed in Python 3.9+ using NumPy, SciPy, MNE-Python \parencite{harris2020numpy, virtanen2020scipy, gramfort2013mne}, and the FOOOF library \parencite{donoghue2020fooof}.

\subsubsection{Participants and Recordings}

\textbf{Discovery Dataset: Longitudinal Meditation Recordings.}
The initial discovery and characterization of SIEs was conducted using EEG recordings from a single experienced meditation practitioner (male, age range 44--48 years, $>$10 years meditation experience). Recordings were collected during eyes-closed meditation sessions spanning November 2019 to March 2024, comprising 34 sessions with a total recording duration of approximately 7.5 hours. Three consumer-grade EEG devices were used across sessions to enable cross-device validation:

\subsubsection{EEG Preprocessing}
Raw EEG signals were high-pass filtered at 1 Hz using a 4th-order zero-phase Butterworth filter \parencite{butterworth1930filter} to remove slow drifts while preserving oscillatory activity in the Schumann Resonance frequency range. All electrodes were positioned according to the international 10-20 system \parencite{jasper1958tentwenty}.

\subsubsection{Schumann Resonance Harmonic Detection}

\textbf{Canonical Frequencies.}
We derived canonical frequencies and search bandwidths from long-term magnetometer data recorded by the Space Observing System in Tomsk, Russia: SR1 = 7.6 Hz ($\pm$0.6 Hz), SR2o = 13.75 Hz ($\pm$0.75 Hz), SR3 = 20 Hz ($\pm$1.0 Hz), SR4 = 25 Hz ($\pm$2.0 Hz), and SR5 = 32 Hz ($\pm$2.5 Hz).

\textbf{Methodological Note on Search Windows.}
The search windows were derived from Tomsk magnetometer observations of Earth's Schumann Resonances. Critically, the empirical SR frequencies (7.6, 20, 32 Hz) happen to align closely with $\phisym^n$ predictions ($\phisym^0$, $\phisym^2$, $\phisym^3$ positions)---a correspondence that motivated this investigation but also means Study 1 constitutes a \textit{constrained} test of $\phisym^n$ organization.

\textbf{Limitations of within-window precision analysis:} By design, 100\% of detected harmonic frequencies fall within these predefined windows. A naive null model (uniform random sampling within windows) yields expected $\phisym$-error of 4.75\%, compared to observed 0.64\%. However, this comparison is methodologically limited: (1) FOOOF is a peak-finding algorithm that locates the strongest spectral peak within each window, not a uniform sampler; (2) inherent EEG spectral structure---including alpha ($\sim$10 Hz), beta ($\sim$20 Hz), and gamma peaks---would produce consistent peak locations regardless of $\phisym^n$ architecture. Consequently, Study 1 \textit{characterizes} SIE phenomenology and harmonic relationships, but cannot independently validate $\phisym^n$ organization. Study 2's agnostic spectral analysis (Section 4), which detects peaks without reference to predefined windows, provides the unconstrained validation test.

\textbf{Spectral Parameterization Using FOOOF.}
To separate oscillatory (periodic) activity from aperiodic (1/f) background noise, we employed FOOOF spectral parameterization \parencite{donoghue2020fooof}:
\begin{equation}
\log_{10}(PSD(f)) = L(f) + \sum_{i} G_{i}(f)
\end{equation}
where $L(f) = b - \chi \times \log_{10}(f)$ represents the aperiodic component and $G_i(f)$ represents Gaussian periodic peaks. FOOOF parameters: frequency range 1--50 Hz, maximum 20 peaks, minimum peak height 0.0001 log units, peak width limits 0.2--20.0 Hz.

\subsubsection{Seven-Stage Detection Pipeline}

We developed a seven-stage pipeline to systematically identify, refine, and validate candidate SIE events:

\textbf{Stage 1: Envelope Thresholding.}
A composite SR envelope was computed by averaging all EEG channels, bandpass filtering at the fundamental frequency ($f_0 \pm 0.6$ Hz), computing the Hilbert envelope, and z-score normalizing. Candidate onsets were identified where $z(t) \geq 3.0$.

\textbf{Stage 2: FOOOF Harmonic Refinement.}
For each candidate window, FOOOF was applied to extract session-specific harmonic frequencies within SR search windows.

\textbf{Stage 3: Kuramoto Phase Synchronization.}
The precise ignition onset $t_0$ was refined using the Kuramoto order parameter:
\begin{equation}
R(t) = \left| \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^{N} e^{i\phi_k(t)} \right|
\end{equation}
where $\phi_k(t)$ is the instantaneous phase of channel $k$ at SR1 frequency. Ignition onset was defined as the time of maximum $dR/dt$ where $R(t) \geq 0.6$.

\textbf{Stage 4: Coherence Characterization.}
Magnitude-squared coherence (MSC) and phase-locking value (PLV) \parencite{lachaux1999plv} were computed for each harmonic:
\begin{equation}
PLV = \frac{1}{T}\left|\sum_{t=1}^{T} e^{i\Delta\phi_{xy}(t)}\right|
\end{equation}

\textbf{Stage 5: Harmonic Organization.}
The Harmonic Stack Index (HSI) quantified the ratio of overtone power to fundamental power: $HSI = P_{\text{overtones}} / P_f$.

\textbf{Stage 6: Quality Metrics.}
Spectral slope ($\beta$) and Frequency Specificity Index (FSI = $P_{SR}/P_{total}$) were computed.

\textbf{Stage 7: Composite Scoring.}
A composite SR-Score was computed using the three observed Schumann harmonics (SR1, SR3, SR5):
\begin{equation}
\text{SR-Score} = \frac{Z_{\text{weighted}}^{0.7} \times \text{MSC}_{\text{weighted}}^{1.2} \times \text{PLV}_{\text{weighted}}}{1 + \text{HSI}}
\end{equation}
with $\phisym$-derived weights: SR1 (0.618), SR3 (0.326), SR5 (0.146).

\subsubsection{Null Control Analyses}

Six null control analyses validated that SIE characteristics represent genuine neural organization:

\textbf{Null Control A (Phase Randomization):} For each SIE, phases were randomized while preserving amplitude spectra (100 surrogates per event), testing whether synchronization reflects genuine phase coupling.

\textbf{Null Control B (Random Temporal Windows):} 1,366 random 3-second baseline windows were sampled from periods $>$10 seconds from any SIE, testing whether SR organization is event-specific.

\textbf{Null Control C (Uniform Random Triplets):} 10,000 random frequency triplets sampled uniformly from physiologically plausible ranges tested whether $\phisym$ precision arises by chance.

\textbf{Null Control D (Peak-Based Random Triplets):} 10,000 triplets sampled from actual FOOOF peaks (N = 244,955 peaks from 661 sessions) provided a more stringent test controlling for EEG spectral structure.

\textbf{Null Control E (Shuffled Bootstrap):} SR1, SR3, SR5 frequencies were independently permuted across events (1,000 iterations), testing whether ratio precision requires within-event coordination. Results: composite error $p = 0.030$ (marginally significant); individual ratios SR3/SR1, SR5/SR1, SR5/SR3 all $p > 0.05$ (non-significant). Interpretation: observed ratio precision is consistent with marginal distributional properties rather than event-level frequency coordination.

Individual SIE events exhibited a stereotyped six-phase temporal evolution:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Baseline:} Low-to-moderate global synchronization ($R \approx 0.4$--$0.6$)
    \item \textbf{Coherence Rise:} Rapid increase in phase alignment preceding amplitude changes, with $R$ typically rising to $>0.7$ over 1--3 seconds

\end{enumerate}

\section{Theoretical Framework: The $\phisym^n$ Hypothesis}

The discovery of precise golden ratio relationships in SIE harmonic frequencies (Section 2.3) raised a fundamental question: Does this organization reflect a deeper architectural principle governing all EEG frequency organization? We formalized the pattern into a testable theoretical framework.

\begin{criticalpoint}
\textbf{Attribution:} The theoretical framework for $\phisym$-ratio organization of neural oscillations was established by Pletzer et al.\ \parencite{pletzer2010golden}, who proved mathematically that the golden ratio uniquely prevents spurious synchronization while enabling controlled coupling. The present study provides large-scale empirical validation of this framework and extends it by (1) identifying the absolute fundamental frequency $f_0 = 7.6$ Hz, (2) characterizing the boundary-attractor structure in continuous EEG, and (3) discovering transient high-coherence states (SIEs) that represent amplified expression of the $\phisym^n$ substrate.
\end{criticalpoint}

\subsection{Golden Ratio Mathematics and Neural Relevance}

\subsubsection{Definition and Properties}
The golden ratio is defined as the positive solution to $x^2 = x + 1$:
\begin{equation}
\phisym = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} = 1.6180339887...
\end{equation}

The golden ratio possesses two unique mathematical properties relevant to coupled oscillator systems:

\textbf{Property 1: Maximal Anti-Commensurability.}
The golden ratio has the continued fraction representation $\phisym = [1; 1, 1, 1, ...]$, which converges more slowly than any other irrational. This means the rational approximations to $\phisym$ (the Fibonacci ratios 1/1, 2/1, 3/2, 5/3, 8/5...) are the \textit{worst possible} among all irrationals of comparable magnitude.

\textit{Implication:} Coupled oscillators at frequency ratio $\phisym$ maximally resist mode-locking. The phase relationship never settles into a stable pattern because $\phisym$ cannot be well-approximated by any simple ratio. This enables \textbf{segregation}: oscillators at different $\phisym^n$ positions maintain independence rather than collapsing into synchrony.

\subsubsection{Mathematical Foundation: Synchronization Avoidance}

Pletzer et al. (2010) provided the first rigorous mathematical proof that the golden ratio uniquely prevents spurious synchronization between neural oscillations \parencite{pletzer2010golden}. Their key results establish:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Mathematical impossibility:} Two oscillations at frequency ratio $\phisym$ can never synchronize in a mathematical sense---their excitatory phases meet at most once regardless of initial phase relationship

    \item \textbf{Minimal physiological coupling:} When phases do come close enough for physiological interaction (within threshold $\delta$), these coincidences are \textit{least frequent} and \textit{most irregular} for $\phisym$ among all possible frequency ratios

    \item \textbf{Unique optimality:} The golden mean is the ``most irrational number''---its continued fraction expansion $[1; 1, 1, 1, ...]$ converges more slowly than any other irrational, making it maximally resistant to rational approximation and thus to mode-locking

    \item \textbf{Noise robustness:} These properties are preserved under stochastic perturbation, ensuring $\phisym$-organization remains stable in noisy neural environments
\end{enumerate}

This theoretical framework provides the mathematical foundation for why the brain would organize frequencies according to $\phisym^n$: it is the \textit{unique} solution maximizing desynchronization while permitting controlled, irregular coupling.

\subsubsection{Cross-Frequency Coupling: Independent Derivation of $\phisym$}

While Pletzer et al. addressed synchronization \textit{avoidance}, Kramer \parencite{kramer2022golden} independently derived $\phisym$ as the unique solution for cross-frequency \textit{coupling}---addressing the complementary problem of how separate rhythms communicate when integration is required.

Using a damped harmonic oscillator model, Kramer showed that resonance between a driver oscillation (frequency $f_D$) and target oscillation ($f_T$) requires sinusoidal gain modulation at a specific frequency $f_S$ satisfying:
\begin{equation}
f_S = f_T - f_D, \quad f_S = f_D - f_T, \quad \text{or} \quad f_S = f_D + f_T
\label{eq:kramer_resonance}
\end{equation}

The critical question is: for frequencies organized as $f_k = f_0 \times c^k$, what value of $c$ ensures the gain frequency $f_S$ is \textit{itself} a member of the original frequency set? Consider neighboring rhythms $f_k$, $f_{k+1}$, $f_{k+2}$ where the gain frequency must couple two adjacent rhythms to the next. Using the additive condition ($f_S = f_D + f_T$):
\begin{equation}
f_{k+2} = f_{k+1} + f_k \quad \Rightarrow \quad f_0 c^{k+2} = f_0 c^{k+1} + f_0 c^k \quad \Rightarrow \quad c^2 - c - 1 = 0
\end{equation}

The unique positive solution is $c = \phisym$. This derivation establishes that $\phisym$ is not merely ``good'' for neural organization---it is the \textit{only} scaling factor enabling cross-frequency coupling where all participating frequencies (driver, target, and gain) belong to the same $\phisym^n$ set. Alternative factors like Euler's number ($e$) require gain frequencies outside the original set; integer ratios (powers of 2) produce non-selective ``coupling superhighways'' \parencite{hoppensteadt1997weakly}.

\textit{Convergence of independent derivations.} Pletzer et al. derived $\phisym$ from synchronization avoidance; Kramer derived $\phisym$ from cross-frequency coupling requirements. \subsubsection{FOOOF Spectral Parameterization}
We employed FOOOF (Fitting Oscillations and One-Over-F) \parencite{donoghue2020fooof} for spectral parameterization. FOOOF separates the power spectrum into:
\begin{equation}
P(f) = L(f) + \sum_{i} G_i(f)
\end{equation}
where $L(f) = b - \log(f^\chi)$ is the aperiodic component and $G_i(f)$ are Gaussian peaks with center frequency $\mu_i$, amplitude $a_i$, and bandwidth $\sigma_i$.

FOOOF parameters: frequency range 1--50 Hz, peak width limits 0.2--20.0 Hz, maximum 20 peaks, minimum peak height 0.0001 log units. Peaks were retained if power $>$ 0.1 log units above aperiodic fit, bandwidth 1--8 Hz, and frequency within 1--48 Hz.

\subsubsection{Lattice Coordinate Analysis}
To analyze peaks independent of band-specific effects, we computed the lattice coordinate for each peak:
\begin{equation}
u = \left[\log_\phisym(f/f_0)\right] \bmod 1
\end{equation}
This maps all frequencies to the unit interval $[0, 1)$, where $u = 0$ corresponds to boundaries, $u = 0.5$ to attractors, and $u = 0.618$ to primary noble positions.

\section{Study 3: Multi-Channel Spatial Coherence Analysis}

Studies 1 and 2 established $\phisym^n$ organization through transient event detection and single-channel spectral parameterization. A critical question remains: does this architecture reflect genuine network-level organization, or could it arise from uncorrelated single-channel phenomena? Generalized Eigendecomposition (GED) addresses this by identifying frequencies where multi-channel activity exhibits coherent spatial structure---a fundamentally different criterion than spectral peak amplitude.

\subsection{Methods: GED Spatial Filtering}

Generalized Eigendecomposition (GED) \parencite{cohen2017ged} identifies spatial filters that maximize narrowband signal-to-noise ratio across channels. For each target frequency $f$, GED solves:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{S} \mathbf{w} = \lambda \mathbf{R} \mathbf{w}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{S}$ is the covariance matrix of narrowband-filtered data ($f \pm 0.5$ Hz), $\mathbf{R}$ is the broadband reference covariance, and the leading eigenvalue $\lambda$ quantifies spatial coherence at that frequency.

\subsubsection{Eigenvalue Sweep Protocol}

GED spatial filtering employed a continuous frequency sweep from 4.5--45.0 Hz in 0.1 Hz steps. At each target frequency $f$, data were narrowband filtered ($f \pm 0.5$ Hz, 4th-order Butterworth) and the covariance matrix $\mathbf{S}$ computed over 10-second windows with 5-second overlap. The reference covariance $\mathbf{R}$ was computed from the full broadband signal (1--50 Hz).

\textbf{Regularization:} To ensure numerical stability, covariance matrices were regularized using shrinkage toward the identity:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{R}_{reg} = (1-\alpha)\mathbf{R} + \alpha \cdot \text{tr}(\mathbf{R})/N \cdot \mathbf{I}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 0.01$ and $N$ is the number of channels.

\textbf{Peak Detection:} From each session's eigenvalue profile $\lambda(f)$, local maxima were detected using prominence-based thresholding (minimum prominence = 0.1 $\times$ session maximum). Peaks below 4.5 Hz or above 45.0 Hz were excluded.

\subsubsection{Lattice Coordinate Analysis}

Peak frequencies were mapped to lattice coordinates using:
\begin{equation}
u = \left[\log_\phisym(f/f_0)\right] \bmod 1
\end{equation}
This projects all peaks onto the unit interval $[0, 1)$, where position types correspond to fixed offsets: boundaries at $u = 0$, attractors at $u = 0.5$, 1\degree\ nobles at $u = 0.618$, etc.

Enrichment at each position was computed as the fractional deviation from uniform expectation within $\pm 0.05$ windows:
\begin{equation}
\text{Enrichment}_p = \frac{N_{\text{observed},p} / N_{\text{total}}}{0.10} - 1
\end{equation}
where $N_{\text{observed},p}$ counts peaks within $\pm 0.05$ of position $p$.

\textbf{Critical distinction}: FOOOF (Study 2) asks ``where are spectral peaks in single channels?'' while GED asks ``at what frequencies does the multi-channel signal show coherent spatial structure?'' Convergence across both methods provides strong evidence for $\phisym^n$ organization.

\subsubsection{Combined Dataset}
GED spatial filtering was applied to 3,261 sessions across three datasets spanning diverse cognitive contexts: PhySF (N=46 sessions; meditation and cognitive flow), MPENG (N=900 sessions; gaming engagement), and EEGEmotions-27 (N=2,315 of 2,342 sessions; 27 excluded due to insufficient data length for stable eigendecomposition). Meditation and VEP datasets were excluded: Muse (4 channels) and Insight (5 channels) provide insufficient spatial dimensionality for stable eigendecomposition, while the Files dataset (5 sessions) contributed too few sessions for meaningful aggregate statistics.

\section{Study 4: Independent Replication and Structural Specificity}
\label{sec:study4}

Studies 1--3 establish \textit{that} $\phisym^n$ organization exists. A critical follow-up question is: \textit{why $\phisym$ specifically?} Pletzer et al.\ \parencite{pletzer2010golden} provide a theoretical answer---$\phisym$ is the ``most irrational number''---but the claim has not been tested empirically against competing exponential bases. This section reports an independent replication of the core lattice structure in a large public dataset, followed by a structural specificity analysis that tests whether irrational bases (particularly $\phisym$) systematically outperform rational alternatives.

\subsection{Methods: EEGMMIDB Dataset and Spectral Parameterization}
\label{sec:study4_methods}

We analyzed the EEG Motor Movement/Imagery Dataset (EEGMMIDB) from PhysioNet \parencite{goldberger2000physiobank, schalk2004bci2000}, comprising 64-channel EEG recordings from 109 subjects performing resting-state and motor tasks. The BCI2000 system recorded at 160~Hz with the international 10-10 electrode system, yielding 1,526 sessions (109 subjects $\times$ 14 runs).

Spectral parameterization via SpecParam/FOOOF \parencite{donoghue2020fooof} was performed at two spectral resolutions: nperseg~$= 640$ (0.25~Hz resolution, 1,863,167 peaks) and nperseg~$= 256$ (0.625~Hz resolution, 1,420,997 peaks). Liberal detection parameters (peak threshold $= 0.001$, minimum height $= 0.0001$, width limits $= [0.2, 20]$~Hz) maximized sensitivity. This dataset is fully independent of the primary (PhySF/MultiPENG/VEP) and emotions (EEGEmotions-27) datasets used in Studies~1--3, sharing no subjects, devices, or recording sites.

\subsection{Results: Core Lattice Replication}
\label{sec:study4_replication}

The EEGMMIDB dataset replicated the core $\phisym^n$ lattice structure observed in Studies~2 and~3. At the aggregate level (1.86M peaks, nperseg~$= 640$), boundary positions ($u = 0$) showed $0.41\times$ depletion relative to uniform expectation, while 1\degree\ noble positions ($u = 0.618$) showed $1.10\times$ enrichment. Cross-resolution analysis confirmed stability: the same qualitative pattern held at nperseg~$= 256$ (1.42M peaks), establishing that $\phisym^n$ organization is robust to spectral resolution choices.

\subsection{Analysis: Structural Specificity}
\label{sec:study4_specificity}

Having confirmed that $\phisym^n$ organization replicates in an independent dataset, we tested whether $\phisym$ is specifically optimal or whether other exponential bases produce equivalent structure.

\subsubsection{Methods}
\label{sec:study4_specificity_methods}

Nine exponential bases were tested: four irrational ($\phisym = 1.618$, $\sqrt{2}$, $e$, $\pi$) and five rational ($1.4$, $3/2$, $1.7$, $1.8$, $2$). Each base $b$ was given its own natural positions derived from $1/b$: boundary $= 0.0$, attractor $= 0.5$, noble $= 1/b$, and symmetric counterparts, with positions too close together ($<0.02$) suppressed. This corrects a methodological flaw in the companion study that used $\phisym$-derived positions for all bases.

For each base, we computed the structural score:
\begin{equation}
\text{SS}(b) = -E_{\text{boundary}}(b) + E_{\text{attractor}}(b) + \overline{E}_{\text{nobles}}(b)
\label{eq:structural_score}
\end{equation}
where $E$ denotes enrichment at each position in that base's lattice coordinates $u = [\log_b(f/f_0)] \bmod 1$. High SS indicates the mode-locking pattern predicted by nonlinear dynamics: peaks avoiding integer lattice points (boundaries) and clustering at half-integer and noble positions.

\textbf{Bootstrap gap test.} To test whether irrational bases collectively outperform rational bases, we defined the gap statistic $G(f_0) = \overline{\text{SS}}_{\text{irr}} - \overline{\text{SS}}_{\text{rat}}$. Peak-level bootstrap resampling (1,000 iterations of the full 1.42--1.86M peak dataset) yielded bootstrap distributions of $G$ at each anchor frequency $f_0$ across 7.0--10.0~Hz (0.10~Hz steps), providing 95\% confidence intervals with arbitrary resolution. A complementary exact permutation test exhaustively evaluated all $\binom{9}{4} = 126$ assignments of 4 bases to the ``irrational'' group.

\textbf{Non-exponential control.} To verify that exponential scaling itself is the correct functional form, we tested nine linear grids ($f_0 + n \times k$ for $k \in \{3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 12\}$~Hz). For each step size $k$, the linear lattice coordinate $u = [(f - f_0)/k] \bmod 1$ was computed and the structural score calculated using boundary ($u = 0$) and attractor ($u = 0.5$) positions.

\subsubsection{Results: $\phisym$ as Global Optimum}
\label{sec:study4_results}

At the peak anchor frequency ($f_0 = 8.50$~Hz, nperseg~$= 640$), $\phisym$ achieved the highest structural score among all bases (SS $= 45.6$), followed by base~2 (SS $= 33.6$), $\pi$ (SS $= 24.5$), and $e$ (SS $= 17.5$). Rational bases showed systematically lower or negative scores: $3/2$ (SS $= -24.8$), $1.7$ (SS $= -27.9$). The bootstrap gap was strongly positive at this $f_0$: $G = +28.9$ (95\% CI: $[+28.5, +29.4]$, bootstrap $p < 0.001$; exact permutation $p = 0.063$).

\textbf{Irrational dominance window.} The 95\% bootstrap CI on $G$ excluded zero across a broad range of anchor frequencies: $f_0 = 7.1$--$9.4$~Hz (nperseg~$= 256$) and $f_0 = 7.4$--$9.4$~Hz (nperseg~$= 640$). Within this ``irrational dominance window,'' the class-level gap was robustly positive (all bootstrap $p < 0.001$). Critically, the Schumann Resonance fundamental variation range (7.0--8.5~Hz) falls entirely within the irrational dominance window at both resolutions (Figure~\ref{fig:structural_specificity}).

\textbf{Cross-resolution replication.} The bootstrap gap pattern replicated across spectral resolutions: peak $G = +37.3$ at $f_0 = 8.30$~Hz (nperseg~$= 256$, exact permutation $p = 0.024$) and peak $G = +28.9$ at $f_0 = 8.50$~Hz (nperseg~$= 640$, exact permutation $p = 0.063$). The two tests answer different questions: the exact permutation tests whether the irrational/rational labeling is specifically meaningful (stricter, single-comparison), while the bootstrap tests whether the class-level gap is reliably positive (broader structural claim). Both support irrational dominance, with different statistical power profiles.

\subsection{The Independence-Convergence Paradox}

The most theoretically significant finding is the \textbf{independence-convergence paradox} revealed in Study 1:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Independence:} Harmonic frequencies (SR1, SR3, SR5) vary completely independently across events (all pairwise $|r| < 0.03$)
    \item \textbf{Convergence:} Despite independence, harmonic \textit{ratios} maintain $<1\%$ deviation from $\phisym^n$ predictions
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Resolution:} The $\phisym^n$ relationships are encoded in the \textbf{population-level marginal distributions} of each harmonic rather than through event-level coordination. Each oscillatory generator is independently constrained to a frequency distribution whose mean satisfies $\phisym^n$ relationships relative to other harmonics.

\textbf{Evidence from shuffled bootstrap:} When SR1, SR3, SR5 frequencies were independently permuted across events (1,000 iterations), destroying within-event pairings while preserving marginal distributions, the results provided limited support for event-level coordination. The composite error showed only \textit{marginally significant} improvement over the null ($p = 0.030$, borderline at $\alpha = 0.05$). More critically, \textit{no individual ratio} (SR3/SR1, SR5/SR1, SR5/SR3) reached statistical significance (all $p > 0.05$). This failure to demonstrate significant within-event coordination for any individual ratio indicates that the observed $\phisym^n$ precision emerges primarily from the marginal distributions of each harmonic---shaped by the search windows and underlying EEG spectral structure---rather than from coordinated frequency generation within individual events.

This interpretation underscores why Study 2's continuous spectral analysis provides stronger evidence: it detects peaks agnostically without constrained search windows, confirming $\phisym^n$ architecture through methodology independent of the detection pipeline assumptions that limit Study 1's conclusions.

\textbf{Biological mechanism:} What biophysical process could produce population-level constraints among independently varying frequencies? One possibility is that each frequency band is constrained by local biophysical properties---membrane time constants, network architecture, receptor kinetics---that independently converge on $\phisym^n$ values. The GABA-A receptor decay time constant ($\sim$25 ms) directly determines gamma oscillation frequency \parencite{bartos2007synaptic}. If these timescales have been evolutionarily tuned to produce compatible frequency relationships, independent variation within each system could still satisfy population-level $\phisym^n$ constraints.

\subsubsection{Theoretical Connection to Pletzer et al.}

The independence-convergence paradox finds theoretical precedent in Pletzer et al.'s \parencite{pletzer2010golden} mathematical analysis. They proved that oscillations at $\phisym$-ratio frequencies show:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Phase independence:} Excitatory phases never synchronize in a mathematical sense, preventing mode-locking between frequency bands
    \item \textbf{Controlled coupling:} Physiological phase meetings occur rarely and irregularly, enabling spontaneous coupling/uncoupling
\end{enumerate}

Our empirical finding---independent frequency variation with preserved ratio precision---is consistent with this theoretical framework. The $\phisym^n$ architecture enforces ratio relationships through distributional constraints while permitting independent frequency fluctuation within each band.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Summary of Findings}

This study provides large-scale empirical validation of the golden ratio ($\phisym^n$) organization of human EEG frequency architecture theorized by Pletzer et al.\ \parencite{pletzer2010golden}, and extends the framework by identifying the absolute fundamental frequency $f_0 = 7.6$ Hz, characterizing transient high-coherence states, and demonstrating structural specificity of irrational bases. Evidence emerged through a four-phase investigation:

\textbf{Phase 1 (Study 1): Discovery of SIEs.} Analysis of meditation EEG revealed transient high-coherence neural states (Schumann Ignition Events) exhibiting precise frequency organization. Across 1,366 events, 91 participants, five cognitive contexts, and three EEG devices, SIEs showed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Multi-band synchronization at specific harmonic frequencies (SR1, SR3, SR5)
    \item Harmonic ratios deviating $<1\%$ from $\phisym^n$ predictions
    \item Complete independence of individual frequencies (all $|r| < 0.03$) despite ratio precision
    \item Stereotyped temporal dynamics with coherence preceding amplitude (``ignition'' signature)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Phase 2 (Study 2): Single-channel spectral validation.} Testing whether $\phisym^n$ organization extends beyond transient events using spectral parameterization. 857,945 FOOOF peaks across primary (244,955) and EEGEmotions-27 (612,990) datasets confirmed the predicted hierarchy with Kendall's $\tau = 1.0$ in both:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Boundary depletion: $-18\%$ (primary), $-20\%$ (emotions)
    \item 1\degree\ Noble enrichment: $+39\%$ (primary), $+26\%$ (emotions)
    \item Gamma showing strongest adherence ($+144.8\%$ at 1\degree\ noble)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Phase 3 (Study 3): Multi-channel spatial coherence.} A critical question remained: does $\phisym^n$ organization reflect genuine network-level structure? GED analysis of 1,584,561 peaks across 3,261 sessions confirmed $\phisym^n$ organization using spatial coherence criterion:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Perfect hierarchy: Kendall's $\tau = 1.0$, 1\degree\ Noble $+27.5\%$
    \item Session-level consistency: 77.8\% showed attractor $>$ boundary
    \item Extended 12-position hierarchy confirmed monotonic enrichment gradient
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Phase 4 (Study 4): Independent replication and structural specificity.} The EEGMMIDB dataset (109 subjects, 64 channels, 1.42--1.86M peaks) replicated the core lattice structure in a fully independent dataset. A structural specificity analysis tested nine exponential bases with their own natural lattice positions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\phisym$ achieved the highest structural score (SS $= 45.6$) among all bases
    \item Irrational bases collectively outperformed rational bases across $f_0 = 7.1$--$9.4$~Hz (bootstrap $p < 0.001$)
    \item The ``irrational dominance window'' entirely contains the SR variation range (7.0--8.5~Hz)
    \item Non-exponential (linear grid) controls scored near zero, validating the exponential functional form
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Methodological convergence:} FOOOF (spectral), GED (spatial), and EEGMMIDB (independent replication) yield consistent qualitative patterns, confirming $\phisym^n$ reflects network-level organization visible through independent methodological criteria and across independent datasets.

The \textbf{substrate-ignition model} integrates these findings: a continuous $\phisym^n$ lattice underlies neural frequency organization, with SIEs representing transient amplification of this architecture.

\subsection{Mechanistic Interpretations}

\subsubsection{Why Gamma Shows Strongest Adherence}
The dramatic gamma enrichment at $\phisym^n$ positions ($+144.8\%$ vs. $<10\%$ for other bands) is consistent with gamma's stringent functional requirements. Gamma oscillations subserve temporal binding, feature integration, and conscious perception---functions demanding precise phase relationships within $\sim$25 ms windows \parencite{canolty2010functional, lisman2013thetagamma}. The GABA-A receptor decay constant ($\tau \approx 25$ ms) creates a natural oscillatory bottleneck near 40 Hz \parencite{bartos2007synaptic}. $\phisym^n$ organization may provide the anti-mode-locking properties needed to maintain gamma oscillator independence while enabling cross-frequency integration (Fibonacci coupling).

Notably, the GABA-A receptor decay time constant ($\tau \approx 25$ ms) produces gamma oscillations near 40 Hz, which closely matches the $\phisym^{3.5}$ attractor position (40.95 Hz). Whether this correspondence reflects evolutionary tuning of receptor kinetics to $\phisym^n$ constraints, convergent biophysical optimization, or coincidence cannot be determined from spectral data alone.

Lower-frequency bands serve functions---gating, memory consolidation, homeostatic regulation---that tolerate or benefit from frequency flexibility. The $\phisym^n$ architecture is universal, but expression strength reflects functional requirements.

\subsubsection{Two Synchronization Subsystems}
Analysis of cross-frequency amplitude correlations revealed two partially dissociable subsystems:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Theta-anchored system (SR1):} Moderate coupling to higher harmonics ($r = 0.32$--$0.54$), associated with hippocampal-cortical communication and working memory \parencite{hyman2005medial}
    \item \textbf{Beta-gamma complex (SR3--SR6):} Tightly coupled (all $r > 0.6$), associated with sensorimotor integration and long-range cortical coordination \parencite{engel2010beta}
\end{itemize}

This architecture may enable independent recruitment of theta-based and beta-gamma coordination during SIE ignition, with full integration occurring through $\phisym^n$ constraints.

\subsubsection{Band-Specific Position Preferences: Theta vs. Gamma}
\label{sec:theta_gamma_inverse}

The extended noble hierarchy (Section~\ref{sec:coupling_geometry}) provides a framework for understanding band-specific frequency positioning. Empirical analysis of position-type enrichment within each band reveals differential adherence to the predicted coupling geometry:

\textbf{Theta band (4.7--7.6 Hz): Inverse Noble Enrichment Confirmed.}
Theta oscillations serve as phase-organizing carriers for gamma activity through theta-gamma phase-amplitude coupling (PAC) \parencite{lisman2013thetagamma}. Band-stratified analysis reveals that theta strongly favors inverse noble positions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item 4\degree\ Inverse: $+47.2\%$ enrichment
    \item 3\degree\ Inverse: $+24.0\%$ enrichment
    \item 1\degree\ Noble: $+2.2\%$ (near baseline)
\end{itemize}

This pattern supports the coupling geometry prediction: theta oscillators preferentially occupy positions maximally protected from the upper (theta/alpha) boundary while remaining accessible for upward coupling to gamma. The strong inverse noble enrichment suggests that avoiding alpha mode-locking is a primary constraint on theta frequency positioning.

\textbf{Gamma band (32--52 Hz): 1\degree\ Noble Dominance.}
Contrary to initial predictions, gamma oscillations do \textit{not} favor inverse noble positions. Instead, gamma shows overwhelming preference for the central 1\degree\ noble:
\begin{itemize}
    \item 1\degree\ Noble: $+144.0\%$ enrichment
    \item 3\degree\ Inverse: $-39.2\%$ depletion
    \item 4\degree\ Inverse: $-99.7\%$ depletion (near complete avoidance)
\end{itemize}

This unexpected finding suggests that gamma's primary constraint is \textit{maximal anti-mode-locking} (achieved at the 1\degree\ noble) rather than upper-boundary protection. The $\sim$25 ms temporal binding window may require frequency precision that only the most stable position---the 1\degree\ noble, with its Fibonacci-ratio approximations---can provide. The severe depletion at inverse noble positions indicates that gamma oscillators actively avoid these locations, possibly because the sum-structure of inverse nobles introduces subtle resonance pathways incompatible with gamma's precision requirements.

\subsubsection{Cross-Context Replication: EEGEmotions-27 Dataset}
The EEGEmotions-27 dataset \parencite{huy2025eeg} provides comprehensive independent validation in affect-driven contexts distinct from meditation and cognitive tasks. Despite recording during emotion induction paradigms across 27 fine-grained emotional categories, $\phisym^n$ organization was robustly preserved:

The attenuated enrichment magnitudes ($+27\%$ vs.\ $+39\%$ at 1\degree\ noble) suggest emotional processing may introduce additional frequency variability while preserving the underlying $\phisym^n$ architecture. This pattern---preserved structure with reduced precision---is consistent with the substrate-ignition model: the $\phisym^n$ lattice provides a stable architectural constraint that persists across cognitive and affective states, with expression magnitude modulated by task demands and state-dependent frequency variability. The fact that 2.5$\times$ more peaks from an entirely independent dataset and recording context replicate all qualitative findings provides strong evidence for the universality of $\phisym^n$ neural organization.

\subsubsection{The Golden Mean as ``Most Irrational Number''}

Pletzer et al. \parencite{pletzer2010golden} established that $\phisym$ is the ``most irrational number''---its continued fraction expansion $[1; 1, 1, 1, ...]$ converges more slowly than any other irrational, making rational approximations maximally poor. This property has direct neural implications:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Anti-mode-locking:} Phase relationships between $\phisym$-spaced frequencies never settle into stable patterns, preserving functional independence of frequency bands
    \item \textbf{Noise appearance:} Mechanisms to avoid synchronization (amplitude down-regulation, frequency shifts) create irregular patterns resembling noise---yet these ``noisy'' patterns obey strict mathematical rules
    \item \textbf{Healthy variability:} The spontaneous, irregular coupling/uncoupling enabled by $\phisym$ architecture may underlie healthy neural network dynamics, analogous to healthy heart rate variability
\end{enumerate}

Study~4's structural specificity analysis provides direct empirical support for this theoretical argument. Among nine exponential bases, rational bases (1.4, 3/2, 1.7, 1.8, 2) showed systematically negative structural scores at the optimal anchor frequency---meaning that EEG spectral peaks are \textit{anti-correlated} with the positions predicted by rational exponential scaling. This anti-correlation is precisely what mode-locking avoidance predicts: if the brain actively avoids rational frequency relationships to prevent pathological synchronization, peaks should be depleted at positions where rational-base lattice coordinates predict enrichment.

The class-level gap between irrational and rational bases ($G = +28.9$ at $f_0 = 8.50$~Hz, 95\% CI: $[+28.5, +29.4]$, bootstrap $p < 0.001$) demonstrates that this is not specific to $\phisym$ alone but extends to irrational bases collectively. However, $\phisym$'s position as the global optimum among all bases tested---with the highest structural score at 45.6, exceeding the next-best base 2 (SS $= 33.6$)---is consistent with its unique status as the most poorly approximable irrational number.

Pathological states may involve breakdown of $\phisym$ organization---either through excessive coupling (as in epileptic hypersynchrony) or excessive isolation (as in disorders of integration).

\subsubsection{Relationship to Klimesch Frequency Architecture}

Our findings complement the frequency architecture model proposed by Klimesch and colleagues \parencite{pletzer2010golden, klimesch2013algorithm}. While their framework uses individual alpha frequency ($\sim$10 Hz) as a reference point for relative band organization, our discovery identifies the absolute frequency lattice at $f_0 = 7.6$ Hz. The two approaches are consistent: both identify $\phisym$ as the critical scaling factor, but operate at different levels of description---Klimesch's work addresses inter-band ratios while ours identifies the underlying coordinate system anchored to a specific fundamental.

The convergence of $f_0$ estimates from geophysical (Schumann Resonance) and neural (SIE detection) sources suggests that 7.6 Hz represents a privileged frequency in the brain's oscillatory organization---potentially the ``ground state'' from which individual alpha frequency variation emerges as a population-level distribution centered on $\phisym^{0.5} \approx 9.7$ Hz.

\subsection{Relationship to Schumann Resonance}

As noted in Section 1.3, the term ``Schumann Ignition Events'' references the frequency correspondence between neural oscillations and Earth's Schumann Resonances without implying direct electromagnetic coupling. Several aspects warrant careful interpretation:

\textbf{Correspondence, not demonstrated coupling.} The measured SR fundamental (7.83 Hz) provides the poorest fit to neural frequencies (3.28\% error). The empirical $f_0 = 7.6$ Hz from Tomsk monitoring and neural SIE data provides better fit (1.45\% error). Without concurrent magnetometer recording, we cannot assess whether SIEs correlate with actual SR activity.

\textbf{Three hypotheses:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Evolutionary tuning:} Neural frequencies evolved to match planetary electromagnetic environment
    \item \textbf{Biophysical convergence:} Independent optimization to similar frequencies due to shared physical constraints (characteristic length scales, resonance conditions)
    \item \textbf{Direct coupling:} Real-time electromagnetic interaction between brain and ionosphere
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Parsimony and direct coupling.} Given the extremely weak SR field strengths ($\sim$picoTesla) and the absence of plausible coupling mechanisms, direct electromagnetic interaction (hypothesis 3) is the least supported explanation.

\subsubsection{The Case for Evolutionary Tuning}

While biophysical convergence provides a parsimonious explanation, evolutionary tuning merits serious consideration:

\textbf{Temporal opportunity.} Schumann Resonances have existed for approximately 4 billion years, since Earth developed a conductive ionosphere capable of supporting electromagnetic cavity resonances. This timescale---spanning the entire evolution of nervous systems from the simplest neural networks to the human brain---provides ample opportunity for frequency optimization through natural selection.

\textbf{Selective pressure for a temporal scaffold.} If SR frequencies provide an optimal ``temporal scaffold'' for neural coordination, organisms whose oscillatory frequencies aligned with this environmental constant would have gained multiple selective advantages:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Improved internal synchronization:} A stable external frequency reference could serve as a zeitgeber for coordinating distributed neural processes, analogous to how circadian rhythms synchronize to the solar day
    \item \textbf{Enhanced inter-organism coordination:} In social species, shared oscillatory references could facilitate behavioral synchronization, collective decision-making, and coordinated group behavior---functions with clear fitness implications
    \item \textbf{Stable reference clock:} Unlike internal oscillators subject to metabolic drift, SR frequencies provide an environmentally stable reference that could anchor neural timing across varying physiological states
\end{itemize}

\textbf{The $\phisym^n$ connection.} Intriguingly, the $\phisym^n$ architecture we document may represent the \textit{optimal way} to organize frequencies around an SR-anchored fundamental. The golden ratio's unique mathematical properties---maximal resistance to mode-locking combined with Fibonacci coupling pathways---could have been evolutionarily selected \textit{given} an SR-anchored $f_0$. Under this interpretation, evolutionary tuning and biophysical optimization are complementary: organisms evolved to anchor at SR frequencies ($f_0 = 7.6$ Hz) and to organize harmonics according to $\phisym^n$ mathematics because this combination optimizes the segregation-integration balance.

\textbf{Empirical constraint from structural specificity.} Study~4's $f_0$ sweep analysis provides a new empirical constraint on the SR relationship. The ``irrational dominance window''---the range of anchor frequencies where irrational bases collectively outperform rational bases with 95\% bootstrap confidence---spans $f_0 = 7.1$--$9.4$~Hz. The SR fundamental variation range (7.0--8.5~Hz) falls entirely within this window at both spectral resolutions tested. This overlap is consistent with the evolutionary tuning hypothesis: if neural oscillations were tuned to an SR-anchored fundamental, we would expect the SR frequency range to lie within the region where irrational (anti-mode-locking) organization provides the greatest advantage over rational alternatives. The $f_0$ sweep range (7.0--10.0~Hz) was chosen to span the theta-alpha transition broadly, not specifically to target SR frequencies; the overlap with the SR range is therefore an empirical finding rather than a design artifact.

\textbf{Cross-species predictions.} If evolutionary tuning occurred, we would predict that $\phisym^n$ organization with $f_0 \approx 7.6$ Hz should be conserved across species with independently evolved nervous systems. Preliminary evidence suggests similar frequency organization in mammals despite 17,000-fold brain volume variation \parencite{buzsaki2013scaling}, though systematic cross-species validation of $\phisym^n$ architecture remains a critical direction for future research.

\textbf{The non-exclusive nature of explanations.} Evolutionary tuning and biophysical convergence are not mutually exclusive. The most likely scenario may be that biophysical constraints (membrane time constants, axonal conduction velocities, synaptic kinetics) create a landscape of ``natural'' frequencies, and evolutionary pressure fine-tuned these frequencies toward SR alignment because of the coordination advantages such alignment provides. The observed $f_0$ convergence (7.60 Hz geophysical, 7.63 Hz neural) within 0.4\% may reflect this dual optimization.

\subsubsection{The SR2/SR2o Mismatch}

Table~\ref{tab:harmonic_definitions} reveals a notable exception to the $\phisym^n$ framework: the geophysically canonical 2nd Schumann Resonance harmonic (SR2o $\approx$ 13.75 Hz) does not align with the $\phisym^n$-predicted frequency (SR2 $= \phisym^1 \times 7.6 = 12.3$ Hz). Both frequencies were reliably detected---SR2 in 1,050 events (measured: 11.98 Hz), SR2o in 1,150 events (measured: 13.76 Hz)---suggesting both are genuine neural phenomena rather than artifacts.

This mismatch has important theoretical implications:

\textbf{Two independent organizational systems.} The Earth's ionospheric cavity produces Schumann Resonances at frequencies determined by electromagnetic waveguide physics ($f_n \approx 7.8\sqrt{n(n+1)}$ Hz), yielding the 2nd harmonic near 14.3 Hz. Neural oscillations, in contrast, appear organized according to $\phisym^n$ mathematics with the 2nd position at 12.3 Hz. These represent distinct physical systems with different governing equations.

\textbf{Frequency proximity without identity.} The $\sim$1.8 Hz gap between SR2 (12.3 Hz) and SR2o (14.3 Hz) is large enough to represent distinct phenomena but small enough to fall within the same broad ``low beta'' band. This proximity---combined with the precise alignment of SR1, SR3, SR5 with both systems---may explain why earlier literature conflated neural and geophysical frequencies.

\textbf{Evidence for biophysical convergence.} The existence of both SR2 and SR2o as reliably detected neural peaks strengthens the ``biophysical convergence'' interpretation: neural and geophysical systems have evolved/emerged to occupy similar but non-identical frequency ranges due to shared physical constraints, rather than through direct electromagnetic coupling.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Consumer-grade EEG:} Limited spatial resolution (4--14 channels) constrains source localization. However, GED analysis exploits available spatial structure and cross-device consistency argues against device-specific artifacts.

    \item \textbf{No concurrent magnetometer:} Cannot test SR coupling hypotheses without simultaneous SR field measurement.

    \item \textbf{Population demographics:} Participants skewed toward tech-savvy users of consumer EEG; clinical populations may show different patterns.

    \item \textbf{FOOOF limitations:} Spectral parameterization is less reliable at low frequencies (delta/theta) where 1/f noise dominates.

    \item \textbf{EEGEmotions-27 dataset characteristics:} The emotion induction context may introduce systematic frequency variability compared to resting-state or meditation recordings, potentially explaining attenuated effect magnitudes in this dataset.

    \item \textbf{Descriptive, not mechanistic:} We document $\phisym^n$ organization but cannot fully explain why neural oscillations organize according to golden ratio mathematics. Pletzer et al. \parencite{pletzer2010golden} provide theoretical reasons why $\phisym$ is mathematically optimal for avoiding spurious synchronization, but the biophysical mechanisms enforcing $\phisym^n$ constraints remain unclear. One possibility---that GABA-A receptor kinetics and membrane time constants have been evolutionarily tuned to produce compatible $\phisym$-spaced frequencies---requires direct experimental investigation.

    \item \textbf{No behavioral correlates:} We did not measure whether SIE occurrence or $\phisym^n$ precision predicts cognitive performance.

    \item \textbf{Single-subject discovery:} The initial discovery dataset relied on one experienced meditator, though validation extended to 91 participants.

    \item \textbf{Retrospective power analysis:} As a discovery study, sample sizes were determined by available data rather than a priori power calculations. However, sensitivity analyses (Table~\ref{tab:power_analysis}) confirm that observed effect sizes ($d = 0.89$--$1.44$) substantially exceeded minimum detectable effects ($d = 0.09$), indicating adequately powered conclusions. The frequency independence finding (all $|r| < 0.03$) was supported by 80\% power to detect $r \geq 0.08$, confirming genuine independence rather than insufficient sensitivity.

    \item \textbf{Validation, not discovery:} The theoretical framework for $\phisym$-ratio organization of neural oscillations was established by Pletzer et al.\ \parencite{pletzer2010golden}, who proved mathematically that the golden ratio uniquely prevents spurious synchronization. Our contribution is empirical validation and extension---identifying the absolute fundamental frequency $f_0 = 7.6$ Hz, characterizing SIE transient states, and quantifying the boundary-attractor structure---not independent discovery of $\phisym$ architecture.

    \item \textbf{Search window constraints:} The SIE detection pipeline uses fixed frequency search windows that partially constrain possible outcomes. While observed precision (0.64\% ratio error) significantly exceeds the $\sim$4.75\% error expected from random sampling within windows, Study 1 findings should be interpreted with awareness that detection geometry influences the parameter space. Study 2's continuous spectral analysis (FOOOF parameterization of all peaks regardless of SIE detection) provides complementary evidence not subject to this constraint.

    \item \textbf{$f_0$ shift between studies:} The peak structural score in Study~4 occurred at $f_0 = 8.3$--$8.5$~Hz, approximately 1~Hz above the $f_0 = 7.6$~Hz identified in Studies~1--3. While we interpret this as reflecting the difference between a transient synchronization anchor and a population-level spectral centroid (Section~\ref{sec:study4_f0shift}), this interpretation requires further validation. The shift could also reflect dataset-specific properties (e.g., motor task activation in EEGMMIDB vs.\ meditation/cognitive tasks in the primary datasets).

    \item \textbf{Rational/irrational classification:} The structural specificity analysis partitions nine exponential bases into four irrational and five rational by mathematical definition. This is a fixed partition---different subsets of bases could yield different statistical outcomes. The exact permutation test ($\binom{9}{4} = 126$ assignments) addresses this by exhaustively evaluating all possible 4-vs-5 groupings.

    \item \textbf{Structural score interpretation:} Negative structural scores for rational bases indicate that peaks are \textit{depleted} at positions predicted by rational exponential scaling relative to chance. This is anti-correlation, not evidence of active rejection---the brain does not ``know'' about or ``avoid'' rational lattice coordinates. The observation reflects statistical patterning in peak distributions, not an active mechanism.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Future Directions}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Concurrent EEG-magnetometer recording:} Direct test of SR coupling hypotheses requires simultaneous measurement of brain activity and Schumann Resonance field strength.

    \item \textbf{High-density EEG with source localization:} Extend GED analysis to high-density recordings to clarify spatial organization of $\phisym^n$ generators and validate the frontal hub topology suggested by transfer entropy analysis.

    \item \textbf{Trivariate cross-frequency coupling:} Standard bivariate measures (phase-amplitude coupling) may miss critical trivariate interactions predicted by the golden triplet framework \parencite{kramer2022golden}. Development of trivariate coupling measures---potentially extending bicoherence analysis to capture three-rhythm resonance ($f_{k-1} + f_k \rightarrow f_{k+1}$)---could reveal coupling patterns invisible to current methods.

    \item \textbf{Cross-species validation:} Test whether $\phisym^n$ architecture is evolutionarily conserved across species with different brain sizes. Preliminary evidence suggests similar frequency organization in mammals despite 17,000-fold brain volume variation \parencite{buzsaki2013scaling}.

    \item \textbf{Developmental trajectory:} Does $\phisym^n$ organization emerge with neural maturation, or is it present from early development?

    \item \textbf{Clinical biomarkers:} Do disorders with oscillatory abnormalities (schizophrenia, Parkinson's, ADHD) show disrupted $\phisym^n$ organization? Could restoration serve as treatment target?

    \item \textbf{Entrainment studies:} Test whether tACS at $\phisym^n$ frequencies produces stronger entrainment or superior cognitive effects compared to non-$\phisym^n$ frequencies \parencite{herrmann2016eeg}.

    \item \textbf{Behavioral correlates:} Examine whether SIE precision or aggregate $\phisym^n$ alignment predicts cognitive performance, creativity, or meditative depth.

    \item \textbf{Data-driven boundary validation:} Cohen \parencite{cohen2021gedbounds} developed gedBounds, a multivariate method that identifies empirical frequency boundaries through covariance-based clustering. Applying this approach to test whether data-driven boundaries converge on $\phisym^n$ integer positions would provide independent validation from a fundamentally different analytical framework. The method also enables examination of individual differences in boundary positioning relative to the population-level $\phisym^n$ lattice.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusions}

This study makes six principal contributions to our understanding of neural oscillatory organization:

\textbf{First, we validate $\phisym^n$ architecture in continuous spectral organization.} Systematic analysis of 244,955 spectral peaks---detected agnostically via FOOOF without reference to $\phisym^n$ predictions---confirms the predicted boundary-attractor structure: boundaries depleted ($-18\%$), attractors enriched ($+21\%$), noble positions maximally enriched ($+39\%$). Independent replication in 612,990 peaks from the EEGEmotions-27 dataset strengthens this finding. This analysis provides the most robust evidence for $\phisym^n$ organization, as it does not depend on event detection pipeline assumptions.

\textbf{Second, we confirm $\phisym^n$ architecture at the network level through multi-channel spatial coherence.} Generalized Eigendecomposition analysis of 1,584,561 peaks across 3,261 sessions---using spatial coherence rather than single-channel spectral prominence---reproduces the identical position hierarchy (Kendall's $\tau = 1.0$), with 77.8\% session-level consistency. This methodological convergence with single-channel FOOOF analysis establishes that $\phisym^n$ organization reflects genuine multi-channel network architecture, not artifacts of spectral parameterization.

\textbf{Third, we establish Schumann Ignition Events as reproducible transient phenomena.} Across 1,366 events, 91 participants, five cognitive contexts, and three consumer EEG devices, SIEs showed consistent spectral, temporal, and network characteristics. The coherence-first temporal signature suggests these represent qualitatively distinct neural states. These findings should be interpreted with awareness that the detection pipeline's search windows partially constrain outcomes, though observed precision exceeds geometric baselines.

\textbf{Fourth, we observe $\phisym^n$ ratio relationships in SIE harmonics that complement the continuous architecture.} Analysis of harmonic ratios revealed $<1\%$ deviation from golden ratio predictions. Shuffled bootstrap analysis indicates this precision emerges primarily from population-level distributional properties rather than within-event coordination, consistent with the substrate-ignition model where continuous $\phisym^n$ organization is transiently amplified during SIE states.

\textbf{Fifth, we propose the substrate-ignition model as an integrative framework.} The $\phisym^n$ lattice exists continuously as an architectural constraint; SIEs represent transient amplification of this substrate. This model explains both the precision of transient events and the robustness of aggregate spectral organization.

\textbf{Sixth, we demonstrate that $\phisym^n$ organization is structurally specific to irrational bases and replicate all core findings in an independent dataset.} Analysis of 1.42--1.86 million peaks from the EEGMMIDB dataset (109 subjects, 64 channels) replicated the boundary-depletion and noble-enrichment pattern. Structural specificity testing of nine exponential bases---each evaluated with its own natural lattice positions---revealed that $\phisym$ achieves the highest structural score (SS $= 45.6$), irrational bases collectively outperform rational bases across a broad frequency window (bootstrap $p < 0.001$), and non-exponential models score near zero. The ``irrational dominance window'' ($f_0 = 7.1$--$9.4$~Hz) entirely contains the Schumann Resonance variation range, providing a new empirical constraint on the SR relationship.

The core finding can be summarized in a single equation:
\begin{equation}
f(n) = f_0 \times \phisym^n \quad \text{where } f_0 = 7.60 \text{ Hz}, \quad \phisym = 1.6180339...
\end{equation}

Whether the correspondence between neural $f_0$ and Earth's Schumann Resonance reflects evolutionary optimization, biophysical convergence, or coincidence cannot be determined without concurrent geomagnetic measurement. What the present findings establish is that human neural oscillations exhibit precise $\phisym^n$ ratio organization---a mathematical architecture that may represent a universal solution to the segregation-integration balance required for complex neural computation.
